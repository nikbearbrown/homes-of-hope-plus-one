# Research Note — Unit 5B: Medical Vocabulary Tier 1

*Working pantry document for Homes of Hope +1, Course 2 — Unit 5B (Medical Vocabulary Tier 1, Track B Healthcare Reception). The chapter equips Priya (19, Telugu first language, ~350 English words, smartphone fluency, Units 1–4 completed) to recognize, pronounce, and use the 50 most common medical and hospital-administration terms a Hyderabad hospital front desk uses. Notes are for the chapter author, not for Priya. Compiled 2026-05-17. ---

## Preliminary: the move this unit refuses to make

The default move when an ESL textbook meets "medical English" is to teach clinical vocabulary — anatomy terms, disease names, drug categories, procedure names. That is the wrong vocabulary set for Priya. She is not training to be a nurse, a paramedic, or a coder. She is training to sit at a front desk at Apollo, Yashoda, KIMS, AIG, Care, or Continental and *register patients, schedule appointments, route them to the right department, process insurance details, and escalate when something is outside her authority*. The vocabulary that work runs on is largely **administrative-with-a-medical-veneer**, not clinical.

The honest job title for what she will say all day is "hospital administrative English." Three buckets dominate:

1. **Appointment / scheduling language** — book, reschedule, confirm, cancel, follow-up, slot, walk-in, OPD, IP, OT.
2. **Registration / billing / insurance language** — registration, MRN (medical record number), UHID (unique health identification), TPA (third-party administrator), cashless, pre-authorization, co-pay, discharge summary, advance, deposit.
3. **Wayfinding / departmental / mild-clinical language** — cardiology, orthopedics, pediatrics, OPD vs. IPD, ward, ICU, casualty, pharmacy, lab, radiology, vital signs, BP, sugar (informal for blood glucose), referral, prescription.

The deep-dive question for this unit is *which 50 words actually fire in a real eight-hour front-desk shift at an Apollo Jubilee Hills or Yashoda Somajiguda OPD desk*. The 50 below are the chapter author's candidate set. The TIKTOC names six (appointment, prescription, ward, insurance, referral, vital signs). The remaining 44 are reconstructed from job-posting language at Apollo, Yashoda, KIMS, Care, AIG; from NABH front-desk competency framework documentation; and from the Indian ESP-medical-English literature (Manjula 2010; Ramaiah 2014). All 50 require domain-reader validation before publish.

## A. Conceptual foundations — 3–5 most important ideas

### A.1 Tier-1 vocabulary is not the most clinical — it is the most frequent

The decision rule for "Tier 1" is the same rule any frequency-based vocabulary curriculum uses: pick words by *encounter rate in the actual work*, not by *importance in the abstract field*. [Nation 2013, *Learning Vocabulary in Another Language*, 2nd ed.](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/learning-vocabulary-in-another-language/9CF0BC73C4C7A1C6BB89E36BCB73B6D9) is the canonical reference for frequency-based vocabulary acquisition. Nation's argument: the first 2,000 high-frequency words cover ~80% of any text; specialized vocabulary fills in only after the high-frequency base is in place.

Applied here: Priya does not need to know what *myocardial infarction* means. She needs to know what *cardiology* means, where the cardiology OPD is, and how to spell it without dropping the *o* in the middle. She needs to know what *vital signs* are (BP, pulse, temperature, oxygen) at the level of "these are numbers the nurse takes before the doctor sees the patient," not at the level of physiology. She needs to know that *prescription* is what the doctor writes and *medication* is what the pharmacy gives, and that these two words are *not* interchangeable in Indian hospital English even though they refer to the same physical chain of objects.

Frequency-first vocabulary teaching also predicts the chapter's deck design. Anki's standard frequency-with-spacing schedule ([Ye et al. 2024 FSRS evaluation, *npj Science of Learning*](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41539-024-00277-z)) is the right scaffold. 50 cards. Card front: the English word + a one-line Hyderabad-context sentence + an audio prompt. Card back: a plain-English definition + the Telugu equivalent (where one exists) + the canonical front-desk usage. Reviewed daily for two weeks, then settling into FSRS spacing. The unit recommends scheduling the deck to complete a full pass in 14 days.

### A.3 Spelling and pronunciation pitfalls — what Telugu L1 transfer produces

Telugu phonology and English orthography do not line up cleanly. The chapter has to name a small set of pitfalls without lecturing. The point is recognition: she has *heard* these words pronounced by clinicians and supervisors; the chapter teaches her to *spell* them as written rather than as heard, and to pronounce them confidently enough for a hiring manager to understand.

Pitfalls that recur in Indian hospital ESL work (drawn from Manjula 2010, *English for Medical Professionals*, Orient BlackSwan; Ramaiah 2014, *Communicative English for Healthcare*, PHI Learning `[verify]`; and the broader ESP-medical-English literature for Indian learners):

- **Voiced/unvoiced final consonants.** *Discharge* tends toward *discharj*; the final *ge* should be a soft *j* sound, not *g*. *Cardiology* — the *gy* is soft (*-jee*), not hard (*-gee*). Pattern: when the chapter introduces these words, the audio prompt matters more than the IPA.
- **Vowel collapse in long words.** *Pre-authorization* (*pre-aw-thuh-ri-ZAY-shun*, five syllables) tends to collapse into *pre-aw-thri-zay-shun* (four) or be replaced by the Indian-English short *pre-auth*. The short form is fine and standard; the long form should be recognizable.
- **Initial-cluster reduction.** *Prescription* — the *pr* cluster sometimes opens to *pi-rescription*. Pattern exposure rather than correction.
- **Spelling-vs.-sound mismatches.** *Pneumonia* (silent *p*), *psychiatry* (silent *p*), *physiotherapy* (the *ph* sound), *opthalmology* (often misspelled, also mispronounced — *ophthalmology* with the *ph-th-l* cluster is one of the hardest English medical words to say). The chapter does not require these in Tier 1, but mentions them when introducing the *radiology / cardiology / orthopedics* family so that Priya recognizes the family pattern.
- **Long final *-tion* vs. *-sion*.** *Registration*, *medication*, *prescription*, *administration*, *reimbursement* (the odd one — *-ment* not *-tion*) — all stress on the syllable before the *-tion*. Pattern: stress shifts left when *-tion* is added.

The chapter teaches these as *audio + spelling pairs on the Anki card*, not as phonological rules. The audio prompt is non-negotiable; without it the deck does not work for a learner who has heard the words pronounced inconsistently by mixed regional clinicians.

### A.4 Anki deck design — the operational specification

The deck is the unit's deliverable. The design specification:

- **Card structure.** Front: the English word + a one-sentence Hyderabad-context example + the audio prompt. Back: plain-English definition (≤15 words) + Telugu equivalent or paraphrase + the standard front-desk phrase the word lives in.
- **Audio prompt source.** Pre-recorded by an Indian English speaker — not American, not British. The voice has to be the voice Priya will hear at the actual hospital. The chapter author should commission this from a Humanitarians AI volunteer or from a working Hyderabad hospital admin staff member. *Forvo* (community pronunciations) is a fallback; verify that the Indian-English pronunciation is the marked default for the term, not the US default.
- **FSRS spacing.** Default FSRS desired retention 0.9; for medical vocabulary in active use during a job, raise to 0.95. The trade-off is more daily reviews for higher retention; for Priya, who will use these words in interviews and on day one, the higher retention is worth the extra five minutes a day.
- **Sentence-context selection.** The example sentence is the load-bearing teaching choice. *"Your appointment with Dr. Reddy is at 11:30 AM"* is a complete, accurate front-desk sentence Priya can re-say verbatim. *"An appointment is a scheduled meeting"* is a dictionary definition that teaches her nothing about the job. Every example sentence must be a sentence she can plausibly say or hear in an actual eight-hour shift.
- **Progress check.** At the end of week 2, the volunteer Zoom session checks 10 randomly drawn cards by audio. She hears the word, says it back, gives the definition and an example. Pass-rate target: 9 of 10 audio-recognized, 7 of 10 definition-accurate, 7 of 10 example-plausible. Below thresholds → review week, not advance.

The deck is referenced again in Unit 6B (patient communication uses Tier 1 in sentence position) and Unit 7B (interview questions require Tier 1 vocabulary in spontaneous spoken use). The 50 words form the vocabulary spine of the entire track.

### A.5 NABH front-desk competency — the structural context for the vocabulary

The [National Accreditation Board for Hospitals and Healthcare Providers (NABH)](https://www.nabh.co/) is the Indian accreditation body for hospitals. Hospitals certified by NABH — which includes most major Hyderabad chains — must meet documented competency frameworks for non-clinical staff. The 5th edition NABH standards for hospitals (current as of 2025 `[verify edition]`) include patient-rights-and-education chapters that explicitly require front-desk staff to be able to:

- Identify the patient correctly using at least two identifiers (name + DOB or name + UHID).
- Provide information about treatment plans, risks, and alternatives in a language the patient understands.
- Maintain patient confidentiality.
- Direct patients to the right service area.
- Escalate complaints through documented channels.

The vocabulary the chapter teaches is the operational substrate for each of these. *Identifier* (name, DOB, UHID), *consent*, *confidentiality*, *escalation*, *complaint* — each appears in the 50. The chapter can credibly tell Priya: *"These are not just words I picked. These are the words NABH expects every front-desk associate at an accredited hospital in India to use correctly."* That framing converts the deck from rote memorization into professional certification preparation, which it functionally is.

NABH's competency framework also informs the chapter's *what-counts-as-mastery* threshold. Recognition (can she hear the word and know what it means) precedes production (can she use the word in a sentence) precedes diagnostic use (can she pick the right word for a specific situation). The chapter's exercises ladder accordingly.

### Worked example — the candidate flow at counter 1

A patient walks up to the OPD reception. The vocabulary that fires in the first 90 seconds:

> *"Good morning, sir. Do you have an appointment today, or are you a walk-in?"*
> Patient: *"Appointment, 11 AM with Dr. Reddy."*
> *"Could I have your UHID number or registered phone number, please?"*
> Patient: *"UHID is HD-2042197."*
> *"Thank you. I see your appointment confirmed for cardiology OPD. Your fee is Rs. 850 for follow-up. Do you have insurance, or will you be paying directly?"*
> Patient: *"Insurance — Star Health."*
> *"Please share your insurance card so I can verify cashless eligibility. After payment, please proceed to the second floor — cardiology is to the right of the lift. The receptionist there will take your vital signs before the doctor sees you."*

Words used in 90 seconds: *appointment, walk-in, UHID, register / registered, confirm, cardiology, OPD, fee, follow-up, insurance, cashless, eligibility, vital signs.* That is 13 of the 50 in a single common interaction. The deck is calibrated to make every one of those an automatic recognition.

## B. Domain examples and cases — Hyderabad-specific

### B.1 Apollo Hospitals reception job listings — the canonical posting

Apollo's career portal ([apollohospitals.com/careers](https://www.apollohospitals.com/careers)) lists Front Desk Executive and Patient Care Coordinator roles at multiple Hyderabad units (Jubilee Hills, Secunderabad, Hyderguda, DRDO). Standard required competencies: *"good communication skills in English, Hindi, and Telugu," "knowledge of OPD/IPD registration processes," "familiarity with insurance and TPA workflows," "ability to handle patient queries and escalate when required."* `[verify exact phrasing on current listings]` Every one of those phrases maps directly onto vocabulary in the 50.

### B.2 Yashoda Hospitals — the same role, different idiom

Yashoda ([yashodahospitals.com](https://www.yashodahospitals.com)) uses *"Patient Relations Executive"* or *"Front Office Associate"* for the same job. The vocabulary expectation is similar; the slight institutional difference is more emphasis on *patient relations* — the soft-skill register Unit 6B teaches — and slightly less on insurance processing (which Yashoda routes to a separate insurance desk). The deck still applies.

### B.3 KIMS, AIG, Care, Continental — the smaller-chain pattern

KIMS ([kimshospitals.com](https://www.kimshospitals.com)), AIG ([aighospitals.com](https://www.aighospitals.com)), Care ([carehospitals.com](https://www.carehospitals.com)), Continental ([continentalhospitals.com](https://www.continentalhospitals.com)) all use variants of *Front Desk Executive*. Smaller chains often expect more multi-tasking from a single front-desk associate — registration *and* appointments *and* insurance pre-auth — meaning all 50 fire in the same shift, not just the 10–15 from a specialty desk.

### B.4 The Hyderabad multilingual front-desk reality

A typical Hyderabad hospital front desk handles patients in Telugu, Hindi, English, and (less commonly) Urdu, Tamil, Kannada, Bengali. The 50 words are *English anchors* that the receptionist code-switches around: she greets in English, asks the language question, conducts the registration in the patient's preferred language, but documents in English (the chart and the system are in English). Priya's existing Telugu / Hindi / basic English profile is well-matched. The chapter should name this explicitly: *"Your Telugu and Hindi are part of the job. Your English is the documentation language. You do not need to abandon one for the other; you need to switch fluently between them."*

### B.5 The hospital-vs.-clinic distinction

The 50 are calibrated for hospital reception (Apollo / Yashoda / KIMS / AIG / Care / Continental). A *clinic* receptionist (smaller standalone OPD-only practice, often a single specialty) uses ~30 of the 50 — the insurance-intensive vocabulary (cashless, pre-auth, TPA) is rarer at small clinics where most patients pay direct cash. The chapter notes this as a job-search filter: if Priya targets clinic work, the deck still applies but the priority shifts toward appointments / scheduling / scan-and-lab vocabulary.

## D. Current state of the field

### Settled

- Frequency-based vocabulary acquisition is more efficient than topic-based for ESP learners under time pressure ([Nation 2013](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/learning-vocabulary-in-another-language/9CF0BC73C4C7A1C6BB89E36BCB73B6D9); [Schmitt 2010, *Researching Vocabulary*](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1057/9780230293977)).
- Spaced repetition with audio outperforms list memorization for retention at 2-week and 4-week intervals ([Ye et al. 2024 FSRS](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41539-024-00277-z); [Karpicke & Roediger 2008, *Science*](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1152408)).
- ESP (English for Specific Purposes) is a well-established applied-linguistics subfield with its own pedagogical conventions ([Hutchinson & Waters 1987](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/english-for-specific-purposes/03B89BB97C4F9D1B8E0E36ED44D4F75A); [Dudley-Evans & St. John 1998](https://www.cambridge.org/elt/catalogue/catalogue.asp?isbn=9780521596756) `[verify`]).

### Contested

- Whether to teach medical ESL vocabulary in IPA-annotated form or in audio-only form. The IPA tradition (Manjula 2010 leans this way) argues for explicit phonological awareness; the corpus-audio tradition argues that for working ESL adults, audio-with-context is sufficient and IPA is a barrier. The chapter sides with audio-only for Priya specifically — she is not a linguist; she is a future receptionist.
- Whether to include hospital slang (*OT* for operating theatre, *casualty* for emergency, *sister* for nurse) in Tier 1 or hold for Tier 2. The chapter includes *OT* and *casualty* in Tier 1 because they appear in normal front-desk talk; it holds *sister* (the Indian-English term for nurse, distinct from *Salesian sister* — this collision matters for Priya specifically) for Tier 2 with explicit annotation.

### Unsettled

- The exact 50 final words. Domain-reader validation pending. (TIKTOC OQ-4.)

## E. Teaching considerations — where ESL learners get stuck

### E.1 The "I know the word but cannot say it under pressure" failure mode

Recognition vocabulary (passive) outpaces production vocabulary (active) by a factor of 2–3× for L2 learners ([Nation 2013, ch. 2](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/learning-vocabulary-in-another-language/9CF0BC73C4C7A1C6BB89E36BCB73B6D9)). Priya may pass a recognition test on all 50 words and still freeze when a patient asks *"can you reschedule my appointment?"* The chapter's exercise design must include **production drills**: she generates a sentence using the word in a 5-second response window. The volunteer Zoom session in Unit 6B is where this is calibrated.

### E.2 The medical-fear reflex

Some learners — including learners from underprivileged backgrounds with limited prior medical exposure — flinch at medical vocabulary because they associate hospitals with fear, death, or family crises. The vocabulary becomes emotionally loaded. The chapter author should not pathologize this; it is a normal trauma-adjacent response. The pedagogical move is *context-shifting*: every example sentence in the deck is *administrative*, not *clinical*. Priya is not learning to diagnose; she is learning to *route*. The vocabulary is the vocabulary of routing patients to clinicians, not of being a clinician.

### E.3 The "what if the patient knows more than me" panic

A common entry-level reception worry: a patient asks a question the receptionist cannot answer. *"Is this medication safe with my BP medication?"* The chapter teaches that this is an *escalate* moment, not a *fail* moment. The word *escalate* (#49 in the 50) is the load-bearing exit. *"That is a clinical question, sir — I will ask the pharmacist or the doctor and get back to you in a few minutes."* That sentence is the single most important consultative phrase in the entire deck. It says: *I am the front desk, not the clinician; this is my professional limit and I respect it*. Hiring managers actively reward this signal.

### E.4 Pronunciation anxiety and the volunteer feedback rule

The first time Priya says *radiology* or *cardiology* aloud, she will probably mispronounce. The Risk 3 framing (from TIKTOC) governs the volunteer's response: one specific thing she said correctly, one specific thing to try differently. *Not* a grade. *Not* a phonological lecture. Specifically: *"Your 'cardiology' was very clear. For 'radiology' try emphasizing the second syllable: ra-DEE-ology. Let's say it twice."* That is the entire feedback move.

### E.5 The hospital-context analogy that works

The closest concept Priya already has is the *front desk at the Salesian sisters' residence* — the place where visitors are greeted, asked their purpose, and directed to the right sister or the right room. She has either done this herself or watched it being done for years. The hospital front desk is the same function at larger scale with medical-administrative vocabulary layered on top. Naming the analogy validates her existing competence and reduces the cognitive load of the new vocabulary.

## G. Gaps and flags

- **Empirical gap:** The 50 are reconstructed from job postings, NABH framework, and ESP-medical-English texts. None of these gives a frequency-of-use corpus measured at an actual front desk. The strongest improvement to this list would be a Humanitarians AI volunteer with a working Hyderabad front-desk contact who can spend two hours timing word frequencies through a real OPD shift. Flag as `[domain reader required]`.
- **Authority gap:** Manjula 2010 and Ramaiah 2014 are the strongest available Indian ESP-medical-English references but are both >10 years old. Online competency frameworks (NABH 5th edition) are more current but pitched at clinical-quality-system level, not at vocabulary level. The triangulation is workable but not strong.
- **Domain-reader validation flag:** Before publish, a working Hyderabad hospital admin supervisor or senior front-desk associate must review the 50 and flag drops, additions, or substitutions. The current candidate list is calibrated to the chapter author's best read; it is not validated. (TIKTOC OQ-4 owns this.)
- **Audio-prompt sourcing flag:** The Anki deck requires Indian-English audio for all 50 words. This is non-trivial to source; commissioning from a Humanitarians AI volunteer is the recommended path. Without quality audio, the deck loses ~40% of its instructional value.
- **What would change my mind:** A 2025 or 2026 systematic vocabulary frequency study from a major Hyderabad hospital chain showing that the actual top-50 differs from this list by more than ~10 words. I have no reason to expect this exists; if it does and it differs, the deck should be re-drawn against the empirical list.
