# Research Note — Unit 6C: Donor and Partner Communication

*Working pantry document for Homes of Hope +1, Course 2 — Unit 6C (Donor and Partner Communication, Track C NGO Administration). The chapter teaches Priya (19, Telugu first language, ~350 English words, Units 1–4 done, Unit 5C four-move paragraph rehearsed) to write a professional introduction email to an international donor or partner organization, appropriate in register and free of cultural errors that would undermine credibility. Notes are for the chapter author. Compiled 2026-05-17.*

---

## Preliminary: the unit's thesis

Track C is the one track where a register closer to international English actually matters — because the audience is a Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation (BMGF) program officer in Seattle, a UNICEF coordinator in Geneva, a USAID grants officer in Washington, or a UK-based individual philanthropist. The Indian Business English register that gets Priya hired into a BPO seat and a hospital front desk is not the register that gets a small Hyderabad NGO's program officer taken seriously by a US foundation. The register has to shift.

But the shift is *narrow*. It is not a wholesale move to British or American English. It is the removal of three specific Indian-English markers that read as outdated or unprofessional to international donors, and the addition of two specific international-NGO conventions. The unit is precisely about those five adjustments. Everything else in Priya's register from Unit 1 stays.

The single failure mode the chapter must prevent: *Priya copies a 1990s Indian-government letter format ("Respected Sir, Kindly do the needful at your earliest possible convenience...") into an email to a Seattle program officer who does not recognize the register, finds it formal-to-the-point-of-archaic, and quietly down-rates the application*. Equally, the chapter must prevent the opposite: *Priya writes a TikTok-casual email ("Hey Sarah! Hope this finds you well, just wanted to circle back...") that lands as unprepared, junior, and culturally tone-deaf*. The target is between: *plain, specific, respectful international English*.

## A. Conceptual foundations — 3–5 most important ideas

### A.1 The introduction email structure — six components

A professional introduction email to an international donor follows a stable structure ([Indiana University Lilly Family School of Philanthropy materials](https://philanthropy.iupui.edu/) `[verify`]; [BMGF grantee handbook](https://www.gatesfoundation.org/about/policies-and-resources/grantee-handbook); [Foundation Center / Candid email-outreach materials](https://learning.candid.org/)). The chapter teaches it as six numbered components:

1. **Subject line that describes the email.** *"Introduction — [Org Name], girls' education program in Telangana — exploring partnership"*. Not *"Hello"*. Not *"Grant Application from India"*. The donor's inbox has 200 emails; the subject line is the difference between an opened and a deleted message.
2. **Greeting.** *"Dear Dr. Smith"* if the program officer holds a doctorate; *"Dear Ms. Smith"* otherwise; *"Dear Sarah Smith"* on first email if title is unknown. Indian *Sir/Madam* in initial international email reads as overformal and slightly outdated — the chapter teaches the substitution. After the first reply, follow the contact's lead: if they sign *"Sarah"*, the next email opens *"Hi Sarah"*.
3. **Organizational-credibility paragraph (50–70 words).** Who the org is, when founded, what it does, where, scale. *"Asha Hyderabad is a community-based organization registered in 2009 under the Karnataka Societies Act `[verify Andhra/Telangana registration mechanism]`. We run after-school education and health programs for 1,200 adolescent girls across three districts of Telangana. Our work is funded by individual Indian donors (60%), Tata Trusts (25%), and small international foundations (15%)."*
4. **Specific ask paragraph (60–80 words).** What Priya wants the donor to consider, anchored in the donor's stated priorities. *"We are exploring partners for the next phase of our girls' secondary-education retention program (2026–2029). Your foundation's recent funding of similar programs in Bihar and Jharkhand suggested an alignment with our approach. We would like to share a 4-page concept note and explore whether a 30-minute call would be useful at your convenience."*
5. **Meeting/call request.** Specific, time-zone aware. *"We are available between 6:30 PM and 9:00 PM IST (Mon–Thu), which corresponds to 8:00–10:30 AM Pacific Time. If a different window works better for you, please suggest one."* Naming the time zone *and* the conversion is the credibility move.
6. **Signature.** Full name, role, organization, phone (with country code: +91), email, organization website. *"Priya Brown / Program Associate / Asha Hyderabad / +91 98XXX XXXXX / priya@ashahyd.org / www.ashahyd.org"*. The country code matters; *9899X XXXXX* without +91 confuses an international reader.

The chapter teaches this as a literal six-component template. Priya fills each component with her organization's specifics. The structure is the credibility scaffold; her judgment lives inside the slot-fills.

### A.2 The five register adjustments — what changes between Indian Business English and international donor English

The chapter names exactly five register adjustments. Three removals from Indian-English defaults; two additions of international-NGO conventions.

**Remove 1 — *Sir/Madam* in opening salutation.** Indian default. International default uses *Dear [Title] [Surname]* or *Dear [First name] [Last name]*. The chapter teaches: *Sir/Madam* in body text (rare) is also avoided.

**Remove 2 — *Kindly do the needful* and *Kindly revert at your earliest convenience*.** Indian-English business standard. Reads to a Seattle reader as archaic or unclear (*"the needful" — what specifically?*). Replacements: *"Please let me know if this would be useful"* or *"I look forward to your thoughts"* or *"Please let me know if a call would work"*. The chapter teaches three plain-English substitutions and drills them.

**Remove 3 — *Respected Sir / Yours faithfully / Yours sincerely* register.** 1950s Indian government letter register. Reads as bureaucratically distant in international email. Replacements: *Dear [Name]* opener, *Best regards / Kind regards / With thanks* close. The chapter teaches both *Best regards* and *Kind regards* as equivalent international-standard sign-offs and notes that *Sincerely* is slightly more American while *Kind regards* is slightly more British — both are professional, neither is wrong.

**Add 1 — Time-zone naming.** Indian email default omits time-zone. International donor email *must* state IST + the donor's local time, or at minimum offer multiple windows. *"Available 6:30–9:00 PM IST Mon–Thu (8:00–10:30 AM PT)"* is a credibility-marker; *"Available evenings"* reads as unprepared.

**Add 2 — Specific organizational scale and funding-source statement.** International donors want to know: *Is this a real organization? Who else funds it? What scale do they operate at?* The credibility paragraph from §A.1 carries this. Indian-default introduction emails often skip this and go straight to the ask. The chapter teaches: state scale and funding mix in the first paragraph; do not assume the donor knows.

The chapter teaches these as five named substitution drills: bad → good pairs. Priya practices each pair until the substitution is automatic.

### A.3 Cultural register errors that undermine credibility — named, not implied

The chapter is explicit about errors. Cultural code-switching is a *learnable skill*, not a forbidden topic. The four most damaging errors, each named, each diagnosed, each fixed:

1. **Over-formal British-Indian register.** *"Respected Madam, I am writing this email to most humbly enquire whether your esteemed foundation might consider..."* — reads as supplicant rather than partner. International donors fund *organizations*, not *humble petitioners*. The fix: confident-but-respectful register from the Unit 1 consultative-formal anchor.
2. **First-name address on initial email.** *"Hi Sarah, hope this finds you well!"* on first contact reads as presumptuous to many international donors (especially in foundation philanthropy, where senior program officers expect a degree of formality on first contact). The fix: *Dear Ms. Smith* on first email; switch to first name after the contact does.
3. **Untranslated regional jargon.** *"Our intervention focuses on SHGs in mandals across the district, particularly in tribal sub-districts where ICDS coverage is poor."* — SHG (self-help group), mandal (sub-district), ICDS (Integrated Child Development Services) are Indian-administrative-development vocabulary that an international reader may not parse. The fix: define on first use, then use the acronym. *"Our work is with women's self-help groups (SHGs — savings-and-credit collectives of 10–20 women) in sub-districts (mandals) of Telangana, particularly in areas where the government's child-nutrition program (ICDS) has thin coverage."*
4. **Time-zone insensitivity.** *"Available next Tuesday at 4 PM."* — at 4 PM IST it is 5:30 AM in Seattle. International donors will read this as *Priya did not check what time this is for me*, which signals junior preparation. The fix: §A.2 Add 1.

The chapter teaches: these are *errors of unfamiliarity, not of intelligence*. The Indian writer who has never had reason to think about Pacific Time has no reason to know. The unit's job is to make her aware.

### A.4 International donor norms at the program-officer level

The chapter teaches Priya what she is talking *to* — not just what she is writing. The program officer's day at a major international donor:

- **BMGF.** Program officers manage portfolios of 10–30 active grants. They receive 100+ unsolicited contacts per month. Initial unsolicited contact is almost never how grants get made; relationships are usually built through conferences, mutual contacts, or invited calls. Priya's introduction email is reasonably positioned as *exploratory*, not as *grant application*. ([BMGF grant process overview](https://www.gatesfoundation.org/about/policies-and-resources/grantee-handbook).)
- **UNICEF.** Country offices and regional offices have specific procurement and partnership procedures. *Implementing-partner agreements* are common in India through UNICEF India. Direct international-HQ contact is less likely to bear fruit; the Hyderabad NGO is more likely to be in dialogue with UNICEF India staff. ([UNICEF India partnerships](https://www.unicef.org/india/partnerships).)
- **UNESCO.** Smaller direct-implementation budget than UNICEF; works through implementing partners on specific thematic priorities (girls' education, literacy, heritage). ([UNESCO partnerships](https://www.unesco.org/en/partnerships).)
- **USAID.** Operates through bilateral cooperation agreements with the Government of India and through implementing partners. Direct funding to small Indian NGOs is rare but happens through Annual Program Statements (APS) and specific calls. ([USAID India](https://www.usaid.gov/india).)
- **UK-based foundations.** Comic Relief, BBC Children in Need, the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation UK office, individual major donors via charities like the British Asian Trust. ([British Asian Trust](https://britishasiantrust.org).)

The chapter teaches: *not every international email leads to a grant; the introduction email is the start of a multi-month, sometimes multi-year relationship-building process*. Setting Priya's expectation correctly is part of the unit. She is writing an introduction, not asking for money.

### A.5 The voice exemplars — CRY, HelpAge, Akshaya Patra international-partnership pages

The chapter recommends Priya read three Indian NGOs' international-partnership pages as voice models:

- **CRY International / CRY America.** [CRY America](https://www.america.cry.org/) operates as a US 501(c)(3) and produces communication aimed at US donors. The register is plain-language American philanthropy.
- **HelpAge International collaboration with HelpAge India.** [HelpAge International](https://www.helpage.org/) and [HelpAge India](https://www.helpageindia.org/) have a clear partnership-page register that handles the geographic-and-cultural bridge.
- **Akshaya Patra USA / Akshaya Patra UK / Akshaya Patra Foundation.** [Akshaya Patra Foundation](https://www.akshayapatra.org/) operates internationally and its international donor communications are mature. Good voice exemplar.

The chapter assigns: *read one international-partnership page from each, note one phrase that feels professionally calibrated, and one phrase that feels overly formal*. Priya develops register sense through pattern exposure, not through rules.

### Worked example — the introduction email, first draft and corrected draft

**The brief.** Priya works for *Asha Hyderabad* (illustrative — not a real organization). She has been asked by her director to send an introduction email to Sarah Thompson, Program Officer for South Asia Girls' Education at the (illustrative) Whitman Family Foundation, a US-based foundation that has recently funded similar programs in Bihar and Jharkhand. Priya finds the program officer's name and email from the foundation's website.

**Attempt 1 — first draft (over-formal, errors flagged):**

> Subject: Grant request from India
>
> Respected Madam,
>
> I am writing this email to most humbly enquire about possible support from your esteemed foundation for our girls' education programme in Telangana. We are doing very good work for underprivileged adolescent girls in three mandals and our SHG and ICDS-linked interventions have shown holistic transformation.
>
> We have been working since 2009 with full dedication. Kindly do the needful at your earliest convenience and let us know if you can support our project. I would be most grateful if you could schedule a meeting at your earliest possible time.
>
> Thanking you in advance.
>
> Yours faithfully,
> Priya

What is wrong:
- **Subject line** — "Grant request from India" is the kind of email Sarah Thompson deletes without reading. Generic. No org name. Signals broadcast, not relationship.
- **"Respected Madam"** — overformal Indian register; reads as supplicant.
- **"Most humbly enquire / esteemed foundation / full dedication"** — supplicant register, asymmetric power-language.
- **"Underprivileged adolescent girls"** — abstract noun phrase; says nothing specific.
- **"SHG and ICDS-linked interventions / holistic transformation"** — untranslated Indian-administrative jargon plus development-jargon; the reader cannot parse either.
- **No specific ask** — "support our project" is undefined; no concept note offered; no call requested with concrete time windows.
- **"Kindly do the needful"** — archaic register.
- **"Yours faithfully"** — 1950s register.
- **No signature block** — no full name, role, phone, website.

**Attempt 2 — corrected draft (target register):**

> Subject: Introduction — Asha Hyderabad, girls' secondary-education retention program in Telangana
>
> Dear Ms. Thompson,
>
> I am writing to introduce Asha Hyderabad, a community organization that runs after-school education programs for 1,200 adolescent girls across three districts of Telangana. We were established in 2009 and are currently funded by a mix of individual Indian donors (~60%), Tata Trusts (~25%), and small international foundations (~15%).
>
> Your foundation's recent funding of girls' secondary-education programs in Bihar and Jharkhand suggested an alignment with our work. We are planning the next three-year phase of our program (2026–2029), and we would value the opportunity to share a 4-page concept note and explore whether a longer conversation might be useful.
>
> If a 30-minute introductory call is possible, I am available between 6:30 PM and 9:00 PM IST Monday–Thursday, which corresponds to 8:00–10:30 AM Pacific Time. If a different window works better, please suggest one.
>
> Thank you for considering this introduction.
>
> Best regards,
> Priya Brown
> Program Associate, Asha Hyderabad
> +91 98XXX XXXXX | priya@ashahyd.org | www.ashahyd.org

What changed and why:
- **Subject line** — names the organization, the specific program area, and the country region; the program officer can immediately route or file.
- **"Dear Ms. Thompson"** — international standard for first contact when the contact's gender is known but doctorate is not.
- **Credibility paragraph (first paragraph)** — states scale (1,200 girls), age and time (2009), and funding mix (transparency about funder diversity is a credibility signal).
- **Specific ask (second paragraph)** — anchors in the donor's prior funding to demonstrate research; offers a concept note (not a 30-page proposal); asks for a call, not a grant.
- **Time-zone aware availability** — IST + PT conversion + Mon–Thu specificity.
- **"Best regards" close** — international-standard sign-off.
- **Full signature block** — name, role, org, phone with +91, email, website.

Same writer, same organization, same week. Two different reads. The chapter teaches the substitution by working through this kind of paired example.

## B. Domain examples and cases — Hyderabad-specific

### B.1 The Hyderabad NGO international-funding landscape

Hyderabad NGOs that receive significant international funding tend to be larger ones with established partnerships: CARE India (Hyderabad office), Save the Children India, MV Foundation (USAID and Ford Foundation history), Naandi Foundation (Hindustan Unilever, Ford). Smaller Hyderabad NGOs — the kind Priya will most plausibly work for entry-level — pursue international funding more sporadically and often through smaller foundations (Asha for Education chapters in the US, Sankara Foundation, Pratham USA in some cases).

### B.2 Asha for Education — the diaspora-philanthropy model

[Asha for Education](https://ashanet.org/) is a US-based diaspora-volunteer-run organization that funds Indian education programs. It operates with a different register from BMGF — more direct, less hierarchical, often with a India-tech-diaspora cultural register. The introduction email to an Asha chapter would tilt slightly less formal than to a BMGF program officer. The chapter notes this variation.

### B.3 Indian FCRA compliance — the practical constraint

The [Foreign Contribution Regulation Act](https://fcraonline.nic.in/) governs how Indian NGOs receive foreign funds. Registration under FCRA is required to accept foreign donations directly; without it, an NGO can only receive foreign funds through an FCRA-registered intermediary. FCRA registration has tightened significantly since 2020. The chapter does *not* deeply teach FCRA (that is a senior-staff competency), but it flags: *if your organization is not FCRA-registered, the introduction email should specify the receiving partner organization for any potential funding*. Priya's senior colleagues handle this; the awareness matters.

### B.4 The Tata Trusts / Reliance Foundation / Azim Premji Foundation register

Major Indian philanthropic foundations have their own register expectations, which lean Indian-business-formal more than international-foundation-formal. The Tata Trusts ([tatatrusts.org](https://www.tatatrusts.org/)), Reliance Foundation ([reliancefoundation.org](https://www.reliancefoundation.org/)), and Azim Premji Foundation ([azimpremjifoundation.org](https://azimpremjifoundation.org/)) sit between the Hyderabad-local register and the international register. The chapter notes this *as a separate register* — Track C will benefit from knowing both.

### B.5 The volunteer-run small US-foundation contact

A common scenario for Hyderabad NGOs is contact with small US foundations run by 1–3 individuals (a family foundation, a diaspora-led fund, a small interest foundation). These contacts tend to be more personal and less hierarchical. The introduction email register relaxes slightly but the structure holds. The chapter teaches: *adjust by signal, not by template — read the contact's email register and respond at the same level*.

## D. Current state of the field

### Settled

- Plain-language introduction emails outperform supplicant-register or overly-formal-register emails for foundation outreach ([Foundation Center / Candid outreach training](https://learning.candid.org/); [Indiana University Lilly Family School of Philanthropy curriculum](https://philanthropy.iupui.edu/)).
- International donor relationships are built over multiple touch points; the introduction email is a relationship-opener, not a grant request ([BMGF grantee handbook](https://www.gatesfoundation.org/about/policies-and-resources/grantee-handbook); [Bridgespan Group materials on funder relations](https://www.bridgespan.org/)).
- Time-zone awareness and specific availability windows correlate with response rates in international professional outreach ([HBR practitioner literature on cross-cultural business email](https://hbr.org/topic/cross-cultural-management) `[verify`]).

### Contested

- Whether to address an unknown program officer by *Dear Ms. Smith* or *Dear Sarah Smith* on first email. Foundation philanthropy tilts to *Dear Ms. Smith*; tech-sector and younger family foundations tilt to first-name. The chapter teaches *first email = Dear Title Surname; follow contact's lead on reply*.
- Whether the credibility paragraph should foreground the org's Indian funders (signaling local rootedness) or its international funders (signaling international vetting). The chapter teaches: *both, in the funding-mix sentence*.

### Unsettled

- The optimal length of the introduction email. Practitioner consensus: 150–250 words; some advisers recommend longer with embedded data, others shorter with attachments. The chapter targets 200 words including signature.

## E. Teaching considerations — where ESL learners get stuck

### E.1 The supplicant-register reflex

Indian learners writing to international donors often default to *humble petitioner* register. This is a function of school-letter training (*"Most humbly I beg to state that..."*) and Indian-government correspondence conventions still common in older bureaucratic contexts. The chapter's countermove: *you represent an organization; you are extending an introduction between two organizations; your register reflects parity, not pleading*. The drill: rewrite supplicant openings as parity openings.

### E.2 The over-correction into American breezy

The opposite failure: the learner who has watched too much US tech-startup content and writes *"Hey Sarah! Hope you're well! Just wanted to reach out and..."* in an initial foundation email. Reads as junior, unprepared, presumptuous. The chapter teaches: *the consultative register from Unit 1, slightly adjusted via the five substitutions, is the target — not American casual*.

### E.3 The acronym translation discipline

Indian development vocabulary is acronym-dense: SHG, ICDS, MGNREGA, NREGS, RTE, ECCE, NRHM, NHM, NGO, CBO, FBO. The discipline: *define on first use; bracket the acronym; then use freely*. Drilling this in the email-writing exercise transfers to all donor-facing writing.

### E.4 The time-zone arithmetic discipline

Priya may not have done IST-to-PT conversion before. The chapter teaches a small mental model: *PT is IST minus 12.5 hours (or 13.5 hours depending on daylight saving)*. *ET is IST minus 9.5 / 10.5 hours*. *UK is IST minus 4.5 / 5.5 hours*. The chapter recommends checking [timeanddate.com](https://www.timeanddate.com/worldclock/converter.html) for any meeting scheduling — and naming the conversion explicitly in the email.

### E.5 The signature-block credibility weight

A signature block with no phone number or no organizational website reads as *this person may not be from a real organization*. The chapter is explicit: even if the website is rudimentary, include it. Even if the phone is the office landline, include it. The signature is part of the credibility paragraph; missing elements raise unstated questions.

## G. Gaps and flags

- **Empirical gap:** Direct measurement of response rates by register variation for Indian-NGO-to-international-donor email is not in the literature at the scale that would support strong claims. The chapter triangulates from practitioner experience and adjacent cross-cultural email research.
- **Authority gap:** No single 2024–2026 guide to "Indian NGO to international donor email register" exists. The chapter assembles from BMGF handbook, Candid materials, and Asha-for-Education practitioner content.
- **FCRA-currency flag:** FCRA rules have changed multiple times since 2020. The chapter's FCRA mention should be reviewed against current rules at publish.
- **Domain reader:** A working program officer at a small Hyderabad NGO with international-donor outreach experience should review the worked example before publish.
- **What would change my mind:** Evidence that international donor inboxes are responding *better* to a more Indian-formal register than to the adjusted register the chapter teaches. I do not expect this; the practitioner consensus is durable.
