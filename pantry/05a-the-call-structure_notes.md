## Preliminary: what the unit refuses to do

The default failure mode for "teach a BPO call" pedagogy is teaching a script in American English with the implicit goal of accent neutralization. That is not the entry-level Hyderabad job market in 2026. Concentrix, Genpact, Foundever, Teleperformance, HGS Hinduja, and Tech Mahindra BPO all hire entry-level voice agents for domestic Indian customers and for international (US/UK/Australian) customers, but the entry-level filter at the phone screen is *clarity, pacing, and structure compliance* — not accent. Accent neutralization is Tier 2 training, paid for and delivered by the employer after hire, not a candidate-side prerequisite ([NASSCOM Strategic Review 2024](https://nasscom.in) `[verify section`]; converging Naukri recruiter blog evidence `[verify with current Hyderabad BPO recruiter]`).

The chapter teaches the *structure* — the seven-or-so canonical moves every BPO call passes through — because the structure is what makes a 19-year-old fresher sound competent on a call she has never made before. Vocabulary fills slots; pacing carries register; the structure holds the whole thing together when she does not yet know what comes next. Without structure, an underprepared agent reverts to L1 (Telugu) under cognitive load — the failure mode Unit 6A treats in detail and Unit 5A is designed to prevent.

This unit's load-bearing pedagogical move: *teach the call as a song with seven verses, not as a conversation*. Conversation is improvisation. Songs have known structure. Priya can perform a song she has rehearsed even when nervous. She cannot improvise a conversation in her second language under timed conditions yet. The structure is the scaffold that lets her sound conversational while she is in fact executing a rehearsed sequence.

## A. Conceptual foundations — 5 most important ideas

### A.1 The canonical seven-move BPO call structure

Practitioner consensus across BPO training literature converges on a seven-move structure for inbound voice-process calls. The naming varies; the moves are stable.

1. **Opening / Greeting + Identification.** Agent answers within 3 rings, identifies self and company, signals readiness to help. The "five-second opening" rule (see A.2). Industry-standard phrasing: *"Thank you for calling [Company]. My name is [Name]. How may I help you today?"*
2. **Caller verification / Account capture.** For most processes, the agent must verify the caller before discussing the account. *"May I have your name and registered mobile number, please?"* — sometimes followed by a security question (date of birth, last four digits of an ID).
3. **Discovery / Problem clarification.** The agent listens, takes notes, and confirms understanding before acting. The active-listening verbal markers (see A.3): *"I understand," "let me make sure I have this right," "I am sorry to hear that,"* and the canonical *paraphrase-back*: *"So if I have understood correctly, you are saying that..."*
4. **Hold / Internal action.** If the agent needs to check anything, she places the caller on hold using prescribed phrasing (see A.4). Industry KPI: *hold time* is measured separately from talk time. Excessive hold without check-in is penalized.
5. **Resolution / Information delivery.** The agent provides the answer or executes the action — a refund, an account update, a service activation, a transfer, an escalation. The resolution language uses *future indicative*: *"I am going to [action]," "I will [action]," "What I can do for you is..."*
6. **Confirmation + Next steps.** Before closing, the agent confirms the caller has what they need, summarizes what was done, and states next steps with timeframes. *"To confirm, I have raised a ticket for the billing dispute. You will receive an SMS within 24 hours."*
7. **Closing / Branded sign-off.** Asks if anything else is needed, thanks the caller, uses the company's branded close phrase. Industry KPI: *branded close compliance* is monitored (see A.6).

This structure is documented across the practitioner BPO training literature: [Brett Day's *Customer Service Skills for Success*, 7th ed., McGraw-Hill 2018](https://www.mheducation.com/highered/product/customer-service-skills-success-lucas/M9781259954078.html) `[verify exact ed.]` (the standard US textbook in the customer-service-training space, used as a reference in NASSCOM training partner curricula); [Marie Cross's *The Call Centre Training Handbook*, Kogan Page 2019](https://www.koganpage.com) `[verify title and year — Cross has written widely on call-center scripting; cross-check the canonical reference]`; the [Call Centre Helper magazine archive](https://www.callcentrehelper.com/) (UK practitioner publication, free articles on opening / closing / hold language); and the [International Customer Management Institute (ICMI) call-center resources](https://www.icmi.com/).

The chapter does not need to argue for this structure; it is the practitioner consensus, and the Hyderabad BPO ecosystem trains to a variant of it. The chapter teaches the seven moves by name, gives Priya a slot-filler template for each, and rehearses transitions between them.

### A.2 The five-second opening rule — what's in the first sentence

The opening is overweighted in the call's overall quality signal because two things are true of it. First, it is the only part of the call the customer judges before they have stated their problem — so it is judged entirely on *how it sounds*, not on whether it solved anything. Second, the QA monitoring teams in BPOs sample opening compliance disproportionately because it is the easiest move to audit ([Robert Spector and Patrick McCarthy's *The Nordstrom Way*, 3rd ed. Wiley 2012, on opening-script discipline in service businesses](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/The+Nordstrom+Way+to+Customer+Experience+Excellence%2C+3rd+Edition-p-9781119375364) `[verify`]; converging Call Centre Helper QA scorecard examples).

What the five-second opening contains, in order:

- **Branded greeting.** *"Thank you for calling [Company]."* — *"Thank you for calling Concentrix Customer Care."* / *"Good morning, Genpact Banking Services."*
- **Agent identification.** *"My name is [Name]."* — in Indian BPO, the agent typically uses an Indian first name (the offshored-Indian-agent-pretending-to-be-an-American-with-a-fake-name practice is largely defunct in 2026; it was a 2000s phenomenon, faded in the mid-2010s, and is not Concentrix or Genpact policy today `[verify with current BPO industry HR-policy documentation]`. For domestic Indian process work, agents have always used their own names. For international voice processes, current practice is also own-name with a clear pronunciation).
- **Permission / readiness signal.** *"How may I help you today?"* (more formal, common in Indian BPO scripts) or *"How can I assist you today?"* (slightly less formal, more common in US-account voice processes).

The whole opening is one or two seconds of speech. Five seconds is the upper limit before the caller starts to feel held; under three seconds the agent has rushed and the caller does not retain her name. This is the unit's first explicit *pacing* lesson.

The variants Priya needs to recognize:

- **Inbound vs. outbound.** Outbound (the agent calls the customer) adds *"Am I speaking with [Name]?"* and the campaign-disclosure phrase — *"This is a courtesy call regarding..."* Entry-level voice process in Hyderabad is more commonly inbound, so the chapter centers inbound, but should name outbound as the variant.
- **Domestic vs. international.** Domestic Indian process keeps *"Sir / Madam"* in the opening; international process drops the honorific to *"How may I help you today?"* without *Sir*. The choice is process-specific and the agent will be trained on the specific script when hired. The chapter teaches both forms.

### A.3 Active listening as a phrase set, not a feeling

The customer-service training literature treats "active listening" as a teachable behavior, not a personality trait — measurable through specific verbal markers ([Carl Rogers's foundational 1957 paper on therapeutic reflection](https://doi.org/10.1037/h0045357), ported into the customer-service literature by [Madelyn Burley-Allen's *Listening: The Forgotten Skill*, 2nd ed. Wiley 1995](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/Listening%3A+The+Forgotten+Skill%2C+2nd+Edition-p-9780471015871); reinforced in [Stephen Covey's *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People*, Free Press 1989](https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/The-7-Habits-of-Highly-Effective-People/Stephen-R-Covey/9781982137274), specifically Habit 5).

For BPO scripting, active listening operationalizes as four canonical phrase types:

- **Acknowledgement of speech-completion.** *"I see," "I understand," "okay," "got it,"* — short, frequent, signal that the agent is tracking. Frequency matters: roughly one acknowledgement every 10–15 seconds of customer speech.
- **Acknowledgement of feeling.** *"I am sorry to hear that," "I understand this has been frustrating," "I can imagine how this must feel."* Used when the customer expresses emotion. Critical for de-escalation; treated at length in Unit 6A.
- **Paraphrase confirmation.** *"So if I understand correctly, you are saying that..."* / *"Let me make sure I have this right — you ordered the phone on the 15th, it was supposed to be delivered on the 17th, but you still haven't received it, is that correct?"* This serves three functions: it confirms accuracy, it shows the agent listened, and it buys her time to think.
- **Information-confirming question.** *"Thank you for that information. Could you also tell me the order number, please?"* The *thank you for that information* phrase acknowledges what was given before requesting more. Without it, the agent sounds like an interrogator.

The chapter must teach these as *scripted insertions*, not as moments where Priya has to generate empathic language from scratch. Under cognitive load, the scripted phrase is what comes out; without the script, silence or L1 reversion comes out.

### A.4 Hold and transfer language — the most error-prone moves

Hold language is one of the highest-frequency QA-scorecard failure points for entry-level agents ([Call Centre Helper QA scorecard sample](https://www.callcentrehelper.com/contact-centre-quality-monitoring-scorecard-138708.htm) `[verify current URL`]). Two reasons: the agent often forgets the *permission* step before placing the hold, and the agent often forgets the *check-in* step if the hold extends beyond ~45 seconds.

Canonical hold language, with the four required moves:

1. **Permission.** *"Could you please give me a moment while I check that for you?"* / *"Would you mind if I put you on hold for a minute while I look into this?"* The customer is supposed to say yes. The agent waits for the yes — does not assume it.
2. **Action statement.** *"I am going to put you on a brief hold."* — signals what is about to happen so the customer is not surprised by silence.
3. **Time estimate.** *"It will take me about two minutes."* — sets expectation. If she returns earlier, good; if she takes longer, she has to check in.
4. **Check-in if hold exceeds estimate.** *"Thank you for holding, I am still working on this for you. May I have another minute, please?"* — the check-in resets the customer's patience.

Transfer language follows a similar four-move structure:

1. **Explanation of why transfer is needed.** *"I would like to connect you with our specialist team who can resolve this faster for you."* Note the framing: *resolve this faster*, not *I cannot help you*. The transfer is presented as added value, not as a brush-off.
2. **Confirmation of caller's consent.** *"Is that all right with you?"*
3. **Information bridge.** *"I will pass on your details to my colleague so you don't have to repeat yourself."* This is the critical step; failing to pass details means the customer has to re-narrate the problem to the new agent, which is the canonical bad-customer-service moment.
4. **Branded transfer phrase.** *"Please hold the line while I connect you."* — then warm transfer if process allows, cold transfer if not.

The chapter must teach the hold and transfer phrasings as memorized slot fillers, not as language Priya constructs each time. The slot-filler approach is consistent with [Pawley & Syder's 1983 "two puzzles" paper on native-like fluency through formulaic language](https://www.jstor.org/stable/3586338) `[verify]` and with corpus-linguistics work on phraseology in ESL acquisition ([Wray 2002, *Formulaic Language and the Lexicon*, Cambridge UP](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/formulaic-language-and-the-lexicon/16D1F69F44A1DDE07A28D62A6B9B2A8E)). Native-sounding professional speech is largely formulaic; teaching Priya the formulas is the fastest route to native-sounding output.

### A.5 The branded close — and why companies measure compliance

The closing is the second-most-monitored move on QA scorecards because, like the opening, it is auditable in seconds and it shapes the customer's lasting impression. The five-move structure of a proper close:

1. **Anything-else check.** *"Is there anything else I can help you with today?"* — gives the customer a chance to raise a secondary issue rather than calling back. KPI link: First Call Resolution (FCR), measured as percent of calls in which all customer issues are resolved on the first call.
2. **Summary of action taken.** *"To summarize, I have raised a billing dispute ticket. You will receive an SMS within 24 hours with the resolution."* Recall and reinforcement.
3. **Customer-effort thanks.** *"Thank you for being our customer / for choosing [Company]."* — the relational close.
4. **Branded close phrase.** This varies by company and is part of QA compliance. Examples vary; representative forms include *"Thank you for calling [Company], have a great day."* / *"Have a wonderful day."* / company-specific phrasings the agent is trained to use verbatim.
5. **Wait for customer to disconnect first.** Industry convention is the customer hangs up first; if the agent hangs up first and the customer was mid-thought, the call gets escalated. The agent waits 2–3 seconds of silence before disconnecting.

KPI link: branded-close compliance is a binary metric per call in most BPO QA scorecards. An agent who consistently fails to use the branded close phrase will be flagged for retraining even if her resolution rate is high. The chapter has to teach Priya that *the branded close is non-optional*, even when she is tired at the end of a long shift.

This is consistent with the broader emotional-labor literature ([Hochschild 1983, *The Managed Heart*, University of California Press](https://www.ucpress.edu/book/9780520272941/the-managed-heart)) on the cost of "surface acting" — performing prescribed emotional displays at the end of an exhausting interaction — which Unit 6A treats in detail.

### B.2 What "voice process" actually means at entry level

A voice process is a process in which the agent talks to the customer on the phone. It contrasts with non-voice (chat, email, social media) and blended (a mix). Entry-level Indian-domestic voice processes typically run:

- **Inbound, scripted.** The customer calls a 1-800 number. The agent answers using the canonical opening. Common processes: telecom (Jio, Airtel, Vi, BSNL customer care for plan changes, recharge issues, network complaints), e-commerce (Flipkart, Amazon India, Myntra returns and order status), banking and credit cards (HDFC, ICICI, Axis customer service for transaction queries), utility billing (electricity, gas, water provider complaints).
- **Average call duration.** 4–8 minutes for entry-level inbound, varying by process.
- **Call volume per shift.** 50–80 calls per 8-hour shift for entry-level voice, with the rest of the time on after-call work (notes, ticket creation, breaks).
- **Three KPIs Priya will be measured on from week one:**
  - **AHT (Average Handle Time).** Talk time + hold time + after-call work, averaged across calls. Target varies by process; commonly 4–6 minutes for entry-level domestic voice. Going over costs the agent on her scorecard. Hard rules around AHT shape why structure is non-optional — the structure is what keeps the call within AHT.
  - **CSAT (Customer Satisfaction).** Customer rating, usually a 1–5 or 1–10 scale via post-call SMS or IVR. Target commonly ≥80% positive.
  - **FCR (First Call Resolution).** Percent of customers who do not have to call back about the same issue within a window (often 7 days). Target commonly ≥75%.

The chapter has to teach Priya that *the structure is what serves the KPIs*. The opening serves CSAT and branded compliance; discovery and paraphrase serve FCR (because resolving the wrong issue causes a callback); hold and transfer language serve CSAT; the close serves branded compliance and CSAT. The structure is not bureaucratic decoration. It is the operational shape of the job.

### B.3 The TRAI guidelines and the regulatory frame

The Telecom Regulatory Authority of India (TRAI) publishes the [Telecom Consumers Protection Regulations](https://www.trai.gov.in/sites/default/files/Regulation_25052017_0.pdf) `[verify current version]` and the [Quality of Service of Basic Telephone Service and Cellular Mobile Telephone Service Regulations](https://www.trai.gov.in/), which include customer-care call-handling standards for licensed telecom operators: call answer time, IVR menu complexity, mandatory agent identification, complaint registration, and complaint-resolution SLAs. For Hyderabad BPO voice agents on a telecom process (Jio, Airtel, Vi, BSNL customer-care contracts), TRAI rules are part of the operational floor and are reflected in the QA scorecard.

Adjacent: TRAI's [Unsolicited Commercial Communication (UCC) regulations](https://www.trai.gov.in/) constrain outbound calling (DNC lists, call-time windows). The chapter does not need to teach TRAI rules in detail — those come in employer-side onboarding — but should name TRAI as the reason certain phrases (mandatory identification, mandatory complaint registration when the customer asks to register a complaint) are non-negotiable.

### B.4 Worked example — a complete inbound utility-billing call

This is the canonical worked example the chapter must include. The scenario: a domestic Indian customer calls his electricity provider's customer care to dispute a bill. Priya is the agent. Hyderabad-specific anchors: Telangana State Southern Power Distribution Company Limited (TGSPDCL) is the regional utility; for the chapter the company can be named or generic.

**Customer:** Sridhar Reddy, 47, called from Saidabad, complaining his March bill is much higher than usual.

---

**[Opening — 5 seconds]**
Priya: *Thank you for calling [Company] Customer Care. My name is Priya. How may I help you today, Sir?*

**[Caller verification — 15 seconds]**
Sridhar: *Hello, my bill is showing 4,800 rupees this month but normally I pay only 1,200. There must be some mistake.*
Priya: *I am sorry to hear that, Sir. I will be happy to check this for you. May I have your service number and registered mobile number, please?*
Sridhar: *Service number is 102-456-789, mobile is 9876543210.*
Priya: *Thank you. Could you also confirm the name on the account, please?*
Sridhar: *Sridhar Reddy.*

**[Discovery — 30 seconds]**
Priya: *Thank you for that information, Mr. Reddy. So if I have understood correctly, you are saying that your March bill is around 4,800 rupees, but your usual bill is around 1,200 rupees. Is that correct?*
Sridhar: *Yes, that's correct. Last month was 1,300. Suddenly this month it is four times that.*
Priya: *I understand. That is a significant difference. Has there been any change recently — a new appliance, an air conditioner, anything that might use more electricity?*
Sridhar: *No, nothing new.*
Priya: *Thank you. I am going to check your billing details on the system. Could you please give me a moment while I check that for you? It will take about two minutes.*
Sridhar: *Yes, please go ahead.*

**[Hold — 90 seconds]**
*(Priya places call on hold. Checks billing system, sees previous readings, current reading, and notes that the current reading appears unusually high compared to historical pattern. Considers possible reasons: meter misreading, faulty meter, actual usage spike.)*

**[Return from hold]**
Priya: *Thank you for holding, Mr. Reddy. I have checked your account.*

**[Resolution — 45 seconds]**
Priya: *I can see that your previous month bill was for 142 units, and this month the meter reading is showing 480 units. That is a very unusual jump. I would like to raise a meter-reading verification request so that our field team can come and check the meter at your home. They will verify the reading and, if there is a misreading, the bill will be corrected.*
Sridhar: *Yes, please do that. When will they come?*
Priya: *The field team will visit within five working days. You will receive an SMS confirmation with a complaint reference number, and another SMS when the visit is scheduled.*

**[Confirmation + Next steps — 20 seconds]**
Priya: *To confirm, Mr. Reddy: I have raised a meter-reading verification request for your service number 102-456-789. You will receive an SMS within the next 30 minutes with the complaint reference number. Our field team will visit within five working days. If there is a meter error, the bill will be corrected and a revised bill will be sent. Is that clear?*
Sridhar: *Yes, thank you very much.*

**[Closing — 15 seconds]**
Priya: *Is there anything else I can help you with today, Sir?*
Sridhar: *No, that's all.*
Priya: *Thank you for calling [Company] Customer Care, Mr. Reddy. Have a good day.*

---

Total call: roughly 3 minutes 40 seconds plus hold time, well within typical AHT.

Three things the chapter should annotate explicitly when walking through this example:

- Priya never code-switched to Telugu, even though the customer's name and accent are Telugu-coded. The customer might have welcomed Telugu (some domestic processes allow regional-language switching when the customer prefers it), but the script structure is in English and the agent stays in English unless the process explicitly permits otherwise. This is the Unit 6A connection — staying in English under load.
- Priya inserted three active-listening markers (*I am sorry to hear that, I understand, thank you for that information*) without thinking about them, because they are scripted slot fillers. They serve CSAT without slowing the call.
- The paraphrase (*so if I have understood correctly, you are saying that...*) bought Priya time to think about what to do next, and signaled to the customer that she was listening. Two-for-one.

### B.5 The Indian-English-is-the-target frame

The stereotype that Indian BPO voice work requires "accent neutralization" to sound American is largely a 2000s artifact and substantially American resentment about offshoring rather than a current Indian BPO industry quality standard. Multiple lines of evidence converge:

- The major Indian BPO players (Tata, Infosys BPM, Wipro BPS, Genpact, HGS) hire in volume for domestic Indian process work, where Indian English is the explicit operating language. ([NASSCOM BPM Strategic Review 2023](https://nasscom.in) `[verify section and year`])
- Even for international voice processes, current accent-training emphasis is on *clarity and pacing*, not on producing American or British phonology. The "MTI" (Mother Tongue Influence) reduction training that BPO Tier 2 programs offer is about not letting Telugu, Hindi, or Tamil phonology obscure intelligibility — not about producing a midwestern-American accent. ([Sailaja 2009, *Indian English*, EUP, on the "MTI" pedagogy](https://edinburghuniversitypress.com/book-indian-english.html); converging Hyderabad BPO recruiter blog evidence `[verify with current recruiter`])
- The 2010s-era public discussion of Indian BPO agents adopting American names and pretending to be in Wichita is, by 2026, mostly anachronistic. Companies like Concentrix and Genpact today emphasize their Indian presence as a feature, not a thing to hide. `[verify with current Concentrix / Genpact corporate communications`]

The chapter's framing: *Indian Standard Business English is the target. Your voice is the right voice for this job. The chapter teaches you the structure that lets your voice sound competent.*

This connects directly to Unit 1's register pedagogy. The chapter inherits the Indian-English-as-legitimate frame and applies it to the spoken register specifically.

## D. Current state of the field

### Settled

- The seven-move call structure (greeting / verification / discovery / hold / resolution / confirmation / close) is practitioner consensus across major customer-service-training literatures (Brett Day, Marie Cross, ICMI, Call Centre Helper). [Day, McGraw-Hill](https://www.mheducation.com/) `[verify ed.]`; [ICMI](https://www.icmi.com/).
- Active listening is a teachable phrase-based behavior, not a trait. [Burley-Allen 1995](https://www.wiley.com/); [Rogers 1957](https://doi.org/10.1037/h0045357).
- Formulaic language is the fastest route to native-sounding L2 fluency in workplace contexts. [Wray 2002](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/formulaic-language-and-the-lexicon/16D1F69F44A1DDE07A28D62A6B9B2A8E); [Pawley & Syder 1983](https://www.jstor.org/stable/3586338) `[verify]`.
- Indian English is a legitimate variety; the entry-level Hyderabad BPO hiring filter is clarity and structure, not accent. [Sailaja 2009](https://edinburghuniversitypress.com/book-indian-english.html); [Kachru 1985](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-971X.1985.tb00423.x).
- AHT, CSAT, and FCR are the three universally tracked entry-level voice-process KPIs across the Indian BPO industry. [NASSCOM Strategic Review series](https://nasscom.in) `[verify`].

### Contested

- Whether scripts should be memorized verbatim or treated as guidelines. The verbatim-script tradition (Concentrix, Foundever) emphasizes branded-close compliance and QA-scorecard reliability. The guideline tradition (some newer BPOs, especially those serving SaaS clients) prefers agents who can paraphrase. For the chapter's target audience — entry-level fresher — verbatim is the safer teaching frame. Paraphrase is a Tier 2 skill she will earn after 6 months on the job.
- Whether AHT pressure damages CSAT (the "rushed agent" complaint) or whether the two are correlated (well-structured calls are both fast and satisfying). The trade literature splits; [Holman 2003 on call-center work organization](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/0958519021000029054) `[verify`] argues AHT pressure damages quality. The pedagogical takeaway for Priya: the structure is what makes both AHT and CSAT achievable at once; speed without structure is hollow.

### Unsettled

- The actual industry-wide compliance rate for verbatim branded close among Indian BPO entry-level agents. Anecdotal evidence suggests 60–80% compliance after Tier 1 training; rigorous data is internal to companies and not published. `[verify with current QA scorecard sample]`
- The optimal teaching sequence for the seven moves. Some practitioner trainers teach opening + close first (because they are auditable and serve confidence), then fill in the middle. Others teach the full structure linearly. The chapter should adopt the opening + close → middle approach because it gives Priya a complete callable structure from her first practice round.

## E. Teaching considerations — where ESL learners get stuck

### E.1 The "sounds like a robot" objection

When Priya first practices the seven-move structure, the phrasing will feel artificial — *"Thank you for that information"* is not a sentence she has ever produced before. The pedagogical move is the same as in Unit 1: name the feeling and pass through it. Formulaic language always feels artificial before it automates. The threshold is roughly 10 practice repetitions ([Schmitt 2010, *Researching Vocabulary*, Palgrave](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1057/9780230293977)). After 10 calls, the phrases are hers. Before 10 calls, she has to trust the structure and repeat it anyway.

### E.2 The pacing problem

A consistent entry-level failure is rushing — speaking too fast under nerves. The fix is operational, not motivational. The chapter teaches a *target pacing* of roughly 120–140 words per minute, slightly slower than her natural conversational Telugu pace. Practice exercise: she reads the canonical opening (*Thank you for calling [Company]. My name is Priya. How may I help you today?*) aloud, times herself, and adjusts. Target is 4–5 seconds. If she comes in under 3, she is rushing. If she comes in over 6, she is dragging.

Pacing also serves comprehension on her end. A faster pace means the customer's response is harder for her to parse. A slower pace gives her processing time. This is consistent with the L2 listening-comprehension literature ([Vandergrift & Goh 2012, *Teaching and Learning Second Language Listening*, Routledge](https://www.routledge.com/Teaching-and-Learning-Second-Language-Listening-Metacognition-in-Action/Vandergrift-Goh/p/book/9780415883718)).

### E.3 The L1-reversion failure mode

When cognitive load spikes — the customer goes off-script, asks a question Priya hasn't rehearsed, raises a complaint about something she can't immediately address — the L2 user's strongest pull is to revert to L1. For Priya this means Telugu, and on a process that requires English, code-switching to Telugu is a fail. The chapter's preventive move: build the structure so deeply through rehearsal that even under load, the *next move* is automatic. The seven-move structure works exactly because it gives her a known next step regardless of where the call is.

Unit 6A treats this failure mode at length under the "switching languages under stress" research. Unit 5A's job is to lay the structural foundation that Unit 6A's de-escalation phrases can attach to.

### E.4 The "I don't have a script in front of me" anxiety

In the actual job, Priya will have a process-specific script displayed on her screen alongside the customer's account information. She will not be performing the seven-move structure from memory alone. The chapter should reassure her of this — the structure is what she needs to internalize so that when the on-screen script disappears (because the customer asked something off-script), she has the structural fallback. The on-screen script and the internalized structure are complementary, not redundant.

### E.5 Where the volunteer Zoom session does the work the Kindle cannot

The Kindle can teach the seven-move structure, the slot-filler phrases, and the worked-example pattern. It cannot tell Priya whether her produced call has the right *pacing*, the right *tone of warmth*, the right *recovery* when she stumbles. That is the volunteer's job in the Week 3 / Week 4 Zoom session — the first Track A mock call.

The volunteer brief, parallel to Unit 1's three-question diagnostic, should be:

1. Did the agent complete the opening within 5 seconds with greeting + identification + permission? (Yes / no)
2. Did the agent use at least one paraphrase confirmation in the discovery phase? (Yes / no)
3. Did the agent close with a summary + branded close + thanks? (Yes / no)

Three binary judgments, 10 seconds each. The volunteer then names one specific thing she did well and one specific thing to try differently, per the Risk 3 mitigation in the TIKTOC. Not a grade. A coaching note.

## G. Gaps and flags

- **Empirical gap.** The seven-move structure is practitioner consensus, not an RCT. There is no peer-reviewed study that pits the seven-move structure against, say, a five-move or nine-move variant for entry-level outcomes. The chapter should claim the structure as *industry-standard practice*, not as an evidence-based optimum.
- **Domain gap.** The exact branded-close phrases for Concentrix, Genpact, Foundever, Teleperformance, HGS, Tech Mahindra are not publicly documented and vary by client process. The chapter should teach the *form* (5-move close) without prescribing a specific company's branded phrase, because the agent will be trained on the client-specific phrase when hired. `[verify with current Hyderabad BPO trainer]`
- **Authority gap.** Brett Day and Marie Cross are practitioner authors, not academic researchers. The chapter relies on practitioner consensus where the academic literature is thin. This is acceptable for an applied skill but should be flagged.
- **Register-of-the-chapter flag.** The chapter itself must be written in the same Indian Standard Business English register the chapter is teaching. The worked examples should sound like Hyderabad, not like a US customer-service-training video. Names, scenarios, and product references should be Hyderabad-anchored (TGSPDCL, HMWSSB, Jio, Tata Sky, Apollo Pharmacy, Bigbasket, Flipkart, Amazon India).
- **What would change my mind.** A 2025 or 2026 large-N study showing that Hyderabad BPO entry-level hiring weights accent neutralization over structural compliance at the phone-screen stage. I do not expect this study exists or would find that result; if it does, the chapter pedagogy moves toward phonological training rather than structural training.
- **Still puzzling.** The exact share of entry-level Hyderabad voice-process attrition (~30–40% in the first 90 days per industry consensus `[verify]`) attributable to structure failure vs. KPI pressure vs. shift fatigue. The three are entangled in the available reporting. The chapter's pedagogy assumes structure failure is at least one of the three; that assumption is defensible but not numerically anchored.
