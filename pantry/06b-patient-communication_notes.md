# Research Note — Unit 6B: Patient Communication

*Working pantry document for Homes of Hope +1, Course 2 — Unit 6B (Patient Communication, Track B Healthcare Reception). The chapter teaches Priya (19, Telugu first language, ~350 English words, Units 1–4 done, Unit 5B's 50-word deck in progress) to greet a patient, collect basic information, explain a simple procedure, and direct someone to the right department in professional English. Notes are for the chapter author. Compiled 2026-05-17.*

---

## Preliminary: the unit's thesis in one sentence

A receptionist who can greet, register, explain, and redirect using a structured script outperforms a receptionist with better English using no script — because the script removes the variance the patient cares about most: *did the front desk know what to do with me?* The chapter has to teach the script, name the soft-skill register the script sits in, and inoculate Priya against the two failure modes that wreck most front-desk interactions: clinician-overreach and emotional shutdown.

The companion claim: the patient is *not* a customer. Hyderabad BPO trains its agents to "delight the customer" with cheerful resolution language. Hospital reception trains its associates to "respect the patient" with calm, accurate routing language. The register is different; the failure modes are different; the hiring criteria are different. Track B's central distinction from Track A lives in this unit.

## A. Conceptual foundations — 3–5 most important ideas

### A.1 AIDET — the canonical hospital-reception script, and why it works

AIDET is a five-step communication framework developed by the [Studer Group](https://www.huronconsultinggroup.com/insights/studer-group) (a US healthcare consultancy) in the late 1990s and adopted across thousands of hospitals worldwide. The acronym: **A**cknowledge, **I**ntroduce, **D**uration, **E**xplanation, **T**hank you. The five-step structure is empirically supported by patient-satisfaction studies — [Scott 2012, *Studer Group whitepaper*](https://www.huronconsultinggroup.com/-/media/Files/Studer-Group-Files/Studer-AIDET-Patient-Communication.pdf) `[verify exact URL]` and the broader patient-experience literature ([HCAHPS data analyses in *JAMA*, *Health Affairs*](https://www.ahrq.gov/cahps/surveys-guidance/hospital/index.html)) — though the most rigorous causal evidence is for *clinician*-patient AIDET use, not front-desk use. The pattern transfers cleanly to the front desk because the structural problem (patient is anxious, time is short, the staff member needs to convey competence and care quickly) is identical.

The chapter's adapted AIDET for Hyderabad hospital reception:

- **Acknowledge.** Make eye contact. Greet the patient by name once it is known. *"Good morning, sir."* If she does not yet know the name, the gendered honorific (*sir / madam*) carries the acknowledge function alone in Indian context. Use of *ji* as a politeness suffix in mixed Hindi/English contexts is acceptable in less-formal settings; uncommon at Apollo/Yashoda formal-register desks, common at smaller clinics.
- **Introduce.** *"My name is Priya, I am the registration associate."* The role-naming is important — it signals to the patient *what this person can and cannot help me with*. Clinicians introduce by name + specialty; front-desk by name + role.
- **Duration.** *"This will take about 5 minutes."* Specifying the duration is the move that cuts the patient's uncertainty in half. Patient anxiety is largely about *how long is this going to take*. Stating it explicitly — even approximately — is empirically the single most effective patient-satisfaction lever in admin interactions ([Sweeney, Webb, & Mazor 2008, *Journal of Healthcare Management*](https://journals.lww.com/jhmonline/Abstract/2008/03000/Patient_Satisfaction_With_the_Time_Spent_Waiting.6.aspx) `[verify exact citation]`).
- **Explanation.** *"I will need your insurance card and a phone number where we can reach you. Then I will check whether your appointment is confirmed in the system. After that I will print your token, and you can go up to the cardiology OPD on the second floor."* The explanation enumerates the steps in order. It is the cognitive scaffold that lets the patient anticipate what is happening, not be subjected to it.
- **Thank you.** *"Thank you for waiting, sir. Wishing you a good consultation."* The close is brief and warm. In Hyderabad context, the close also often carries a final routing reminder: *"The lift is to your right; please take it to the second floor."*

AIDET is structurally a *consultative* register (Unit 1) with a *medical* vocabulary (Unit 5B). The chapter's job is to slot the five steps into the front-desk flow Priya will encounter at Apollo / Yashoda / KIMS / AIG.

### A.2 The Calgary-Cambridge and SEGUE frameworks — why they are wrong for the front desk

Two other healthcare communication frameworks dominate the literature: the **Calgary-Cambridge model** ([Kurtz, Silverman, & Draper 2005](https://www.routledge.com/Teaching-and-Learning-Communication-Skills-in-Medicine/Kurtz-Silverman-Draper/p/book/9781857756586) `[verify`]) and **SEGUE** (Set the stage, Elicit information, Give information, Understand patient perspective, End encounter — [Makoul 2001, *Academic Medicine*](https://journals.lww.com/academicmedicine/Fulltext/2001/06000/The_SEGUE_framework_for_teaching_and_assessing.8.aspx)).

The chapter should mention both *to disqualify them for this role*. Both are designed for **clinician-patient consultations** — they assume the staff member is going to *elicit information* (symptoms, history), *give information* (diagnosis, treatment plan), and *understand the patient's perspective* on a medical issue. The front desk does none of these things. The front desk acknowledges, registers, explains the administrative process, and routes.

The pedagogical move: name the two larger frameworks, then explain that they belong to the clinician's job, not the receptionist's. This is also a *boundary-of-role* teaching — Priya learns that her job has explicit limits, that the clinician's job is different, and that overstepping (asking about symptoms, attempting to interpret a diagnosis) is professionally inappropriate as well as legally risky. AIDET is the right tool because it stops at the door of clinical content.

### A.3 The Indian hospital greeting protocol — *sir / madam* still standard

Indian hospital front-desk register retains *sir* and *madam* (or *ma'am*) as the default address terms for patients whose names are not yet known. The frequency far exceeds American or British equivalents and the pattern is durable across institutional types and patient demographics. The pattern observations:

- **First-time encounter (name unknown).** *"Good morning, sir."* / *"Good morning, madam."*
- **Name known from form.** *"Good morning, Mr. Rao."* / *"Good morning, Mrs. Reddy."* — Mr./Mrs./Ms. + surname is the Apollo-Yashoda formal-register default. *Mr. Rao* is preferred over *Rao sir* in writing on documents; spoken, *Rao sir* is also acceptable in Indian-English register.
- **English-speaking foreigners.** *"Good morning, Mr. Smith."* — drop the *sir* once the name is known; use of *sir* with a Western name in conversation can read as over-deferential.
- **Elderly patients.** Many older patients respond well to *Uncle / Aunty* in less-formal registers (small clinics, neighborhood hospitals). At Apollo/Yashoda/KIMS, this is generally avoided for *patients* but is standard for *attendants* in the wider context. The chapter teaches the distinction.
- **Senior physicians.** Never *uncle / aunty / sir-ji*. Always *Doctor / Dr. Reddy / Sir* (with capital S, used sparingly and only when not addressing by name). Doctors are addressed by title at all times in front of patients; this is a status marker the front desk must reinforce.

The chapter teaches these as four cards in a flashcard set, not as a rule. The cards: *patient name unknown* → *sir/madam*; *patient name known* → *Mr./Mrs./Ms. + surname*; *attendant elderly informal* → *Uncle/Aunty*; *physician* → *Doctor + surname*.

### A.4 The standard information-collection script

The patient-registration conversation is a deterministic form-fill. The chapter teaches it as a single script that runs in ~90 seconds. The standard fields (from Apollo and Yashoda registration forms `[verify with current registration form layouts]`):

1. **Full name** (as on ID).
2. **Date of birth** (or age, if DOB not known — common with older rural patients).
3. **Sex / gender** (often pre-filled from the appointment booking).
4. **Phone number** (primary contact).
5. **Address** (often abbreviated to city + locality at OPD; full at admission).
6. **Email** (optional at OPD, sometimes required at admission for insurance correspondence).
7. **ID proof** (Aadhaar number for nearly all Indian hospitals as of 2025; passport or driving license accepted as backup; foreign passport for non-Indians).
8. **Chief complaint** (very brief — "chest pain since 2 days" or "follow-up cardiology"). The receptionist captures this in the patient's words, not in clinical terminology.
9. **Referring doctor** (if any). *"Were you referred by another doctor, sir?"*
10. **Insurance** (if any). Card collected and copied or scanned.
11. **Emergency contact / next of kin** (at admission; often skipped at OPD).

The chapter teaches the *order* as load-bearing. Asking for ID proof before asking for the name reads as bureaucratic. Asking for the name first reads as professional. Asking for chief complaint before name reads as overstepping. The script is consultative: name first, then form fields in logical sequence.

The chapter also teaches the **explicit-confirmation step**. After filling, the receptionist reads back the critical fields: *"Your full name is Lakshmi Devi, date of birth 15 March 1962, phone number ending in 3 4 5 7, here for follow-up cardiology with Dr. Reddy. Is that correct?"* This is an [NABH](https://www.nabh.co/)-mandated patient-identification step (the *two-identifier rule*) and a practical error-prevention move. Priya's exercise: run the full script with herself as both receptionist and patient, then with a Zoom partner.

### A.5 Wayfinding language and the empathic-acknowledgment phrase ladder

**Wayfinding.** Hospital signage in Hyderabad chains is bilingual (English + Telugu at most chains, English + Hindi at some). Patients still ask. The standard phrase: *"[Department] is on the [Nth] floor. Take the lift on your [right/left], and follow the [colored] arrows / signs."* Variations: *"It is the third room on the right as you exit the lift."* / *"There is a separate building for [X]; please cross the courtyard, it is the building with the blue board."*

The chapter teaches three wayfinding sentences as templates. Color-coded signage is common at large chains; the chapter notes that signage colors vary by hospital and the safe pattern is to name the floor + a directional landmark, not just the floor number.

**The empathic-acknowledgment phrase ladder.** This is the hardest sub-skill in the unit. A patient arrives anxious, in pain, sometimes after a difficult diagnosis. The front-desk staff member is not the clinician, cannot offer medical reassurance, and must not. But the patient needs human acknowledgment before being asked for an insurance card. The phrase ladder, from minimal to over-reaching:

- **Right move.** *"I'm sorry you're not feeling well. Let me get you registered quickly so the doctor can see you."* — acknowledges discomfort, redirects to the action she controls (registration), references the clinician (whose job the medical reassurance is).
- **Right move (more anxious patient).** *"I understand this is stressful. Please take a moment; I'll have you registered in just a few minutes."* — names the emotion (anxiety, stress) without diagnosing it, offers control over pace.
- **Borderline (situational).** *"Don't worry, sir, the doctor is very good."* — only acceptable from staff who actually know the doctor and the typical outcome; risky for entry-level associates because it can be heard as a clinical promise.
- **Overreach — avoid.** *"You'll be fine."* / *"It's nothing serious."* / *"My grandmother had the same thing and she recovered."* — these are clinical assurances the front desk has no authority to make. They expose both the patient (false reassurance) and the hospital (liability). The chapter is explicit: these phrases are *never* acceptable from the front desk, regardless of how kind the impulse.

The ladder is a Unit 6B teaching artifact; it draws on the broader literature on *empathic but boundaried* communication in healthcare ([Halpern 2003, *JAMA*](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamainternalmedicine/article-abstract/215336) `[verify]`; the [trauma-informed care](https://www.samhsa.gov/resource/dbhis/samhsas-concept-trauma-and-guidance-trauma-informed-approach) framework adapted for non-clinical staff).

### Worked example — the referral patient flow

A patient arrives at the Apollo Hospitals Jubilee Hills OPD reception with a printed referral note from a GP in Karimnagar for cardiology consultation. Priya is at counter 1.

> *"Good morning, sir. Welcome to Apollo Jubilee Hills. May I help you?"*
> Patient: *"I have a referral, cardiology."*
> *"Thank you, sir. May I see the referral note? And could you tell me your full name, please?"*
> Patient hands the note. *"Subba Rao. Karimnagar."*
> *"Thank you, Mr. Rao. I can see Dr. Sharma has referred you to cardiology — Dr. Reddy is available today at 11:30 AM. That is in about 45 minutes. Is that all right, or would you prefer a later slot?"*
> Patient: *"11:30 is fine."*
> *"Wonderful. I will register you now. Could I have your date of birth?"*
> Patient: *"3rd of June, 1958."*
> *"Thank you. Phone number?"* — captures, reads back. Continues through fields. *"Do you have insurance, sir?"*
> Patient: *"Star Health. Card is here."* — produces card.
> *"Thank you. I will check cashless eligibility while you wait. Meanwhile, here is your token — number C-37 for cardiology OPD. The OPD is on the second floor. Take the lift to your right, then turn left when you exit; you will see the cardiology waiting area. The nurse will check your vital signs before Dr. Reddy sees you. The whole process from now to consultation should take about 50 minutes. Is there anything you'd like to ask, sir?"*
> Patient: *"No, thank you."*
> *"Thank you, Mr. Rao. Wishing you a good consultation."*

What this script does: AIDET-structured. Information collection in standard order. Wayfinding sentence with landmark + direction. Duration estimate. Empathic close. Total interaction: ~3 minutes. Priya can rehearse it on her phone; the Zoom volunteer can run it as a role-play.

## B. Domain examples and cases — Hyderabad-specific

### B.1 Apollo Hospitals — the AIDET-formal end of the register

Apollo's Patient Care Coordinator role explicitly trains AIDET (referenced in Apollo training documents `[verify]` and in industry observation). The front desk is the public face of the brand; Apollo's regional pricing power depends on the perception of competent, gracious service. Priya's track-B preparation aligns directly with Apollo's stated front-desk competencies.

### B.2 Yashoda Hospitals — the patient-relations emphasis

Yashoda's Patient Relations Executive role is positioned slightly differently: less about pure registration efficiency, more about patient-experience management across the visit. The script is the same; the dwell-time per patient is slightly longer; the empathic-acknowledgment register matters more. Priya's preparation is easily portable.

### B.3 KIMS, AIG, Care, Continental — the all-rounder pattern

Smaller chains expect the front desk to do registration + insurance + appointment management + sometimes initial billing in the same role. The 90-second script gets stretched to 3–4 minutes per patient with more administrative content. AIDET still anchors the interaction.

### B.4 Telangana DPDP Act implications at the front desk

The [Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023](https://www.meity.gov.in/data-protection-framework) came into force in stages from 2024. Hospitals are *data fiduciaries* under the Act. The front desk handles personal data (name, DOB, phone, address, ID number, sometimes medical complaint) at every patient interaction. The compliance implications:

- **Stated purpose at collection.** *"I am collecting this information to register you for today's consultation and to maintain your medical records."* The chapter teaches a one-sentence consent line, even though most patients will not pause to absorb it. The line exists for compliance and to signal professional awareness.
- **Visible-display restraint.** Patient names and details should not be visible to other patients in the queue. The chapter notes that this affects how loudly Priya reads back fields and how she handles the queue at the counter.
- **No discussion outside the work context.** *"You cannot discuss what you see at the desk with your family or friends."* This is a Unit 9 (first-week) reinforcement; introduced in 6B because the script crosses confidential territory.

The DPDP Act compliance details belong more in Unit 9, but the awareness belongs here. The chapter flags: *"You are handling private information. Treating it as private — even at the front desk — is part of the professional standard."*

### B.5 The non-Telugu, non-Hindi patient

Hyderabad's hospital catchment increasingly includes patients from Bengal, Maharashtra, Karnataka, and from international medical-tourism sources (Bangladesh, Africa, Middle East). The front desk encounters patients whose Indian regional language Priya does not share. The default move: switch to English. The chapter teaches a short *"Sir, would you prefer English or Hindi?"* opener as a respect-marker. International medical-tourism patients are usually English-fluent and prefer English; the script holds.

## D. Current state of the field

### Settled

- AIDET-style structured patient communication improves patient satisfaction scores and reduces complaints ([Studer Group operational research](https://www.huronconsultinggroup.com/insights/studer-group); [HCAHPS pre-post studies](https://www.ahrq.gov/cahps/surveys-guidance/hospital/index.html)).
- Patient identification using two identifiers is a global patient-safety standard ([WHO Patient Safety Solutions, 2007](https://www.who.int/patientsafety/solutions/patientsafety/PS-Solution2.pdf); reinforced in [NABH 5th Edition Standards](https://www.nabh.co/)).
- Time-uncertainty is a major driver of patient dissatisfaction; explicit duration-stating reduces it ([Sweeney et al. 2008](https://journals.lww.com/jhmonline/Abstract/2008/03000/Patient_Satisfaction_With_the_Time_Spent_Waiting.6.aspx) `[verify]`; converging healthcare-operations literature).
- Trauma-informed and empathic communication frameworks are effective when scoped to staff authority and limits ([Halpern 2003](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamainternalmedicine/article-abstract/215336) `[verify]`; [SAMHSA trauma-informed framework](https://www.samhsa.gov/resource/dbhis/samhsas-concept-trauma-and-guidance-trauma-informed-approach)).

### Contested

- Whether AIDET's empirical base extends from clinician-patient to non-clinical staff-patient interactions with the same effect size. The structural argument is strong; the rigorous causal evidence is mostly clinician-side.
- Whether the *sir / madam* register is a sustaining professional norm or an artifact of Indian deference culture due to fade. The chapter takes the practitioner position: in 2026 Hyderabad it is alive, expected, and rewarded.

### Unsettled

- The optimal length of the registration script in real OPD throughput conditions. Hospital-operations literature suggests 60–120 seconds is the throughput-acceptable window; the chapter's ~90-second target is within range.

## E. Teaching considerations — where ESL learners get stuck

### E.1 The clinician-overreach reflex

Empathic learners — especially those from caregiving backgrounds, which describes most Salesian-residence girls — instinctively want to comfort distressed patients with medical content. *"It will be okay, my aunt had this and she recovered."* The chapter must teach: this is overreach; it is professionally inappropriate; it can expose the patient and the hospital. The acceptable empathic move is **acknowledgment without clinical content**. The phrase ladder in §A.5 is the operational form of this.

### E.2 The emotional-shutdown reflex

The opposite failure mode: the learner becomes so worried about overreaching that she becomes mechanical, robotic, and emotionally absent. The patient leaves the desk feeling registered but not seen. The chapter must teach that *warmth is not overreach*. A small kind sentence — *"I hope your test results come back well"* — at the appropriate moment is not a clinical statement; it is a human one. The line between the two is the one the script clarifies: stay administrative, but be warm administratively.

### E.3 The Telugu-Hindi-English code-switching question

Priya's most useful instinct in the actual job will be code-switching: greeting in English, capturing chief complaint in the patient's preferred language, documenting in English. The chapter explicitly endorses this. It is not a fall-back; it is the professional norm. The constraint: *documents must be in English* (the system is English; the chart is English) and *direct quotations of the chief complaint should be in English* as the patient said it or as Priya's best translation. The volunteer Zoom session can rehearse the switch-points.

### E.4 The patient who is angrier than the script handles

The AIDET script handles cooperative patients. Angry, distressed, abusive patients are a different sub-script — and they appear frequently enough at any front desk that the chapter must include a short section on **escalation**. The escalation phrase: *"Sir, I understand you're upset. Let me ask my supervisor to come and help. Please give me one minute."* Three things this sentence does: it acknowledges, it does not promise resolution, it routes the problem to the right authority. The chapter ladders one practice scenario with an angry patient into the Zoom role-play.

### E.5 The Salesian-residence analogy

The cadence of greeting visitors, asking purpose, and routing them to the right sister or the right room is the cadence the residence runs on. Priya has seen this hundreds of times. The chapter names the analogy and validates her existing competence: *"This is a job you already partly know how to do. The vocabulary is new. The cadence is not."*

## G. Gaps and flags

- **Empirical gap:** AIDET's transfer to non-clinical front-desk roles is supported structurally but not by RCT-grade evidence specific to Indian hospital reception. The chapter relies on construct-validity, not direct empirical proof.
- **Authority gap:** No single 2024–2026 Indian-hospital reception communication manual exists at the level of detail this unit needs. Triangulation between Apollo/Yashoda training documents, NABH framework, and adapted Studer materials is the best available.
- **DPDP flag:** The Digital Personal Data Protection Act compliance specifics at front-desk level are still being implemented across Indian hospital chains as of 2026. The chapter's compliance handling should be reviewed against the most current implementation status before publish. `[verify with hospital admin contact]`
- **Domain reader:** A working Hyderabad front-desk supervisor should review the AIDET adaptation, the registration-field order, and the empathic phrase ladder before publish.
- **What would change my mind:** A 2025–2026 study of Hyderabad hospital patient-satisfaction data showing that strict script-following at the front desk *underperforms* warmer, less-structured interaction. I do not expect this; the script literature is consistent. If it appeared, the chapter's structural emphasis would soften toward improvisation guidance.
