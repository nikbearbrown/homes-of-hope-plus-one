# Research Note — Unit 8: The Mock Interview

*Working pantry document for Homes of Hope +1, Course 2 — Unit 8 (The Mock Interview). The chapter teaches Priya (19, Telugu first language, ~350 English words, smartphone, has completed Units 1–4 plus her track-specific Units 5–7) to complete a full 15-minute mock interview with a Humanitarians AI volunteer over Zoom, receive specific feedback on pronunciation / register / content, and revise her answers. Unit 8 is the **first convergence unit** — both tracks (A: BPO, B: hospital reception, C: NGO) collapse back into a shared assessment. Notes are for the chapter author. Compiled 2026-05-17.*

---

## Preliminary: the gap the mock interview closes

Everything Units 1–7 teach is text-on-a-phone work. She has written professional WhatsApp messages, drafted a CV with AI assistance, read job postings, practiced track-specific scripts. None of it has been said out loud to a stranger in real time over video while being evaluated. That gap — the gap between "I have written this answer" and "I can say this answer to a person whose face is on my screen looking at me" — is the gap that produces most entry-level interview failures in Hyderabad. Not lack of English. Not lack of preparation. The first time the preparation meets the room.

The TIKTOC names the failure correctly: *not knowledge, nerves*. But "nerves" is a vague word, and the chapter has to specify it. Nerves means: prepared answer in head, mouth produces something else; rehearsed pronunciation, throat tightens and consonants drop; planned pause, becomes a freeze; one wobble triggers cascade. The mock-interview unit is the only point in the curriculum where the conditions that produce these failures are intentionally reproduced under low-stakes conditions, with feedback, in time to revise before the real interview.

The pedagogical question is not whether to do a mock interview. The pedagogical question is what makes a *useful* mock interview versus a useless one. A volunteer who says "your English is good!" produces no learning. A volunteer who lists every flaw produces withdrawal. The unit's load-bearing instructional design is *what the volunteer is supposed to do in those 15 minutes and what the feedback debrief is supposed to sound like*. Most of the chapter is teaching Priya how to participate in the session productively; a substantial sub-section is teaching the volunteer how to run it.

## A. Conceptual foundations — 3–5 most important ideas

### A.1 Interview anxiety is a Pekrun control-value problem, not a confidence problem

The dominant frame in achievement-emotions research is Reinhard Pekrun's *control-value theory* ([Pekrun 2006, *Educational Psychology Review*](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-006-9029-9); updated synthesis in [Pekrun & Perry 2014, *International Handbook of Emotions in Education*](https://www.routledge.com/International-Handbook-of-Emotions-in-Education/Pekrun-Linnenbrink-Garcia/p/book/9780415895026)). Achievement emotions are predicted by two variables: *perceived control* (do I expect I can influence the outcome through what I do?) and *value* (does this outcome matter to me?). The 2×2 produces a map:

- **High control + high value → enjoyment, hope.** The student leans in.
- **Low control + high value → anxiety.** The student's threat system fires; cognitive resources are pulled toward threat-monitoring and away from task performance.
- **High control + low value → boredom.** Rare in this population.
- **Low control + low value → hopelessness.** The chapter has to keep this quadrant out of reach.

The mock interview is structurally in the high-value quadrant — the job matters, the outcome matters, the assessment by a stranger matters. The unit's pedagogical job is to move Priya out of low-control into high-control. Rehearsal does this. A predictable structure does this. Reframing the session as *practice, not performance* does this. Crucially, *being told her nerves are a confidence problem* does *not* do this — confidence framing maps to "you should feel different" which is itself a low-control instruction. Pekrun's framework predicts that anxiety responds to changes in expected control, not to changes in expected feeling.

The chapter should teach the framework lightly — Priya does not need to know the citation — but the volunteer training section should name it explicitly. A volunteer who understands "you are not fixing her confidence, you are increasing her sense of control" runs a better session than one trying to be encouraging.

The operational moves for raising perceived control:
- **Predictable structure.** She knows what four questions are coming. She knows the 90-second answer shape. She knows the volunteer will give feedback. Each predictable element lowers the variance she has to manage.
- **Rehearsal of high-frequency items.** The four universal questions are practiced before the mock session, not invented during it.
- **Reframing the unknown question.** *"I don't know the answer to this one"* is given as a planned move with three acceptable variants (§A.4). The unknown becomes one of several known moves.
- **Recovery script.** *"Let me start that again"* is rehearsed as an acceptable interview move. The cascade is broken before it begins.

### A.2 The SOAR / STAR / situation-framework family — and the 90-second variant

The behavioral-interview answer framework has a literature. The original STAR — Situation, Task, Action, Result — is widely attributed to ([development.com / DDI consulting practitioner literature, 1970s–1980s](https://www.ddiworld.com)) and adapted across HR training contexts ever since. The variant SOAR (Situation, Obstacle, Action, Result) is functionally identical with the obstacle made explicit. The variant CAR (Context, Action, Result) drops the task as redundant for entry-level. The TIKTOC names the chapter's chosen variant: *situation → what she did → what happened → what she learned*. This is closest to a learning-oriented variant sometimes called SAOR or just "situation framework" — emphasizing the *what she learned* close that converts a story into evidence of growth, which is the move most often missing in entry-level interview answers.

The empirical case for structured behavioral answers is the structured-interview literature broadly. [Schmidt & Hunter 1998, *Psychological Bulletin*](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/13782725) — the canonical meta-analysis of 85 years of personnel selection research — reports that structured interviews predict job performance at validity coefficient ~0.51, versus ~0.38 for unstructured. The 2016 update ([Schmidt, Oh & Shaffer 2016, unpublished but widely circulated working paper](https://home.ubalt.edu/tmitch/645/articles/2016-schmidt-OH-jones-the%20Validity%20and%20Utility%20of%20Selection%20Methods%20in%20Personnel%20Psychology.pdf) `[verify URL]`) revised some numbers downward but kept structured interviews above unstructured. What does this mean for Priya? The interview she will face is much more likely to be *unstructured* (Hyderabad BPO walk-in: "tell me about yourself, why do you want this job?"), but her *answers* should be structured. The asymmetry is the leverage point. A structured answer in an unstructured interview lands cleanly because the interviewer is bored by unstructured noise.

The 90-second constraint is operationally important and deserves its own anchor. Practitioner consensus across interview-coaching literature ([Manciagli 2014, *Cut the Crap, Get a Job!*](https://www.amazon.com/Cut-Crap-Get-Job-Career/dp/0985213809) `[verify]`; [LinkedIn Learning interview-prep courses](https://www.linkedin.com/learning/) — non-peer-reviewed but converging) puts the ideal behavioral-answer length at roughly 60–120 seconds. Below 60: the candidate looks unprepared. Above 120: the interviewer is calculating their grocery list. The 90-second center allows for: setup (10–15s), the action (40–50s), the result (15–20s), the learning (10–15s). The chapter's exercise should literally include a phone-timer practice.

The variant for thin work history. The standard STAR assumes the candidate has work situations to draw on. Priya has *Salesian sisters' residence + literacy program + community-living responsibilities + AI training*. The chapter's adaptive move is to teach her that situation does not require a paid-job context. *"Last year a new resident, age 9, was struggling with her homework. I was responsible for the evening study time..."* is a complete STAR answer for a customer-service interview. The hiring manager hears: she has been responsible for someone, she took initiative, she got a result, she learned. The translation from residence experience to STAR is the same translation move Unit 3 taught for the CV, ported into spoken form.

### A.3 The "I don't know" question — three acceptable responses and one that ends the interview

The TIKTOC names this as a core teaching item. The literature on it is thin (most interview-prep guidance assumes the candidate will *know* the answer), but practitioner sources converge ([Roose 2019, *NYT*, behavioral-interview practice](https://www.nytimes.com)`[verify]`; [Glassdoor interview-coaching content](https://www.glassdoor.com/blog/) — non-academic, converging). The four moves, three acceptable and one fatal:

**Acceptable 1 — the clarifying question.** *"Could you tell me a little more about what you mean? I want to make sure I answer the question you are actually asking."* This buys her time, signals that she listens for precision, and often produces a re-statement of the question that is easier to answer. It is the highest-status move because it shows she is willing to ask before she answers.

**Acceptable 2 — the honest admission with redirect.** *"I haven't been in that exact situation before. The closest I have done is [X]. Would that be useful to share?"* This is the answer for behavioral questions about experience she does not have. It admits the gap without collapsing, and offers an adjacent experience. The redirect must be sincere; "I haven't done X but I once did Y which is similar" is acceptable only if Y is genuinely similar.

**Acceptable 3 — structured thinking-out-loud.** *"I would need to think about this. Let me try — first I would [step 1], then [step 2], because [reason]. Does that make sense?"* This is the answer for hypothetical / scenario questions she has not rehearsed. Hiring managers explicitly value this move; ([McKinsey case-interview prep materials](https://www.mckinsey.com/careers/interviewing/how-to-do-well-in-the-case-interview) `[verify URL]` document it as a positive signal in consulting hiring, and the move ports to BPO problem-solving questions). The chapter teaches her to use sequencing words — *first, then, because, finally* — to make the structure audible.

**Fatal — silence and visible distress, or guessing with confidence and being wrong.** Silence under five seconds is normal thinking; silence past ten seconds without acknowledgment ("let me think for a moment") triggers the interviewer's discomfort response. The confident-and-wrong move is worse — *"Yes, I have handled difficult customers many times"* when she has not, in an interview where the next question will be "Can you give me an example?" — ends the interview as decisively as silence. The chapter must teach: *the cost of an honest "I don't know, here is what I'd do" is much lower than the cost of a confident guess that the next question exposes*. Hiring managers in Hyderabad BPO walk-in interviews are particularly tuned for this because they conduct 30 such interviews a day and the difference between rehearsed-honest and fabricated-confident is loud.

### A.4 Specific feedback as the active ingredient (Hattie & Timperley; Wisniewski et al.)

The volunteer feedback is what makes or breaks the unit. The literature on what makes feedback effective converges around two anchors:

[Hattie & Timperley 2007, *Review of Educational Research*](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.3102/003465430298487) — the canonical model. Feedback is effective when it answers three questions for the learner: *Where am I going? (Feed Up.) How am I going? (Feed Back.) Where to next? (Feed Forward.)* The most powerful feedback is *task-level* and *process-level*, weakest is *self-level* (praise about the person). For the mock interview, this maps cleanly:

- **Feed Up** = "the job interview you are preparing for will sound like X." (Implicit; Units 1–7 do most of this.)
- **Feed Back** = "in that 90 seconds, the structure was strong but the second sentence dropped your volume — when you said *I helped a new resident*, it sounded uncertain." (Specific, task-and-process level.)
- **Feed Forward** = "next time, say that sentence ending on the *d* of *helped* with the same volume as the start. Like this: *I helped a new resident.* Try it once now." (Specific, actionable, immediately rehearsable.)

[Wisniewski, Zierer & Hattie 2020, *Frontiers in Psychology*](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2019.03087/full) — meta-meta-analysis of feedback research, 435 effect sizes. Average effect size of feedback on learning ~0.48, but with enormous variance. The variance is what matters: feedback works *when it carries information about the task*, not when it carries valence about the person. *"Your English is good!"* (self-level, low information) has near-zero effect; *"the third sentence used a Telugu time-order — *Tomorrow morning 10 o'clock* — try it the English way: *at 10 o'clock tomorrow morning*"* (task-level, high information) has high effect. This is the load-bearing distinction the volunteer must internalize.

The chapter's volunteer-training section should be a checklist of *what good feedback sounds like*:

- Names a specific moment ("when you said X...")
- Describes what happened ("...your voice dropped at the end of the sentence")
- Names what it sounded like to the listener ("...which made the sentence sound like a question instead of a statement")
- Gives the alternative ("...try it again with the same volume on the last word: like this")
- Asks for an attempt ("...can you say that one for me now?")

This is feedback as instruction-plus-rehearsal, not feedback as judgment. The Hattie-Timperley *feed forward* move is the rehearsal at the end — *"can you say that for me now?"* — and is the single most-skipped move in untrained volunteer feedback sessions.

### A.5 Self-recording for self-assessment — the ESL literature

Self-recording (record yourself, listen back, identify what you would change) is a long-established move in speaking-practice pedagogy for second-language learners. [Lynch 2007, *Language Teaching Research*](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1362168807077562) — Edinburgh study of transcription-based self-correction in ESL — found that learners who transcribed and reviewed their own recordings improved measurably on the features they had transcribed, even without explicit instruction. The mechanism is *noticing*: the act of listening to one's own output flags the gap between intended and produced language in a way that produces correction.

The chapter's Exercise 1 (record a 90-second "tell me about yourself" on her phone) is operationally a self-recording task. The pedagogical instruction has to be specific about what to listen for; without scaffolding, the learner will hear "my English is bad" (self-level, useless). The scaffolded version: *Listen once for content (did you say what you meant to say?). Listen again for pace (where were the long pauses? where did you speed up?). Listen a third time for pronunciation (which words did you say uncertainly?). For each, write one thing you would change.* This breaks the listen-back into three task-level passes, which produces noticing.

Video self-recording (the volunteer Zoom session can be recorded with consent) is documented as more powerful than audio-only for interview-prep specifically ([Fukkink et al. 2011, *Educational Research Review*](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1747938X10000345) — meta-analysis of video feedback in teacher training, generalizes to interview practice). The chapter should make Zoom recording standard — Priya watches the mock session back 24 hours later, with the volunteer's feedback notes alongside. The two passes (live experience + recorded review) produce more learning than the live session alone.

### Worked example — Priya does the mock interview

A full walk-through the chapter author should build out. Architecture here.

**Setup.** Priya has applied to a Foundever Hyderabad day-shift posting (from Unit 4 exercise). The mock interview is scheduled on Zoom for Saturday morning at 10 AM. The volunteer is Anjali, a Humanitarians AI volunteer who has worked at Concentrix Hyderabad for three years and was trained on the mock-interview protocol the previous week.

**Pre-session.** Priya has spent the week:
- Recorded three 90-second versions of "tell me about yourself" on her phone. Listened back to each. Picked the version that sounded most like her.
- Written answers in the situation framework to: "Why do you want this job?" "What is your strength?" "What would you do if a customer was angry with you?"
- Reviewed her CV one more time. (Anjali will have it open during the session.)
- Slept well. Eaten breakfast. Put on the clothes she would wear to a real walk-in interview.

**The session — first three minutes.** Anjali opens with what Pekrun would call a *control-raising* frame: *"Hi Priya. This is a practice session, not a test. We will do 15 minutes of mock interview, then we will spend 15 minutes on feedback. If anything goes wrong in the middle, that is exactly what this session is for. Ready?"* The reframe is doing work: *practice, not test* moves the session into the high-control quadrant.

**Question 1: Tell me about yourself.** Priya delivers a 90-second answer she rehearsed. It is structurally good — opens with name, residence context, education, interest in customer service. Two specific problems: she said *"I have completed 12th class"* (factually unclear — she is *in* 12th, not done with it) and her voice dropped on the last sentence ("...I am looking for an entry-level position").

**Question 2: Why do you want this job?** Priya's answer is generic: *"I want to work in BPO because it is a good opportunity."* This is the kind of answer the chapter has warned against — it could be said by any candidate to any BPO posting.

**Question 3: What is your strength?** Priya: *"My strength is hard work and punctuality."* Anjali asks her to give an example. Priya gives a good example from her residence experience: helping a younger resident with homework consistently for four months. The example saves the answer.

**Question 4: What would you do if a customer was angry?** Priya freezes. Twelve seconds of silence. Anjali waits. Priya finally says *"I would... I would try to help the customer."* Anjali continues to the next question.

**Question 5 (filler): How is your day going?** Anjali asks this to give Priya a recovery moment. Priya answers normally. The session closes on time.

**Feedback debrief — 15 minutes, the load-bearing part.** Anjali opens by asking Priya: *"What did you feel went well? What did you feel went badly?"* This is Hattie-Timperley *feed up* in the form of a question — anchoring the feedback in the learner's own perception before adding the volunteer's view.

Anjali's specific feedback, in the Hattie-Timperley structure:

1. **The 12th class wording.** "When you said *I have completed 12th class*, the recruiter will check your CV which says you are *in* 12th, expected 2026. The wording doesn't match the CV. Try: *I am in my final year of 12th class, expected to complete in 2026.* Say that for me now." Priya says it. Anjali nods. "Good — now it matches your CV and sounds confident."

2. **Voice dropping at the end.** "When you said *I am looking for an entry-level position*, your voice got quieter on *position*. It made the whole sentence sound uncertain. Try ending the sentence with the same volume you started. Like this: *I am looking for an entry-level position.* You hear how the last word is still strong? Try it now." Priya tries. First attempt still trails off. Second attempt lands. Anjali: "Yes. That is what to practice for the next week."

3. **The why-do-you-want-this-job answer.** "Your answer was true but generic — every candidate today will give the same answer. The interviewer wants to know *why this job, at this company, for you specifically*. From your CV I see you have helped at the sisters' residence. Could you connect that to wanting to talk to customers? Try one sentence." Priya: *"I want to work in customer service because at the residence I learned how to listen to people who are upset and help them, and I think this is the same skill."* Anjali: "Yes. That is now an answer only you could give. Practice this version this week."

4. **The angry-customer freeze.** "When you didn't know what to say, you went silent for 12 seconds. That is too long. The interviewer would start to worry. The three things you can do when you don't have an answer ready — do you remember from the chapter?" Priya recalls: clarifying question, honest admission with redirect, thinking-out-loud. Anjali: "Good. For that question, you could have said: *Let me think for a moment. First, I would listen to the customer and let them finish what they want to say. Then I would say I am sorry they are having this problem. Then I would try to understand what specifically happened so I can help.* That is thinking-out-loud, and it is a complete answer."

5. **Pronunciation, register, content summary.** Pronunciation: clear, no significant issues; one Telugu-time-order slip in Question 1 corrected on the fly. Register: professional throughout, no slang, one *Sir* over-saturation in Question 3 (used three times in 90 seconds, should be once). Content: strong on residence-experience translation in Question 3; weak on specificity in Question 2 (now fixed); freeze on Question 4 to address with practice.

**Closing.** Anjali assigns Priya to: (a) re-record the four answers, applying the corrections, by Wednesday; (b) practice the angry-customer answer three times out loud, watching herself in a mirror or recording herself, by Friday; (c) watch the Zoom recording of today's session at least once between now and the next check-in. Anjali sends the recording and a written summary of the feedback within 24 hours.

**The unit-completion gate.** The session counts as completed once Priya has done it. The unit does not require she gets a "passing" mock interview; it requires she does the session and applies the feedback. The TIKTOC is explicit on this: *she does not pass this unit without the live session*. Implementation: completion = scheduled, attended, received feedback, submitted revised answer recordings. The actual performance quality is irrelevant for unit completion; what matters is the loop.

## B. Domain examples and cases — Indian and Hyderabad-specific

### B.1 Indian professional interview culture — five specific norms that differ from American conventions

The chapter has to teach these explicitly because American interview-prep content dominates online English-language resources and gives Priya the wrong defaults.

**Eye contact.** American interview-prep teaches *sustained eye contact = confidence*. Indian professional culture is more variable. In Hyderabad BPO and corporate contexts, eye contact with a supervisor or interviewer is professional and expected when speaking and listening, but *prolonged* unbroken eye contact (American "look them in the eye for the whole answer") can read as challenging or disrespectful, particularly with older or more senior interviewers. The operational rule: meet the interviewer's eyes when greeting, when starting an answer, and at the end of an answer; let the gaze move naturally between the interviewer's face and the document or screen in front of you during longer answers. ([Verma 2013, *Cross-Cultural Communication*](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/272484671) `[verify]`; converging Indian HR-training content from [TimesAscent](https://www.timesascent.com) and [Naukri career advice](https://www.naukri.com/career-advice).)

For Zoom mock interviews specifically: the chapter should teach her to look at the camera (not at the screen) when she speaks. This *looks* like eye contact to the volunteer. It is a learnable physical skill that produces the right impression.

**Titles and address.** Sir / Madam (or Ma'am) is still standard at entry level in Hyderabad BPO, healthcare, and NGO sectors. ([Sailaja 2009, *Indian English*, Edinburgh University Press](https://edinburghuniversitypress.com/book-indian-english.html); converging practitioner usage.) First-name address from a junior to a senior is *not* standard in most Indian entry-level contexts, regardless of whether the senior uses the junior's first name. The chapter teaches: address the interviewer as *Sir* or *Ma'am* unless and until they explicitly invite first-name use ("Please call me Rohan"). Even then, *Mr. Kumar* in writing remains acceptable. The single *Sir* per message rule from Unit 1 applies to spoken interviews too — once per answer is appropriate, three times in 90 seconds is over-saturation. This differs from older Indian-government interview conventions where *Sir* appeared at every sentence break; modern Hyderabad corporate norms have moved away from this.

**Punctuality.** Indian professional interview punctuality is treated as an unmistakable signal. In Hyderabad BPO walk-in culture, arriving 15–30 minutes early to a walk-in window is *normal*; arriving exactly on time is *acceptable*; arriving even five minutes late is a negative mark. ([Verma 2013](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/272484671) `[verify]`; HR practitioner consensus.) For Zoom: log in 5 minutes before the scheduled start. The chapter has to teach this as different from American norms where exactly-on-time is the standard. *Early = professional* in Indian corporate culture in a way American interview-prep does not capture.

**Dress code by sector.** Covered more fully in Unit 9 but relevant here because mock-interview Zoom sessions should be dressed as the real interview would be. BPO walk-in: business casual — collared shirt or salwar-kameez (kurti + churidar) in muted color; closed-toe shoes; minimal jewelry. Hospital reception interview: similar, slightly more formal — kurti + dupatta is standard; uniform may be supplied later. NGO interview: more variable, generally a clean salwar-kameez or a kurti and pants; less strict than BPO. ([Mannar 2018, *Indian Workplace Dress Conventions*](https://www.indiacareers.com) `[verify]`; converging guidance from Indian career-services content.)

The chapter teaches her to dress for the mock interview as she will dress for the real one. The exercise of dressing changes the mental state in ways that pure mental rehearsal does not — there is a small embodied-cognition literature on this ([Adam & Galinsky 2012, *Journal of Experimental Social Psychology*, "Enclothed Cognition"](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0022103112000200)) suggesting clothing affects task-relevant cognitive processes. The effect sizes are debated, but the operational implication — dress for the practice as for the real — is defensible regardless of the size.

**The question Priya will be asked that no American interview-prep prepares her for.** *"Are you comfortable with the night shift?"* (BPO.) *"What is your father's occupation?"* (Some traditional firms; less common in 2026 but not gone.) *"Where do you stay in Hyderabad?"* (Standard; not invasive in Indian context the way it would read in American.) *"Can you start tomorrow?"* (BPO walk-ins; tests both availability and how she handles a quick decision.) The chapter should prepare her for these question types by name — not because they require specific scripted answers, but because not being surprised by them is half the work.

### B.2 The Hyderabad BPO walk-in interview specifically

The walk-in is the high-conversion event for entry-level BPO hiring (Unit 4, §B.6). The interview format that follows differs from the structured-Zoom mock the unit teaches:

- **Compressed timeline.** A walk-in screening is typically 5–7 minutes, not 15. The interviewer is processing 20–40 candidates a day.
- **Voice-test component.** Some Hyderabad BPOs run a *voice and accent assessment* on the spot — read a paragraph aloud, repeat words, answer a question in English. ([Concentrix India hiring process](https://www.concentrix.com/careers/) `[verify]`; converging description across Naukri-published candidate experiences.) Priya should expect this and not be surprised.
- **Behavioral question + scenario question + closing.** Compressed STAR. The chapter prepares her for the 5-minute version too — same 90-second answer can compress to 45 seconds for a walk-in if needed.

The mock-interview Zoom session in Unit 8 is calibrated to the *fullest* version of the interview she might face. If she can do 15 minutes well, the 5-minute walk-in is easier, not harder. The training-up-to-the-harder-context principle.

### B.3 The Apollo Hospitals reception interview

For Track B candidates. Apollo entry-level non-clinical interviews ([Apollo careers](https://www.apollohospitals.com/careers) `[verify]`) tend to be more conversational than BPO walk-ins. The interviewer is often a department supervisor (Front Desk Manager, OPD Coordinator) rather than HR. Question types:

- *"Why do you want to work in healthcare?"* — direct, sincere answer expected. The chapter teaches Priya to anchor in residence experience: she has helped care for people, she wants to work where helping is the job.
- *"Are you comfortable with patients in distress?"* — situational. Honest answer with grounded example: yes, drawing on residence experience of helping a sick or upset resident.
- *"Can you handle telephone bookings in English / Hindi / Telugu?"* — practical question. Honest answer about her actual three-language comfort levels.
- *"How will you maintain confidentiality of patient information?"* — values question. Sisters' teaching directly applicable.

The mock-interview unit should accommodate Track B candidates with a Track B–appropriate scenario question, but the four universal questions (tell me about yourself / why this job / strength / what would you do if) are sector-universal. The mock-interview format is the same; the specific scenario question varies.

### B.4 The NGO field-coordinator interview

For Track C candidates. The interview is often the most conversational of the three sectors — the NGO interviewer is typically a Program Manager who is herself underpaid and has time. The interview can run 30–45 minutes. Distinct features:

- *"Tell me about your story."* — broader autobiographical question than BPO. The chapter has to teach Priya to tell her story without over-sharing (the residence is not a poverty story; it is a structured-program story) or under-selling (her years in the residence are an asset, not a hidden detail to minimize).
- *"How does the work we do here connect to your own experience?"* — connection question. Direct and sincere answer expected.
- *"Why this organization specifically?"* — research expectation. The chapter teaches Priya to look up the organization before the interview (Unit 4 prepared her for this).
- *"What would you do if you saw a child in distress on a home visit?"* — sector-specific scenario. The chapter walks through a thinking-out-loud answer.

The mock-interview format adapts to a longer NGO conversation by extending to 25 minutes for Track C, but the structure (four universal questions, then sector-specific, then close) holds.

### B.5 The Humanitarians AI volunteer pool

The unit depends on Humanitarians AI volunteers running the mock interviews competently. The volunteer pool composition affects what the chapter can promise:

- Volunteers from BPO sector (Concentrix, Genpact, Tech Mahindra alumni) — for Track A.
- Volunteers from healthcare sector (Apollo, Care Hospitals, Yashoda alumni) — for Track B.
- Volunteers from NGO sector (CRY, Magic Bus, Akshaya Patra, smaller Hyderabad NGOs) — for Track C.

The chapter should not promise specific volunteer profiles. It should describe the architecture: *the volunteer is someone who has hired or worked alongside people in entry-level roles in your track. She will run a 15-minute mock interview as if you were applying for an entry-level role in her sector.* The TIKTOC's noted risk (Risk 1, volunteer quality inconsistency) is a real concern; the chapter's responsibility is to teach Priya how to make a useful session even with a less-experienced volunteer (by asking specific feedback questions herself — *"What is one thing I should change about how I answered Question 2?"*).

## C. Connections and dependencies

- **Depends on:** Unit 1 (register) — spoken professional Indian English is the register the mock interview is conducted in.
- **Depends on:** Unit 2 (AI as work tool) — Priya may have used ChatGPT to draft and rehearse her answers (an acceptable use; the test is whether she can defend them in the live session).
- **Depends on:** Unit 3 (CV) — the volunteer reads the CV before the call. The CV defines what Priya will be asked about. Anything on the CV must be defensible.
- **Depends on:** Unit 4 (job search) — the mock interview is for a specific posting Priya is considering, not a generic one. Anchoring in a real posting makes the questions and answers concrete.
- **Depends on:** Units 5–7 (track-specific) — the sector-specific scenario question requires sector-specific preparation done in the track units.
- **Unlocks:** Unit 9 (first week) — Priya who has done a mock interview has spoken professional English to a stranger once. The first-week unit assumes this baseline.
- **Unlocks:** the real interview. The mock is the rehearsal; the real interview is the next event. The chapter should explicitly point at this transition.

## D. Current state of the field

### Settled

- Structured interviews predict job performance more reliably than unstructured ([Schmidt & Hunter 1998](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/13782725); converging meta-analyses).
- Behavioral interview answer frameworks (STAR / SOAR / situation) improve perceived competence in hiring decisions (practitioner consensus across HR literature; no single empirical anchor but converging).
- Specific task-level and process-level feedback is more effective than general or self-level feedback ([Hattie & Timperley 2007](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.3102/003465430298487); [Wisniewski et al. 2020](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2019.03087/full)).
- Self-recording and review improves second-language speaking skills ([Lynch 2007](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1362168807077562)).
- Achievement anxiety responds to changes in perceived control, not changes in perceived feeling ([Pekrun 2006](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-006-9029-9)).

### Contested

- **The magnitude of the structured-vs-unstructured-interview validity gap.** Schmidt & Hunter's 0.51 vs. 0.38 has been revised by later work; the directional finding holds but the exact numbers shift across methodology choices.
- **Whether mock interviews actually transfer to real interview performance.** Most studies are pre-post designs comparing self-rated confidence before vs. after a mock interview ([Hollandsworth, Glazeski & Dressel 1978, *Journal of Counseling Psychology*](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1979-29571-001); subsequent literature). Transfer to actual offer rates is harder to measure; the studies that do attempt this report positive but modest effects. The chapter should treat mock-interview value as *plausible and supported by adjacent research*, not as a strongly established causal claim.
- **Whether dress-as-cognition effects ("enclothed cognition") are robust.** [Adam & Galinsky 2012](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0022103112000200) has not always replicated cleanly. The implication for interview prep (dress for the real interview during the practice) is defensible on adjacent grounds (rehearsal-fidelity, mental state) even if the specific cognitive-effect numbers are unreliable.

### Unsettled

- **Optimal feedback session length.** 15 minutes is convention; no strong empirical base. Practitioners use it because it fits the time budget; whether 30 minutes would be better is unknown.
- **Whether Zoom mock interviews transfer as well to in-person walk-ins as in-person mock interviews would.** The Zoom format is a constraint of the program's structure (volunteers are remote). The chapter should not over-claim transfer.
- **Whether anxiety reduction or skill acquisition is the active ingredient.** Both happen; which matters more for Priya's actual interview performance is not separable.

## E. Teaching considerations — where ESL learners get stuck

### E.1 The "I sound stupid in English" spiral

The dominant failure mode in the first 30 seconds of the mock interview. Priya hears her own voice in English, hears it as inadequate, and the rest of the interview is conducted under threat. The Pekrun control-value frame predicts exactly this: the perceived-control variable collapses, cognitive resources move to threat-monitoring, performance degrades, the degraded performance confirms the original perception. The downward spiral.

Interventions the chapter can teach:

- **Pre-session physical anchor.** Three slow breaths *before* logging in, hand on her own collarbone, said out loud once: *"This is practice, not the real thing."* The physical anchor and the verbal frame, repeated as a ritual, give the threat-system a recognizable signal that this is a low-stakes context.
- **The "this is the worst I will sound" reframe.** The chapter teaches: *the mock interview is the worst this conversation will sound, because every subsequent interview you do will be after this one. There is no scenario where the mock interview is the high-water mark of your interview English. Every interview improves on it.* This is the truth, and the reframe redirects attention from "I sound stupid" to "this is the floor."
- **The "two-thirds is enough" rule.** If she gets two-thirds of her rehearsed answer out at the right register, the answer worked. Perfectionism is the spiral's fuel; the two-thirds rule is the fuel cutoff.

### E.2 The volunteer who is too kind

A real risk the chapter should name. Some Humanitarians AI volunteers, faced with an under-confident ESL learner from a residential program, will pull punches. *"Your English is good!"* *"You did great!"* *"Nothing to change!"* This is well-intentioned and produces zero learning. The chapter has to give Priya a way to extract useful feedback even from a too-kind volunteer:

**The three questions she asks at the end of the session, regardless of what the volunteer offers spontaneously:**

1. *"What is one thing I should change about how I answered Question 1?"*
2. *"What is one word or phrase I used that sounded uncertain?"*
3. *"If you were the actual hiring manager, what would have made you not call me back?"*

The third question is the most powerful and the one most volunteers will not raise on their own. Asking it directly gives the volunteer permission to give the feedback she would otherwise withhold. The chapter teaches Priya these three questions as a script.

### E.3 The volunteer who is too harsh

The mirror risk, less common but documented. A volunteer who treats the mock interview as a real interview, lists every flaw, and offers no feed-forward, produces withdrawal. The chapter teaches Priya:

- *Distinguish between feedback you can act on (specific, actionable, one thing at a time) and feedback that is judgment (general, evaluative, multiple things at once).* Act on the first kind; do not internalize the second.
- *You can ask the volunteer to slow down or focus.* *"Could you give me the one most important thing to work on this week?"* is a polite, professional request that re-anchors the session in actionable feedback.

### E.4 Telugu-to-English transfer issues that will show up in spoken form

Unit 1's register notes covered written transfer. Spoken transfer adds:

- **Time-order in spoken sentences.** *"I am working in residence from six years"* (Telugu time-order, sustained-action structure) where English wants *"I have been at the residence for six years."* The simple-present-continuous-for-completed-state is a common Indian-English form but reads as non-standard in interview contexts.
- **Question structure as statement.** *"You will tell me when I can start?"* with the falling intonation of a statement, where English wants *"Could you tell me when I can start?"* with a rising or modal-marked structure.
- **Filler patterns.** *"Yaar, basically, the thing is..."* in casual speech is fine; *"actually, basically, basically..."* in interview English is a register slip. The chapter can teach a small set of professional fillers (*"Let me think for a moment"*, *"That is a good question"*) to replace casual fillers.

The chapter should not catalog these as Telugu errors; it should teach the consultative-register alternative. The volunteer's job in feedback is to flag when one of these shows up in the live session and offer the alternative for rehearsal.

### E.5 The Indian-context analogy that works

The closest cultural analogue for Priya is the *parents'-meeting* conversation — the meeting where a Salesian sister or program coordinator speaks with a family-of-origin or potential sponsor about her future. She has been in versions of this conversation before, possibly many times. The shape is the same: respectful address (*Sister*, *Sir*, *Madam*), structured answers ("How are your studies going, child?" → answered with structure, not rambling), questions that test what she has been doing (not what she will do), an evaluation by an adult who is making a decision about her.

The chapter can use this analogue lightly: *the interview is the parents'-meeting conversation, ported into professional English*. The respectful register, the structured answer, the truthfulness, the way one's gaze meets the elder's at appropriate moments — all of these are skills Priya already has in Telugu. The mock interview is the skill exercised in English with a stranger she will probably never meet again. The analogy works because it does not patronize (it draws on a real skill) and it does not over-claim (the mock interview has features the parents' meeting doesn't, which the chapter then teaches).

### E.6 What "I don't know" sounds like in three languages

Worth naming explicitly. In Telugu, *naaku teliyadu* — direct, conventional, not a status-loss. In Hindi, *mujhe nahi pata* — same. In American business English, *I don't know* unaccompanied is acceptable in some contexts but increasingly accompanied by *let me find out* or *let me think about that*. In Indian Business English, *I don't know* alone reads as too blunt; the chapter teaches the consultative form *"I haven't had that experience, but..."* or *"Let me think — first I would..."* — the Acceptable variants from §A.3.

The shift from monolingual-Telugu *naaku teliyadu* (one form, all contexts) to Indian-Business-English *let me think for a moment, then [answer]* (multi-form, register-marked) is a register translation, not a competence shift. The chapter teaches it as such.

### E.7 The Zoom-specific issues

The mock interview is on Zoom because the volunteers are remote. Zoom adds specific friction:

- **Audio lag.** A 0.5-second delay can make Priya start an answer while the volunteer is still finishing the question. The chapter teaches her to pause one beat after the volunteer stops speaking before answering.
- **Camera position.** Looking at the screen (where the volunteer's face is) shows on the volunteer's screen as Priya looking *down*. Looking at the camera (small dot above the screen) shows as eye contact. The chapter teaches: *for important moments (greeting, the start of an answer, the close), look at the camera. For listening, look at the screen.*
- **Lighting.** Backlight (window behind her) silhouettes her face; the volunteer cannot read expressions. The chapter teaches: *light source in front of you, screen between you and a darker background.*
- **Stable internet.** Hyderabad mobile data is generally good but variable. The chapter teaches: *for the mock interview, use the residence wifi if available; have her phone fully charged; have a backup audio option (phone call number from the volunteer) in case video fails.*

These are operational details that practitioners write blog posts about ([Forbes 2020 onward, "Zoom interview tips"](https://www.forbes.com/sites/jackkelly/) `[verify]`). The chapter should compress them into a one-paragraph pre-session checklist.

### Sharpest single finding for Unit 8

The Pekrun control-value frame and the Hattie-Timperley feedback frame, taken together, mean the mock interview's pedagogical effect is determined *entirely* by what the volunteer says in the 15-minute feedback debrief, not by what happens in the 15-minute interview itself. The interview is the data-gathering event. The debrief is the learning event. A chapter that treats the interview as the load-bearing 15 minutes and the debrief as the wrap-up has the proportions wrong. The chapter should give equal or greater attention to debrief structure as to interview structure. The volunteer-side training material — what good feedback sounds like, the three Hattie-Timperley questions, the rehearsal close — is more consequential to the unit's success than any preparation Priya can do alone.
