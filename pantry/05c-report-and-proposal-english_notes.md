# Research Note — Unit 5C: Report and Proposal English

*Working pantry document for Homes of Hope +1, Course 2 — Unit 5C (Report and Proposal English, Track C NGO Administration). The chapter teaches Priya (19, Telugu first language, ~350 English words, Units 1–4 done) to write a one-paragraph situation summary and a basic funding-request sentence in professional English. Notes are for the chapter author. Compiled 2026-05-17.*

---

## Preliminary: the unit's load-bearing claim

A program associate at a small Hyderabad NGO is not asked to write a 20-page proposal. She is asked, often weekly, to write a one-paragraph situation summary for an internal report, a board update, a donor email, or a social-media post. She is also asked to write one-line funding requests — *"we need Rs. 50,000 to run this program for one quarter"* — that her senior colleagues then fold into larger proposals. The unit's job is to make these two units of writing — *the paragraph* and *the sentence* — fast, accurate, and donor-readable.

The chapter resists two failure modes. The first is **development-jargon overload**: paragraphs stuffed with *empowerment, capacity-building, holistic approach, stakeholders, beneficiary-centric* until they say nothing specific. The second is **under-claim**: paragraphs so cautious and qualified that no reader can tell what problem is being addressed or what the program is asking for. The target is a third register: *specific, modest, action-oriented English that names the population, the gap, the consequence, and the proposed action*.

## A. Conceptual foundations — 3–5 most important ideas

### A.1 The four-move situation summary — who / what / so what / therefore

The structure that works for situation summaries in NGO reports is durable across decades of development writing and is encoded in nearly every grant-writing manual ([Robinson 2004, *Grant Writing for Dummies*](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/Grant+Writing+For+Dummies%2C+8th+Edition-p-9781119868194) `[verify edition]`; [Foundation Center / Candid materials](https://www.candid.org/training); [GrantSpace tutorials](https://learning.candid.org/)) and adapted into Indian-NGO style in CRY's, Pratham's, Akshaya Patra's, HelpAge's, and Magic Bus's annual report formats `[verify with current annual reports]`. The four moves:

1. **Who** — the population in specific terms. *"6,400 girls aged 11–14 attending Telangana government schools in three mandals of Mahbubnagar district."* Not *"underprivileged girls in rural India."*
2. **What** — the gap or problem in observable terms. *"of these, only 34% complete Class 8 on schedule; the rest drop out or repeat at least one year."* Not *"face significant educational challenges."*
3. **So what** — the consequence. *"Girls who do not complete Class 8 on schedule have a 71% higher likelihood of marriage before age 18, according to NFHS-5 district-level data."* Not *"this has serious implications for their futures."*
4. **Therefore** — the proposed action and what it produces. *"Our after-school study program in three of these mandals supports 240 girls weekly with structured tutoring, English-and-math practice, and a stipend for school supplies, and has retained 92% in school for the past two years."* Not *"we implement holistic interventions to address these challenges."*

The whole paragraph runs 100–150 words. The chapter teaches it as a literal four-sentence template Priya fills in. The hardest move for new program associates is move 3 — the *so what*. Without it, the reader is told a problem and a program but never told why the program matters. With it, the reader has a reason to care.

### A.2 The funding-request sentence — the canonical "X funds Y for Z over W" form

The funding-request sentence has a canonical structure that has crystallized across the international development sector ([USAID grant guidance](https://www.usaid.gov/work-usaid/get-grant-or-contract); [BMGF program officer guidance](https://www.gatesfoundation.org/about/policies-and-resources/grantee-handbook) `[verify`]; [Indian CSR reporting templates under the Companies Act 2013](https://www.mca.gov.in/MinistryV2/csr_data.html)). The form:

> *"[Amount] funds [specific outcomes] for [number of beneficiaries] over [time period]."*

The chapter teaches three versions at three sizes:

- **Sentence-level (one line, for an email or update).** *"Rs. 8 lakh funds after-school tutoring for 240 girls in three Mahbubnagar mandals over 12 months."*
- **Paragraph-level (for an internal proposal).** *"We are requesting Rs. 8 lakh from the Foundation for the period April 2026 to March 2027. This will fund weekly tutoring sessions for 240 girls across three mandals (60 sessions per girl over the year, plus end-of-term assessments), one part-time tutor stipend per village (three tutors, 12 months), school-supplies and stationery for each participant, and a quarterly parent-engagement event. Based on our two-year retention data (92%), we expect to retain at least 220 of the 240 girls in school through the funding period."*
- **Quarterly-update version.** *"In Q2, the Rs. 2 lakh from your previous grant funded 30 sessions per girl across the three villages, retained 232 of 240 girls in regular attendance, and surfaced two girls requiring additional family-engagement support, which we have now provided."*

The structural rule the chapter teaches: *amount, outcomes, beneficiaries, time, in that order, every time*. The reader (donor, board member, CSR officer) has scanned hundreds of funding requests; the predictable structure is itself the credibility signal.

The hardest sub-skill: writing *outcomes* (girls retained) rather than *inputs* (sessions held). The donor is paying for outcomes; the program tracks inputs. The chapter teaches the substitution explicitly with paired examples: *"60 sessions per girl"* (input — what we did) becomes *"232 of 240 girls retained in school"* (outcome — what changed).

### A.3 Donor-facing register vs. internal-program register — what changes

The two registers diverge in vocabulary, voice, and what counts as evidence. The chapter teaches the difference because Priya will be expected to switch fluently.

**Internal program register** (used in team meetings, work-in-progress reports, program-officer-to-program-officer email):
- Inputs detail. *"We ran 18 sessions in Mahbubnagar this quarter, 16 in Wanaparthy, 14 in Nagarkurnool. Attendance averaged 82%."*
- Operational vocabulary. *"Mobilizers identified two new dropouts in October; door-to-door follow-up brought one back."*
- Honest about problems. *"Tutor turnover in Wanaparthy was higher than expected; we have rehired."*
- First-person plural. *"We learned that the parent-engagement model works better with two events per quarter than one."*

**Donor-facing register** (used in grant applications, donor updates, annual reports, board-update emails):
- Outcomes detail. *"232 of 240 girls retained in school through the funding period (92% retention)."*
- Strategic vocabulary. *"The intervention's design — combining tutoring with stipend support and parent engagement — appears to drive the retention outcome."*
- Acknowledges but does not foreground problems. *"Tutor recruitment in one of three mandals required mid-year renewal; recruitment processes have been strengthened."*
- Active, accountable voice. *"We retained 92% of girls and we believe the parent-engagement model is the largest driver."*

The chapter teaches: *the same program week sounds different in the two registers, both honestly*. The difference is *what the reader needs to know*, not *what we say happened*.

### A.4 The Indian CSR context — Companies Act 2013, Section 135

Indian NGOs since 2014 have a unique funding ecosystem: the [Companies Act 2013, Section 135](https://www.mca.gov.in/MinistryV2/csr_data.html) mandates that any company with net worth ≥ Rs. 500 crore, turnover ≥ Rs. 1,000 crore, or net profit ≥ Rs. 5 crore must spend at least 2% of average net profits on CSR activities. This produced a structural change in NGO funding in India between 2014 and 2024 ([Ministry of Corporate Affairs CSR portal data](https://www.csr.gov.in/) — aggregate CSR spending ~Rs. 25,000+ crore annually as of FY23 `[verify current figure]`).

What this means for Priya's writing:

- **CSR-funded reports follow a specific template.** Most CSR donors require quarterly impact reports against pre-stated KPIs. The format is more compressed than international-foundation reports, more numerical, and uses standard categories: project description, expenditure breakdown, beneficiary count, geographic reach, photos with consent, audit-ready documentation. The four-move situation summary fits inside this template.
- **CSR language register.** CSR reports skew slightly more corporate than international-foundation reports. *"Our intervention aligns with the company's CSR focus area on girls' education and skill development"* is acceptable; the same sentence would read as buzzwordy in a Gates Foundation context.
- **CSR vs. foundation vs. individual-donor language.** Three registers in one organization's writing portfolio:
  - **CSR (Indian company funder):** more corporate, KPI-driven, alignment-language acceptable.
  - **International foundation (BMGF, Ford, Gates):** evidence-driven, outcomes-driven, less alignment-language and more independent-impact-language.
  - **Individual donor / monthly giver (CRY, HelpAge, Pratham's online giving):** story-driven, beneficiary-voiced, smaller numbers, more emotional but still anchored to specific outcomes.

The chapter introduces all three registers; Unit 6C develops the international-foundation register in more depth.

### A.5 Plain language as a deliberate move — the readability literature applied

The grant-writing field has converged toward *plain language* as a quality marker. The international Plain Language Action and Information Network ([PLAIN](https://www.plainlanguage.gov/)) defines plain language as: *"communication your audience can understand the first time they read or hear it."* For NGO writing, this means:

- **Specific nouns over abstract nouns.** *Girls aged 11–14* over *adolescent female beneficiaries*.
- **Concrete verbs over nominalizations.** *Girls dropped out* over *Dropout occurrence was observed*.
- **Active voice over passive.** *Our team conducted 18 sessions* over *18 sessions were conducted*.
- **Numbers as words when small, as digits when large or precise.** *Three villages, 240 girls, Rs. 8 lakh.*
- **One idea per sentence.** Compound sentences with three clauses fail readability tests at the level Priya is writing for.

The readability literature is empirical: [Flesch reading-ease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flesch%E2%80%93Kincaid_readability_tests) and Flesch-Kincaid grade-level metrics correlate with reader comprehension. A development-jargon paragraph typically scores at Grade 14+ (graduate level); a plain-language paragraph scores at Grade 8–10. The chapter does not teach the metrics, but it teaches the *rewrite habit* that brings the score down. Priya's first drafts will read at Grade 14+; her second drafts should read at Grade 10. The chapter teaches the rewrite, not the math.

The AI workflow from Unit 2 ports directly: she drafts, asks ChatGPT *"rewrite this in simpler English, one idea per sentence, Indian-English register,"* reviews the output for accuracy, and corrects.

### Worked example — the four-move situation summary, built

**The brief.** Priya works for a small Hyderabad NGO running an after-school program for girls in three mandals of Mahbubnagar district. The program director asks her to write a one-paragraph situation summary for an internal proposal that will go to a CSR donor.

**Attempt 1 — her current level (development-jargon trap):**
*"Our holistic intervention addresses the multifaceted challenges faced by underprivileged adolescent female beneficiaries in rural Telangana. Through capacity-building and stakeholder engagement, we aim to empower these vulnerable populations and create sustainable transformation in their socio-economic landscape, thereby ensuring positive outcomes for all stakeholders involved."*

What is wrong: every noun is abstract (*holistic intervention, multifaceted challenges, underprivileged adolescent female beneficiaries, sustainable transformation, socio-economic landscape*). The reader cannot tell: how many girls, where, what specifically is failing, what specifically the program does, what specifically it produces. The paragraph could describe any program. That is the failure mode.

**Attempt 2 — over-corrected (cold and bare):**
*"There are 6,400 girls in three mandals. 34% finish Class 8. We tutor 240 of them. 92% stay in school."*

What is wrong: each sentence is technically accurate. But the *who* is incomplete (which mandals? what schools?), the *so what* is missing entirely (no consequence stated), and the *therefore* is too compressed to tell the reader what the program does week-to-week. The reader is told facts but not given the situation.

**Attempt 3 — target (four moves, plain language):**
*"In three mandals of Mahbubnagar district — Devarakadra, Bhoothpur, and Wanaparthy — about 6,400 girls aged 11–14 attend Telangana government schools. Of these, only 34% complete Class 8 on schedule; the rest drop out or repeat at least one year. According to NFHS-5 data, girls who do not complete Class 8 on schedule have a 71% higher likelihood of marriage before age 18. Our after-school study program serves 240 girls weekly across these three mandals with structured tutoring in English and mathematics, school-supplies support, and quarterly parent engagement; over the past two years, 92% of our girls have stayed in school through the program."*

The paragraph: 113 words, four moves clearly executed, every claim citable, plain-language register, specific nouns, no jargon. The reader (CSR officer reviewing 40 proposals this week) finishes the paragraph knowing the population, the gap, the consequence, and what the program does. The four-move template gave Priya the structure; her own knowledge of the mandals filled in the specifics.

### Worked example — the funding-request sentence, three sizes

**Sentence (one line):**
*"Rs. 8 lakh funds after-school tutoring for 240 girls in three Mahbubnagar mandals over 12 months, with a target retention of 90%."*

**Paragraph (in proposal):**
*"We are requesting Rs. 8 lakh from the Foundation for the period April 2026 to March 2027. The funding will support weekly tutoring sessions (60 sessions per girl across the year), tutor stipends for three local part-time tutors (one per mandal), school-supplies and stationery for each of the 240 girls, and quarterly parent-engagement events. Based on our 92% retention over the past two years, we expect to retain at least 216 of 240 girls in continuous school enrollment through the funding period."*

**Quarterly report:**
*"In Q2 (July–September 2026), the Rs. 2 lakh of disbursed funding supported 30 tutoring sessions per girl, retained 232 of 240 girls in regular school attendance (97% retention this quarter), and surfaced two cases requiring additional family-engagement support. Both cases are now stable. Expenditure detail and signed attendance records are attached."*

The three sizes show the same information at three densities. Priya learns to scale.

## B. Domain examples and cases — Hyderabad-specific

### B.1 CRY (Child Rights and You) — outcomes-driven national style

CRY's [annual reports](https://www.cry.org/resources/annual-reports/) are a strong domestic exemplar. The voice: child-centered, outcomes-driven, photo-and-quote anchored, audit-clean financials. The reader can move from a single child's named story to the program-level retention number to the financial summary in two pages. The chapter recommends Priya read one CRY annual report cover-to-cover before writing her own first paragraph.

### B.2 Pratham — research-grade evidence integration

Pratham's [annual reports](https://www.pratham.org/about/annual-reports/) and [ASER reports](https://asercentre.org/) demonstrate the strongest evidence-integration register in Indian NGO writing. Outcomes are stated, then connected to ASER survey data, then triangulated against external benchmarks. The register is closer to academic than to CSR-corporate but still plain-language. For Priya's purposes, Pratham is the model of *evidence-anchored claims*.

### B.3 Akshaya Patra — scale-and-operational-clarity register

Akshaya Patra's [reports](https://www.akshayapatra.org/) describe a much larger program (mid-day meals across multiple Indian states) with very clear operational metrics. The strength: every number is contextualized. *"1.8 million meals served daily"* is followed by mandals, partner schools, kitchen locations. The chapter teaches: *every big number gets context, every small number gets significance*.

### B.4 HelpAge India — relational-register example

HelpAge's [reports](https://www.helpageindia.org/our-work/annual-reports/) lean more relational and slightly more emotional (the elderly-care subject matter). The register is warm but still anchored to outcomes. Useful counter-model when Priya's chapter exercise is on a program with strong narrative dimensions.

### B.5 Magic Bus — youth-skills register

Magic Bus's [reports](https://www.magicbus.org/) describe sport-and-livelihood programs for adolescents. The register tilts toward youth-voice and growth-trajectory framing. Useful for Track C girls whose target organizations are youth-focused.

### B.6 The smaller-Hyderabad-NGO reality

Most entry-level NGO admin roles in Hyderabad are not at CRY/Pratham/Akshaya Patra national headquarters. They are at smaller Hyderabad-based NGOs: MV Foundation (child labor / education, [mvfindia.in](https://mvfindia.in/) `[verify URL]`), Care & Share (sponsorship-funded children's programs), Vidya Bharati (education), Ashray, Mahita (women's livelihood). These smaller NGOs typically use a less polished version of the same four-move structure. Priya's chapter target is *passable at a smaller Hyderabad NGO*, not *publishable at CRY's annual report*. The bar is calibrated accordingly.

## D. Current state of the field

### Settled

- The four-move situation summary structure (who / what / so what / therefore) is robust across grant-writing traditions ([Robinson 2004 et seq.](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/Grant+Writing+For+Dummies%2C+8th+Edition-p-9781119868194); [Foundation Center / Candid materials](https://www.candid.org/training); [GrantSpace](https://learning.candid.org/)).
- Plain-language writing improves donor comprehension and approval rates relative to development-jargon writing ([PLAIN guidelines and readability research](https://www.plainlanguage.gov/)).
- The Indian CSR ecosystem (Companies Act 2013, Section 135) has reshaped NGO funding patterns and reporting expectations ([MCA CSR portal data; corroborating sector reports](https://www.csr.gov.in/)).
- Outcomes-language outperforms inputs-language for donor retention and renewal ([Bridgespan Group materials on donor reporting](https://www.bridgespan.org/) `[verify`]; [Candid grantmaker surveys](https://www.candid.org/)).

### Contested

- Whether *aligning with donor priorities* (CSR-style language) is professionally strong or compromising. The pragmatic position: alignment-language is fine when the alignment is genuine; alignment-language is corrupting when it is performed.
- The right level of negative-finding disclosure in donor reports. Conservative practice withholds problems until they're solved; transparent practice names them as they appear. The chapter teaches transparent.

### Unsettled

- The optimal balance of CSR vs. foundation vs. individual-giving in a small Hyderabad NGO's funding mix. Sector-specific advice varies.

## E. Teaching considerations — where ESL learners get stuck

### E.1 The development-jargon temptation

Indian NGO writing has a strong vocabulary tradition — *empowerment, capacity-building, holistic, multifaceted, stakeholders, beneficiaries, sustainable, transformative* — that has become almost reflexive. Priya will absorb it from samples and use it from instinct. The chapter must actively *strip-out drill* her drafts: she reads her own paragraph, circles every abstract noun, and either replaces it with a specific noun or cuts it. The drill is more pedagogically effective than rules-against-jargon-lists.

### E.2 The "what counts as a number" problem

A common failure: the situation summary states the program *"helps many girls"* — the writer knows the actual number (240) but did not include it because she could not remember whether 240 is impressive. The chapter must teach: *specific numbers always beat vague claims, regardless of size*. *"240 girls"* is professional. *"Many girls"* is amateur. The judgment about whether 240 is impressive is the donor's; the writer's job is to state it accurately.

### E.3 The over-claiming reflex on outcomes

The opposite problem. A nervous writer claims *"transformed the lives of 240 girls"* when the actual evidence is *"retained 232 of 240 girls in school for two years."* The first is unverifiable; the second is auditable. The chapter teaches: *outcome claims must be auditable by the donor's accountant*. If she would not want a CRA auditor reading her sentence aloud, she cannot write the sentence.

### E.4 The AI-hallucinated-NGO-statistic trap

ChatGPT will confidently produce *"In Telangana, 53% of girls in rural government schools fail to complete Class 8"* — a sentence that sounds authoritative and may or may not be accurate. The writer who pastes this into a funding proposal exposes the NGO to credibility damage. The chapter's Unit 2 vetting rule applies with sharper force: *every number generated by AI in a Unit 5C paragraph must be re-verified against a primary source — NFHS-5, ASER, Telangana government statistics — before it goes anywhere*.

### E.5 The Salesian-residence-as-source-material angle

Priya has lived inside a residence run on annual reports to the Salesian sisters' superiors, periodic donor updates to international Catholic donors, and program reviews. She has seen the genre of *NGO writing* her whole adolescence. The chapter names this as competence the writer already partly has, even if she has never produced the text herself.

- **Empirical gap:** No public corpus of *bad* and *good* paired NGO situation summaries exists. The contrast pairs in the chapter are the author's reconstruction; they are illustrative, not empirically validated.
- **Authority gap:** *Grant Writing for Dummies* is the most widely used international reference but lacks Indian CSR specificity. The Indian-CSR materials are mostly compliance documentation from MCA, not pedagogical material. The chapter triangulates; a domain-reader (a working Hyderabad NGO program officer) review is recommended.
- **NFHS-5 currency:** NFHS-5 data is from 2019–21. NFHS-6 collection is underway; whether published figures are current at chapter publish-time should be checked. `[verify NFHS-6 status]`
