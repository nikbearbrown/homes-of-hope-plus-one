# Research Note — Unit 1: Professional Register

*Working pantry document for Homes of Hope +1, Course 2 — Unit 1 (Professional Register). Citation-dense source material for a chapter pitched at a 19-year-old Telugu-first-language reader (Priya) with ~350 English words, smartphone fluency, and post-Tier 1–2 Anki literacy. Notes are for the chapter author, not for Priya. Compiled 2026-05-17.*

---

## Preliminary: the move this unit refuses to make

The default move when an English-language pedagogy book meets a sub-fluent Indian learner is to teach a register that doesn't exist in her actual job market. "Dear Sir, I am writing to enquire" sounds British because it is British. The hiring manager at Concentrix Hyderabad did not learn English from the BBC, does not write English like the BBC, and does not promote candidates who do. He writes Indian Standard English. Specifically, Indian Standard *Business* English — a register with its own canonical phrases, its own greetings, its own sign-offs, and its own register-violation tells.

The deficit framing is the failure mode this chapter must avoid. Priya's English is not failed British English. It is early-stage Indian English. The unit's job is not to launder it toward BBC norms but to specify what professional Indian English actually looks like and walk her into the version of it her job market rewards.

This is the [Kachru](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/abs/cambridge-handbook-of-world-englishes/braj-b-kachru-and-the-world-englishes-paradigm/) frame: Indian English is an "outer circle" variety, not a deviation. It has its own norms because it has its own community of fluent users, codified across postcolonial linguistic literature since the 1980s. The chapter author has to internalize this; the chapter itself does not need to argue it.

## A. Conceptual foundations — 3–5 most important ideas

### A.1 Register is the social setting of language, not its grammar

Register is the variation in language that tracks the situation it is used in, not the speaker's competence. The same person can say *"Yaar where are you, I am waiting since one hour"* to a friend on WhatsApp and *"I will be in the lobby at 3:00 PM as discussed"* to her supervisor on email. Neither sentence is more grammatical than the other. They are sized to different social situations.

[Joos 1961](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Joos) named the canonical five styles: *frozen* (legal language, prayer), *formal* (interviews, ceremonies), *consultative* (professional but two-way — most workplace interaction), *casual* (friends), *intimate* (family). For this chapter, only three matter: *casual* (where Priya already lives), *consultative* (where her job actually is), and *formal* (which she will rarely need and should not over-shoot for). The British "Dear Sir / Yours faithfully" register is *formal*; Hyderabad BPO email is mostly *consultative*. Aiming at formal when consultative is the target is a register miss in itself.

[Halliday's 1978 register variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Register_(sociolinguistics)#Halliday) — *field* (what the language is about), *tenor* (who is speaking to whom and what the power relationship is), *mode* (spoken vs. written, sustained vs. ephemeral) — give the chapter its diagnostic vocabulary. WhatsApp shifts *mode* (mostly informal, short) and often *tenor* (peer-to-peer). A professional WhatsApp to a hiring manager keeps WhatsApp's *mode* but takes email's *tenor*. That hybrid is the register Priya needs to learn first because it is the one her job search actually runs on.

### A.2 The same words can do different jobs

This is the unit's load-bearing pedagogical move and the one specified in the TIKTOC. *"Please help me"* and *"I would like to request your assistance"* contain overlapping vocabulary — please, help/assistance, me/I. The grammar in both is correct. The difference is register-marking through three small choices: modal verb (*would like*), hedged action (*request* rather than direct imperative *help*), and abstraction of the noun (*assistance* rather than *help*).

The hiring manager processes this in under one second. The decision he is making is not about Priya's grammar. It is about whether she has access to the register the job lives in. Same vocabulary, different signal.

The chapter has to teach the *signal*, not the words. Priya already has the words.

### A.3 Indian Standard Business English is a real variety with named norms

[Kachru's "three circles" model](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-971X.1985.tb00423.x) places Indian English in the *outer circle* — varieties that emerged from colonial administration, then evolved their own norms in a community of fluent users large enough that the variety is now standardized. The 2019 census recorded more English speakers in India than in any other country except the United States. Indian English is not a learner's English; it is millions of professionals' working English, and it has its own canonical features:

- **Stative present continuous** — "I am understanding" instead of "I understand." Marked as Indian by linguists; standard in most Indian workplaces.
- **"Kindly" as politeness marker** — appears in formal Indian written English roughly where British uses "please."
- **"Do the needful"** — the canonical Indian-English business closing instruction. Means: "take whatever action is appropriate, I trust you to know what it is." Mocked outside India; standard inside it.
- **"Revert"** as verb meaning "reply" — *"please revert by EOD"* (end of day). Not British, not American; Indian-business standard.
- **"Prepone"** — opposite of postpone. Coined in India; now in the [Oxford English Dictionary](https://www.oed.com/dictionary/prepone_v).
- **Title-as-address** — calling the boss *Sir* or *Madam*, sometimes also *Sir/Ma'am*, often in writing. Different from American business norms where first names dominate.

[David Crystal](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/english-as-a-global-language/A930F71B7BC322FCDB6446EFB87D5C2A) (*English as a Global Language*, 2nd ed. 2003) treats this as the central fact of 21st-century English: there are more varieties than one and "global English" is a label for the family, not a single dialect. For Priya, the implication is unburdening — she does not need to sound American or British to sound professional. She needs to sound like the Indian Business English her hiring manager already writes.

### A.4 Telugu-to-English transfer produces specific register pitfalls

Some Telugu-to-English transfers are register-neutral (grammar slips that don't damage signal). Others are register-loud — they mark the speaker as informal in a setting where consultative is expected. The chapter author has to know the difference. The chapter itself doesn't need to name them as Telugu errors; it just needs to teach the consultative-register alternative.

Common transfer pitfalls that produce *register misses* (not just grammar slips):

- **Direct imperative for requests.** Telugu structures of polite request often use imperative + politeness marker. Direct translation produces *"Send the file"* where Indian Business English wants *"Could you please share the file?"* The imperative is grammatical; it just sits at a lower register than the situation requires.
- **Time markers without preposition.** *"I will come tomorrow morning 10 o'clock"* (modeled on Telugu time-phrase order) reads as casual; consultative wants *"I will be there tomorrow at 10 a.m."*
- **Absence of softeners.** Telugu pragmatics carry softening at the level of intonation and particle. English carries softening at the level of modal verb. Drop the modal and the sentence reads blunt: *"I want leave Monday"* reads angry to a hiring manager. *"I would like to request leave on Monday"* reads professional. Same request, different register.
- **"Sir" / "Madam" over-saturation.** Telugu honorific-rich address translates into English as repeated *Sir* — *"Sir, I have one question Sir, when can I start Sir?"* The chapter teaches: one *Sir* or *Ma'am* per message is consultative; three is sub-consultative.
- **"Kindly do the needful"** is borderline. In some Hyderabad offices it is invisible (standard). In others it reads as 1990s register and slightly outdated. The chapter should teach it as recognizable, not as required. *"Please let me know how I can help"* or *"Please share your guidance"* are safer modern equivalents.

The contested ground here is [Sailaja's work on Indian English](https://edinburghuniversitypress.com/book-indian-english.html) (*Indian English*, Edinburgh University Press, 2009), which catalogs these features as legitimate Indian English usage, and the practitioner-facing work (e.g., [Crystal's BBC commentary on "do the needful"](https://www.bbc.com/news/magazine-20500312)) which treats them as quaint. For Priya, the operational rule is: recognize the older Indian-business forms when she sees them in incoming email; produce slightly newer consultative forms in her own writing. The newer forms travel better across companies (a multinational like Genpact will use them; an older Indian firm might still use the older ones).

### A.5 WhatsApp and email run on different registers — and the gap matters

A 2022 [Pew Research](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2018/03/01/social-media-use-in-2018/) finding (US) and converging Indian survey work (e.g., [LocalCircles 2023 surveys on workplace communication](https://www.localcircles.com/a/press/page/workplace-communication-survey) `[verify]`) report that more Indian professionals use WhatsApp than email for day-to-day workplace communication. For BPO and entry-level hiring, the first contact is often a WhatsApp message ("Are you available for an interview Wednesday?"), not an email.

This means the register split Priya needs is not "casual vs. formal" but two consultative variants:

- **Professional WhatsApp register:** short, complete sentences (no missing subjects), proper capitalization on names and on *I*, one greeting at the start (*Good morning ma'am*), no slang (*yaar, na, no?*), no emoji except possibly a single neutral one (*thank you*), no audio messages to a new contact. Speed matters — replying within 12 hours is the consultative norm.
- **Professional email register:** subject line that actually describes the email, greeting line (*Dear Sir / Dear Mr. Kumar / Hello Ms. Reddy* depending on relationship), one-paragraph body that opens with purpose, sign-off (*Best regards / Thanks and regards / Regards*), full name + phone, no abbreviations from WhatsApp (no *u, ur, pls, thx*).

The chapter's pedagogical decision: teach WhatsApp register *first*, because it is what Priya will use first in the actual job search, and because the email register layers on top of WhatsApp register more easily than the reverse. A girl who can produce professional WhatsApp can produce professional email with two added structural conventions (subject line, sign-off). A girl who learned email register first often produces over-formal WhatsApp that looks bizarre to a Hyderabad hiring manager.

### Worked example — the same vocabulary at different registers

Priya wants to ask a hiring manager when she can start, after receiving a verbal job offer over a phone call.

**Attempt 1 — her current level (casual-WhatsApp register):**
*"sir when i can start? pls tell me. thank u"*

What is wrong: not the words. The words are correct. The register signals: no capitalization on *I* or *Sir*; *pls* and *u* are SMS-era casual; *when i can start* is question-as-statement (Telugu/Hindi structure transfer) rather than English question structure; *pls tell me* is direct imperative.

**Attempt 2 — over-corrected (formal register, wrong for the situation):**
*"Respected Sir, I am writing this message to most humbly request you to kindly inform me regarding the exact date on which I shall be commencing my service with your esteemed organization. Kindly do the needful at your earliest possible convenience. Yours faithfully, Priya."*

What is wrong: the situation is consultative (she has a job offer, the manager already likes her), not formal. *Respected Sir* and *Yours faithfully* are 1950s Indian-government-letter register. The hiring manager reads this and worries he hired someone who sounds like a court summons. Over-correction is its own register miss.

**Attempt 3 — target (consultative WhatsApp / short email):**
*"Good morning Sir, Thank you for offering me the position yesterday. Could you please let me know my joining date? I will plan accordingly. Best regards, Priya."*

Why this works: greeting matches the medium (*Good morning Sir* is standard Indian business opening for a known contact); *Thank you for offering me the position yesterday* anchors the request in context the manager remembers; *Could you please let me know* is a modal-hedged question — consultative; *I will plan accordingly* signals professional initiative; *Best regards* closes at the register the message opened at.

Same vocabulary, three different signals. The chapter's job is to teach Priya the third one and to give her enough pattern exposure to recognize when she has slipped into the first or shot past into the second.

## B. Domain examples and cases — Hyderabad-specific

### B.1 The Hyderabad BPO hiring-manager profile

The relevant hiring manager for Track A (BPO) is typically a Team Leader or HR Recruiter at a Hyderabad operations center for Concentrix, Genpact, Foundever (formerly Sitel), Tech Mahindra, or one of the smaller Cyberabad outfits ([NASSCOM 2024 BPM industry report](https://nasscom.in/knowledge-center/publications) `[verify URL`]). His own English is Indian Standard Business English. He uses *please revert*, *do the needful*, *EOD*, *PFA* (please find attached), *kindly*, *regards*. He does not use BBC English and does not expect it from candidates. He does expect:

- Subject line that describes the email (not just *"Job application"*)
- A greeting line (not jumping into the request)
- A clear short body (he will read it in under 30 seconds)
- A sign-off with full name and phone number
- Capitalization on *I* and on proper nouns (small visual cues but they signal effort)

He flags out within seconds: *all-lowercase, no greeting, no sign-off, mixed Telugu/English in the email body, requests without modal hedging, multiple exclamation marks, emoji*.

### B.2 The Apollo Hospitals reception hiring scenario (Track B)

For Track B (healthcare reception), the relevant contact is often an HR Executive at the hospital itself or at a placement agency feeding the hospital. The register expectation is slightly more formal than BPO because healthcare in India remains a status-conscious sector where titles and respect markers matter more (the patient interaction will involve doctors, who are addressed as *Doctor / Dr. Reddy / Sir* at all times in front of patients).

Practitioner guidance from [Apollo's careers portal](https://www.apollohospitals.com/careers) [verify exact phrasing on current iteration] frames entry-level reception roles as customer-facing professional roles requiring "good communication skills in English, Hindi, and a regional language" — note the order. English first, regional language last. The register has to match.

### B.3 The NGO administrative hiring scenario (Track C)

For Track C (NGO administration — CRY, HelpAge India), the register skews more international because donor-facing communication is often with US/UK/EU partners. This is the one track where slightly closer-to-BBC register is actually rewarded. The chapter should flag this in Unit 6C, not Unit 1; Unit 1's job is the Indian-business baseline.

### B.4 The "kindly do the needful" question

The canonical [Indian-English business marker](https://www.bbc.com/news/magazine-20500312) — *please do the needful* or *kindly do the needful* — is the test case for the chapter's register pedagogy. Is it correct? Yes. Is it understood? Yes. Is it modern? Increasingly not. Should Priya use it?

The answer the chapter should give: recognize it when received (because the older Indian-business forms still travel), but produce slightly more direct modern equivalents (*"Please let me know if you need anything from my side"* or *"Please share your guidance on the next step"*). This positions her at the consultative register that multinational Hyderabad employers favor in 2026 without making her look foreign or out of touch in a local Indian firm.

## D. Current state of the field

### Settled

- Register is a real linguistic variable, separable from grammar and vocabulary ([Halliday 1978](https://www.bloomsbury.com/us/language-as-social-semiotic-9780713159677/); [Biber & Conrad 2009, *Register, Genre, and Style*](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/register-genre-and-style/04A07B8B0E5C4DAEB1D90B72EAFD7DBC)).
- Indian English is a legitimate variety of English with codified features, not a deficient form of British English ([Kachru 1985](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-971X.1985.tb00423.x); [Sailaja 2009](https://edinburghuniversitypress.com/book-indian-english.html); [Crystal 2003](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/english-as-a-global-language/A930F71B7BC322FCDB6446EFB87D5C2A)).
- L2 learners can acquire register faster through pattern exposure than through explicit rule instruction ([Biber, Conrad & Reppen 1998, *Corpus Linguistics*](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/corpus-linguistics/3F7E4B07D69D9B8F18F1F3F7C9C8E0AA); [Schmitt 2010, *Researching Vocabulary*](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1057/9780230293977)).
- WhatsApp has become a workplace register in its own right, especially in South Asian and Latin American business contexts ([Carr 2017, *Convivality*](https://academic.oup.com/joc/article/67/2/E1/4082397) `[verify]`; multiple LocalCircles and KPMG India workforce surveys 2022–2024 `[verify specifics]`).

### Contested

- Whether "Indian English" features (*do the needful, prepone, revert*) are modern, professional, and travel-worthy, or quaint and increasingly limited to older firms. Practitioner journalism splits; [Lalitha Sundaram in *The Wire*](https://thewire.in) `[verify exact piece]` and similar writers defend them as legitimate; HR-blog writers and consultants like to flag them as outdated. The pedagogical position for this chapter: teach recognition, teach modern equivalents for production.
- Whether ESL register can be taught explicitly at all or only acquired implicitly through exposure. The [Long 1996 interaction hypothesis](https://www.routledge.com/Handbook-of-Second-Language-Acquisition/Ritchie-Bhatia/p/book/9780125890427) leans implicit; the explicit-instruction tradition (Ellis, Nation) holds that for L2 adults with time pressure, named-pattern instruction accelerates acquisition. The chapter sides with explicit instruction *for adults under time pressure* — Priya has 8 weeks, not 4 years.

### Unsettled

- The exact share of Hyderabad entry-level hiring that runs through WhatsApp vs. email vs. portal-only. Anecdotal evidence puts WhatsApp very high; rigorous survey evidence is absent. `[verify with a current Hyderabad recruiter or with a Naukri/Apna user-behavior study]`

## E. Teaching considerations — where ESL learners get stuck

### E.1 The "all-or-nothing" register failure mode

ESL learners under time pressure tend to flip between two unhelpful modes: *casual everywhere* (because that's the register they have) or *maximally formal everywhere* (because they read formal as "safer"). Both produce register misses.

The fix is pattern exposure to consultative register specifically — the middle one. The chapter has to show consultative register over and over, in WhatsApp and in email, with named features, so it stops looking like an in-between and starts looking like *the* register.

### E.2 The translation reflex

Priya's instinct is to compose a message in Telugu and translate. This produces Telugu-structure English: time markers in Telugu order, requests without modal hedging, *Sir* over-saturation. The chapter cannot ask her to stop thinking in Telugu — that is asking her to be someone she is not. What it can do is teach her recognizable English patterns she can compose *into directly* without translating from Telugu first.

This is exactly the [pattern-not-rule pedagogy](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/corpus-linguistics/3F7E4B07D69D9B8F18F1F3F7C9C8E0AA) the corpus-linguistics ESL tradition supports: give her the slot-filler templates of consultative English — *"Could you please [verb]?"*, *"I would like to [verb]"*, *"Thank you for [noun phrase]"* — and let her substitute her own content into the slot, rather than asking her to assemble a sentence from rules.

### E.3 The Indian-context analogy that works

For Priya, the right analogy is the difference between how she speaks to her sister and how she speaks to a senior Salesian sister. Same person, same vocabulary mostly, but the *tone*, the *titles*, the *softening* all shift. She already knows how to do this in Telugu — automatically, without thinking about it. The chapter's move: name what she is already doing in Telugu (register-shifting) and show her the English version of the same skill.

This connects to the sisters' values point in §9 of CLAUDE.md and to the TIKTOC's "the values she learned... are professional assets here." Respecting hierarchy through language is something the Salesian environment has already taught her. The chapter is teaching her the English version of a skill she already has in Telugu.

### E.4 The "I sound like a robot" fear

When a learner first produces consultative register, she will often say it sounds robotic, unnatural, "not me." This is correct — and unavoidable. New registers feel artificial until they automate, the same way a new language does. [Schmitt 2010](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1057/9780230293977) on vocabulary acquisition: a word feels artificial until ~10 exposures in context; then it automates. Register patterns are the same. The chapter should name the feeling out loud — *"This will feel fake at first. That is normal. After ten messages it will not feel fake."* — and not try to talk her out of it.

### E.5 Where the volunteer Zoom session does the work the Kindle cannot

The Kindle can teach pattern recognition. It cannot tell Priya whether her produced message hits the register or misses by a notch. That is the volunteer's job in the Week 2 Zoom session. The chapter has to set up the volunteer with three named diagnostic questions:

1. Does the message open with a greeting? (Yes / no)
2. Does the request use a modal hedge? (Yes / no / over-formal)
3. Does the message close with a sign-off and full name? (Yes / no)

Three binary judgments the volunteer can make in 10 seconds. The volunteer feedback rule from Risk 3 in the TIKTOC applies: one specific thing she did well, one specific thing to try differently. Not a grade.
