# Unit 6A — Handling Difficult Calls

*When the customer is angry, the script protects you. Not the other way around.*

---

## Learning outcomes

By the end of this unit, you will be able to:

- **(Apply)** Respond to an angry-customer scenario in professional English using the HEARD de-escalation phrases, without becoming flustered or switching to Telugu.
- **(Evaluate)** Identify which response strategies reduce escalation and which inflame it — and name the four cardinal mistakes that guarantee a call goes wrong.

---

## The problem this unit solves

You have already learned the seven-move structure in Unit 5A. You can carry a cooperative customer through a call from opening to close.

This unit is for the call where the customer is *not* cooperative.

The hardest moment in your first month as a voice-process agent is not the first call. The first call you survive — the adrenaline carries you. The hardest moment is the *second minute of the third angry call of the day*. Your script is fresh, but it was built for a customer who is listening. This customer is not listening. He is venting. He is repeating the same complaint. He may be loud. He may be unfair. He may have already been on hold for 22 minutes with a different agent before you picked up the line, and now he is taking that wait out on you.

Three things tend to happen to a new agent in that second minute. First, the customer's anger lands somewhere inside you and your own emotional load goes up. Second, the part of your brain that produces English narrows — because the cognitive load is too high. Third, you reach for whatever language comes fastest. The fastest language is Telugu. The Telugu spills out. The QA recording flags it. The call escalates to a supervisor. You feel small.

That whole sequence is preventable.

This unit teaches the prevention. Not by making you emotionally tougher — no chapter can do that in six days. By giving you *language you can produce under cognitive load*. A short sequence of rehearsed English phrases that go into specific slots when the customer signals anger. You will not have to *decide* what to say. You will only have to *recognize* the moment and *reach* for the phrase you have already practiced.

The de-escalation move set has a name. It is called **HEARD**. Five letters. Five steps. We will spend most of this unit on it.

One honest sentence before we begin. This work is hard. The phone screen does not test for the emotional weight of difficult calls. The job does. Nobody who has done voice-process work for a year would call it easy. We are going to teach you the moves that make it doable. We are not going to pretend the work is light. It is honorable work. It pays you, it builds your career, and it is the same work that every BPO worker in Hyderabad does for many years before moving up. You are joining a real profession. The profession's tools deserve respect, and this unit's job is to put them in your hands.

## Opening case

Two girls take their first angry-customer mock call on the same Saturday afternoon. Both have completed Units 1–5A. Both have practiced the seven-move structure.

(One of them is Priya — the running learner-vignette in this book, a composite, not a real student.)

**Girl 1.** The volunteer is playing Anil Kumar, 38, calling his bank's credit card customer care about a transaction of 18,750 rupees to a merchant he does not recognize. He has been on hold once already for 22 minutes with a different agent. He is angry. The mock call opens.

Girl 1: *"Thank you for calling Customer Care, this is [name], how may I help you today, Sir?"*

Anil (loud): *"Look madam, I have been waiting for one hour on your customer care, you people are useless, I want this fraud transaction removed from my account NOW!"*

Girl 1: *"Sir please calm down, sir please don't shout, I am here to help you only."*

Anil: *"Don't tell me to calm down! Just remove the transaction! Are you going to do it or not?"*

Girl 1: *"Sir actually I have to check first, sir, please give me your card number, sir."*

Anil: *"I already gave my card number to the last agent! Why do I have to give it again? Where is your supervisor?"*

Girl 1: *"Sir supervisor is busy sir, you can tell me only, please cooperate sir..."*

The call goes another minute. Girl 1 is using *Sir* once every six seconds. The customer is asking for the supervisor by the end of minute two. The call ends in escalation.

What went wrong was not the seven-move structure. Girl 1 had the structure. What went wrong was that she did not have *de-escalation inserts* — the specific phrases that go inside the structure when the customer signals anger. Without them, her reflexes took over. The reflexes produced *"sir please calm down"* and *"sir please cooperate."* Both of those are escalators. Both are exactly what *not* to say.

**Girl 2 — Priya.** Same scenario. Same Anil Kumar. Same opening line.

Priya: *"Thank you for calling Customer Care, this is Priya, how may I help you today, Sir?"*

Anil (loud): *"Look madam, I have been waiting for one hour on your customer care, you people are useless, I want this fraud transaction removed from my account NOW!"*

*(Priya does not interrupt. She lets him finish. Three seconds of silence after he stops.)*

Priya: *"Mr. Kumar, I am very sorry that you had to wait such a long time, and I can hear that this transaction has been very stressful for you. I want to fix this for you. Could you please tell me what happened, from the beginning?"*

Anil takes 30 seconds to explain the transaction. Priya does not interrupt. When he finishes, Priya thanks him for explaining, names the specific feeling — *frustration with the transaction plus the long wait* — apologizes for the inconvenience and the wait, then walks through what she is going to do. By minute four, Anil is calmer. He gives her his verification details. The dispute is raised. The call closes in 8 minutes with a satisfied customer.

Both girls had the same English. Both had the seven-move structure. The difference was the five HEARD phrases. Girl 2 had them. Girl 1 did not.

This unit gives you the five HEARD phrases.

## Core content

### 1. Why anger is hard for an L2 speaker — and how the structure helps

We named this in Unit 5A but it deserves another paragraph here, because this is the unit where it matters most.

When a customer raises his voice, your cognitive system is doing four things at the same time. First, it is *parsing his English* — which is harder when he is fast or when his accent (Hindi-English, Telugu-English, US-English) is unfamiliar. Second, it is *suppressing your own anxiety response* — the body's fight-or-flight system is firing because someone is shouting at you. Third, it is *retrieving the right script section* — which moves are you supposed to do now? Fourth, it is *producing English output* — turning thought into spoken sentences.

That is a lot of work for one brain in one moment.

When the load gets too high, the brain does what every brain does — it takes the path of least resistance. For a bilingual brain whose dominant language is Telugu and whose work language is English, the path of least resistance is *back to Telugu*. The English does not so much fail as get pushed aside. Telugu words come out instead. This is called **L1 reversion**. *L1* is your first language — Telugu. *L2* is your second — English. (Researchers Albert Costa and Mikel Santesteban documented this in their 2004 study in the *Journal of Memory and Language* — [link](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0749596X04000245). Under cognitive load, bilingual speakers switch toward L1 faster than they switch back to L2.)

You should know two things about L1 reversion before this unit teaches you the fix.

**First** — it is a normal human response, not a personal weakness. Every bilingual brain does this. The neuroscience is the same for you, for a Concentrix supervisor with ten years on the floor, for a fluent English-speaking Telugu academic. Under enough load, everybody slides toward L1. The difference is how much load they can take before the slide begins.

**Second** — the fix is structural, not emotional. Telling yourself *stay calm, speak English* in the moment does not work. Your brain is not listening to your conscious instruction at that point. The fix is *load reduction* — having a rehearsed English phrase ready to come out automatically, so the brain does not have to construct one. That is what HEARD is. Five English chunks, one for each step. You do not invent. You reach.

(*Telugu scaffolding.* In Telugu you do this without thinking. When a respected elder visits the residence and asks how things are, you do not invent an answer. You reach for *"baagunnaanu sister-gaaru"* — the fixed polite phrase. The chunk arrives in your mouth before your brain has decided what to say. HEARD will work the same way in English, after enough practice. Practice is the bridge.)

### 2. There is a word for this work, and it is real

A sociologist named Arlie Hochschild wrote a book in 1983 called *The Managed Heart* (University of California Press — [publisher page](https://www.ucpress.edu/book/9780520272941/the-managed-heart)). She studied flight attendants and bill collectors. She gave a name to what they did all day. She called it **emotional labor**.

*Emotional labor* means: **the work of producing the right feeling on the outside, regardless of the feeling on the inside, because the job requires the outside feeling.** A flight attendant smiles at the passenger who insulted her three minutes ago. A bill collector stays firm with a debtor who is crying. A BPO agent stays warm with a customer who is calling her useless. None of those workers are pretending the inside matches the outside. They are doing work to keep the outside steady. The work is real. It costs energy. It tires you in a different way than physical work tires you.

Hochschild's distinction matters. She separated *surface acting* (faking the display while feeling something else) from *deep acting* (cultivating the displayed feeling internally). Both are work. Surface acting wears you out faster. Deep acting takes more time but costs less per call.

A researcher who studied this specifically for Indian BPO agents — Kiran Mirchandani, in her 2012 book *Phone Clones: Authenticity Work in the Transnational Service Economy* (Cornell University Press — [publisher page](https://www.cornellpress.cornell.edu/book/9780801450723/phone-clones/)) — documented how the work landed on Indian agents in the 2000s. Many were asked to adopt American names and accents while taking calls from US customers. The emotional cost was high. The work was honorable, the workers were proud of it, *and* the cost on the body and mind was real. Both things were true at once.

The 2026 Hyderabad BPO industry has moved away from the American-name-and-fake-accent layer of that emotional labor. You will be hired as Priya, and you will be Priya on the call. But the emotional labor of staying warm with an angry customer is still there. It is part of the job. It is part of every voice-process job in the world.

Three short things you should know about it.

**One** — the work is honorable. Customer service is the work that keeps a company running. Without you on the call, the customer leaves. The business stops. You are the human face of whatever company hired you. That is real.

**Two** — the script protects you. *You* are not on trial when the customer is angry. The script is your professional armor. The customer is angry at the bill, at the company, at the wait — not at Priya the 19-year-old from Secunderabad. The script lets you handle the call without taking the anger personally. The script is doing some of the carrying for you.

**Three** — the work is harder some days than others. There is no day when it is easy. The unit cannot promise you a job where the calls feel light. The unit can promise you the tools that make the calls *doable* instead of impossible. That is enough. The rest is the sisters in your life, the friends in your training batch, the seven hours of sleep you protect on the nights before a shift.

We will return to this when we talk about emotion regulation for the agent in §5.

### 3. HEARD — the five steps

The de-escalation framework most widely taught in customer-service training is called **HEARD**. It has five steps. Each letter is the first letter of a step.

- **H — Hear.** Let the customer finish. Do not interrupt. Use short acknowledgement markers only. Silence is the move.
- **E — Empathize.** Name the feeling. Attach it to the specific complaint.
- **A — Apologize.** Apologize for the situation. Not necessarily for the cause.
- **R — Resolve.** State what you will do, in specific steps, with a time frame. (Same shape as the resolution move in Unit 5A.)
- **D — Document / Diagnose.** Confirm what was done. Summarize. Close. (At entry level, this is the short close from Unit 5A.)

The HEARD framework is documented widely — Disney Institute teaches a version of it ([*Be Our Guest*, revised ed., Disney Editions 2011](https://www.amazon.com/Be-Our-Guest-Revised-Updated/dp/142314844X) `[verify ed.]`), so do many healthcare verbal-de-escalation programs. A separate book by George J. Thompson — *Verbal Judo*, 2nd ed., William Morrow 2013 ([HarperCollins page](https://www.harpercollins.com/products/verbal-judo-george-j-thompson)) — covers similar territory with sharper language for the psychology behind it. The core insight across all of them is the same: *de-escalation is a trained behavior, not a personality trait*. You do not have to be a calm person. You have to have the moves.

HEARD goes *inside* the seven-move structure from Unit 5A. The two work together:

- The Hear, Empathize, and Apologize steps go inside the **discovery** move.
- The Resolve step goes inside the **resolution** move.
- The Document/Diagnose step goes inside the **wrap** and **close** moves.

The seven-move structure is the scaffold. HEARD is the language that fills the scaffold when the customer is angry. With both together, you have a complete tool. Without HEARD, the scaffold has no language for the emotional content of the call.

### 4. The HEARD phrases — what to memorize

Here is the chunk set. Memorize each one as one piece. You do not invent these on the call.

**H — Hear. The phrase to open the Hear step:**

*"Please tell me what happened, Sir, from the beginning. I am listening."*

This signals: *I have time, you have the floor*. The next 30 seconds are the customer's. You do not interrupt. You add short acknowledgements every 5 to 10 seconds — *"yes,"* *"I see,"* *"I am listening"* — so the customer knows the line is open. Then silence. Real silence. Not nervous silence — *attentive* silence.

Most new agents cannot do this. The L2 instinct under pressure is to *fill the silence*. Don't. The fill is what makes it worse.

**E — Empathize. Five phrases. Pick the one closest to the moment:**

- *"I can hear that this has been frustrating for you."*
- *"I understand why you are upset."*
- *"That sounds like a very difficult experience."*
- *"I am sorry you have had to deal with this."*
- *"I completely understand why you are upset — [name the specific thing]."*

The fifth one is the strongest. Notice what it does. It does not say *"I understand"* alone — *"I understand"* alone, in customer service, often reads as dismissive. *You don't understand, you weren't there*, the customer thinks. The fix is to **attach the empathy to the specific complaint**. *"I completely understand why you are upset — a transaction you don't recognize on your card, and then a long wait without resolution. That is a very frustrating experience."*

When the empathy attaches to the specific thing, the customer recognizes it. The recognition is the de-escalation.

**A — Apologize. Two safe forms:**

- *"I am sorry for the inconvenience this has caused you."*
- *"I am sorry that this has happened. Let me see what I can do for you."*

The second one is stronger because it has an action pivot — *let me see what I can do*. It signals you are about to move from acknowledging to acting.

Two phrases to *avoid* in the Apologize step:

- *"I am sorry for our company's mistake."* — You are not authorized to admit company fault. The QA team will flag this. In some processes it creates legal exposure. The situational apology is the safe move.
- *"I am sorry but..."* — The word *but* nullifies the apology. The customer hears the *but* and reads everything before it as fake. Take *but* out of every apology you ever make in your professional life. Substitute a full stop.

This is a contested area in the literature. Beverly Engel's *The Power of Apology* (Wiley 2001) `[verify exact title and year]` and Jennifer Robbennolt's legal research on apology and liability ([SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=472624)) `[verify]` show that targeted apologies that name a specific company action sometimes rebuild trust faster than situational apologies. They are right for a senior account manager with authority. For an entry-level agent without authority to admit company fault, the *situational apology* is the safe default — and is the default the BPO will train you on. Use it until you are senior enough to have the authority.

**R — Resolve. The shape from Unit 5A, with one addition for difficult calls:**

*"Here is what I am going to do for you, Sir. First, I am going to [action 1]. Then, [action 2]. You will receive [outcome] within [time frame]. Is that all right with you?"*

The addition is the *Is that all right with you?* at the end. With a cooperative customer, you can skip it. With an angry customer, you check that he is on board with the plan before you act. Two reasons. One — it gives him control, which is what an angry customer is missing. Two — it forestalls the *I didn't agree to that* objection that escalates calls later.

If you cannot resolve, the R step becomes the **escalation**:

*"This requires our specialist team to review. I am going to transfer you to a senior colleague who can resolve this faster for you. Is that all right with you, Sir?"*

We will come back to escalation in §6. It is part of the job, not a failure.

**D — Document / Diagnose. The short close from Unit 5A:**

*"To confirm, Sir: I have [summary of action]. You will receive [next step] within [time frame]. Is there anything else I can help you with today?"*

You confirm. You summarize. You check for anything else. Then the branded close.

That is HEARD. Five steps. Roughly six to eight phrases to memorize as fixed chunks. You will use these on every angry call for the rest of your BPO career.

### 5. Four things that *guarantee* the call goes wrong

Customer-service trainers have converged on four moves that, used in any combination, will escalate any call. Avoid them.

**Failure 1 — Arguing or contradicting.**

*"No Sir, that is not correct."*
*"Sir actually, what happened was..."*

The word *actually* and the word *no* at the start of a sentence both tell the customer: *you are wrong, I am right*. He came in with a complaint. You just confirmed his worst fear — that the company will not believe him. The call escalates.

The substitute is *acknowledge first, correct second*:

*"I understand it looks that way from the bill, Sir. Let me check the details and explain what happened, if that's all right."*

Notice the move: you acknowledge his reading of the situation before you correct it. The correction goes second. The acknowledgement goes first. After the acknowledgement, the customer is willing to hear the correction.

**Failure 2 — Denying or minimizing.**

*"That's not a big problem, Sir, don't worry."*
*"This is normal, Sir."*
*"Many customers face this."*

You are telling him his emotional reaction is wrong. He gets bigger. The call escalates.

The substitute is to acknowledge the size of the problem *from his view* before you explain the resolution:

*"I can see this is a serious problem for you, Sir. I want to make sure we fix it properly."*

You do not have to agree that it is objectively serious. You only have to agree that, from where he is sitting, it is serious. That is true. That is what an upset customer needs to hear.

**Failure 3 — Talking over the customer.**

You cut him off, even with intent to help. He reads it as *she is not listening*. He repeats himself longer.

The substitute is silence with short acknowledgements. *"Yes... I see... go on..."* until he pauses. Then enter the Empathize step. The cost of the 30 seconds you let him talk is much smaller than the cost of the call you cut short.

**Failure 4 — Defending the company unprompted.**

*"Our company has the best service, Sir."*
*"This never happens, Sir."*
*"We always treat our customers well."*

These signal *I am loyal to my employer over you*. The customer's response is to test that loyalty further. He will tell you the next four things that happened to prove the company wrong. The call gets longer.

The substitute is *situational apology plus action*. You neither defend nor concede. *"I am sorry for the inconvenience this has caused you. Let me see what I can do."*

And one Indian-context bonus mistake — the canonical one from §1:

**Failure 5 — *"Sir please calm down."***

Saying *please calm down* to an angry customer is the single fastest way to guarantee escalation. The customer hears *you are the problem*. The substitute is the Hear step opener — *"Please tell me what happened, Sir, from the beginning. I am listening."* That sentence is your reflexive replacement for *please calm down*. Drill it until it is the thing that comes out of your mouth instead.

(The customer-service trainer George J. Thompson, in *Verbal Judo*, calls *"please calm down"* the most dangerous phrase in customer service. He is right.)

### 6. Emotion regulation — the agent side

The HEARD framework manages the customer's emotions. The other half of the problem is your own emotions. You will not last six months in a voice-process job if you do not have moves for the agent side.

Three operational moves. Each one is small. Each one helps.

**The 4-second reset.** Between calls — usually 15 to 30 seconds of after-call work before the next call connects — take one slow breath. Four seconds in through the nose, four seconds out through the mouth. Just one breath. This is enough to bring your parasympathetic nervous system back online so the next call does not arrive with the last call's adrenaline still in you. (You will see this called *box breathing* in some materials — [Healthline overview](https://www.healthline.com/health/box-breathing) `[verify reference quality]`. The technique is the same.)

**The "this is not personal" reframe.** The customer is not angry at *Priya*. The customer is angry at the bill, at the wait, at the company. *Priya* is the channel he reached. The words still hit. The reframe is not denial — the reframe is recognition that the words are about the situation, not about you. After the call, the words leave the room with the call.

**The escalation route as relief.** You are not alone on this call. There is a supervisor. There is an escalation team. There are processes for handling abuse. **You do not have to absorb an abusive customer.** If a customer becomes abusive, you are allowed to escalate. Different companies have different rules — some allow escalation after a single profane utterance, some after a clear pattern, some after the customer demands a supervisor `[verify current Concentrix / Genpact / Foundever abuse-escalation policies, 2026]`. You will be told the specific rule on your process when you are hired. Until then, know this: **the right to escalate is part of the job, not a failure inside the job.**

The escalation script. Memorize it as one chunk:

*"I understand, Sir. Let me connect you with my supervisor who can assist you further. Please hold the line for one moment while I transfer the call."*

When you say that sentence, you transfer. You do not justify. You do not apologize for transferring. The transfer is the right move and the script supports you.

This unit is not a clinical mental-health intervention. It cannot solve the deep cost of difficult work. What it can do is give you three operational moves for the agent side that the better-trained agents around you also use. The sisters' continued presence, your training-batch friends, your sleep and your food — those carry the rest. The job is real work. The supports are real supports. Both at once.

### 7. Cultural register — domestic and international callers sound different

You will take two kinds of angry calls. The de-escalation moves are the same. The register dials are slightly different.

**Domestic Indian callers** — Indian customers calling on telecom, electricity, banking, e-commerce processes. The features:

- Honorifics matter. *Sir, Madam, Ma'am*. You use them. The customer probably uses them too.
- Anger is often signaled but rarely escalates to profanity.
- The escalation threat is often *"I will speak to your manager"* or *"I will file a complaint at the consumer court."*
- The de-escalation register: high honorific density. You say *Sir* slightly more often than you would in writing. Two or three times per minute is normal in spoken Indian Business English when the customer is upset.

**International callers** — US, UK, or Australian customers calling on international voice processes. The features:

- Lower honorific density. *Sir* once or twice per call, not five times.
- First names used more often.
- Profanity is more common, especially from US callers.
- The complaint is usually framed more directly — *"This is unacceptable,"* *"I want a refund right now."*
- The de-escalation register: slower pacing, because the international customer may have a hearing-comprehension issue with your Indian-English phonology when he is stressed. More emphasis on affirmative phrases like *"absolutely,"* *"I can definitely help with that."* These are read as *capable* in US business culture.

For most of you, your first voice-process job will be **domestic Indian process**. Hindi-Telugu-English mixed environment, lower English bar than international, day shift more often than night. The chapter's HEARD phrases are calibrated to that context first. You will adapt to international when you get there.

(One Telugu scaffold. In Telugu, *"chaala kshaminchandi sir, mee samasya na ardham aindi"* would be the natural way to acknowledge a customer's anger. The HEARD English equivalent is *"I am very sorry for the inconvenience, Sir, and I completely understand your concern."* Same shape — apology, acknowledgement of the specific complaint. Different language. The point of the chunk-memorization work is to make the English chunk arrive as fast as the Telugu one would.)

## Worked example — Anil Kumar calls about a fraud transaction

This is the central worked example of the unit. Two versions of the same scenario. Read them side by side. The difference is HEARD.

**The scenario.** Customer is Anil Kumar, 38, Hyderabad resident. He calls his bank's credit-card customer care about a transaction of 18,750 rupees to a merchant called "MERCH-PVT-3387" which he does not recognize. He has not used the card for any online purchase recently. He has already called once today, was on hold for 22 minutes, the previous agent took his details and then the call disconnected. He is angry.

---

### Version 1 — Wrong. The agent has the seven-move structure but no HEARD.

Agent: *Thank you for calling Customer Care, this is [name], how may I help you today, Sir?*

Anil (loud): *Look madam, I have been waiting for one hour on your customer care, you people are useless, I want this fraud transaction removed from my account NOW!*

Agent: *Sir please calm down, sir please don't shout, I am here to help you only.*

Anil: *Don't tell me to calm down! Just remove the transaction! Are you going to do it or not?*

Agent: *Sir actually I have to check first, sir, please give me your card number, sir.*

Anil: *I already gave my card number to the last agent! Why do I have to give it again? Where is your supervisor?*

Agent: *Sir supervisor is busy sir, you can tell me only, please cooperate sir...*

Anil: *Cooperate?! You are telling me to cooperate?! I am the customer!*

*(The call goes another minute. The agent says "Sir" 14 times in those minutes. The customer asks twice more for the supervisor. The call ends in escalation.)*

**What went wrong, step by step:**

- *Please calm down / please don't shout* — the canonical do-not move. Tells the customer he is the problem. Guarantees escalation.
- *Sir actually I have to check first* — the word *actually* is a contradiction marker. The customer hears *you are wrong, I am right* on top of his existing frustration.
- No acknowledgement of the 22-minute wait. The customer named that. The agent ignored it.
- *Please cooperate, Sir* — accuses the customer of being uncooperative. He is not uncooperative. He is frustrated. The phrasing inverts the moral position. Now he has to defend his right to be upset.
- *Sir* density above six per minute is a nervousness signal. The customer reads it as agent panic. Agent panic raises the agent perception that the call is not under control. He demands a supervisor.

The call was lost in the first 20 seconds. The seven-move structure was present. HEARD was not.

---

### Version 2 — Right. Same agent, HEARD inserted.

Agent: *Thank you for calling Customer Care, this is Priya, how may I help you today, Sir?*

Anil (loud): *Look madam, I have been waiting for one hour on your customer care, you people are useless, I want this fraud transaction removed from my account NOW!*

*(**H — Hear.** Priya does not interrupt. She lets him finish. Three seconds of silence after he stops.)*

Priya: *Mr. Kumar, I am very sorry that you had to wait such a long time, and I can hear that this transaction has been very stressful for you. I want to fix this for you. Could you please tell me what happened, from the beginning?*

*(Anil takes 30 seconds to explain. The transaction of 18,750 rupees to "MERCH-PVT-3387". He does not recognize it. He has not used the card for any online purchase recently. He has already called once, was on hold for 22 minutes, the agent took his details and then disconnected. Priya does not interrupt. She says "yes" twice while he talks.)*

*(**E — Empathize.**)*

Priya: *Thank you for explaining, Mr. Kumar. I completely understand why you are upset — a transaction you don't recognize on your card, and then a long wait without resolution. That is a very frustrating experience.*

*(**A — Apologize.**)*

Priya: *I am sorry for the inconvenience this has caused you and for the wait on the earlier call. Let me see what I can do for you right now.*

*(**R — Resolve.**)*

Priya: *Here is what I am going to do, Sir. First, I am going to verify the transaction with you on the system. Then, if it is an unrecognized transaction, I am going to immediately raise a dispute and temporarily block any further transactions on the card so it stays safe. The dispute investigation usually completes within 7 to 10 working days, and during that time the amount of 18,750 rupees will be temporarily credited back to your account as a provisional refund. Is that all right with you?*

Anil (calmer): *Yes, please do that.*

Priya: *Could you please confirm the last four digits of your card number for verification?*

Anil: *4892.*

Priya: *Thank you. And the date of birth on the account, please?*

Anil: *15th August 1986.*

Priya: *Thank you, Sir. I have verified your account. Now I am going to raise the dispute. Could you please give me a moment while I do that? It will take about two minutes.*

*(**Hold — 90 seconds.** Priya raises the dispute and processes the provisional credit.)*

Priya: *Thank you for holding, Mr. Kumar. I have raised the dispute, reference number DSP-2026-0517-0042. The card has been temporarily blocked from any further transactions and a new card will be issued to you within 5 working days. The provisional credit of 18,750 rupees will reflect in your account within 24 hours. You will receive an SMS confirmation within the next 30 minutes with the dispute reference number.*

*(**D — Document / short close.**)*

Priya: *To confirm, Sir: I have raised dispute DSP-2026-0517-0042 for the unrecognized transaction of 18,750 rupees. Your card is temporarily blocked, a new card will arrive in 5 working days, and the provisional credit will reflect in 24 hours. Is there anything else I can help you with today?*

Anil: *No, that is fine. Thank you, Priya.*

Priya: *Thank you for your patience, Mr. Kumar. We will resolve this for you. Have a good day.*

*(Two seconds of silence. Anil disconnects.)*

Total call: 8 minutes 10 seconds. AHT slightly above target for a regular call but well within the range allowed for a dispute call. `[verify current 2026 AHT targets for credit-card dispute calls at major Indian BPOs]`

**The differences, side by side:**

- The right version uses Anil's name twice in the first minute. The wrong version uses *Sir* impersonally five times.
- The right version acknowledges the 22-minute hold **before** asking for verification. The wrong version ignored it.
- The right version explains the resolution as a sequence — *first this, then this, then this* — so the customer knows what is coming. The wrong version offered no sequence.
- The right version uses *I am going to* (future indicative, agent agency) instead of *I will check / I have to check* (passive, deferring).
- Most importantly: the right version treats the customer's anger as *appropriate to the situation*, not as a problem to manage out of the customer. The wrong version treated the anger as the problem.

Both calls took roughly the same length of time. One ended in resolution. One ended in escalation.

This is HEARD doing its work. Five steps. Six memorized phrases. The difference is real.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1 — *"Sir please calm down."*** Already covered in §5. Listed again here because this is the single highest-leverage thing you will change about how you handle anger. The drill: 20 repetitions, out loud, of the substitute — *"Please tell me what happened, Sir, from the beginning. I am listening."* That phrase becomes your reflex instead. After 20 repetitions, it starts to come out automatically. Do them this week.

**Mistake 2 — *"I understand"* with no specific attachment.** *"I understand, Sir"* spoken in a flat tone, with no specific attachment to the complaint, lands as dismissive. The customer hears *you are not listening, you are just saying words*. The fix is the attached version — *"I completely understand why you are upset — a transaction you don't recognize, and then a long wait without resolution."* The attachment is what makes the empathy land.

**Mistake 3 — Apologizing for the company.** *"I am sorry for our company's mistake."* You are not authorized to admit company fault. The QA team will flag this. Use the situational apology — *"I am sorry for the inconvenience this has caused you."* — instead. Situational, not causal.

**Mistake 4 — *"I am sorry but..."*** The word *but* nullifies the apology. The customer hears the *but* and reads everything before it as fake politeness covering for what comes after. Cut *but* from every apology. Use a full stop. *"I am sorry for the inconvenience. Let me see what I can do."* — two sentences, no *but*, no nullification.

**Mistake 5 — Switching to Telugu under pressure.** This is the L1-reversion failure mode. The prevention: when you feel the Telugu coming, reach for the rescue phrase from Unit 5A — *"Could you please give me a moment, Sir? I want to make sure I give you the right information."* That phrase is a hold-permission move structurally, but it works as a language reset. It gives you 30 seconds to breathe and re-enter in English. Pre-script it. Memorize it. Use it before the Telugu spills, not after.

**Mistake 6 — Feeling you have to absorb abuse.** You do not. If a customer becomes abusive, you are allowed to escalate. The script: *"I understand, Sir. Let me connect you with my supervisor who can assist you further. Please hold the line for one moment while I transfer the call."* This is part of the system, not a failure inside the system. The sisters' values include not absorbing harm from people who do not deserve to inflict it. The same principle applies on the floor.

**Mistake 7 — Treating empathy as friendship.** HEARD's empathy is *professional empathy*. It is not friendship. You are extending tactical recognition to a customer because the recognition is what de-escalates the call. You are not required to feel personally moved. This is the same kind of distinction the sisters teach — *we treat people with respect even when they do not return it, because that is who we are, not who they are*. The professional version of that is HEARD.

## Exercises

These are graded by Bloom level. Exercise 4 is the load-bearing one — you will use it in the Zoom session this week.

### Exercise 1 — Drill the HEARD chunks *(Bloom: Remember)*

Each of these phrases should be in your mouth, not on your paper, by end of this week. Copy them onto one sheet. Say each one out loud, 10 times slowly, then 10 times at normal speaking pace.

1. *Please tell me what happened, Sir, from the beginning. I am listening.* **(H)**
2. *I can hear that this has been frustrating for you.* **(E)**
3. *I completely understand why you are upset — [specific thing].* **(E)**
4. *I am sorry for the inconvenience this has caused you.* **(A)**
5. *Here is what I am going to do for you, Sir. First, [action 1]. Then, [action 2]. Is that all right with you?* **(R)**
6. *To confirm, Sir: I have [summary]. Is there anything else I can help you with today?* **(D)**

Plus three rescue phrases:

7. *Could you please give me a moment, Sir? I want to make sure I give you the right information.* **(L1-reversion rescue)**
8. *I understand, Sir. Let me connect you with my supervisor who can assist you further. Please hold the line for one moment while I transfer the call.* **(Escalation)**
9. *I see this is a serious problem for you, Sir. I want to make sure we fix it properly.* **(Acknowledgement of size, alternative to minimizing)**

Nine chunks. Memorize them this week. After this week, they should arrive automatically.

### Exercise 2 — Spot the failure mode *(Bloom: Analyze)*

Each of these agent responses contains one of the four cardinal failures. Name which failure (arguing, denying/minimizing, talking over, defending the company), and write the HEARD-substitute response.

a) *"Sir, that is not correct. The bill is right. The system never makes mistakes."*

b) *"Don't worry sir, this happens to many customers, it is normal."*

c) *(Customer is mid-complaint)* *"Sir, I understand, let me just check —"*

d) *"Our company is the best in India, sir, we have millions of happy customers."*

e) *"Sir, please calm down, please don't shout."*

For each one, write your substitute response in the consultative register. Use a HEARD chunk where one fits.

### Exercise 3 — Rewrite a bad apology *(Bloom: Apply)*

Each of these apologies has a problem. Identify the problem and rewrite the apology to the chapter's standard.

a) *"I am sorry but actually it is the customer's responsibility to check the bill before paying."*

b) *"I am sorry for our company's mistake, sir, our system was wrong."*

c) *"Sorry sir."*

d) *"Sir I am extremely deeply most sincerely sorry for this whole problem."*

For each one, write the version you would actually say on the call.

### Exercise 4 — A full angry-customer mock call with ChatGPT *(Bloom: Create + Evaluate)*

This is the unit's central exercise. Same workflow as Unit 5A Exercise 4 — drafted with AI, evaluated with AI, calibrated with the volunteer.

**Step 1.** Open ChatGPT or Gemini on your phone. Prompt:

> *"You are an angry Indian customer calling your bank's credit card customer care about a transaction of 18,750 rupees to a merchant you do not recognize. You have already been on hold for 22 minutes with a different agent before this call. You are loud and frustrated, but not abusive. Speak in 1–2 sentences at a time. I am a customer-service agent at a Hyderabad BPO. The call starts when I greet you. Let me know when you are ready to begin."*

**Step 2.** When ChatGPT is ready, begin the call. Open with the Unit 5A branded opening. When the customer expresses anger — which will be the first thing he says — switch into the HEARD sequence. Stay in English the whole call. If you feel yourself about to switch to Telugu, use the rescue phrase (chunk 7 from Exercise 1).

**Step 3.** When the call ends, ask ChatGPT to evaluate:

> *"Please evaluate my call as a Hyderabad BPO trainer would. Specifically: Did I let the customer finish before I spoke (the Hear step)? Did I use a specific empathy attachment (not just 'I understand' alone)? Did I use a situational apology (not 'I am sorry but...' and not blaming the company)? Did I structure the resolution as 'first this, then this, with a time frame'? Did I avoid the four failure modes — arguing, denying, talking over, defending the company? Did I stay in English? Where could I have done better? Please answer in simple English."*

**Step 4.** Read the evaluation. Mark two things to work on.

**Step 5.** Run the prompt again with a *different* scenario — angry e-commerce delivery, angry telecom billing, angry electricity outage. Three different scenarios is the right number. The HEARD chunks should arrive the same way regardless of scenario.

**Step 6.** Bring the chat transcripts and your work items to the Zoom session this week. The volunteer will play a *moderately* angry customer (not the worst case) and run a live 5-minute mock. The volunteer's feedback is for warmth, pacing, and voice quality — the parts AI cannot calibrate.

### Exercise 5 — Practice the 4-second reset *(Bloom: Apply)*

This one takes 30 seconds and you do it now.

1. Set your phone timer for 4 seconds.
2. Breathe in through your nose for the 4 seconds. Slow and even.
3. Reset the timer.
4. Breathe out through your mouth for 4 seconds. Slow and even.

That is the 4-second reset. Use it between any two practice calls. Use it between any two real calls when you have a job. One breath. That is all. The parasympathetic system does the rest.

You can:

- Name the five HEARD steps in order — Hear, Empathize, Apologize, Resolve, Document.

- Identify the four cardinal failure modes — arguing, denying/minimizing, talking over, defending the company unprompted — and produce the substitute moves.
- Recognize the L1-reversion moment and use the rescue phrase to stay in English.
- Use the situational apology (not the cause-attributing apology) on every call.
- Attach the empathy phrase to the specific complaint, instead of saying *"I understand"* alone.
- Use the escalation script when a customer becomes abusive or asks for a supervisor.
- Reset between calls with one slow breath in and one slow breath out.

That is the full de-escalation skill set of a Tier 1 entry-level voice agent. It will take you about 50 angry calls before the chunks come out automatically. The first 10 will feel forced. The next 20 will feel like you are following a recipe. By call 50, the recipe is the thing you do. The 50th call is much easier than the first.
