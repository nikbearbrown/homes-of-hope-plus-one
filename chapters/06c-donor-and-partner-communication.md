# Unit 6C — Donor and Partner Communication

*The introduction email. Five adjustments from Indian English to international donor English.*

---

## Learning outcomes

By the end of this unit, you will be able to:

- **(Apply + Create)** Write a professional introduction email to an international donor or partner organization — using the six-component email structure, removing the three Indian-English markers that read as outdated to international donors, and adding the two international-NGO conventions that signal credibility.

---

## The problem this unit solves

You have written your four-move situation summary from Unit 5C. You have your funding-request sentence. Your director now asks you to send an introduction email to a Program Officer at a US-based foundation that has recently funded similar girls' education programs in other Indian states.

You sit down to write. You reach for the register you learned in school for *letters to authority*. *"Respected Madam, I am writing this email to most humbly enquire..."* You feel good about the formality. You finish, hit send, and wait.

The reply does not come.

Three weeks later, your director asks if you have heard back. You have not. You re-read the email. The English is correct. The grammar is fine. The structure is logical.

The email failed for a reason no one taught you. **Your formal register read to the Seattle program officer as outdated, supplicant, and slightly archaic.** She read three lines, mentally filed your organization as *not yet ready for international funding*, and moved to the next email in her inbox.

That is the failure mode this unit prevents.

Track C is the one track where a register closer to *international English* actually matters. Your audience is a Gates Foundation officer in Seattle, a UNICEF coordinator in Geneva, a USAID grants officer in Washington, a British Asian Trust officer in London. The Indian Business English register that gets a candidate hired into a BPO seat and a hospital front desk is not the register that gets a small Hyderabad NGO taken seriously by these donors.

The shift is small. It is not a wholesale move to British or American English. **It is the removal of three specific Indian-English markers that read as outdated, and the addition of two international-NGO conventions.** This unit teaches those five adjustments and the six-component email structure they live inside.

There is one other failure mode to prevent. **Over-correction into American breezy.** *"Hey Sarah, hope this finds you well, just wanted to circle back..."* lands as junior, unprepared, and culturally tone-deaf. The target is between Indian over-formal and American over-casual: *plain, specific, respectful international English*.

The redirect-to-capability move you will learn in Unit 7C is also previewed here. Your email is your organization's first capability signal to the donor. Get the email right and the door opens. Get it wrong and no one tells you why it closed.

She shows the draft to Anjali. Anjali reads it. Pauses. Says: *"Priya, this is a polite Indian letter from 1990. Sarah Thompson does not read 1990 Indian letters. She reads 2026 international foundation emails. Let me show you what changes."*

## Core content

### 1. The introduction email — six components, in order

A professional introduction email to an international donor has a stable structure. The structure is referenced across donor-relations training materials ([Foundation Center / Candid](https://learning.candid.org/); [BMGF grantee handbook](https://www.gatesfoundation.org/about/policies-and-resources/grantee-handbook)). Six components. Always in this order.

**Component 1 — Subject line that describes the email.**

> *"Introduction — Asha Hyderabad, girls' secondary-education retention program in Telangana"*

Names the organization. Names the specific program area. Names the geographic region. The program officer can route or file in two seconds.

What does not work:
- *"Hello"* — generic; deleted.
- *"Grant Application from India"* — generic; reads as broadcast.
- *"Urgent!! Please respond"* — reads as unprofessional; deleted.

A good subject line is half the email's success.

**Component 2 — Greeting.**

> *"Dear Ms. Thompson,"*

If the program officer holds a doctorate: *"Dear Dr. Smith,"*. If you cannot tell whether she has a doctorate, use *Ms./Mr.* — you will not offend by skipping the doctorate; you may offend by inventing one. If the name is ambiguous on gender (the spelling does not signal it clearly to you) and you cannot find the answer on the foundation's website, use *"Dear Sarah Smith,"* — first name plus last name is acceptable on first contact when the title is uncertain.

What does not work on a first email to a foundation:
- *"Respected Madam,"* — overformal Indian register.
- *"Hi Sarah,"* — first-name address on first email reads as presumptuous in foundation philanthropy.
- *"Dear Sir/Madam,"* — when you have the program officer's actual name, using *Sir/Madam* signals you did not check.

After the first reply, follow the contact's lead. If she signs *"Sarah"*, your next email opens *"Hi Sarah,"* or *"Dear Sarah,"*. The relationship register is set by the contact, not by you.

**Component 3 — Organizational-credibility paragraph (50–70 words).**

This is the paragraph that compresses your four-move situation summary from Unit 5C. Names: who the organization is, when it was founded, what it does, where, scale, and funding mix.

> *"I am writing to introduce Asha Hyderabad, a community organization that runs after-school education programs for 1,200 adolescent girls across three districts of Telangana. We were established in 2009 and are currently funded by a mix of individual Indian donors (~60%), Tata Trusts (~25%), and small international foundations (~15%)."*

Funding-mix transparency is a credibility signal. The donor wants to know: *Is this a real organization? Who else funds it? At what scale do they operate?* The credibility paragraph answers all three.

A note on FCRA: under the [Foreign Contribution Regulation Act](https://fcraonline.nic.in/), only FCRA-registered Indian organizations can accept foreign donations directly. If your organization is not FCRA-registered (and many small Hyderabad NGOs are not), the credibility paragraph should mention the receiving partner organization that handles foreign funds on your behalf. This is the kind of detail your senior colleague will check with you before the email goes out.

**Component 4 — Specific ask paragraph (60–80 words).**

What you want the donor to consider, anchored in the donor's stated priorities. The ask is *exploratory* on first email, not a grant request.

> *"Your foundation's recent funding of girls' secondary-education programs in Bihar and Jharkhand suggested an alignment with our work. We are planning the next three-year phase of our program (2026–2029), and we would value the opportunity to share a 4-page concept note and explore whether a longer conversation might be useful."*

This paragraph does three things:

- *Anchors in the donor's prior funding* — *"Bihar and Jharkhand"* — which demonstrates you did your homework on their portfolio.
- *States your specific plan* — *"the next three-year phase of our program (2026–2029)"* — which signals there is a concrete thing to discuss, not just a vague request for money.
- *Offers a concept note, not a 30-page proposal* — which respects the program officer's time and is the international-NGO standard for first contact.

**Component 5 — Meeting / call request, time-zone aware.**

> *"If a 30-minute introductory call is possible, I am available between 6:30 PM and 9:00 PM IST Monday–Thursday, which corresponds to 8:00–10:30 AM Pacific Time. If a different window works better, please suggest one."*

The time-zone naming is the credibility signal. You named IST. You named the donor's local time. You named multiple days. You offered to flex if your window does not work.

The wrong version: *"I am available next Tuesday at 4 PM."* — at 4 PM IST that is 5:30 AM in Seattle. The program officer reads this as *you did not check what time this is for me*, which signals junior preparation. That is the kind of small slip that closes the door silently.

**Component 6 — Signature.**

> *Best regards,*
> *Priya Brown*
> *Program Associate, Asha Hyderabad*
> *+91 98XXX XXXXX | priya@ashahyd.org | www.ashahyd.org*

The complete signature block includes: closing line (*Best regards* or *Kind regards*); full name; role and organization; phone with country code (+91 for India); email; organization website.

Each missing element raises an unstated question. No website? *Is this organization real?* No country code? *I cannot call this number from the US.* No role? *Who am I talking to?* Include all of them. The signature is part of the credibility paragraph by other means.

### 2. The five register adjustments — Indian Business English → international donor English

The chapter names exactly five adjustments. Three are *removals* from Indian-English defaults. Two are *additions* of international-NGO conventions. Memorize all five. Practice the substitutions until they are automatic.

**Remove 1 — Sir / Madam in opening salutation on initial email.**

Indian default: *"Respected Sir,"* / *"Respected Madam,"*. International foundation default: *"Dear [Title] [Surname]"* or *"Dear [First name] [Last name]"*. Use *Sir/Madam* in Indian Business English correspondence (BPO, hospital, internal Indian NGO mail). Do not use it on first contact with international donors.

This is not a judgment on Indian English. It is a recognition that the international foundation reader has not been trained on the Indian conventions and reads *Respected Sir* as either *formal in a way I do not understand* or *outdated*. Either reading hurts you.

**Remove 2 — *Kindly do the needful* and similar archaic phrases.**

*Kindly do the needful at your earliest convenience.* *Kindly revert at the earliest.* *I shall await your esteemed reply.*

These are Indian Business English business-letter standard phrases. They are not wrong in Indian English. They are unfamiliar to international foundation readers. *The needful* — what specifically? *Revert* — to what? *Await* — for how long?

Replacements you can use right now:

- *Kindly do the needful* → *Please let me know if this would be useful* / *I look forward to your thoughts.*
- *Kindly revert at your earliest convenience* → *Please let me know if a call would work* / *I appreciate any guidance you can share.*
- *I shall await your esteemed reply* → *Thank you for considering this.*

Drill the substitutions. Write each pair out three times by hand. The replacement should come faster than the original after a few weeks.

**Remove 3 — Respected Sir / Yours faithfully / Yours sincerely register.**

Indian 1950s-government-letter register. *"Respected Sir, ... Yours faithfully."* Reads as bureaucratic distance to an international foundation reader.

Replacements:

- Opening: *Dear Ms. Thompson,* (or *Dear Sarah Thompson,* if title uncertain).
- Closing: *Best regards,* / *Kind regards,* / *With thanks,*

*Best regards* and *Kind regards* are both international-standard. Neither is wrong. *Sincerely* leans slightly American. *Kind regards* leans slightly British. Pick one and stay consistent across your correspondence.

**Add 1 — Time-zone naming.**

Indian-default emails often omit the time zone. International donor emails must state IST plus the donor's local time, or at minimum offer multiple specific windows.

- Wrong: *"Available evenings."* / *"Available Tuesday."*
- Right: *"Available 6:30–9:00 PM IST Mon–Thu (8:00–10:30 AM PT)."*

The donor's program officer thinks in her local time. She has not been thinking in IST. You doing the conversion is a small concrete signal that you understand the asymmetry of distance and time, which signals you understand other asymmetries too.

A rough conversion to memorize:

- IST is **+5:30 from UTC.**
- US Pacific Time (PT) is roughly **IST minus 12:30 to 13:30** (depending on daylight saving).
- US Eastern Time (ET) is roughly **IST minus 9:30 to 10:30**.
- UK is roughly **IST minus 4:30 to 5:30**.
- Singapore / Hong Kong (HKT/SGT) is roughly **IST plus 2:30**.

For any meeting scheduling, [timeanddate.com](https://www.timeanddate.com/worldclock/converter.html) does the conversion exactly. Always check; do not guess.

**Add 2 — Specific organizational scale and funding-source statement.**

International donors want to know in the first paragraph: *Is this a real organization? Who else funds it? At what scale do they operate?* The credibility paragraph from §1 carries this information.

Indian-default introduction emails often skip this and go straight to the ask. They assume the donor will somehow know who the organization is. International donors do not know. Most NGOs they fund are organizations they have never heard of before the first email.

Always include the founding year, the operating scale (beneficiary count, geographic range), and the funding mix (at least named categories — *individual Indian donors, corporate foundations, international foundations* — even if you do not give exact percentages on first email). This single paragraph does more credibility work than any other in the email.

### 3. Cultural register errors that undermine credibility — named, not implied

The chapter is explicit about errors. Cultural code-switching is a learnable skill. The four most damaging errors are named here. Each gets diagnosed. Each gets a fix.

**Error 1 — Over-formal British-Indian register.**

> *"Respected Madam, I am writing this email to most humbly enquire whether your esteemed foundation might consider..."*

Reads to the foundation reader as *supplicant*, not *partner*. International donors fund *organizations*, not *humble petitioners*. The asymmetric power register makes the donor uncomfortable.

Fix: confident-but-respectful register from the Unit 1 consultative-formal anchor. *"Dear Ms. Thompson, I am writing to introduce..."* — direct, respectful, parity.

**Error 2 — First-name address on initial email.**

> *"Hi Sarah, hope this finds you well!"*

On first contact, in foundation philanthropy, this reads as presumptuous. Senior program officers at major foundations expect a degree of formality on first email. The relationship has not yet been established; presuming familiarity skips a step.

Fix: *Dear Ms. Smith* on first email. Switch to *Hi Sarah* or *Dear Sarah* only after she signs her own reply with *Sarah*. Follow her lead; do not lead her.

Some smaller, younger family foundations and diaspora-volunteer organizations use first-name register more readily. *Asha for Education* chapters in the US, for example, often run on volunteer culture and first-name address. *Read the contact's email register and respond at the same level.* But default to formal on first email when you do not know.

**Error 3 — Untranslated regional development jargon.**

> *"Our intervention focuses on SHGs in mandals across the district, particularly in tribal sub-districts where ICDS coverage is poor."*

SHG (self-help group), mandal (sub-district), ICDS (Integrated Child Development Services) — these are Indian-administrative-development vocabulary. An international reader may not parse any of them. *Tribal sub-districts* is a phrase loaded with India-specific meaning that does not translate cleanly.

Fix: *define on first use, then use the acronym freely*.

> *"Our work is with women's self-help groups (SHGs — savings-and-credit collectives of 10–20 women) in sub-districts (mandals) of Telangana, particularly in areas where the government's child-nutrition program (ICDS) has thin coverage."*

The discipline transfers to all donor-facing writing. Acronyms in NGO English include SHG, ICDS, MGNREGA, NREGS, RTE, ECCE, NRHM, NHM, NGO, CBO, FBO. Define each on first use. Use the acronym after.

**Error 4 — Time-zone insensitivity.**

> *"Available next Tuesday at 4 PM."*

At 4 PM IST that is 5:30 AM in Seattle. The program officer reads this as *you did not check what time this is for me*. It signals junior preparation. Most readers will not say anything; they will quietly note it and the relationship calibration shifts down.

Fix: §2 Add 1. Always name the donor's local time alongside IST.

These errors are *errors of unfamiliarity, not of intelligence*. The Indian writer who has never had reason to think about Pacific Time has no reason to know. The unit's job is to make you aware.

### 4. What you are writing *to* — the program officer's day

The chapter teaches you the structure of the email. It also teaches you something more important: *what the person on the other end is doing all day*. The introduction email lands differently if you understand the inbox it lands in.

**BMGF (Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation).** Program officers manage portfolios of 10–30 active grants. They receive 100+ unsolicited contacts per month. Initial unsolicited contact is almost never how grants get made; relationships are usually built through conferences, mutual contacts, or invited calls. Your introduction email is positioned as *exploratory*, not as *grant application*. ([BMGF grant process overview](https://www.gatesfoundation.org/about/policies-and-resources/grantee-handbook).)

**UNICEF.** Country offices and regional offices have specific procurement and partnership procedures. *Implementing-partner agreements* are common in India through [UNICEF India](https://www.unicef.org/india/partnerships). Direct international-HQ contact is less likely to bear fruit; your Hyderabad NGO is more likely to engage with UNICEF India staff first.

**USAID.** Operates through bilateral cooperation agreements with the Government of India and through implementing partners. Direct funding to small Indian NGOs is rare but happens through specific calls and Annual Program Statements (APS). ([USAID India](https://www.usaid.gov/india).)

**UK-based foundations.** Comic Relief, BBC Children in Need, the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation UK office, individual major donors via charities like the [British Asian Trust](https://britishasiantrust.org/). Each has its own initial-contact convention.

**Diaspora-philanthropy organizations.** [Asha for Education](https://ashanet.org/), Pratham USA, Sankara Foundation, smaller diaspora-led funds. These tend to be more direct, less hierarchical, often with a tech-diaspora cultural register. Slightly less formal on first email is acceptable.

**Major Indian philanthropic foundations.** Tata Trusts, Reliance Foundation, Azim Premji Foundation. These sit between the Hyderabad-local register and the international register — lean Indian-business-formal more than international-foundation-formal. Track C will benefit from knowing all three registers.

The single most important reset: **not every international email leads to a grant. The introduction email is the start of a multi-month, sometimes multi-year relationship-building process. You are writing an introduction, not asking for money.** The right expectation set keeps you patient and keeps your tone calibrated. The wrong expectation set produces emails that read as transactional and slightly desperate.

### 5. The redirect-to-capability move — first preview

You will meet the *redirect-to-capability* move properly in Unit 7C. But the move starts in the introduction email. Here is the preview.

The natural Indian-English instinct on the introduction email is to *tell the donor about the beneficiaries' suffering* — to make the donor feel the urgency, to elicit sympathy. *"In our area, girls suffer terribly from poverty and lack of opportunity..."*

This is the wrong move. The donor's job is to fund *organizations that can solve problems*, not to feel sorry for the people who have the problems. The redirect-to-capability move is: *name the problem briefly, name what your organization does about it, lead with outcomes*.

Wrong (sympathy-eliciting):

> *"The girls in our area face terrible hardships, struggling with poverty, early marriage, and limited education..."*

Right (capability-signaling):

> *"In three mandals where adolescent girls face high rates of early school dropout, our after-school study program has retained 92% of 240 girls in continuous school enrollment over two years."*

Same situation. Different framing. The first reads as *please help these girls*. The second reads as *here is what we can do; we are seeking partners*. The second gets funded.

You will see this move developed in Unit 7C for interviews. The same logic applies to the introduction email. **Lead with what your organization can do, not with what the beneficiaries cannot do.**

### 6. Voice exemplars — three Indian NGOs that get the register right

Reading is the fastest way to develop register sense. Three Indian NGOs run international-partnership pages that exemplify the calibrated register:

**[CRY America](https://www.america.cry.org/)** — operates as a US 501(c)(3); communication aimed at US donors; plain-language American philanthropy register.

**[HelpAge International](https://www.helpage.org/) / [HelpAge India](https://www.helpageindia.org/)** — partnership pages handle the geographic and cultural bridge cleanly.

**[Akshaya Patra Foundation](https://www.akshayapatra.org/)** — Akshaya Patra USA, UK, and the parent foundation operate mature international donor communications.

Spend 30 minutes on each before drafting your own first email. Note one phrase from each that feels professionally calibrated. Note one phrase that feels slightly over-formal — you will probably find one even in mature examples; nobody is perfect. The pattern exposure builds the register sense; the rules in this chapter accelerate it.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Supplicant register.** *"Most humbly I beg to state..."* / *"I would be most grateful..."*. This is the school-letter training and the Indian-government-correspondence convention pulling you back. Countermove: *you represent an organization; you are extending an introduction between two organizations; your register reflects parity, not pleading*. Drill the supplicant openings into parity openings until the substitution is automatic.

**Mistake 2 — American breezy.** *"Hey Sarah! Hope you're well! Just wanted to reach out and..."*. This is the opposite mistake. You have watched US tech-startup content and absorbed the register. It reads as junior and unprepared to a foundation reader. The target is consultative-international, not American casual.

**Mistake 3 — Skipping the credibility paragraph.** Cutting straight to the ask. *"Dear Ms. Thompson, We would like funding for our program."* The donor has no idea who you are. The credibility paragraph is not optional. It is the paragraph that decides whether the rest of the email gets read.

**Mistake 4 — Using untranslated acronyms.** SHG, ICDS, MGNREGA, NREGS, RTE, ECCE — all of these are India-specific. Define on first use. Use the acronym after. Five seconds of effort. Saves the reader twenty seconds of confusion.

**Mistake 5 — Missing the time-zone.** Even one missing time-zone reference signals junior preparation. Always state IST plus the donor's local time. Always offer multiple windows.

**Mistake 6 — Missing the country code on the phone number.** *"9899X XXXXX"* without *+91* makes the number uncallable from outside India. Always include the country code in international correspondence.

**Mistake 7 — Asking for too much on the first email.** *"Could you fund our Rs. 50 lakh program for the next three years?"* on first contact is premature. The introduction email asks for *interest*, *a call*, *a chance to share a concept note*. The grant ask comes later, after the relationship is built. Get the ask scale right.

**Mistake 8 — Treating the email as transactional.** *"If you cannot fund, please let us know so we can move on."* This is the wrong frame. International donor relationships are built over months. Even a polite no on the first email can become a yes 18 months later if the next interaction lands well. Patience is part of the register.
