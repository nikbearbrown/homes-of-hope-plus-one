# Unit 5C — Report and Proposal English

*One paragraph. One sentence. The two units of writing that run a small NGO.*

---

## Learning outcomes

By the end of this unit, you will be able to:

- **(Create)** Write a one-paragraph situation summary (100–150 words) and a basic funding-request sentence in professional English, using the four-move structure (who / what / so what / therefore) and the canonical *X funds Y for Z over W* form — and using AI to assist drafting while you verify accuracy and tone yourself.

---

## The problem this unit solves

A program associate at a small Hyderabad NGO is not asked to write a twenty-page proposal.

You will be asked, often weekly, to write a one-paragraph *situation summary* — for an internal report, a board update, a donor email, a social media post. You will also be asked to write a one-line *funding request* — *"we need Rs. 50,000 to run this program for one quarter"* — that your senior colleagues then fold into bigger proposals.

These two units of writing — *the paragraph* and *the sentence* — are the day-to-day deliverables of NGO admin work. The whole annual report is built from them. The donor email is built from them. The board update is built from them.

Your problem is not that you cannot write a long document. Your problem is that you have never been taught how to make these two short units carry the work they have to carry. This unit teaches that.

Two failure modes to avoid along the way. **One — development-jargon overload.** Paragraphs full of *empowerment, capacity-building, holistic approach, stakeholders, beneficiary-centric* until they say nothing specific. You will see this writing everywhere. Do not copy it. **Two — under-claim.** Paragraphs so cautious and qualified that no reader can tell what problem is being addressed or what the program is asking for. Either failure costs the NGO the grant.

The target is a third register: *specific, modest, action-oriented English that names the population, the gap, the consequence, and the proposed action — in 120 words*.

## Opening case

Priya is sitting at her director's desk on her first morning as a Program Associate at a small NGO based in Banjara Hills, Hyderabad. The NGO runs an after-school study program for girls in three mandals of Mahbubnagar district. The director — Ms. Anjali — has emailed her the brief.

(Priya here is the running learner-vignette — a composite, not a real student.)

> *Priya, welcome. First task: please draft a one-paragraph situation summary for the proposal we are sending to the Whitman Family Foundation next week. About 120 words. Cover who we serve, the problem, the consequence, and what we do. I will edit it before it goes out. — Anjali.*

Priya has six years of residence experience. She has just completed Course 2. She has the Unit 2 AI workflow.

She opens ChatGPT. She types:

> *"Write a 120-word situation summary for an NGO proposal about girls' education in rural Telangana."*

ChatGPT produces:

> *"Our holistic intervention addresses the multifaceted challenges faced by underprivileged adolescent female beneficiaries in rural Telangana. Through capacity-building and stakeholder engagement, we aim to empower these vulnerable populations and create sustainable transformation in their socio-economic landscape, thereby ensuring positive outcomes for all stakeholders involved."*

Priya pastes it into the email draft. Sends to Anjali.

Anjali replies in twenty minutes:

> *Priya — that paragraph could be about any program in any country. It tells me nothing. How many girls? Which mandals? What is the gap? What do we actually do for them? Try again, please.*

Priya is stuck. The AI gave her the words. The words were wrong.

What Priya needed — and what this unit will give you — is a *structure* that forces the writing to be specific. The structure is four moves: **who / what / so what / therefore.** Each move is one or two sentences. Each move names something specific the reader can use. The whole paragraph runs 100–150 words.

By the end of this unit, Priya will rewrite that paragraph in 113 words, with four moves clearly executed, and Anjali will reply *"perfect — this goes in."*

## Core content

### 1. The four-move situation summary — *who / what / so what / therefore*

This structure is durable across decades of NGO writing. It shows up in nearly every grant-writing manual (Robinson, *Grant Writing for Dummies* `[verify edition]`; [Foundation Center / Candid materials](https://www.candid.org/training)). Indian NGOs use it in CRY's, Pratham's, Akshaya Patra's, HelpAge's, and Magic Bus's annual reports — sometimes with small variations. Four moves. Always in this order.

**Move 1 — Who.** The population in *specific terms*. Numbers. Ages. Location. Setting.

- Wrong: *"underprivileged girls in rural India."*
- Right: *"6,400 girls aged 11–14 attending Telangana government schools in three mandals of Mahbubnagar district."*

The first version could be about anyone. The second names the exact group. The difference is not in the words. The difference is *whether the reader can picture who you mean*.

**Move 2 — What.** The gap or problem in *observable terms*. Something a person could count or measure.

- Wrong: *"face significant educational challenges."*
- Right: *"of these, only 34% complete Class 8 on schedule; the rest drop out or repeat at least one year."*

*Educational challenges* could mean anything. *34% complete Class 8 on schedule* is an audit-able statistic. The donor's accountant can check it against district data.

**Move 3 — So what.** The *consequence* of the problem. Why does it matter? What happens next if nothing changes?

- Wrong: *"this has serious implications for their futures."*
- Right: *"Girls who do not complete Class 8 on schedule have a 71% higher likelihood of marriage before age 18, according to NFHS-5 district-level data."*

The first version is empty. The second connects the educational problem to a different downstream problem (early marriage) and cites a specific source. **This is the move most amateur writers skip.** Without it, the reader is told a problem and a program, but never told *why the program matters*. Add the *so what*. It changes the paragraph from description to argument.

**Move 4 — Therefore.** The *proposed action* and *what it produces*.

- Wrong: *"we implement holistic interventions to address these challenges."*
- Right: *"Our after-school study program in three of these mandals supports 240 girls weekly with structured tutoring in English and mathematics, school-supplies support, and quarterly parent engagement; over the past two years, 92% of our girls have stayed in school through the program."*

The first version says nothing about the program. The second names what the program does (tutoring, supplies, parent engagement), at what scale (240 girls weekly), in what location (three mandals), with what measured outcome (92% retention).

Four sentences. About 120 words total. Every claim audit-able. Every noun specific.

### 2. Why the *so what* move is the one most writers skip

Move 3 — the consequence — is the hardest move for new writers. You will be tempted to skip it. The temptation comes from two places:

**Temptation 1 — You think the problem is obvious.** *"Of course girls dropping out is bad — why do I have to spell out what happens next?"* The reader, sitting in Seattle or Mumbai with forty proposals on her desk, does not have the same starting assumptions you do. She needs you to connect the educational problem to the downstream consequence she is paying for. The grant-maker funds *outcomes*, not *activities*. Without Move 3, she cannot see which outcome you are aiming at.

**Temptation 2 — You do not have the data.** *"I do not know the exact statistic on early marriage rates for girls who drop out of Class 8."* This is a research problem, not a writing problem. The data is usually findable in NFHS surveys, ASER reports, district statistical handbooks, Census data. ([NFHS-5 portal](http://rchiips.org/nfhs/nfhs-5_Report.shtml) `[verify NFHS-6 status]`; [ASER reports](https://asercentre.org/).) The thirty minutes you spend finding the statistic is the thirty minutes that earns the grant. Skip it and the paragraph collapses.

When you cannot find the exact statistic, *write the version of the sentence you would use if you had it*, and flag it for your senior colleague to fill in. Do not write the sentence without the statistic. A *so what* claim without a citation is the kind of writing donors stop trusting.

### 3. The funding-request sentence — *X funds Y for Z over W*

The second unit of writing this chapter teaches. The canonical structure of the funding-request sentence is the same across the international development sector:

> *"[Amount] funds [specific outcomes] for [number of beneficiaries] over [time period]."*

This template has crystallized over decades of grant writing ([USAID guidance](https://www.usaid.gov/work-usaid/get-grant-or-contract); [Indian CSR templates under Companies Act 2013](https://www.mca.gov.in/MinistryV2/csr_data.html); [Candid grant-writing training](https://learning.candid.org/)). The reader has scanned thousands of funding sentences. The predictable structure is itself a credibility signal. **If you rearrange the order, the reader has to work harder, and the proposal underperforms.**

Three sizes of the funding-request sentence:

**Size 1 — One line. For an email or short update.**

> *"Rs. 8 lakh funds after-school tutoring for 240 girls in three Mahbubnagar mandals over 12 months, with a target retention of 90%."*

One sentence. Names amount, action, beneficiaries, time, and target outcome. Twenty-five words.

**Size 2 — Paragraph. For an internal proposal.**

> *"We are requesting Rs. 8 lakh from the Foundation for the period April 2026 to March 2027. The funding will support weekly tutoring sessions (60 sessions per girl across the year), tutor stipends for three local part-time tutors (one per mandal), school-supplies and stationery for each of the 240 girls, and quarterly parent-engagement events. Based on our 92% retention over the past two years, we expect to retain at least 216 of 240 girls in continuous school enrollment through the funding period."*

Three sentences. Names amount, period, what the funding will cover in line-item detail, and the expected outcome. About 90 words.

**Size 3 — Quarterly update sentence. For a progress report.**

> *"In Q2 (July–September 2026), the Rs. 2 lakh of disbursed funding supported 30 tutoring sessions per girl, retained 232 of 240 girls in regular school attendance (97% retention this quarter), and surfaced two cases requiring additional family-engagement support — both cases are now stable."*

One sentence. Names the period, the amount disbursed, the activity, the outcome, and any exceptions found. About 50 words.

The structural rule: **amount, outcomes, beneficiaries, time — in that order, every time.**

### 4. Outcomes vs. inputs — the hardest sub-skill

A common failure: the writer reports what the program *did* (sessions held, books distributed) instead of what the program *changed* (girls retained, learning gains measured). The first is *inputs*. The second is *outcomes*.

The donor pays for outcomes. The program team tracks inputs. **You have to write outcomes.**

The paired example:

- Input version: *"We ran 60 tutoring sessions per girl across three mandals."* — This is what you did. It is correct. It is also not enough.
- Outcome version: *"232 of 240 girls (97%) stayed in regular school attendance over twelve months."* — This is what changed because of what you did. This is the sentence the donor cares about.

Most reports need *both* — inputs and outcomes — but the outcome sentence comes *first*, and the input sentence comes after as supporting detail. The reverse order signals *we tracked our activities but cannot tell you whether they worked*. That signal kills future funding.

A practical drill: read any paragraph you have written. Circle every verb. Is the verb describing *what you did* or *what changed*? *Conducted, distributed, organized, ran* are input verbs. *Retained, improved, completed, gained* are outcome verbs. Aim for at least one outcome verb in every paragraph.

### 5. Donor-facing register vs. internal-program register — what changes

Same week of program work. Two different audiences. Two different paragraphs. You will be expected to switch fluently.

**Internal-program register** — used in team meetings, work-in-progress reports, program-officer-to-program-officer email. Inputs-heavy. Operational. Honest about problems. First-person plural.

> *"We ran 18 sessions in Mahbubnagar this quarter, 16 in Wanaparthy, 14 in Nagarkurnool. Attendance averaged 82%. Tutor turnover in Wanaparthy was higher than expected; we have rehired. Mobilizers identified two new dropouts in October; door-to-door follow-up brought one back."*

Operational vocabulary. *Mobilizer*. *Door-to-door follow-up*. *Rehired*. The reader is another program person who needs to know how the program is actually running.

**Donor-facing register** — used in grant applications, donor updates, annual reports, board-update emails. Outcomes-heavy. Strategic. Acknowledges problems without foregrounding them. Active accountable voice.

> *"232 of 240 girls were retained in school through the funding period (92% retention). The intervention's design — combining tutoring with stipend support and parent engagement — appears to drive the retention outcome. Tutor recruitment in one of three mandals required mid-year renewal; recruitment processes have been strengthened."*

The same program week. Same data. Different framing. **The difference is what the reader needs to know, not what we say happened.**

The donor needs to know: *what changed, why, how confident are we, what did we learn.* The internal team needs to know: *what we did, where, with whom, what went wrong, what we are fixing.* Both registers are honest. They tell different parts of the same story to different audiences.

### 6. The Indian CSR context — Companies Act 2013, Section 135

Indian NGOs since 2014 have a funding ecosystem most other countries do not have. The [Companies Act 2013, Section 135](https://www.mca.gov.in/MinistryV2/csr_data.html) requires any company with net worth ≥ Rs. 500 crore, turnover ≥ Rs. 1,000 crore, or net profit ≥ Rs. 5 crore to spend at least 2% of average net profits on Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) activities. This rule has reshaped Indian NGO funding between 2014 and 2024. Aggregate CSR spending is in the range of Rs. 25,000+ crore annually `[verify current figure with MCA CSR portal]`.

What this means for your writing:

**CSR-funded reports follow a specific template.** Most CSR donors require quarterly impact reports against pre-stated KPIs (key performance indicators). The format is more compressed than international-foundation reports. More numerical. Categories are standardized: project description, expenditure breakdown, beneficiary count, geographic reach, photos with consent, audit-ready documentation. The four-move situation summary fits inside this template. The funding-request sentence becomes part of the KPI section.

**CSR register skews slightly more corporate.** *"Our intervention aligns with the company's CSR focus area on girls' education and skill development"* is acceptable in a CSR report. The same sentence would read as buzzwordy in a Gates Foundation context. The chapter teaches: *adjust to the donor*.

Three donor registers you will encounter:

- **CSR (Indian company funder):** more corporate, KPI-driven, alignment-language acceptable. *"Aligned with the company's CSR focus area..."*
- **International foundation (BMGF, Ford, Gates):** evidence-driven, outcomes-driven, less alignment-language, more independent-impact-language. *"Our two-year retention data suggests..."*
- **Individual donor / monthly giver:** story-driven, beneficiary-voiced, smaller numbers, more emotional but still anchored to specific outcomes. *"Sunita, age 12, was the first girl in her family to..."*

Unit 6C develops the international-foundation register in more depth.

### 7. Plain language as a deliberate move

The grant-writing field has converged toward *plain language* as a quality marker. The international [Plain Language Action and Information Network (PLAIN)](https://www.plainlanguage.gov/) defines plain language as *"communication your audience can understand the first time they read or hear it."* For NGO writing, this means five small habits:

**Habit 1 — Specific nouns over abstract nouns.** *Girls aged 11–14* over *adolescent female beneficiaries*.

**Habit 2 — Concrete verbs over nominalizations.** A *nominalization* (new word) is a verb turned into a noun — *implement* becomes *implementation*, *intervene* becomes *intervention*. The verb form is clearer. *Girls dropped out* over *Dropout occurrence was observed*.

**Habit 3 — Active voice over passive.** *Our team conducted 18 sessions* over *18 sessions were conducted*.

**Habit 4 — Numbers as words when small, as digits when large or precise.** *Three villages, 240 girls, Rs. 8 lakh.*

**Habit 5 — One idea per sentence.** Compound sentences with three clauses fail readability tests at the level you are writing for.

You do not need to memorize the readability metrics. You need the *rewrite habit*: read your first draft, find the abstract nouns, replace them with specific nouns; find the passive verbs, switch them to active; find the three-clause sentences, split them. Your first draft will read at a complicated level. Your second draft should read at a simpler one.

The AI workflow from Unit 2 ports directly to this rewrite. You draft. You ask ChatGPT: *"Rewrite this in simpler English, one idea per sentence, Indian Standard Business English register, plain-language style."* You read every line. You correct where the AI shifted toward American register or invented a statistic.

### 8. The AI-hallucinated-statistic trap — the sharpest risk in this unit

You met AI hallucination in Unit 2. In NGO writing, the risk gets sharper.

ChatGPT will confidently produce sentences like:

> *"In Telangana, 53% of girls in rural government schools fail to complete Class 8."*

The sentence sounds authoritative. It may or may not be true. If you paste it into a funding proposal without verifying against a primary source — NFHS-5, ASER, the Telangana government statistical handbook, the district education office — and the number is wrong, the NGO's credibility is damaged. The donor's program officer will check. If she finds the statistic does not match her own data, the proposal is dead and the next proposal from the same NGO is read with suspicion.

The rule: **every number generated by AI in an NGO situation summary must be re-verified against a primary source before it leaves the draft.** Treat AI numbers as *suggestions for what to look up*, not as *facts to use directly*.

This is more important in NGO writing than in BPO or hospital writing. The NGO's credibility runs on its accuracy. Once it leaks, it is hard to rebuild.

## Worked example — Priya's situation summary, three drafts

Back to the brief from the opening case. Anjali asked for a 120-word situation summary on the girls' education program in Mahbubnagar.

### Attempt 1 — Priya's current level (development-jargon trap)

> *"Our holistic intervention addresses the multifaceted challenges faced by underprivileged adolescent female beneficiaries in rural Telangana. Through capacity-building and stakeholder engagement, we aim to empower these vulnerable populations and create sustainable transformation in their socio-economic landscape, thereby ensuring positive outcomes for all stakeholders involved."*

What is wrong:

- *Holistic intervention* — abstract noun. The reader cannot picture what kind of intervention.
- *Multifaceted challenges* — abstract noun. What challenges, specifically?
- *Underprivileged adolescent female beneficiaries* — abstract noun phrase. How many? Where? What ages?
- *Capacity-building and stakeholder engagement* — jargon nouns. What activities, specifically?
- *Sustainable transformation in their socio-economic landscape* — empty phrase. Says nothing.

Every noun is abstract. The paragraph could be about any program in any country. Anjali rejected it because that is the failure mode.

### Attempt 2 — Over-corrected (cold and bare)

Priya tries again. This time she strips everything out:

> *"There are 6,400 girls in three mandals. 34% finish Class 8. We tutor 240 of them. 92% stay in school."*

What is wrong:

- Move 1 (*who*) is incomplete. Which mandals? Which schools? What ages?
- Move 3 (*so what*) is missing. The consequence is not stated. The reader does not know why Class 8 completion matters.
- Move 4 (*therefore*) is too compressed. What does the tutoring program actually do? How often? What does it cost?

Each sentence is technically accurate. The reader still cannot tell the situation. *Bare facts without structure* fails just as badly as *jargon without facts*.

### Attempt 3 — Target (four moves, plain language)

Priya rewrites once more. She uses the four-move template. She fills each slot with specifics.

> *"In three mandals of Mahbubnagar district — Devarakadra, Bhoothpur, and Wanaparthy — about 6,400 girls aged 11–14 attend Telangana government schools. Of these, only 34% complete Class 8 on schedule; the rest drop out or repeat at least one year. According to NFHS-5 data, girls who do not complete Class 8 on schedule have a 71% higher likelihood of marriage before age 18. Our after-school study program serves 240 girls weekly across these three mandals with structured tutoring in English and mathematics, school-supplies support, and quarterly parent engagement; over the past two years, 92% of our girls have stayed in school through the program."*

113 words. Four moves clearly executed. Every claim audit-able. Plain-language register. Specific nouns throughout. No jargon.

What changed:

- **Move 1 (who)** — *"In three mandals of Mahbubnagar district — Devarakadra, Bhoothpur, and Wanaparthy — about 6,400 girls aged 11–14 attend Telangana government schools."* Numbers, names, ages, schools.
- **Move 2 (what)** — *"Of these, only 34% complete Class 8 on schedule; the rest drop out or repeat at least one year."* Observable. Audit-able.
- **Move 3 (so what)** — *"Girls who do not complete Class 8 on schedule have a 71% higher likelihood of marriage before age 18."* Plus citation. Connects the educational problem to the downstream consequence.
- **Move 4 (therefore)** — *"Our after-school study program serves 240 girls weekly... 92% of our girls have stayed in school."* Names the program. Names the activities. Names the outcome.

Anjali reads it. Reply in three minutes: *"Perfect. This goes in. Now do the funding-request paragraph."*

## Worked example — the funding-request sentence, three sizes

Same program. Three different needs. Priya writes all three.

### Size 1 — One line (for an email)

> *"Rs. 8 lakh funds after-school tutoring for 240 girls in three Mahbubnagar mandals over 12 months, with a target retention of 90%."*

24 words. Amount. Outcomes (target retention). Beneficiaries (240 girls). Time (12 months). Anchored in geography (three mandals).

### Size 2 — Proposal paragraph

> *"We are requesting Rs. 8 lakh from the Foundation for the period April 2026 to March 2027. The funding will support weekly tutoring sessions (60 sessions per girl across the year), tutor stipends for three local part-time tutors (one per mandal), school-supplies and stationery for each of the 240 girls, and quarterly parent-engagement events. Based on our 92% retention over the past two years, we expect to retain at least 216 of 240 girls in continuous school enrollment through the funding period."*

83 words. Names amount. Names period. Names what the funding covers, in line items. Names the expected outcome with an audit-able number (*at least 216 of 240*).

### Size 3 — Quarterly update

> *"In Q2 (July–September 2026), the Rs. 2 lakh of disbursed funding supported 30 tutoring sessions per girl, retained 232 of 240 girls in regular school attendance (97% retention this quarter), and surfaced two cases requiring additional family-engagement support — both cases are now stable. Expenditure detail and signed attendance records are attached."*

53 words. Period. Amount disbursed. Inputs (sessions). Outcome (retention). Exceptions (two cases, both stable). Reference to attachments (audit-ready).

Same program, three densities. The structure (*amount, outcomes, beneficiaries, time*) is identical. The level of detail is calibrated to the audience.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Skipping Move 3 (*so what*).** Already named in §1 and §2. The single most common amateur failure. Without the consequence, the paragraph is description; with it, the paragraph is argument. Add the *so what* even when it feels obvious — especially when it feels obvious.

**Mistake 2 — Inputs where outcomes belong.** *"We ran 60 sessions per girl"* is what you did. *"We retained 92% of girls in school"* is what changed. The donor pays for the second. Lead with outcomes. Inputs come second as supporting detail.

**Mistake 3 — Over-claiming on outcomes.** *"Transformed the lives of 240 girls."* This is unverifiable. The donor's accountant cannot audit *transformation*. The right version: *"Retained 232 of 240 girls in regular school attendance for two years."* That sentence is audit-able. Rule of thumb: *if you would not want a CSR auditor reading the sentence aloud to a board, you cannot write the sentence.*

**Mistake 4 — Pasting AI statistics without verification.** Already named in §8. The single sharpest risk in NGO writing. ChatGPT will produce confident-sounding statistics. They may be hallucinated. Verify every number against a primary source — NFHS, ASER, district statistical handbook — before the draft leaves your screen.

**Mistake 5 — Vague claim count.** *"Helps many girls."* You know the actual number is 240. You did not write it because you were not sure 240 sounded impressive. The judgment about whether 240 is impressive belongs to the donor, not you. Your job is to state it accurately. *Many* is amateur. *240* is professional.

**Mistake 6 — Mixing donor and internal register in the same paragraph.** A donor-facing paragraph that includes *"tutor turnover was a problem"* in the middle of an outcome statement reads as unfinished. Save the internal-register operational details for the internal report. Surface the same fact in donor language only when relevant — *"recruitment processes have been strengthened"* — at the end of the paragraph, not in the middle.

**Steps:**

3. Paste your draft into ChatGPT or Gemini with this prompt: *"This is a situation summary for a CSR donor under Companies Act 2013 Section 135. ## What you can do now

You can:

- Write a 100–150 word situation summary using the four-move structure (*who / what / so what / therefore*).
- Write the funding-request sentence at three sizes (one-line, proposal paragraph, quarterly update).
- Distinguish inputs from outcomes and lead with outcomes in donor-facing writing.
- Switch between internal-program register and donor-facing register on the same data.
- Identify and remove the most common jargon nouns and abstract phrases.
- Verify AI-generated statistics against primary sources before they go into a draft.
- Adjust register across the three Indian NGO funding sources — CSR, international foundation, individual donor.

That is enough to write the daily one-paragraph deliverables a Hyderabad NGO program associate is asked for.

## For volunteers / sisters' notes

*A note for Humanitarians AI volunteers running this week's Zoom session, and for the Salesian sisters supporting the girls between sessions.*

The girls will bring their Exercise 3 paragraphs (situation summary, 120 words) and their Exercise 4 funding-request sentences (three sizes). One main move this week: **read each girl's paragraph and ask the three-question check.**

For each paragraph:

1. **Who-question:** *"Can I picture the population from Move 1?"* If you cannot, name what is missing — usually a number, a location, an age, or a setting.
2. **What-question:** *"Can I audit the statistic in Move 2 against a real source?"* Ask her where she got the number. If she cannot name a source, that is a research gap. Send her back to find one.
3. **So-what question:** *"Does Move 3 connect the problem to a consequence the donor will care about?"* This is the move most girls will get partially right and need to sharpen. Ask: *"What happens if we do not solve this problem?"* Her answer to your spoken question is often the Move 3 sentence she should have written.

After the three-question check, give three specific notes per paragraph: one move that landed well, one move to strengthen, one phrase to cut (usually a jargon noun).

For the sisters: the daily writing habit matters more than the long session. Ten minutes a day rewriting yesterday's paragraph in fewer words. The plain-language reflex only builds with repetition.
