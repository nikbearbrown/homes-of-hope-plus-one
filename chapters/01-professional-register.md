# Unit 1 — Professional Register

*How to sound professional in written English when you know 500 words.*

---

## Learning outcomes

By the end of this unit, you will be able to:

- **(Apply)** Write a professional WhatsApp message and a basic professional email in the right register. No switching to Telugu. The prompt will be a workplace situation — for example, asking a hiring manager about an open job.

---

## The problem this unit solves

You know the words. You do not yet know the register.

*Register* (new word — used a lot in this book) means: **the kind of language you use for a kind of situation.** It is not grammar. It is not vocabulary. It is the *sound* of language fitting the situation.

You speak to your friend one way. You speak to a senior Salesian sister another way. Same person — you. Same language. Different register.

You already do this in Telugu. You do it without thinking. In English, you have to learn it on purpose, because you are starting fresh.

This unit will show you. *"Please help me"* and *"I would like to request your assistance"* use almost the same words. One sounds like a friend. One sounds like a job application. The hiring manager reads both in one second. He picks up the phone for one of them. He scrolls past the other.

You will learn which is which. Then you will write your own.

## Opening case

Two girls send two WhatsApp messages on the same morning. Both want a job at a small BPO office in Madhapur, Hyderabad. ("BPO" — new word. It stands for *Business Process Outsourcing*. A BPO office is a company that takes phone calls or chat messages for other companies, usually customer service. Concentrix is a BPO. Genpact is a BPO. The Hyderabad BPO sector is one of the largest in India.) Both girls have basic English. Both have completed the same kind of literacy training.

**Message 1:**

> *sir job application kya hai sir, i have basic english sir, when i can come sir pls tell me sir*

**Message 2:**

> *Good morning Sir. I would like to apply for the Customer Service position posted on Naukri yesterday. I am a fresher and available for a walk-in interview this week. Could you please share the office address and timing? Thank you. Best regards, Priya.*

(Priya is the running learner-vignette in this book — a composite, not a real student. Her situation matches many girls in Homes of Hope residences across Hyderabad.)

The hiring manager reads both. He spends three seconds on each. He calls Priya back the same afternoon. He does not call the first girl.

Why?

The first girl is not less smart. She is not less hireable. She used the *casual* register — the register you use with a friend. The hiring manager is not her friend. He is looking for someone who can write to a customer in a way that does not embarrass the company. The first message would embarrass the company.

Priya used the *consultative* register. That is the register the BPO job lives in. She did not use formal British English ("Yours faithfully, I have the honour to submit..."). She did not use casual English either. She used the middle one.

This middle register — *consultative* — is the one you have to learn first. It is the register your job market actually uses.

## Core content

### 1. What register means, and why it matters in Hyderabad

Register is not the same as grammar. A sentence can be perfectly correct in grammar and still wrong in register.

Look at the sentence *"Send the file."*

The grammar is correct. The vocabulary is correct. But if you write it to your boss, it sounds like an order. Your boss is your senior. You do not give him orders. He gives you orders. The register has missed.

The fix is one small word — *please*, or one modal verb — *could*. *"Please send the file"* — better. *"Could you please share the file?"* — best. Same meaning. Different register.

This is the load-bearing idea of this unit. **The signal is not in the words. It is in the way you arrange the words.**

Three short notes on Hyderabad context:

**You are not aiming at BBC English.** That is a different country and a different century. You are aiming at *Indian Standard Business English*. This is a real version of English. Millions of professionals in India use it every day. Linguists who study English — like Braj Kachru and Pingali Sailaja — have written long books about it (Kachru 1985; Sailaja, *Indian English*, Edinburgh University Press, 2009). Your hiring manager at Concentrix or Apollo Hospitals writes in this version. So do you.

**You are not aiming at American English either.** *"Hey, hope this finds you well!"* — this is American business email. It is not wrong. It is just not the right register for a Hyderabad office. American AI tools (ChatGPT, Gemini) will produce American register unless you tell them not to. You will learn how in Unit 2.

**You are aiming at clear, polite, professional Indian English.** Plain. Direct. With small softeners — *could you, would you, please, thank you*. This is the register your hiring manager already writes. You match it. You sound like you belong.

### 2. The five register shifts you must learn

These five small moves convert almost any casual message into a consultative one. Memorize them. Use them. After ten messages they will feel automatic.

**(1) Greeting.** Casual messages start with the request — *"sir job apply"*. Consultative messages start with a greeting — *"Good morning Sir,"* or *"Hello Ms. Reddy,"* or *"Dear Sir,"*. One greeting at the top. Not three. Not zero.

**(2) Request.** Casual messages give direct orders — *"send the file"*. Consultative messages soften the request with a *modal verb*. A modal verb is a small helper word — *could, would, can, may*. Use one. *"Could you please share the file?"* or *"Would it be possible to schedule a meeting?"* The modal does the politeness work. You will use *could you please* a lot.

**(3) Apology.** Casual: *"sorry sir busy"*. Consultative: *"I am sorry for the delay. I will share the document by tomorrow morning."* Two parts. Acknowledge the problem. Say what you will do next. Never just *sorry* alone.

**(4) Update.** Casual: *"work done"*. Consultative: *"I have completed the task. Please find the file attached."* Or: *"The work is finished. I will send the file by 6 PM today."* Specific. Time-bound. Clear.

**(5) Sign-off.** Casual messages just stop. Consultative messages close — *"Best regards, Priya"* or *"Thanks and regards, Priya [last name] +91 [phone]"*. One closing line. Full name. Phone if it is the first time you are writing to this person.

These five shifts cover most of what you will write in your job search.

### 3. WhatsApp and email are not the same register

In Hyderabad, your first contact with a hiring manager will often be on WhatsApp — not on email. The recruiter at Concentrix or Foundever does not send a printed letter. He sends a WhatsApp. *"Are you available for an interview Wednesday?"* You reply on WhatsApp.

This matters because **WhatsApp has its own professional register.** It is shorter than email. It still has the five shifts above. Here is the rule:

**Professional WhatsApp** — complete sentences (do not drop words). Capitalize *I* and proper nouns (*Priya*, *Concentrix*, *Apollo*). One greeting at the top. No slang. No *yaar, na, no?*. No emoji except possibly one *thank you* hands or *good morning* smile at the end if the conversation has already become friendly. Reply within 12 hours, ideally faster.

**Professional email** — adds two things to the WhatsApp register: a *subject line* that describes the email (not just *"job"* — try *"Application for Customer Service Associate position — Priya [last name]"*), and a more formal sign-off — *"Best regards, Priya [last name] +91 [phone]"*.

Learn WhatsApp register first. Email register sits on top of it. A girl who can write a professional WhatsApp can write a professional email by adding two lines. The reverse — learning email first, then trying to write WhatsApp — produces strange, over-formal WhatsApp messages that look as wrong as the casual ones.

### 4. Telugu-to-English transfer — what to watch for

This is the part many books skip because they do not want to seem critical. We will not skip it. Your goal is a job, not a polite teacher. The chapter author respects your Telugu and your English. We are going to name the specific places where Telugu structure, when carried directly into English, lands at the wrong register. None of these are *errors* in your English. They are *register misses* that the hiring manager will notice.

**(a) Direct request without a softener.** Telugu polite requests often use a direct verb plus a politeness marker. Carried into English: *"Send the file"*, *"Tell me the timing"*. The English imperative without a modal verb sounds blunt. Fix: add *could you please* or *would you please* in front. *"Could you please share the file?"*

**(b) Time-phrase order from Telugu.** *"I will come tomorrow morning 10 o'clock"* — this is Telugu time-phrase order. English uses *at*: *"I will come tomorrow at 10 a.m."* Small fix. Big register lift.

**(c) Too many *Sir* or *Ma'am* in one message.** *"Sir, I have one question Sir, when can I start Sir?"* In Telugu, repeated honorifics show respect. In Indian Business English, one *Sir* per message is consultative. Three is sub-consultative. Pick the *Sir* that opens the message and drop the rest.

**(d) No question word at the start of a question.** *"When I can start?"* — this is Telugu/Hindi question structure. English questions start with the question word and flip the verb: *"When can I start?"* It is a small inversion. The hiring manager hears it.

**(e) Mixing Telugu and English in the same message.** *"sir job kab start hai please tell"*. Inside the residence, this is fine. To a hiring manager, switch to English fully or to Telugu fully. Mid-sentence switching reads as casual. Pick one and stay there.

A small honest note: *"kindly do the needful"* is a famous old phrase in Indian Business English. It is not wrong. Some hiring managers still use it. Others — especially in younger multinationals — read it as slightly outdated. **Rule for this book:** recognize *kindly do the needful* when you see it in messages you receive. In messages you send, prefer the newer forms — *"Please let me know how I can help"* or *"Please share your guidance on the next step."* You will look modern without looking foreign.

### 5. Using AI to check your register

You can paste your message into ChatGPT or Gemini and ask: *"Is this register professional enough for a Hyderabad job application?"* The AI will give you feedback. This is useful.

But — and this is important — the AI defaults to American business register. If you do not tell it where the job is, it will rewrite your message in *"Hey, hope this finds you well, just wanted to circle back..."* style. That is the wrong register for your job market.

**The fix is one phrase you add to your prompt: *"for a Hyderabad job."*** Full prompt:

> *"Is this message at the right professional register for a Hyderabad BPO job application? Please give me feedback in simple English and rewrite it in Indian business English if needed: [paste message]"*

That single phrase — *for a Hyderabad job, in Indian business English* — pulls the AI into your register. Without it, the AI helps you sound American. With it, the AI helps you sound hireable in Hyderabad.

Unit 2 will go deeper into this. For now, remember: AI is a useful register-checker. You still decide. You read every line. You correct anything that does not match your job market.

## Worked example — Priya asks about a joining date

Priya received a verbal job offer over the phone yesterday from a Customer Service hiring manager at a Hyderabad BPO. She wants to ask when she can start. She opens WhatsApp and types her first attempt.

(Priya here is the running learner-vignette — a composite, not a real student.)

### Attempt 1 — her current level

> *sir when i can start? pls tell me. thank u*

What is wrong with this message?

Not the words. The words are correct. The register signals are wrong:

- No capital *I*, no capital *Sir*. Small visual signal of low effort.
- *pls* and *u* — SMS-era casual abbreviations. Wrong register for a BPO hiring manager.
- *"when i can start"* — Telugu/Hindi question structure carried into English. English wants *"when can I start"*.
- *"pls tell me"* — direct imperative. No modal softener.
- No greeting at the top. No sign-off at the bottom.

The hiring manager reads this and pauses. Did he offer the job to a serious candidate? He wonders.

### Attempt 2 — over-corrected

Priya re-reads her message. She decides it sounds too casual. She rewrites:

> *Respected Sir, I am writing this message to most humbly request you to kindly inform me regarding the exact date on which I shall be commencing my service with your esteemed organization. Kindly do the needful at your earliest possible convenience. Yours faithfully, Priya.*

This is now too formal. *Respected Sir* and *Yours faithfully* are 1950s Indian-government-letter register. The hiring manager reads this and worries he hired someone who sounds like a court summons. He already offered her the job yesterday — they spoke on the phone — there is no need for a court summons. The over-formal register is its own register miss.

### Attempt 3 — target consultative

Priya tries again. This time she applies the five shifts.

> *Good morning Sir,*
>
> *Thank you for offering me the position yesterday. Could you please let me know my joining date? I will plan accordingly.*
>
> *Best regards,*
> *Priya [last name]*
> *+91 [phone]*

This is the target. Let us walk through what she did:

- *Good morning Sir* — greeting matches the medium and the relationship. (Shift 1.)
- *Thank you for offering me the position yesterday* — anchors the message in the context the manager remembers. He offered her a job; she is acknowledging.
- *Could you please let me know my joining date?* — modal-hedged question. Polite. Consultative. (Shift 2.)
- *I will plan accordingly* — signals professional initiative. She is not just asking; she is also saying she will act on the answer.
- *Best regards, Priya [last name] +91 [phone]* — clean close with full name and phone. (Shift 5.)

Same vocabulary as Attempt 1. Same request as Attempt 1. Different register. The hiring manager reads it, smiles, and replies in two minutes: *Your joining date is Monday 15th. Please report by 10 AM. Welcome to the team.*

That is the goal of this unit. Same words. Right register.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1 — *"My English is not good enough yet for professional messages."*** This is the most common reason girls do not write the message at all. It is also wrong. You have 350 English words. That is enough vocabulary to produce a professional WhatsApp message. The register shifts are five small moves. You do not need more words. You need the moves. After you make them, your message is hireable.

**Mistake 2 — Aiming too high.** When in doubt, girls reach for the most formal English they have heard — *"Respected Sir, I shall most humbly..."*. This is the over-correction trap. The right target is the middle register, not the top one. Aim for *Good morning Sir, Could you please...*, not *Respected Sir, I beg to submit*.

**Mistake 3 — Using *Sir* and *Ma'am* like commas.** One *Sir* per message is plenty. The Telugu instinct is to add a *Sir* at the start, the middle, and the end of every sentence. In English, this reads as casual or even satirical (people sometimes do this as a joke). Use *Sir* once at the start and let it carry through.

**Mistake 4 — Stopping mid-message because you do not know the next word.** This is the *translation reflex* — you compose in Telugu in your head, hit a word you cannot translate, freeze. Fix: use the slot-filler templates from this unit. *"Could you please [verb]?"* is a slot. Put any verb you know in the slot. *"Could you please help"*, *"Could you please tell"*, *"Could you please send"* all work. You do not need new words. You need patterns.

**Mistake 5 — Trusting the AI's first draft without reading every line.** The AI can produce a message in two seconds. It will sound smooth. It may also be in the wrong register, contain a wrong fact about you, or use a phrase your hiring manager does not use. We will go deeper on this in Unit 2. For now: **read every line of any AI output before you send it.**

### Exercise 1 — Rewrite three casual messages *(Bloom: Apply)*

Below are three casual WhatsApp messages. Rewrite each one in consultative register. Use the five shifts. Do not change the meaning. Change the register.

a) *"sir send me job posting"*

b) *"i cant come tomorrow sir, sick"*

c) *"work done sir, what next"*

Try each one on your own first. Then ask ChatGPT or Gemini: *"Is this at the right register for a Hyderabad BPO message?"* Compare its rewrite with yours.

### Exercise 2 — Find the register error *(Bloom: Analyze)*

Each of these sentences has one register error. Find it. Name what kind of error it is (use the categories in §4).

b) *"Please send the file fast."*

c) *"I will reach tomorrow morning 9 o'clock to your office Sir."*

d) *"Sir please Sir tell me Sir the timing."*

e) *"hey just wanted to circle back about the application thanks"*

### Exercise 3 — Write a professional follow-up to a job application *(Bloom: Create + Evaluate)*

This is the load-bearing exercise. You will use it in your Zoom session this week.

**Situation:** You have applied online for an entry-level customer service position at a Hyderabad BPO three days ago. You have not heard back. You want to send a short professional WhatsApp message to the HR contact listed on the posting, asking about the status of your application.

**Steps:**

1. Write the message yourself first, in your current English. Do not use AI yet.
2. Then paste your message into ChatGPT or Gemini with this prompt: *"Please check this WhatsApp message for a Hyderabad BPO job follow-up. Is the register right for Indian business English? If not, please rewrite it and tell me what you changed."*
3. Read the AI's rewrite line by line. Do you agree with each change? Did the AI add anything that is not true about you? Did the AI use any phrase that sounds American rather than Indian? Mark anything you want to change back.
4. Produce your final version, in your voice, at the right register.
5. Bring this final version to your Zoom session. The volunteer will read it and give you feedback.

This is the GPT Tutor workflow you will use again and again in this course. You draft. AI helps. You review. You decide. **You — not the AI — own the message you send.**
