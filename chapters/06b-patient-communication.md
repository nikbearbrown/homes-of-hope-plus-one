# Unit 6B — Patient Communication

*How to greet, register, and direct a patient with confidence — in 90 seconds.*

---

## Learning outcomes

By the end of this unit, you will be able to:

- **(Apply)** Greet a patient, collect basic registration information, explain a simple procedure, and direct someone to the right department in professional English — using a structured script (AIDET) anchored in the Tier 1 vocabulary from Unit 5B.

---

## The problem this unit solves

You know the fifty words now. You still do not yet know how to put them in the right order.

A patient walks up to your counter at 9:15 AM. She is anxious. She is in mild pain. Her husband is on the phone behind her. She has a referral from a GP in Karimnagar and an appointment slip she has folded four times. She speaks Telugu first, some Hindi, basic English.

You have ninety seconds before the next patient is at the counter. In those ninety seconds you need to:

- Acknowledge her — by name, if you know it; with *sir* or *madam* if you do not yet.
- Introduce yourself — name and role.
- Tell her how long this will take.
- Explain what you are about to do, in the order you will do it.
- Thank her at the close.

And on top of that you need to actually do the work — collect her phone number, look up her appointment, check her insurance, hand her the token, point her to the second floor.

A receptionist with good English and no script tries to remember all this in real time and freezes. A receptionist with average English and a memorized script delivers it in ninety seconds, every time, without thinking about it. **The script is the job.** This unit teaches the script.

There is also a load-bearing register lesson in this unit. **The patient is not a customer.** Track A learned to *delight* the customer. Track B will learn to *respect* the patient. The two registers sound different — and the difference matters more than the English level.

## Opening case

Priya is back at counter 1 at Apollo Jubilee Hills, shadowing Lakshmi. It is the second day. A patient walks up — a woman, about 55, holding a folded referral note. Her son stands one step behind her.

(Priya here is the running learner-vignette — a composite, not a real student.)

The woman looks tired. Her face is tight. She is in pain, but politely.

Lakshmi looks up, makes eye contact, and smiles small.

> Lakshmi: *"Good morning, madam. Welcome to Apollo. My name is Lakshmi — I am the registration associate. May I help you?"*
> Woman: *"I have referral. Dr. Reddy. Cardiology."*
> Lakshmi: *"Thank you, madam. May I see the referral note, please? And could you tell me your full name?"*
> Woman: *"Lakshmi Devi. Karimnagar."*
> Lakshmi (smiles wider — same name): *"Thank you, Mrs. Devi. I can see Dr. Sharma has referred you to cardiology — Dr. Reddy is available at 11:30. That is about 45 minutes from now. Is that all right, or would you prefer a later slot?"*
> Woman: *"11:30 is fine."*
> Lakshmi: *"Wonderful. I will register you now — this will take about 5 minutes. I need your date of birth, your phone number, your address in short, and your insurance card if you have one. Then I will give you a token and directions. Could I have your date of birth?"*

Five minutes later, the woman is sitting in the cardiology waiting area on the second floor with a numbered token in her hand. Her son knows where the chai shop is. They know the doctor is expected at 11:30. They know the nurse will check vital signs before the doctor sees her.

Priya watches the next patient walk up to the counter. Lakshmi resets. Same script.

That is the goal of this unit. **A script that runs at sentence speed and respects the patient's dignity, every time, regardless of how the rest of the day is going.**

## Core content

### 1. The patient is not a customer — and what changes when you remember that

This is the load-bearing register lesson of Track B.

Track A — BPO — trained you (or some of your classmates) to *delight the customer*. The customer has a complaint about her broadband bill. Your job is to *resolve* it, sound cheerful, hit your call-handling time, escalate if she stays angry. The customer is paying for a service. The script is bright, fast, energetic. *"How may I delight you today, ma'am?"*

The patient is not paying for a service the way a broadband customer is. The patient is paying for *care*. She is scared, or in pain, or tired, or grieving, or all four. She is at the hospital because something is wrong. She did not come because she wanted to come. The script she needs sounds different.

Three substitutions are the heart of this distinction. Memorize them.

- *Customer* (BPO register) becomes *patient* (healthcare register).
- *Resolve* (BPO register) becomes *support* (healthcare register).
- *Delight* (BPO register) becomes *dignity* (healthcare register).

These three changes reorganize every sentence you speak at the front desk. *"How may I resolve your issue today?"* lands wrong at a hospital. *"How may I help you today?"* lands right. *"I will delight you, ma'am"* would be strange to say to anyone, anywhere — but it is *especially* strange to say to a patient. *"I will help register you for your consultation, ma'am"* — that is the consultative register Track B requires.

The voice change is also small but real. Slightly lower in pitch. Slightly slower in pace. Slightly more pause-discipline. You do not perform brightness. You perform *competence with warmth*. The two are not the same. A patient leaves a good front desk feeling *registered and respected* — not *entertained and energized*.

Unit 7B will return to this. The hospital interviewer is trained to listen for the difference. If you arrive at the interview with the BPO register, you under-perform — not because your English is wrong but because the register is.

### 2. AIDET — the five-step script every Hyderabad hospital trains

AIDET is a five-step framework for patient-staff communication. It was developed by the Studer Group in the United States in the late 1990s and is now used in thousands of hospitals worldwide, including most NABH-accredited hospitals in India. ([Studer Group / Huron AIDET materials](https://www.huronconsultinggroup.com/insights/studer-group) `[verify exact URL]`.) It is taught to clinicians most often, but the structure works just as well at the front desk — sometimes better, because the front desk is the patient's first interaction with the hospital.

The acronym: **A**cknowledge — **I**ntroduce — **D**uration — **E**xplanation — **T**hank you.

**A — Acknowledge.** Make eye contact. Greet the patient. If you know the name from the appointment system, use it. If not, use *sir* or *madam*. *"Good morning, madam."* *"Good morning, Mr. Rao."* The acknowledgment is one short sentence. Done with eye contact, it does the work.

**I — Introduce.** Tell the patient who you are and what your role is. *"My name is Priya, I am the registration associate."* The role-naming is doing real work — it signals to the patient *what kind of help you can give and what kind of help comes from someone else*. Clinicians introduce by name plus specialty (*"I am Dr. Reddy, cardiology"*). Front desk introduces by name plus role.

**D — Duration.** Tell the patient how long this will take. *"This will take about 5 minutes."* This is the single most effective patient-satisfaction move in the entire interaction. ([Sweeney, Webb, & Mazor 2008, *Journal of Healthcare Management*](https://journals.lww.com/jhmonline/Abstract/2008/03000/Patient_Satisfaction_With_the_Time_Spent_Waiting.6.aspx) `[verify`].) Most patient anxiety at the front desk is about *how long is this going to take* — not about anything you said or did. Stating the duration up front cuts that anxiety in half. Even an approximate duration is better than none. *"About 5 minutes for registration, then a short wait."*

**E — Explanation.** Tell the patient what is going to happen, in the order it will happen. *"I will need your insurance card and a phone number where we can reach you. Then I will check whether your appointment is confirmed in the system. After that I will print your token, and you can go up to the cardiology OPD on the second floor."* The explanation is a small map of the next five to ninety minutes. It is the cognitive scaffold the patient needs.

**T — Thank you.** Close with thanks. *"Thank you for waiting, sir."* *"Wishing you a good consultation."* The close is short and warm. In Hyderabad context, the close also often carries a routing reminder: *"The lift is to your right; please take it to the second floor."*

AIDET is structurally a *consultative* register (Unit 1) with the *medical-administrative* vocabulary (Unit 5B). The chapter's job is to slot the five letters into the front-desk flow Priya will encounter on day one.

There are other patient-communication frameworks — *Calgary-Cambridge*, *SEGUE*. You will hear about them if you read further. They are designed for clinician-patient conversations: a doctor eliciting symptoms, giving a diagnosis, agreeing on a treatment plan. Front-desk staff do not do any of those things. You do not elicit symptoms. You do not give diagnoses. You acknowledge, register, explain the administrative process, and route. AIDET is the right tool because it stops at the door of clinical content. The other frameworks would push you across that door, which is not your job.

### 3. The Indian hospital greeting protocol — *sir / madam* is still standard

Indian hospital front-desk register keeps *sir* and *madam* as the default address when you do not know the patient's name yet. The frequency at Apollo, Yashoda, KIMS, AIG, Care, Continental is higher than in American or British hospitals. This is durable across institutional types and patient demographics. Do not drop it because an American friend said it sounds old-fashioned.

Four cases to remember:

**Case 1 — Name unknown.** *"Good morning, sir."* / *"Good morning, madam."* That is the default. Always.

**Case 2 — Name known from the appointment.** *"Good morning, Mr. Rao."* / *"Good morning, Mrs. Reddy."* The honorific (*Mr. / Mrs. / Ms.*) plus surname is the Apollo and Yashoda formal-register default. *Mr. Rao* is preferred over *Rao sir* in written documents. In spoken Indian-English register, *Rao sir* is also acceptable.

**Case 3 — Foreign patient with an English name.** *"Good morning, Mr. Smith."* Drop the *sir* once the name is known. Using *Mr. Smith sir* with a Western patient reads as over-deferential to a Western ear.

**Case 4 — Senior physician.** Never *uncle, aunty, sir-ji.* Always *Doctor* + surname. *"Dr. Reddy is in OPD 4, sir."* Doctors are addressed by title in front of patients at all times. This is a small status marker the front desk reinforces. Hiring managers notice when a candidate slips this.

A small note: *Uncle / Aunty* is appropriate for elderly *attendants* (family members) in less-formal settings — neighborhood hospitals, small clinics. At Apollo, Yashoda, KIMS, it is generally avoided for *patients*. The chapter teaches the formal end of the register because that is what the big chains expect.

### 4. The standard registration script — ten fields, in this order

Patient registration is a near-deterministic form-fill. The fields are the same at every Hyderabad hospital. The order is what differs slightly between chains. The order below is the Apollo and Yashoda default; small variations at KIMS, AIG, Care, Continental do not change the structure.

1. **Full name** (as on ID proof). *"Could I have your full name, please?"*
2. **Date of birth** (or age, if DOB unknown — common with older rural patients). *"Date of birth?"*
3. **Sex / gender** (often pre-filled from the appointment). *"Just confirming — female, yes?"*
4. **Phone number** (primary contact). *"Phone number, please?"*
5. **Address** (city + locality at OPD; full at admission). *"And your address — locality and city is enough for now."*
6. **Email** (optional at OPD, required at admission for insurance correspondence). *"Email, if you have one?"*
7. **ID proof** (Aadhaar number for nearly all Indian hospitals; passport or driving license accepted). *"Could I see your Aadhaar card?"*
8. **Chief complaint** — the patient's stated reason for the visit, in her own words. *"And what brings you in today, sir? In short."*
9. **Referring doctor** (if any). *"Were you referred by another doctor, sir?"*
10. **Insurance** (if any). *"Do you have insurance? Could I see your card?"*

Then — *and this is the part many learners skip* — the **read-back step**. You read the critical fields back to the patient and confirm.

> *"Your full name is Lakshmi Devi, date of birth 15 March 1962, phone number ending in 3-4-5-7, here for follow-up cardiology with Dr. Reddy. Is that all correct, madam?"*

This step is not optional. It is the NABH *two-identifier rule* in operational form — patients must be identified by at least two identifiers (name + DOB, or name + UHID) before any service is delivered. It is also a simple error-prevention move; you will catch the wrong phone number, the wrong DOB, the wrong doctor name before they end up on the chart. ([NABH 5th Edition Standards](https://www.nabh.co/) `[verify edition]`; the two-identifier rule is also a WHO Patient Safety Solution from 2007.)

Memorize the order. The fields will become automatic after about 50 patients.

### 5. Wayfinding — three sentences that cover most of it

Hospital signage in Hyderabad is bilingual (English plus Telugu at most chains, English plus Hindi at others). Patients still ask. The standard wayfinding sentence has three components: floor + landmark + direction.

**Template 1 — the simple case.** *"[Department] is on the [Nth] floor. Take the lift on your [right/left], and follow the [colored] arrows."*

> *"Cardiology is on the second floor. Take the lift to your right and follow the blue arrows."*

**Template 2 — the same-floor case.** *"It is the [Nth] room on the [right/left] as you exit the lift."*

> *"It is the third room on the right as you exit the lift."*

**Template 3 — the cross-building case.** *"There is a separate building for [X]; please cross the courtyard. It is the building with the [colored] board."*

> *"Radiology is in a separate building. Please cross the courtyard — it is the building with the green board."*

A note on colors: signage colors vary by hospital. Apollo uses one color scheme; Yashoda uses another; KIMS uses a third. **The safe pattern is to name the floor and a directional landmark, not just the floor number.** A patient who is lost cannot find *"the second floor"* if she does not know which lift gets her there.

### 6. The empathic phrase ladder — warmth without overreach

Some patients arrive at your counter anxious, in pain, or grieving. They need acknowledgment before they need an insurance card. But you are not the clinician — you cannot give medical reassurance, and you must not. The line between *warm acknowledgment* and *clinical overreach* is the hardest sub-skill in this unit.

Here is the ladder, from the right move to the wrong one.

**Right move — basic.** *"I'm sorry you're not feeling well. Let me get you registered quickly so the doctor can see you."*

This acknowledges the discomfort. It redirects to the action you control — registration. It references the clinician — *the doctor* — whose job the actual reassurance is. One sentence, two clauses. Clean.

**Right move — more anxious patient.** *"I understand this is stressful. Please take a moment; I'll have you registered in just a few minutes."*

This names the emotion (*stress, anxiety*) without diagnosing it. It offers the patient some control over pace. It still routes to the clinician.

**Borderline move — depends on the situation.** *"Don't worry, sir. The doctor is very good."*

This is only acceptable from staff who actually know the doctor and the typical outcome. For an entry-level receptionist, this is risky — it can be heard as a *clinical promise* (*"the doctor will fix this"*). Avoid it in your first six months. Use the basic move instead.

**Wrong move — never.** *"You'll be fine."* / *"It's nothing serious."* / *"My grandmother had the same thing and she recovered."*

These are clinical assurances. The front desk has no authority to make them. They expose the patient (false reassurance) and the hospital (legal risk). The empathic impulse behind them is kind. The professional move is to redirect that impulse to the *basic* sentence above. *Acknowledgment without clinical content.* Always.

This ladder draws on the broader literature on *empathic but boundaried communication* in healthcare ([Halpern 2003, *J Gen Intern Med*](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1046/j.1525-1497.2003.21017.x) `[verify`]; [SAMHSA's trauma-informed care framework](https://www.samhsa.gov/resource/dbhis/samhsas-concept-trauma-and-guidance-trauma-informed-approach), adapted for non-clinical staff). The principle: name the emotion, acknowledge the situation, route to the right authority. Do not perform expertise you do not have.

### 7. The angry-patient sub-script — three sentences

The AIDET script handles cooperative patients. Some patients are not cooperative on the day they meet you. They are angry — sometimes at the hospital, sometimes at the situation, sometimes at the world. They reach your counter loud. You did not cause this; you have to handle it anyway.

The three-sentence move:

> *"Sir, I understand you're upset. Let me ask my supervisor to come and help. Please give me one minute."*

What this sentence does:

- *I understand you're upset* — acknowledges without arguing.
- *Let me ask my supervisor to come* — routes to the right authority. You are not the supervisor; you do not have to solve this alone.
- *Please give me one minute* — gives the patient something concrete to wait for, which lowers the temperature.

You do not match the patient's volume. You do not promise resolution. You acknowledge and route. Then you call the supervisor on the internal line. (This is the *escalate* word from your fifty — word 49 — in action.)

If the patient is verbally abusive or you feel physically unsafe, call security. Hyderabad hospitals have visible security at most chains. *"I will need to call security, sir, please step back from the counter."* The chapter cannot teach you the courage to use this sentence; it can only tell you that the sentence exists and that experienced receptionists use it without apology.

### 8. The DPDP Act — what it means at the front desk

The [Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023](https://www.meity.gov.in/data-protection-framework) is the Indian law that governs how organizations handle personal data. It came into force in stages from 2024. Hospitals are *data fiduciaries* under the Act — meaning they have legal responsibility for the personal data they collect.

You handle personal data at every interaction: name, date of birth, phone, address, ID number, sometimes medical complaint. Three things change at the front desk because of this Act:

**Change 1 — A stated purpose at the start of collection.** *"I am collecting this information to register you for today's consultation and to maintain your medical records."* Most patients will not pause to absorb this sentence. It is there for legal compliance and to signal professional awareness. Say it once at the start of registration.

**Change 2 — Visible-display restraint.** The patient's name, contact, and complaint should not be visible or audible to other patients in the queue. Read fields back at a lower voice. Position the screen away from people behind the patient. This is small — but a hiring manager will notice whether you do it.

**Change 3 — No discussion outside work.** What you see at the desk stays at the desk. You do not discuss patient names, diagnoses, or stories with your family, your friends, or anyone outside your work team. This is part of the consent / confidentiality bargain.

Unit 9 (your first week) will go deeper on this. For now: **you are handling private information. Treating it as private is part of the professional standard.** The DPDP Act gives this standard legal force.

## Worked example — Priya runs the AIDET script for a referral patient

The patient is Mr. Subba Rao, 67 years old, from Karimnagar. He arrives at the Apollo Hospitals Jubilee Hills OPD reception with a printed referral note from his GP for cardiology consultation. Priya is at counter 1. She has Lakshmi's coaching from yesterday in her head and her Anki deck running.

**A — Acknowledge:**

> *"Good morning, sir. Welcome to Apollo Jubilee Hills. May I help you?"*

Priya looks up, makes eye contact, smiles small. The patient's name is not yet known — *sir* carries the acknowledge function alone.

**I — Introduce:**

> *"My name is Priya — I am the registration associate."*

Two pieces of information. Her name. Her role. Twelve words.

The patient holds out the referral note. *"I have a referral. Cardiology."*

Priya: *"Thank you, sir. May I see the referral note? And could you tell me your full name, please?"*

Patient: *"Subba Rao. From Karimnagar."*

Priya (the smile widens slightly): *"Thank you, Mr. Rao."*

**D — Duration:**

> *"I will register you now — this will take about 5 minutes. Then there is a short wait before Dr. Reddy sees you, about 30 minutes."*

She has just removed half the patient's anxiety. He now knows the schedule.

**E — Explanation:**

> *"I will need your date of birth, your phone number, your address in short, and your insurance card if you have one. Then I will print your token, and you can go up to the cardiology OPD on the second floor. Could I have your date of birth, sir?"*

She runs the ten fields in standard order. She reads the critical fields back: *"Mr. Subba Rao, date of birth 3 June 1958, phone ending in 9-8-7-4, here for cardiology referral from Dr. Sharma, Karimnagar. Is that all correct, sir?"*

Patient: *"Correct."*

She checks the insurance card. *"Star Health, I will verify cashless eligibility while you wait."*

**T — Thank you:**

> *"Thank you, Mr. Rao. Here is your token — C-37 for cardiology OPD. The OPD is on the second floor. Take the lift to your right, then turn left when you exit; you will see the cardiology waiting area. The nurse will check your vital signs before Dr. Reddy sees you. The whole process from now to consultation should take about 35 minutes. Is there anything you'd like to ask, sir?"*

Patient: *"No, thank you."*

> *"Thank you, Mr. Rao. Wishing you a good consultation."*

Total interaction: about 3 minutes 30 seconds. AIDET-structured. Registration fields in standard order. Wayfinding sentence with floor plus landmark plus direction. Duration estimate. Empathic close.

If you want to test yourself on this: cover the labels (*A, I, D, E, T*) and read the script aloud. Where does each move start? Where does it end? The day you can run the five moves without thinking about which letter you are on, you are ready for the interview in Unit 7B.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Performing BPO brightness.** The biggest single mistake Track B candidates make at the desk and in the interview. *"Welcome to Apollo, sir, how may I delight you today!"* lands wrong. The patient is here because something is wrong. He does not want to be delighted. He wants to be registered, respected, and routed. Calm warmth — not sales-floor energy.

**Mistake 2 — Skipping the read-back step.** The patient gave you a phone number; you typed it; you moved on. Then the discharge call goes to the wrong number, the patient never gets the report, the family complains. The read-back step takes 15 seconds. It catches errors and it is required by NABH. Do not skip it because the line is long.

**Mistake 3 — Reaching for medical reassurance.** *"Don't worry, you'll be fine."* This is the empathic impulse pushing you across the line into clinical overreach. The patient leaves feeling reassured. Then the doctor delivers a difficult diagnosis. Your reassurance becomes a lie the patient remembers. The acceptable empathic move is acknowledgment, not promise. *"I'm sorry you're not feeling well. Let me register you so the doctor can see you."* Stop there.

**Mistake 4 — Stiff and robotic from fear of overreach.** The opposite mistake. You become so worried about overreaching that you turn into a form-fill machine. *"Name. DOB. Phone. Address. ID. Insurance. Token. Second floor. Next."* The patient leaves feeling registered but not seen. The middle is the right place — warm administratively. Small kind sentences at the right moments. *"Thank you for waiting, madam."* *"Take your time, sir."* These do not cross any line. They humanize the desk.

**Mistake 5 — Code-switching at the wrong moment.** Hyderabad front desks code-switch between Telugu, Hindi, and English constantly. This is professional, not casual. **But documents must be in English.** The system is English. The chart is English. Spoken Telugu when the patient prefers Telugu is correct. Written Telugu on the registration form is wrong. Keep the spoken language flexible; keep the written language English.

**Mistake 6 — Confusing *Sir* count.** Three *Sir*s in one sentence reads as over-deferential. *"Sir, can I have your number, Sir, please, Sir?"* One *Sir* per sentence. Two in a 90-second interaction is fine. Five becomes performative.

**Pass 2 — small wrinkle.** Same patient, but this time you interrupt the registration at field 6 (email) to say *"I'm sorry, my phone is ringing — can we restart?"* Watch how she handles the restart. The point of this pass is *not* to trip her; it is to see whether her script holds under a small reality-check.

The angry-patient three-sentence move (Exercise 4) is the only one that has to come out under pressure. Listen for whether her voice stays steady when she rehearses it. If she gets faster or louder, gently model the slower, lower version. Voice steadiness is a learnable physical skill — slow the breath before the sentence.

For the sisters: between sessions, the script-rehearsal habit matters most. Five minutes a day reading the script aloud, alone, in the mirror, beats 30 minutes once a week. The volunteer will notice the difference at the next session.

A flag for both groups: **the AIDET adaptation, the registration field order, and the empathic phrase ladder in this chapter should be reviewed by a working Hyderabad front-desk supervisor before the chapter is published as final.** Until that review is in, treat the script as a strong starting structure that may need three to five small adjustments.
