# Unit 8 — The Mock Interview

*Practice until the nerves are smaller than the preparation.*

---

## Learning outcomes

By the end of this unit, you will be able to:

- **(Evaluate + Create)** Complete a full 15-minute mock job interview with a Humanitarians AI volunteer over Zoom, receive specific feedback on pronunciation, register, and content, and revise your answers based on that feedback.
- **(Apply)** Answer the four universal interview questions in a 90-second structured answer — *situation, what you did, what happened, what you learned.*
- **(Apply)** Handle the *I-don't-know* moment with one of three professional responses, instead of freezing.

---

## The problem this unit solves

You can do everything this course has taught you. On your phone. Alone. In your own time.

You have not yet said any of it out loud, in English, to a stranger looking at you over a video call, who is deciding whether to hire you.

That gap is the one this unit closes.

Not knowledge. *Nerves.*

"Nerves" is a vague word. Let us make it specific. Nerves means: you have the answer in your head, and your mouth says something else. You have rehearsed a sentence, and your throat tightens, and the last word comes out small. You planned a pause, and the pause becomes a freeze. One small wobble, and the next sentence falls apart with it.

This is the gap between Unit 7 and Unit 9 — between *I can write a professional message* and *I can sit on a Zoom call and be hired*. Closing it is the work of this unit.

You will close it by doing a real mock interview. Not a fake one. A 15-minute interview with a real Humanitarians AI volunteer who has worked in your sector. Then 15 minutes of specific feedback. Then revision.

One important thing first. **The 15 minutes of feedback is the main event.** The 15 minutes of interview is the way the feedback collects what to talk about. Most girls assume the interview is the test and the feedback is the wrap-up. It is the other way around. The feedback is where you learn. The interview is where the learning material is gathered.

This unit is the *gate* of Course 2. You do not pass this unit without the live Zoom session. Practice, not evaluation — but you must show up.

## Opening case

Two girls do mock interviews on the same Saturday morning. Both have completed Units 1–7. Both are applying for a Customer Service Associate role at a Hyderabad BPO.

(One of them is Priya — the running learner-vignette in this book, a composite, not a real student.)

**Girl 1** logs in to Zoom at 10:00 AM, exactly on time. The volunteer asks the first question — *"Tell me a little about yourself."* Girl 1's first sentence is good. Her second sentence drops in volume. Her third sentence trails off. The volunteer continues. By Question 3, Girl 1 is producing one-sentence answers and looking down at her hands. The interview ends. The volunteer says, gently: *"Your English is good. You did well."*

Girl 1 logs off. She is not sure what to do differently. She got "good" feedback and she does not feel good. The next real interview is on Wednesday and she does not know what to change.

**Girl 2 — Priya** logs in to Zoom at 9:55 AM. Five minutes early. Camera on. Light from the window in front of her, not behind her. She has on the kurti she will wear to a real walk-in interview. Before logging in, she put a hand on her own collarbone and said out loud, once: *"This is practice, not the real thing."*

The volunteer — Anjali, a Humanitarians AI volunteer who worked at Concentrix Hyderabad for three years — opens with: *"Hi Priya. This is a practice session, not a test. We will do 15 minutes of mock interview, then we will spend 15 minutes on feedback. If something goes wrong in the middle, that is exactly what this session is for. Ready?"*

Priya nods. The interview begins.

Priya's interview is not perfect. Her Question 1 answer has a wording problem (she says *"I have completed 12th class"* when her CV says she is *in* 12th). Her Question 4 answer — *what would you do if a customer was angry?* — produces a 12-second silence. She finally says *"I would try to help the customer."* The volunteer continues to Question 5.

When the interview ends, the feedback begins. *This* is the part that matters. Anjali tells Priya — specifically — what to change about Question 1, where her voice dropped, why Question 2 sounded generic, and what three things she could have said when she froze on Question 4. She does not say *"your English is good."* She says *"when you said X, your voice got smaller on the last word — try ending with the same volume you started with — like this — say it for me now."*

Priya says it. The first try still trails off. The second try lands. Anjali says: *"Yes. That is what to practice this week."*

By the end of the 30 minutes, Priya has four specific things to work on. She knows exactly what they are. She is going to record herself doing them three times before Wednesday. By Wednesday she will be a better interview candidate than Girl 1 — not because she had better English, but because she had better feedback and she used it.

Both girls did the same mock interview. The volunteer made all the difference. **The feedback debrief is the unit.**

## Core content

### 1. Why a mock interview works — perceived control, not confidence

You have been told the problem is *confidence*. Build your confidence. Believe in yourself. Walk in with confidence.

This advice does not work. Telling a nervous girl to feel confident is like telling a hungry girl to feel full. The feeling does not arrive when you ask for it.

The actual problem is something more useful — and more fixable. Researchers who study why students feel anxious before a test or an interview have found two things matter, together. Not one. Two. ([Pekrun 2006, "The Control-Value Theory of Achievement Emotions," *Educational Psychology Review*](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-006-9029-9).)

**One — do I think I can affect the outcome?** Researchers call this *perceived control*. Can my preparation, my words, my way of sitting, my way of answering — actually change what happens in this interview? Or is the result decided by something I cannot touch?

**Two — does this outcome matter to me?** Researchers call this *value*. The job interview matters. The job matters. The salary matters. The first job in your life matters.

When the outcome matters *and* you feel you cannot affect it — that is when anxiety arrives. Your body's threat system fires. Your brain pulls energy away from talking and toward watching for danger. You sit in the chair and your mouth produces less English than it usually does. That is not a confidence problem. That is a *perceived-control* problem. You feel you cannot affect what happens. So your body prepares for the worst.

Here is what is interesting. The fix is not *feel different about the outcome*. The fix is *change what you believe you can affect*.

Practice changes what you can affect. Rehearsal changes what you can affect. Knowing the four questions in advance — knowing the shape of a 90-second answer — knowing the three ways to handle *I don't know* — these are not confidence-boosters. They are *control-increasers*. Each one moves a part of the interview from *unknown* to *known*. The body's threat system reads the change. The anxiety steps back.

This is why the mock interview works. You do not become a different person. You do not feel a different feeling. You enter the room knowing more of what is going to happen than you did before. That is what the practice gives you.

(One careful note. The chapter is using the word *control* in a specific way — *I can affect what happens through what I do.* Not *I control whether they hire me.* You do not control that. They do. What you control is your preparation, your answers, your composure when something goes wrong. That smaller thing is the control that matters.)

### 2. The four universal interview questions

Almost every entry-level interview, in every sector, opens with the same four questions. Sometimes the words are slightly different. The shape is the same. Recognize them. Rehearse them. They are the safest 80% of the interview to prepare for.

**(1) Tell me about yourself.**

This is the warm-up question, but it is not soft. The interviewer is taking the first measurement — can you speak about yourself in clear, structured, professional English for one minute without rambling?

The wrong answer: *"I am Priya. I am 19 years old. I live in Hyderabad. I have completed my schooling. I want to work."* — too short, too generic, no shape.

The right answer is a 90-second structured story (we will build this in §3). Name, where you have been, what you have done, what you are looking for, in that order. Not a life history. A focused introduction.

**(2) Why do you want this job?**

The trap question. The wrong answer — the answer the interviewer has heard 50 times this week — is *"I want to work in BPO because it is a good opportunity."* Every other candidate said the same sentence. None of them got the job either.

The right answer connects *this job, at this company,* to *you specifically.* From your CV, your residence experience, your three languages, your interest. One sentence that only you could say.

We will work on this in the worked example.

**(3) What is your strength?**

Most candidates answer: *"My strength is hard work and punctuality."* True. Also generic. The fix is to *give one specific example* immediately after the strength. *"My strength is consistency. For example, at the residence I helped a younger student with her homework every evening for four months. She passed the exam she had failed twice. That kind of steady work is what I am good at."*

The example is the answer. The strength is the label on top of the example.

**(4) What would you do if [scenario]?**

This is the question that breaks the most candidates. The scenario varies by sector. *"What would you do if a customer was angry?"* (BPO.) *"What would you do if a patient was crying?"* (hospital.) *"What would you do if you saw a child in distress on a home visit?"* (NGO.)

You cannot rehearse the exact scenario. You can rehearse the *shape* of an acceptable answer. We will teach that shape in §4 — the three responses to *I don't know.*

### 3. The 90-second answer framework

This is the most important practical skill in the unit. You will use it for at least Questions 1 and 3 — possibly all four.

The shape is four parts:

- **Situation** — set the scene. Where, when, what was the context. *(About 10–15 seconds.)*
- **What you did** — the action you took. The core of the answer. *(About 40–50 seconds.)*
- **What happened** — the result. What actually came of what you did. *(About 15–20 seconds.)*
- **What you learned** — one sentence about how this changed how you approach similar situations. *(About 10–15 seconds.)*

Total: about 90 seconds.

Why 90 seconds? Practitioner interview-coaching guidance converges on roughly 60 to 120 seconds for a behavioral answer. Below 60 seconds, you look unprepared. Above 120, the interviewer's mind wanders to their grocery list. 90 seconds is the safe center.

Here is the framework working — Priya answering *"Tell me about a time you helped someone solve a problem."*

> **(Situation.)** Last year, at the Homes of Hope residence, a younger girl who had just joined was struggling with her 8th-class English homework. She had failed her last two English exams. She was about to give up.
>
> **(What I did.)** I was responsible for the evening study time. I started sitting with her for thirty minutes after dinner, four days a week. I did not try to teach her the whole subject. I asked her each evening which one sentence in the chapter she did not understand. We worked on that one sentence until she could read it out loud. Then she would explain to me what it meant in Telugu.
>
> **(What happened.)** After four months of this, she passed her next English exam. Her score was 58 out of 100. The two times before, she had scored 32 and 28.
>
> **(What I learned.)** I learned that helping somebody is not the same as teaching them everything. Helping somebody is figuring out the one small piece they cannot get past on their own, and standing with them until they can get past it.

Read it aloud. That is about 90 seconds at a normal pace.

Notice four things this answer does not have. It does not have *"I have always been passionate about helping others."* It does not have *"I am a quick learner."* It does not have *"I work well in team environments."* It does not have *"I would describe myself as a hard-working person."* All four of those sentences are *generic filler*. The interviewer has heard each one 30 times this month. None of them are evidence. The example is the evidence.

A 90-second specific answer beats a five-second generic answer every time. This is the whole secret.

(One note on what counts as a *situation*. You may worry your situations are not "professional." You have not yet had a paid job. That is fine. The residence is a situation. Your literacy program is a situation. Helping a younger resident is a situation. Coordinating an evening meal is a situation. The interviewer is not measuring whether you had a paid job. He is measuring whether you can describe a situation, an action, a result, and a lesson — in 90 seconds, in clear English. Use your real life. Your life has plenty of situations.)

### 4. The "I don't know" moment — three good responses and one fatal one

You will be asked at least one question you have not rehearsed. Maybe one you do not know the answer to. This will happen. Plan for it.

The mistake girls make is treating "I don't know" as an end of the interview. It is not. It is one move among several. The interviewer is testing how you handle the unknown — because at the actual job, you will face the unknown daily. He is watching what you do when you do not know.

There are four moves. Three are professional. One ends the interview.

**Move 1 — the clarifying question.**

You do not understand the question fully. You ask the interviewer to say more before you answer.

*"Could you tell me a little more about what you mean? I want to make sure I answer the question you are actually asking."*

This is the strongest move. It buys you a few seconds. It signals you listen carefully. It often produces a re-statement of the question that you *can* answer.

**Move 2 — the honest admission with a redirect.**

You have not been in that exact situation. You name the gap, and offer something nearby.

*"I haven't been in that exact situation. The closest I have done is [X]. Would that be useful to share?"*

This is the answer for *"Have you handled an angry customer?"* when you have not handled a customer at all. Yes, you have not. But you have handled an upset younger resident at the home. The two are not identical, but they share what matters — listening, calming, helping. The redirect must be sincere. *"I haven't done X but I once did Y which is similar"* — only if Y is genuinely similar.

**Move 3 — structured thinking-out-loud.**

You do not have an answer ready. You build one in front of the interviewer.

*"Let me think for a moment. First, I would [step 1]. Then I would [step 2], because [reason]. Finally I would [step 3]. Does that make sense?"*

The interviewer is *more impressed* by this than by a rehearsed answer. Because at the actual job, problem-solving in real time is what they need from you. Use sequence words — *first, then, because, finally*. Say them clearly. The interviewer can hear the structure.

This is the right answer for almost all *"What would you do if..."* questions. The four-part scenario answer Priya should have given in the opening case is exactly this move.

**Move 4 — the fatal one.**

Silence past ten seconds with no acknowledgment. Or — worse — a confident wrong guess.

Twelve seconds of silence is too long. The interviewer's discomfort response fires. He decides you cannot handle pressure. The interview is effectively over even if it continues to the next question.

The other fatal move: *"Yes, I have handled difficult customers many times"* — said with confidence — when you have not. The next question will be *"Can you give me an example?"* You will not have one. The interview ends.

The rule is simple. *The cost of "I don't know, here is what I would do" is much lower than the cost of a confident guess.* Especially in Hyderabad BPO walk-in interviews where the recruiter is doing 30 interviews a day and can hear the difference between rehearsed-honest and fabricated-confident from across the room.

If you find yourself frozen for more than five seconds, say one sentence out loud: *"Let me think for a moment."* That sentence is permitted. That sentence is professional. That sentence is what to memorize as your reset move. It buys you three seconds without the silence becoming a problem.

### 5. Indian professional interview culture — five things American advice gets wrong

Most English-language interview-prep content online is written for American hiring conventions. Some of its advice is correct for you. Some is exactly wrong. Five places to watch.

**(a) Eye contact.** American interview prep says *sustained eye contact = confidence.* In Hyderabad — especially with senior interviewers — prolonged unbroken eye contact for a full answer can read as challenging or disrespectful. The right move: meet the interviewer's eyes when you greet, when you start an answer, and at the end of an answer. Let your gaze move naturally during the longer part of the answer. Eyes on the interviewer's face — not on the table, not on your hands. But not staring without break.

For the Zoom mock interview specifically: **look at the camera, not at the screen.** The camera is the small dot at the top of your phone or laptop. When you look at the camera, the volunteer sees what looks like eye contact. When you look at the screen — where the volunteer's face is — the volunteer sees you looking *down*. This is a physical skill. Practice it.

**(b) Titles.** *Sir* and *Madam* (or *Ma'am*) are still the standard at entry level in Hyderabad BPO, healthcare, and most NGOs. ([Sailaja 2009, *Indian English*, Edinburgh University Press](https://edinburghuniversitypress.com/book-indian-english.html).) Use *Sir* or *Madam* with the interviewer — once at the start of the conversation, occasionally during. *Not* every sentence. The single-*Sir*-per-message rule from Unit 1 applies to spoken interviews too. *"Sir, please, Sir, when can I start, Sir?"* — three *Sirs* in ten seconds — is the over-correction. *"Good morning Sir. Thank you for your time."* — once at the top — is consultative.

If the interviewer introduces himself as *"Rohan"* — you still call him *Sir* unless he says *"Please call me Rohan."* Even then, in formal moments — *Sir Rohan* or just *Sir* is fine. This is not over-respect. This is normal Hyderabad corporate register.

**(c) Punctuality.** American interview advice: *arrive exactly on time*. Indian professional interview convention: *arrive 15–30 minutes early.* For a walk-in interview, 15 minutes early is normal. Five minutes early is the minimum acceptable. Exactly on time is — already a small negative mark. Late, even by five minutes, is often disqualifying.

For Zoom: log in five minutes before the scheduled start. Not at 10:00 sharp for a 10:00 interview. At 9:55. The volunteer notes who logs in early and who does not.

This is not a small cultural detail. This is one of the two biggest reasons new hires fail in the first week (you will see this again in Unit 9). The interviewer is already taking a punctuality reading at the interview. Pass it.

**(d) Dress.** Dress for the mock interview as you would for the real one. There is a small effect from the act of dressing — your mental state shifts when you put on the kurti and the closed-toe shoes. ([Adam & Galinsky 2012, "Enclothed Cognition," *Journal of Experimental Social Psychology*](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0022103112000200) — the effect size is debated, but the principle of *rehearsal-as-real-conditions* is solid regardless.) The practical version:

- **BPO interview** — kurti + churidar (white or muted color) with optional dupatta; closed-toe shoes; small earrings; hair tied back. Or western business casual — collared shirt or blouse + trousers + closed-toe shoes. Either works at entry level.
- **Hospital reception interview** — kurti + churidar with dupatta; conservative colors; very short clean nails (infection-control culture); closed-toe shoes.
- **NGO interview** — salwar-kameez or kurti + leggings; closed-toe walking shoes; less strict than BPO.

`[verify specific dress code policies at named employers — Concentrix, Apollo Hospitals, named Hyderabad NGOs — against current 2026 policies before publication]`

For the Zoom mock interview, dress this way. Not pajamas with a kurti over the top. Full kit. The practice is for the real thing.

**(e) Questions you will be asked that American prep does not warn you about.**

In Hyderabad you will hear: *"Are you comfortable with the night shift?"* (BPO.) *"Where do you stay in Hyderabad?"* (Standard, not invasive.) *"Can you start tomorrow?"* (BPO walk-ins.) *"What is your father's occupation?"* (Less common in 2026 but not extinct.) *"Are you comfortable with patients in distress?"* (Hospital.)

None of these need a rehearsed script. They need *not being surprised*. The first time you hear them should not be in the real interview.

### 6. What good feedback sounds like — and why most feedback is bad

The 15-minute feedback debrief is the unit's load-bearing event. You should know what useful feedback sounds like — both so you can absorb it well, and so you can ask for it when the volunteer is not giving it.

Researchers John Hattie and Helen Timperley wrote a famous paper in 2007 about what makes feedback work ([Hattie & Timperley 2007, "The Power of Feedback," *Review of Educational Research*](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.3102/003465430298487)). They found that useful feedback answers three questions for the learner:

- *Where am I going?* (What does a good answer look like?)
- *How am I going?* (What did I just do, specifically?)
- *Where to next?* (What is the one specific thing to try differently next time?)

Most feedback fails because it stops at the first or second question and never gets to the third. *"Your English is good"* — this is praise about you. It is not feedback. It tells you nothing to do differently. It has near-zero effect on your next interview.

*Good* feedback sounds like this:

> *"When you said 'I have completed 12th class' in Question 1, the recruiter would check your CV which says you are still in 12th. The wording does not match. Try: 'I am in my final year of 12th class, expected to complete in 2026.' Say that for me now."*

You say it. The volunteer says: *"Yes. That matches your CV and sounds confident."*

That is the full feedback move. Specific moment. What you did. What it sounded like. The alternative. The rehearsal of the alternative *now*, before the session ends.

If your volunteer says *"Your English is good"* and stops — ask three questions, in this order. Memorize them. They are the unlock for any feedback session.

1. *"What is one thing I should change about how I answered Question 1?"*
2. *"What is one word or phrase I used that sounded uncertain?"*
3. *"If you were the actual hiring manager, what would have made you not call me back?"*

The third question is the most powerful. Most volunteers will not raise it on their own — they do not want to discourage you. Asking it gives them permission to tell you the thing you most need to hear. You may not like the answer. You will be better at the next real interview because of it.

## Worked example — Priya's full mock interview

A complete walk-through, from preparation through feedback through revision. This is the unit's central example. Follow it closely. You will do a version of this yourself this week.

(Priya is the running learner-vignette — a composite, not a real student. Anjali, the volunteer in this example, is also composite.)

**Setup.** Priya has applied to a Foundever Hyderabad day-shift Customer Service Associate posting (the one she found in Unit 4). The mock interview is scheduled on Zoom for Saturday morning at 10:00 AM. The Humanitarians AI volunteer is Anjali — three years at Concentrix Hyderabad, trained on the mock-interview protocol last week.

**Pre-session (the week before).** Priya:

- Records three 90-second versions of *"Tell me about yourself"* on her phone. Listens back to each. Picks the version that sounds most like her. (Exercise 1 of this unit.)
- Writes draft answers in the situation-framework shape to: *Why do you want this job? What is your strength? What would you do if a customer was angry with you?* (Exercise 2.)
- Reads her own CV one more time. (Anjali will have it open during the session — anything on the CV is fair game for the interview.)
- The night before: sleeps. Eats breakfast in the morning. Puts on the kurti and churidar she would wear to a real walk-in interview.
- Five minutes before 10:00: hand on her collarbone. Three slow breaths. Out loud, once: *"This is practice, not the real thing."*

**The session — first three minutes.** Anjali opens with what the chapter has called a *control-raising* frame: *"Hi Priya. This is a practice session, not a test. We will do 15 minutes of mock interview, then 15 minutes of feedback. If something goes wrong, that is exactly what this session is for. Ready?"*

Priya nods. *"Yes, Ma'am. Thank you for your time."*

**Question 1 — Tell me about yourself.** Priya delivers a 90-second answer she rehearsed. Strong structure — name, residence context, education, interest in customer service. Two specific problems:

- She says *"I have completed 12th class."* Her CV says *"in 12th class, expected 2026."* The wording does not match.
- Her voice drops on the last sentence — *"...I am looking for an entry-level position."* The last word *position* trails off into a whisper.

**Question 2 — Why do you want this job?** Priya's answer: *"I want to work in BPO because it is a good opportunity to start my career."* This is the generic answer the chapter warned against. It could be said by anyone, to any posting.

**Question 3 — What is your strength?** Priya: *"My strength is hard work and punctuality."* Anjali asks her to give an example. Priya gives the residence-tutoring example from §3 — four months helping a younger girl with English. The example saves the answer.

**Question 4 — What would you do if a customer was angry with you?** Priya freezes. The seconds tick by. Seven, eight, ten — at twelve seconds, she finally says, in a small voice: *"I would... I would try to help the customer."* That is the entire answer.

Anjali nods. Moves to the next question.

**Question 5 — How is your day going?** Anjali asks this to give Priya a recovery moment after the freeze. Priya answers normally. The session closes at the 15-minute mark.

**Feedback debrief — 15 minutes, the part that matters.**

Anjali opens by asking *Priya*: *"What did you feel went well? What did you feel went badly?"*

Priya: *"I think Question 1 was okay. I think Question 4 was bad. I do not know what to say there."*

Anjali: *"Good. We will fix Question 4 together. First — let me go through what I noticed."*

She then gives feedback in four specific pieces. Notice the shape of each one. Specific moment. What happened. What it sounded like to her. The alternative. A rehearsal now.

**Feedback 1 — the 12th-class wording.**

*"When you said 'I have completed 12th class', the recruiter will check your CV. Your CV says you are in 12th class, expected to complete in 2026. The wording does not match. The recruiter will not believe the CV or the spoken answer — both lose. Try: 'I am in my final year of 12th class, expected to complete in 2026.' Say that for me now."*

Priya: *"I am in my final year of 12th class, expected to complete in 2026."*

Anjali: *"Good — now it matches your CV and it sounds confident."*

**Feedback 2 — the voice dropping.**

*"When you said 'I am looking for an entry-level position', your voice got smaller on the word 'position'. It made the whole sentence sound uncertain — like you were asking, not telling. Try ending the sentence with the same volume you started with. Like this — listen — 'I am looking for an entry-level position.' You hear how the last word is still strong? Try it now."*

Priya tries. First attempt — still trails off. *"I am looking for an entry-level position..."*

Anjali: *"Almost. The first part is good. The last word still got smaller. Try again — and put a little extra strength on the last word."*

Second attempt: *"I am looking for an entry-level position."* The last word lands.

Anjali: *"Yes. That is what to practice this week. Every sentence — the last word as strong as the first."*

**Feedback 3 — the generic Question 2 answer.**

*"Your answer to 'Why do you want this job?' was true — but generic. Every other candidate today will give the same answer. The interviewer wants to know why this job, at this company, for you specifically. From your CV I see you have spent six years at a Homes of Hope residence helping younger residents. Could you connect that to wanting to talk to customers? Try one sentence."*

Priya thinks. Then: *"I want to work in customer service because at the residence I learned how to listen to people who are upset and help them, and I think that is the same skill."*

Anjali: *"Yes. That is now an answer only you could give. Practice this version this week — replace your old answer with this one."*

**Feedback 4 — the freeze on Question 4.**

*"When you didn't know what to say, you went silent for 12 seconds. That is too long. The interviewer would start to worry. The three things you can do when you don't have an answer — do you remember from the chapter?"*

Priya recalls them. Clarifying question. Honest admission with redirect. Thinking-out-loud.

Anjali: *"Good. For 'What would you do if a customer was angry?' — you could use thinking-out-loud. Try this: 'Let me think for a moment. First, I would listen to the customer and let them finish what they want to say. Then I would say I am sorry they are having this problem. Then I would try to understand exactly what happened, so I can help.' That is a complete answer. It takes about thirty seconds. Say it for me now."*

Priya says it. Then says it again, slower. By the third try, it is hers.

**Closing.** Anjali summarizes the four things to work on this week. She sends Priya the Zoom recording within 24 hours, with a one-page written summary. Priya's homework: re-record the four answers, applying the corrections, by Wednesday. Watch the Zoom recording at least once between now and the next check-in.

**The unit-completion gate.** The session counts as complete because Priya scheduled it, attended it, received feedback, and committed to the revision. The session was not "passing" or "failing." There is no "passing" in the mock interview. There is only *done* and *not done*. Priya did it. She passes the unit.

This is important. The unit gates on whether you do the session — not on how well you perform in it. The mock interview can go badly and you still pass. What you cannot do is skip it. The TIKTOC is explicit and the chapter is explicit: this is the gated event of Course 2.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Treating the mock interview as the real interview.** Some girls show up to the mock interview already expecting to fail. They tense up. They under-perform. Then they decide they will also fail the real interview. The mock interview is *practice*. It is the place where mistakes are cheap. It is the worst this conversation will sound, because every interview after this one will be after this one. There is no scenario in which the mock is the high-water mark of your interview English. Every real interview improves on it.

**Mistake 2 — Trying to memorize the four answers word-for-word.** This is the perfectionism trap. Memorized answers sound memorized. The interviewer can hear the difference between an answer you have rehearsed *the shape of* and an answer you have *memorized verbatim*. Rehearse the structure — situation, action, result, lesson. Not the exact words. Different words each time is fine, as long as the shape holds.

**Mistake 3 — Long apologies after a mistake.** You will make a small mistake in the mock interview. A word will come out wrong. A sentence will trail. The instinct is to apologize — *"Sorry, Sir, I am sorry, my English is not good, Sir."* This is a register miss. One *apology* is professional. Five apologies are unprofessional. The professional move: *"Let me start that sentence again."* No apology needed. Just the re-start.

**Mistake 4 — Pasting the volunteer's feedback into ChatGPT and asking it to rewrite all your answers.** Tempting. Wrong. The volunteer's feedback was about *your* delivery, *your* CV, *your* situation. ChatGPT does not know you. It can polish the words but it cannot deliver them. Your job is to take the volunteer's specific feedback and rehearse the change yourself. Out loud. On video. In your own voice.

**Mistake 5 — Skipping the recorded review.** The Zoom session is recorded with your consent. You should watch it within 24 hours. Most girls do not. They feel embarrassed to see themselves. This is a mistake — the recorded review is where most of the learning actually consolidates. Watching yourself the next day, with the written feedback alongside, makes the corrections stick. ([Lynch 2007, *Language Teaching Research*](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1362168807077562) — the "noticing" effect from self-recording is well-documented in ESL pedagogy.) Embarrassment passes in three minutes. The learning lasts.

**Mistake 6 — Saying "naaku teliyadu" out loud when you don't know.** A small honest one. The instinct, when you do not know, is to say it in your strongest language. In Telugu, *naaku teliyadu* — direct, conventional, not a status-loss. In English business interview register, *I don't know* alone reads as too blunt. The chapter has given you the three replacement moves (§4). Use one of those instead. In Telugu you have one move. In Indian Business English you have three. That is not weakness in English — that is more options.

## Exercises

These are graded by Bloom level. Exercise 3 is the unit gate. You cannot complete this unit without it.

### Exercise 1 — Record a 90-second "Tell me about yourself" *(Bloom: Apply)*

On your phone. Three times.

1. Open your phone's voice recorder. Or your phone's video recorder if you prefer.
2. Record a 90-second answer to *"Tell me about yourself."* Use the situation framework — name, residence context, education, what you are looking for. Do not write it down first. Speak.
3. Stop. Listen back to yourself, all the way through. Do not stop in the middle because you do not like the sound. Listen to the whole thing.
4. Record version two. Try to fix one thing you noticed in version one.
5. Listen to version two.
6. Record version three. Pick the version you like best.

When you listen, listen for three things on three separate passes:

- **Pass 1: content.** Did you say what you meant to say? Did anything come out wrong?
- **Pass 2: pace.** Where were the long pauses? Where did you speed up?
- **Pass 3: pronunciation.** Which words did you say uncertainly? Mark them — you will work on those.

Three passes is the difference between *"my English is bad"* (useless) and *"the word at 0:34 sounded uncertain, the pause at 0:51 was too long"* (useful). The first passes the noticing; the second produces the correction. The ESL self-recording research is clear: the noticing is what produces the improvement.

### Exercise 2 — Write three answers in the situation framework *(Bloom: Apply)*

Use the four-part shape: situation, what you did, what happened, what you learned. About 90 seconds each (~ 180 words written).

a) *"Tell me about a time you helped someone."*

b) *"Tell me about a time you had to follow a strict routine or schedule."*

c) *"Tell me about a time you made a mistake and what you did about it."*

You can use ChatGPT or Gemini to check your draft — paste your answer and ask: *"Is this answer at the right register for an Indian Business English entry-level interview? Is the structure clear? Please flag anything that sounds generic."* Read the AI's response carefully. Apply only the changes that are actually true about you. (Unit 2 workflow.)

### Exercise 3 — The Zoom mock interview *(Bloom: Evaluate + Create — unit gate)*

**This is the unit completion gate. You must do this exercise to pass Unit 8.**

The session is 30 minutes total — 15 minutes of mock interview, 15 minutes of feedback.

**Before the session.**

- Schedule the session through the Humanitarians AI program coordinator. They will assign a volunteer from your sector.
- The volunteer will read your CV before the call. Anything on your CV is fair game for questions.
- Log in five minutes early. Camera on. Light in front of you, not behind you. Wear what you would wear to a real walk-in interview.
- Three slow breaths. Hand on your collarbone. Say once out loud: *"This is practice, not the real thing."*

**During the session.**

- Address the volunteer as *Sir* or *Ma'am* once at the start. Once or twice during. Not every sentence.
- Look at the camera (the small dot) when starting and ending each answer. Look naturally during the middle of the answer.
- If you do not know an answer — use one of the three moves from §4. Do not freeze in silence past five seconds. Say *"Let me think for a moment"* as your reset.
- If you make a small mistake — say *"Let me start that sentence again"* — no apology — and restart.

**During the feedback.**

- Listen first. Do not argue. Do not explain. Take notes if you want.
- At the end, ask the three feedback questions if the volunteer has not covered them:
  1. *"What is one thing I should change about how I answered Question 1?"*
  2. *"What is one word or phrase I used that sounded uncertain?"*
  3. *"If you were the actual hiring manager, what would have made you not call me back?"*

**After the session.**

- Within 24 hours: watch the Zoom recording at least once. Alongside the written summary.
- By the next check-in: re-record the four answers, applying the corrections. Send the new recordings to the program coordinator.

## For volunteers / sisters' notes

*A note for Humanitarians AI volunteers running this week's Zoom session, and for the Salesian sisters supporting the girls between sessions.*

**The 15-minute feedback debrief is the unit, not the 15-minute mock interview.** This is the load-bearing pedagogical claim. Treat the interview as the data-gathering event. Treat the debrief as the learning event. A volunteer who gives the interview a tight 15 minutes and the debrief a rushed 5 minutes has the proportions wrong. Equal time, minimum.

**Your job is not to fix her confidence. Your job is to raise her perceived control.** This is the Pekrun 2006 control-value frame ([Pekrun 2006, *Educational Psychology Review*](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-006-9029-9)). Interview anxiety in this population is a perceived-control problem, not a confidence problem. *"You should feel more confident"* is itself a low-control instruction — it tells her to feel something different without giving her a way to act differently. The control-raising moves are: predictable structure, rehearsed responses to predictable situations, the *"this is practice, not test"* reframe at the open, the *"let me think for a moment"* reset script.

**The feedback architecture is Hattie & Timperley 2007.** ([Hattie & Timperley 2007, *Review of Educational Research*](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.3102/003465430298487).) Three questions for every piece of feedback:

- *Feed Up — where is she going?* (What does a good version of this answer sound like?)
- *Feed Back — how is she going?* (What did she just do, specifically?)
- *Feed Forward — where to next?* (What is the one specific thing to try differently, and can she try it now?)

The single most-skipped move in untrained volunteer feedback is the *Feed Forward* rehearsal at the end — *"Try that sentence for me now."* Do not skip it. The rehearsal of the alternative, in the session, while she still has your attention, is where the learning consolidates. ([Wisniewski, Zierer & Hattie 2020, *Frontiers in Psychology*](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2019.03087/full) — meta-meta-analysis confirms task-level, process-level feedback with rehearsal carries the effect.)

**What good feedback sounds like — five-part move.**

1. Name a specific moment. *"When you said X..."*
2. Describe what happened. *"...your voice dropped at the end of the sentence."*
3. Name what it sounded like to the listener. *"...which made the sentence sound like a question."*
4. Give the alternative. *"Try ending with the same volume as the start — like this — listen — say it now."*
5. Have her try. Adjust. Have her try again until it lands.

**The two failure modes to watch in yourself.**

The over-kind volunteer says *"Your English is good!"* and gives no feedback. The over-harsh volunteer lists every flaw and gives no feed-forward. Neither produces learning. The middle move — *one or two specific things, with the alternative and the rehearsal* — is the move. If you can only give her two pieces of useful feedback in 15 minutes, that is enough. Two specific things, well-rehearsed, beats six things, half-explained.

**The sisters' role between sessions.** The girls will replay the Zoom recording at the residence. The sisters can support this — a quiet room, a phone with headphones, the written feedback summary alongside. The sisters do not need to give technical interview feedback themselves. Their role is the *holding* role — making the replay possible, noticing if a girl is avoiding it, gently asking if she has watched it yet. The control-raising work happens in the rehearsal. The rehearsal happens at the residence between sessions.
