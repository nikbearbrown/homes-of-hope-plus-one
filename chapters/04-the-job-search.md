# Unit 4 — The Job Search

*How to find the right posting and know if you qualify.*

---

## Learning outcomes

By the end of this unit, you will be able to:

- **(Analyze)** Find a relevant entry-level job posting in your target sector on Naukri or a company portal, identify the three most important requirements, and decide honestly whether you currently meet them — using AI as a posting interpreter.

---

## The problem this unit solves

Job postings on Naukri are written in a kind of English that makes the job sound bigger than it is.

You will read *"Customer Experience Champion — Driving Operational Excellence at a Fortune 500 Client — Excellent English Mandatory"* and you will decide you are not ready.

That same job, when the girl walks in on Monday, is *take inbound calls from US or UK customers about their internet bill, follow a script, escalate when you cannot resolve, hit 25 calls a day, work 8 hours, take the company-provided pickup home.* Same job. Different reading.

You cannot tell these two readings apart yet. You read *"Excellent English Mandatory"* and decide you are not qualified. You read *"Fortune 500 client"* and think you need to be impressive. You read *"Driving Operational Excellence"* and assume there is some driving-anything part of the job.

All three readings are wrong. And being wrong about any one of them means you do not apply for jobs you would have gotten, and you waste time applying for jobs you are not yet a fit for.

This unit teaches *posting literacy.* You will learn to read what a posting actually says — and what it leaves out. You will use AI as an interpreter (the Unit 2 workflow, applied to postings). You will run a simple two-of-three check. By the end of the unit, you will find a real posting on Naukri, read it honestly, and decide whether to apply.

## Core content

### 1. A posting has three layers — and only one of them matters for the apply/don't-apply decision

**Layer 1 — the marketing layer.** Job title, company brand language, *"we are looking for a passionate, driven, results-oriented..."* filler. Skip-read it. This layer is recruiter language for *"the recruiter wrote this in a hurry."* It tells you almost nothing.

**Layer 2 — the requirements layer.** A bulleted list, usually. Mixed required and preferred items. This is where the apply/don't-apply decision lives, and the layer you have to learn to read carefully.

**Layer 3 — the day-one reality.** What the job actually is once you have it. Almost never directly stated in the posting. Sometimes hinted at. Usually requires asking AI or asking someone-who-has-done-it.

Example: A Layer 2 line *"must be comfortable with US shift timings (5:30 PM – 2:30 AM IST)"* infers a Layer 3 reality of *"you will work night shift, you will commute home at 3 AM with the pickup-drop service, you will sleep 9 AM – 4 PM, your day-life will invert."* That is a different job than the posting describes. For you, with the residence rule against night work before age 21, this is a disqualifying constraint. For another girl, it might be fine.

The chapter's central move: **infer Layer 3 from Layer 2 before you apply.**

### 2. "Required" vs. "preferred" — what the words actually mean

Indian postings rarely use clean *"Required Qualifications / Preferred Qualifications"* headers. The signal is in the words.

These words mean **actually required**:

- *"Must,"* *"mandatory,"* *"essential,"* *"required."* The recruiter will not move forward without these. If you cannot demonstrate this, the application is filtered.

These words mean **not required**:

- *"Should,"* *"preferred,"* *"good to have,"* *"added advantage,"* *"desirable."* The recruiter may consider candidates without these. Apply if these are missing.

One special phrase that means different things in different roles:

- *"Excellent communication skills in English"* — this is the trick line. In a Hyderabad BPO entry-level voice process posting, it more commonly means *"can hold a 90-second customer interaction in clear English."* Not BBC. Not American. Just clear. The Hyderabad BPO sector has structural under-supply of entry-level voice agents. Recruiters mean *"freshers welcome"* when they say it. *"Excellent English"* often becomes *"good enough English to clear our 6-week onboarding training."* Many big BPOs (Concentrix, Foundever, Genpact) run paid pre-process training that takes candidates from basic English to voice-process-ready in 4–8 weeks. **The "mandatory" on the posting is the bar after training, not the bar before.**

One phrase that does mean what it says:

- *"0–1 years experience"* or *"freshers welcome"* — actually means it. Apply.

### 3. The two-of-three rule — and why it works

You will read *"excellent English mandatory"* and decide you are not ready. This is the most expensive mistake in your whole job search.

Why? Because women — including ESL women like you — consistently under-apply for jobs they are actually competitive for. The evidence:

The often-cited [Mohr 2014 piece in *Harvard Business Review*](https://hbr.org/2014/08/why-women-dont-apply-for-jobs-unless-theyre-100-qualified) reports that women apply when they meet roughly **100% of qualifications**, while men apply when they meet about **60%.** The original numbers came from an internal HP study. The exact percentages have been debated. The direction — that women self-filter at rates much higher than the data justifies — has replicated in many later studies, including [LinkedIn's 2019 Gender Insights Report](https://business.linkedin.com/talent-solutions/recruiting-tips/gender-insights-report).

For Hyderabad ESL freshers like you, the self-filtering is even sharper. You have lower baseline confidence in English. You have been told for years your English is not good enough. The posting reads *"excellent English mandatory"* and the trained instinct says *"not yet."* The data says: apply.

**The rule: if you meet *two of three* main requirements, you apply.**

This rule is operational. It is not a precise scientific finding. It is a defensible heuristic that corrects for the well-documented under-application bias. It works because:

- It pushes back against the self-filtering — a real, documented harm.
- It does not produce wildly unrealistic applications. One-of-three is too aggressive. Three-of-three is too conservative. Two-of-three is the middle.
- It is simple. A binary decision you can run on your phone in 30 seconds.

The asymmetry matters. The cost of applying and being filtered out is small — a non-callback, a small bruise. The cost of *not* applying for a job you would have gotten is large — no income, continued under-confidence, another month at the residence wondering when your life will start. **Apply.**

### 4. AI as posting interpreter — the four prompt patterns

You learned the AI workflow in Unit 2 — prompt, review, correct, ask back. In Unit 4, you use the same workflow on a different task: not drafting, but *interpreting* a posting.

Four prompt patterns. Use them in this order.

**Pattern 1 — Day-one reality.**

> *"Explain this job posting in simple English. What does this job actually involve day-to-day? [paste posting]"*

The AI translates the marketing language into plain English. You read. Does this sound like something you could do? Does it sound like something you want?

**Pattern 2 — Top three requirements.**

> *"What are the three most important requirements in this posting? Rank them by priority. [paste posting]"*

This forces the AI to pull out Layer 2. Now you can run your two-of-three check.

**Pattern 3 — Word explanation.**

> *"What does '[specific phrase]' mean in practice for a Hyderabad BPO job? Give me one example. [paste the phrase]"*

For unfamiliar BPO words — *voice process, blended process, semi-voice, KPI, AHT, FCR, OB, IB, escalation* — the AI gives plain-English definitions. **Look these up before you apply, not during the interview.**

**Pattern 4 — Forward prep.**

> *"What questions should I expect in the phone screen for this job? Give me five common ones in simple English."*

The AI produces a likely question list. Not always accurate, but useful prep. You will refine this with the volunteer in your Zoom session.

Caution: AI hallucinates on company-specific details. *"Concentrix offers a 5-day workweek"* may or may not be true today. AI knowledge is partial and confidently stated. **Treat AI's posting interpretation as a first draft — confirm anything specific with the company's actual website before the walk-in.**

### 5. The Hyderabad employer map

Five named employers. Three sectors. The five anchors of your job search.

**Concentrix (BPO).** One of the largest BPO employers in Hyderabad. Major operations across Cyberabad, Madhapur, and HITEC City. Entry roles include Customer Service Associate (voice — international or domestic). Hiring through walk-ins, Naukri, employee referrals. Pickup-drop facility for night shifts. Paid pre-process training that takes basic-English candidates to voice-ready in 6–8 weeks.

**Genpact (BPO/BPS).** Originally founded as a back-office for GE Capital in 1997; spun off in 2005. Big HITEC City presence. Tends to a higher minimum English requirement than Concentrix because of finance, insurance, and healthcare process work. Entry roles: Process Associate, Customer Service Associate, sometimes in F&A (Finance & Accounting).

**Apollo Hospitals (Healthcare).** Founded in Chennai in 1983. Strong Hyderabad presence — Jubilee Hills, Secunderabad, Hyderguda. Entry-level non-clinical roles: Front Desk Executive, Patient Care Coordinator, Insurance Coordinator, OPD Coordinator. Languages: English, Telugu, Hindi — usually in that order on Hyderabad postings. **A more realistic near-term match for your current English level than international BPO voice process** — because the actual patient interaction is often in Telugu or Hindi, with English on documents.

**CRY (Child Rights and You).** NGO founded in 1979 by Rippan Kapur. Has a Hyderabad regional office. Entry-level admin roles are sparse — graduates usually preferred. Track C-relevant. The more realistic Track C entry route may be smaller Hyderabad NGOs — MV Foundation, Mahita, Care & Share, Ashray, Vidya Bharati — where the entry conditions are more accessible.

**HelpAge India.** NGO founded 1978. Smaller Hyderabad presence than CRY. Hires admin and field-coordinator roles. Donor-facing work matters. English + comfort with senior citizens + regional language usually required.

These five are anchors, not the only options. Your actual job search will produce postings from many smaller firms. The five named here are well-documented and recognize their own conventions. **Search broader, anchor on these.**

Where to look:

- **Naukri.com mobile app** — your first stop. Dominant Indian job platform. ~80 million registered candidates [verify with current Info Edge figures]. One-tap apply once your CV is uploaded.
- **Apna.co** — mobile-first Indian platform aimed at entry-level. Growing in Hyderabad. Lower-stakes English than some Naukri postings.
- **LinkedIn India** — useful for slightly higher-tier postings. Profile setup takes longer; payoff is recruiter visibility.
- **Company career portals** — for Apollo, Concentrix, Genpact, CRY, HelpAge — direct portal applications often clean and bypass staffing agencies.
- **The sisters' network** — especially for Track C (NGO). This is, honestly, the highest-conversion channel for Track C entries. NGOs hire through word-of-mouth as much as through postings. The sisters know people. Use them.

### 6. The walk-in interview convention

Specific to India, especially Hyderabad and Bangalore. The posting says *"walk-in interviews Monday–Friday, 11 AM – 4 PM."* You physically go to the office during that window. You hand over a printed copy of your CV. You may be interviewed on the spot if there is capacity. **No appointment required.**

This is the dominant entry-level BPO hiring channel.

What to bring to a walk-in:

- Two printed copies of your CV (in case one gets passed around).
- An ID proof (Aadhaar).
- A pen.
- Time. Arrive in the *early* part of the window. Recruiters tire by mid-afternoon. Reach by 11:15 if the window opens at 11.
- Your "tell me about yourself" answer (Unit 8 will teach this).

The walk-in is the moment where everything you have built in Units 1–4 gets tested in 15 minutes. We will return to it in Units 7 and 8.

### 7. Watch for scams

This is not in the TIKTOC outline, but you need it. Naukri and WhatsApp-mediated job postings attract scam recruiters. Some red flags:

- **Any "training fee" or "registration fee" is a scam.** Real employers do not charge candidates to apply or train. Real BPOs run paid training — they pay you, not the other way around.
- **Any request for bank account details before a written job offer letter is a scam.** Salary discussion is fine. Bank account before written offer is fraud.
- **Any "interview" that happens entirely by WhatsApp text is suspicious.** Real interviews are voice or video. Text-only is a red flag for entry-level roles.
- **Any posting from a personal Gmail or WhatsApp number with no company domain anywhere is suspicious.** Real recruiters use company email at least some of the time.
- **Pressure tactics** — *"This offer is only valid for 24 hours, pay ₹500 to secure your slot"* — are scams. Real BPOs do not work this way.

If a posting feels wrong, ask the sisters. Ask the Humanitarians AI volunteer. Ask the AI: *"Is this BPO posting a scam? What red flags should I watch for? [paste posting]"*. The sisters will know fastest.

>

>

>

### Step 3 — Priya uses AI to interpret

**Prompt 1 (day-one reality):**

> *"Explain this job posting in simple English. What does this job actually involve day-to-day? [paste posting]"*

ChatGPT replies (illustrative):

> *This is an entry-level customer service job at Concentrix in Hyderabad. Day-to-day, you would answer phone calls from customers (mostly from the US or UK based on the shift timing) who have questions or problems about a product or service. You would try to solve their problems on the call. If you cannot solve it, you would pass it to someone more senior (called "escalating"). You would also write notes about each call in a computer system. The work is measured by speed (how long calls take), success rate (how many problems you solve on the first call), and customer satisfaction scores. The shift would be 5:30 PM to 2:30 AM IST, so you would work at night and sleep during the day. The company provides a vehicle to pick you up and drop you home for safety.*

She reads it slowly. Now she sees what the job actually is — not just what the posting calls it.

**Prompt 2 (top three requirements):**

> *"What are the three most important requirements in this posting? Rank them. [paste posting]"*

ChatGPT replies:

> *1. Intermediate (12th pass) — minimum qualification.*
> *2. Excellent English communication — you must hold a conversation with English-speaking customers (US/UK).*
> *3. Comfortable with night shift (5:30 PM – 2:30 AM IST) — because the customers are in US time zones.*

**Prompt 3 (word explanation):**

> *"What does 'voice process' mean in practice for a Hyderabad BPO job? Give me one example."*

ChatGPT replies:

> *Voice process means the customer interaction happens over phone calls (voice). The opposite is non-voice process, which means chat or email. Voice process needs spoken English because you talk to customers in real time. Non-voice process is for people whose written English is stronger than their spoken English. Example: At Concentrix Hyderabad, a voice process role might be answering calls for an American internet service provider, helping customers troubleshoot their connection.*

### Step 4 — Priya runs the two-of-three check

- **Requirement 1: Intermediate (12th pass).** Priya is in her second year of Intermediate, expected pass in 2026. She is *not yet* 12th pass. She is six months away. **Partial match — but she could apply with "Intermediate appearing" status, which some Hyderabad BPOs accept.**
- **Requirement 2: Excellent English communication.** Priya has 350 working English words. She can read a simple message. She has not yet held a 5-minute customer call in English with an American customer. **Probably does not match for international voice process.** The volunteer mock call in Unit 6A will give her a real read on this.
- **Requirement 3: Comfortable with night shift.** Priya cannot work night shift before age 21 (residence rule, plus her own safety). **Does not match.**

**Score: 0 of 3 cleanly. 1 of 3 generously.**

She does not apply to this specific posting. *And — importantly — this is the correct decision.* The two-of-three rule is not *always apply.* The rule is *apply if you meet two of three.* Sometimes that produces a don't-apply, and the don't-apply is also a win. She just saved herself from a posting that does not match her constraints.

### Step 5 — Priya searches differently

She refines her search: *customer service hyderabad fresher day shift.* Fewer results. They are matches for her constraint set.

She finds: *Customer Service Associate — Day Shift — Foundever Hyderabad — Domestic Process — Freshers Welcome.*

She runs the same three prompts. The day-one reality is: take inbound calls from Indian customers (mostly in English, sometimes in Hindi), help them with their account questions, document the interaction. Day shift, 10 AM – 7 PM. Domestic process. Salary range slightly lower than international, but the shift matches.

Top three requirements:

- 12th pass or Intermediate appearing — **match.**
- *"Clear English communication and willingness to learn"* — note that the wording is *clear* not *excellent*, and the phrase *willingness to learn* signals freshers are wanted. **Probable match — volunteer confirms in Zoom.**
- Day shift, 10 AM – 7 PM — **match.**

**Score: 2 of 3 cleanly, possibly 3 of 3.**

She applies.

She also takes one more step. She prompts ChatGPT:

> *"Is there anything about Foundever Hyderabad I should know before I walk in for the interview? What does the company do?"*

The AI gives her two paragraphs of background. (She notes — some of this might be slightly out of date or generic. She decides to also visit foundever.com on her phone to check.) Now she has a sentence to use if HR asks *"Why Foundever?"* in the screen.

She prepares a one-line answer in her voice: *"Foundever has a large Hyderabad presence, runs domestic customer service operations, and I am ready to start my career there with day-shift work."* Specific. True. Hers.

She prints two copies of her CV (from Unit 3). She finds the office address. She plans to reach by 11:15 AM Tuesday.

The walk-in is on. Notice — she also did not waste a day applying for the Concentrix night-shift role. The don't-apply on Posting 1 was the right move. Both decisions are wins.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Reading *"excellent English mandatory"* and stopping.** The single biggest pedagogical risk in this unit. *"Excellent English"* in Hyderabad BPO entry-level voice process usually means *"clear enough to clear our 6-week training."* It is not BBC English. It is not American English. It is *clear enough.* Apply if you meet two of three.

**Mistake 2 — Trying to be 100% qualified before you apply.** This is the well-documented self-filtering. Applications are *evidence-gathering,* not commitments. You can apply for ten jobs, get four callbacks, take none of them, learn from the four phone screens, apply for ten more. Each application is information. **The bar is not perfection. The bar is two of three.**

**Mistake 3 — Stuffing keywords from a posting into your CV without substance.** Naukri's parser flags candidates who use specific keywords. You may be tempted to add words from the posting to your CV even when they do not match what you have done. *Do not.* Honest keyword inclusion (words you can defend in the interview) is good. Dishonest keyword inclusion fails at the interview. **Match the keywords you can substantiate. Skip the ones you cannot.**

**Mistake 4 — Trusting AI's interpretation of a posting as final truth.** AI hallucinates on company-specific facts. *"Concentrix offers a 5-day workweek"* might be wrong. *"Foundever runs domestic process in Hyderabad"* might be partially true. **Always cross-check on the company's own website before you walk in.** AI gives you the first draft. The company's site gives you the meter. Same auto-rickshaw analogy from Unit 2 — first number, then the meter.

**Mistake 5 — Falling for scam postings.** Any *registration fee* is a scam. Any bank account request before a written offer is a scam. Any all-WhatsApp-text "interview" for an entry-level voice role is a scam. **Real BPOs pay you. They do not charge you.** When in doubt, ask the sisters.

## What you can do now

You can:

- Read a posting in three layers — marketing, requirements, day-one reality.
- Translate *"required"* vs. *"preferred"* vs. *"good to have"* into actual filtering rules.
- Use AI as a posting interpreter — day-one reality, top three requirements, word explanations, forward prep questions.
- Run the two-of-three rule honestly on yourself, producing both apply and don't-apply decisions as wins.
- Search Naukri with refined queries that match your real constraints.
- Identify scam postings before they cost you money or safety.
