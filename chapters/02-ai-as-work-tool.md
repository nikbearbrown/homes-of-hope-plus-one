# Unit 2 — AI as Work Tool

*How to use ChatGPT as an assistant without letting it think for you.*

---

## Learning outcomes

By the end of this unit, you will be able to:

- **(Apply)** Use ChatGPT or Gemini on your phone to draft a short message, check your own writing for errors, and ask a clarifying question — all in simple English.
- **(Evaluate)** Read an AI's output line by line and decide which parts are right, which parts are wrong about you, and which parts are generic filler. Then fix them.

---

## The problem this unit solves

You have probably already used ChatGPT once or twice. Maybe to translate a Telugu sentence. Maybe to ask what a word means. Many girls in Homes of Hope residences have done this on their phones.

Two opposite mistakes are common — and both lose you the job.

**Mistake one — *avoid AI.*** "AI is too complicated. I will write everything myself." The result: slower work, more errors, and lower confidence when applying for jobs. A girl who avoids AI is competing against girls who do not.

**Mistake two — *let AI think for you.*** "AI is smart. I will paste the job posting and copy whatever it writes." The result: a polished message that sounds like nothing about you. Generic filler. Sometimes invented facts — an internship you never did, a school you never attended. The hiring manager calls you in. He asks about the internship. You freeze. The interview ends.

The girl who wins is in the middle. She uses AI as an *assistant*. AI drafts. **She decides.** She reads every line. She fixes the lines that are wrong. She asks AI to explain anything she does not understand. Her English builds while she applies.

This is not a soft claim. It comes from a large research study (Bastani et al., *PNAS*, 2025 — see [the paper](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2422633122)). The study ran a real experiment with about 1,000 students. Some students got plain ChatGPT. Some got a version that refused to give them the answer — it could only give hints. The hint-only group learned **127% more** than the no-AI group. The plain-ChatGPT group scored well during practice — but on a later test *without* AI, they scored **17 percentage points lower** than the no-AI group. Same AI. Different way of using it. Opposite outcome.

For you, the lesson is exact. If you paste a posting into ChatGPT, copy what it writes, and send it — you become the second group. You get callbacks for jobs whose interviews you cannot pass. If you draft, then review, then correct, then ask the AI to explain anything you do not understand — you become the first group. Your English builds. The callbacks turn into offers.

This unit teaches you to be the first group.

## Opening case

Two girls apply for the same Customer Service Associate position at Concentrix Hyderabad. Both are 19. Both have basic English. Both use ChatGPT on their phones.

(Both girls here are composite vignettes. Priya, our running learner-vignette, is one of them — the second one.)

**Girl 1.** She opens ChatGPT. She types: *"Write a cover letter for this job: [paste posting]."* ChatGPT produces a fluent, polished, three-paragraph cover letter. She copies it. She pastes her name at the bottom. She sends it.

It includes this line: *"I gained valuable experience in resolving customer concerns with empathy and efficiency during my internship at Apollo Hospitals (June–August 2023)."*

She did not do an internship at Apollo Hospitals. She has never set foot at Apollo Hospitals. The AI made it up.

Two days later HR calls. They invite her for a walk-in interview. She comes in. The recruiter asks: *"Tell me about your internship at Apollo."* She freezes. The interview ends in two minutes. HR notes her name as someone who lied on her application.

**Girl 2 — Priya.** She opens ChatGPT. She types a longer prompt. It includes her real background — that she is 19, a fresher, no internships, lived at Homes of Hope Secunderabad for six years, speaks Telugu, Hindi, and basic English. She asks for a short message in Indian business English to apply for the Concentrix BPO job. ChatGPT produces a draft.

She reads it. Line by line. She catches three things:

- One line sounds generic — *"strong values of punctuality, discipline, and professional conduct."* It is true about her but it sounds like AI filler. She rewrites it in her own words: *"living in a residence with daily routine and shared responsibility taught me to be on time and to take my work seriously."*
- The closing line is missing her phone number. She adds it.
- She does not know what *"BPO industry"* means in exact terms. She asks ChatGPT: *"What does BPO mean in simple English?"* It explains. Now she has a real answer if HR asks her *"Why BPO?"* on the call.

She sends. HR calls. She comes in. The recruiter says *"Tell me about the residence."* Priya has actually lived this. The conversation goes for fifteen minutes. She walks out with a verbal offer.

Same job posting. Same AI. Same model under the hood. **The variable was the review step.** Girl 1 skipped it. Priya did not.

## Core content

### 1. What AI is good at — for your job search

You should use AI for these tasks. It does them well, fast, and free.

**Drafting.** AI can produce a first draft of a message, a cover note, or a CV section in two seconds. Drafting is the slowest part of writing. AI takes that part down to almost zero. You do the review.

**Register translation.** Paste your casual sentence. Ask: *"Make this sound professional in Indian business English."* The AI shifts the register. You learned in Unit 1 what register is. AI is reliable on this task — *if you tell it the register you want.*

**Vocabulary expansion.** You know 350 words. AI knows millions. Ask: *"Give me three professional ways to say 'I can do this work.'"* You will get three options. Pick one. You just expanded your vocabulary for one specific situation. After ten asks like this, your usable vocabulary grows.

**Spelling and basic grammar.** ESL learners often over-correct their own grammar — they put in too many words, leave out small words, freeze on tense. AI smooths this out. The output sounds natural without sounding stiff.

**Explaining words you do not understand.** This is the most important use for you. Paste any word from a job posting. Ask: *"What does this word mean in simple English? Give me one example."* The AI explains. You read. You understand. You are not asking the hiring manager what the word means — you already know.

This last use is what the research literature calls the *GPT Tutor* move. You are using the AI to teach you. You are not using it to think for you.

### 2. What AI gets wrong — and why

This is the part you must take seriously. AI sounds smooth even when it is wrong. The smoothness is not truth. It is style.

Here are the four ways AI is wrong, named so you can spot them. These are real-but-realistic examples — the kind of thing AI actually produces — not quotes from any specific transcript.

**(a) Fabricated facts about you.** The AI does not know your real history. If your prompt does not include your facts, the AI fills the gap with plausible-looking inventions. *"Internship at Apollo Hospitals, June–August 2023."* *"Completed a customer service certification from Bharath Institute."* *"Won the Inter-College debate competition in 2024."* Smooth. Specific. **All made up.** This is the most dangerous AI mistake because the AI presents the invention with full confidence.

Rule: **read every fact-claim in the AI's output. If it says you did something, ask yourself — did I do this?** If not, delete it.

**(b) Wrong facts about the company.** Ask the AI for a cover letter to Genpact. It will confidently describe Genpact's services. Some of what it says may match the 2026 Genpact reality. Some will not. The hiring manager works at Genpact. He knows what his company does. Wrong specifics signal that you did not bother to learn what they actually do.

Rule: **check the company facts against the company's own website or its posting.** Do not rely on AI for what a specific Hyderabad company does today.

**(c) Generic filler.** This is the most subtle mistake. The AI produces: *"I am a passionate and dedicated professional seeking opportunities to contribute meaningfully to your organization while continuing to grow."* The grammar is perfect. The register is consultative. The vocabulary is fine. **But the sentence says nothing.** The hiring manager has read this exact sentence 4,000 times. It signals nothing about *you*.

A clumsy specific sentence beats a polished generic sentence every time. *"I want to work at Apollo Hospitals because I helped at the residence's clinic visits for two years"* — this is awkward, repeats a word, is not even fully grammatical. The hiring manager reads it and learns something real about you. He picks up the phone.

Rule: **if a sentence could have been written about any girl in any city in any year — cut it. Replace it with one specific thing from your real life.**

**(d) Wrong register for Hyderabad.** AI defaults to American business English. Without instructions, ChatGPT produces *"Hey, hope this finds you well! Just wanted to circle back about the application — let me know if you have any questions and I'll loop in our team."*

This is fine for an American tech startup. It is the wrong register for a Hyderabad BPO. Unit 1's framework lets you catch this. You read *"Hey,"* and *"circle back"* and you know — wrong register.

Rule: **in your prompt, always include *"for a Hyderabad job, in Indian business English."*** That single phrase pulls the AI into your register. Without it, you get the US default. Do not skip it.

### 3. The two-step workflow — prompt → review → correct

This is the operational core of the unit. Five steps. Memorize the shape.

**Step 1 — Prompt with context.**

A bad prompt: *"Write a cover letter."* AI has nothing to work with. It fills the gap with generic filler and invented facts.

A good prompt has three parts: **what document you want, who you are, what the situation is.**

> *Write a short professional WhatsApp message in Indian business English to apply for this BPO customer service job at Concentrix Hyderabad. I am 19 years old, a fresher with no work experience. I grew up in a Homes of Hope residence in Secunderabad for six years, where I learned punctuality and community living. I have completed an English vocabulary training program. I speak Telugu, Hindi, and basic English. I have not done any internships. Keep the message under 120 words. Here is the posting: [paste posting].*

This prompt gives the AI your real facts. So the AI has less room to invent. It produces a specific draft, not a generic template.

**Step 2 — Read every line. Out loud if you can.**

This is the slow step. Do not skip it. Read each sentence and ask yourself two questions: *(a) Is this true about me? (b) Does it sound like me, or could it be any girl?*

If you cannot say what a sentence means — in Telugu in your head — you do not yet understand the sentence. Ask the AI: *"What does [paste line] mean in simple English?"* Get the meaning. Then decide if you want to keep the line.

**Step 3 — Mark the lines that need work.**

Mark three kinds of lines:

- **Wrong** — fabricated facts about you, wrong facts about the company, wrong register. Delete or correct.
- **Generic** — sentences that could be about any girl. Replace with one specific thing from your life.
- **Confusing** — words or phrases you do not understand. Ask AI to explain in simple English, then decide whether to use them.

**Step 4 — Correct each marked line.**

Two ways to fix. Either:

- **Rewrite in your own words.** *Preferred.* Even a clumsy sentence in your voice is better than a polished sentence in someone else's voice. Hiring managers can tell the difference. They pick the real one.
- **Ask the AI for a specific rewrite.** *"Replace the passionate-and-dedicated line with one specific thing from my background — I have six years at Homes of Hope Secunderabad."* The AI rewrites with your specifics. Read the new line. Decide if you like it.

The bias should be toward your own words. The AI fills gaps. You fill content.

**Step 5 — Ask the AI to explain anything you do not understand.**

This is the GPT Tutor move. The most important step you will skip if you are in a hurry.

> *"What does 'voice process' mean in practice? Give me one example."*
> *"What does 'AHT' mean? Why does it matter?"*
> *"What is the difference between an in-process and out-of-process call?"*

Your English builds while you apply. After ten of these questions, you have ten new working concepts. After a hundred, you have a working professional vocabulary the AI cannot give you. **The AI gave you the explanation. You did the learning.**

### 4. Prompt patterns you can re-use

You do not need to learn "prompt engineering" as a discipline. You need three slot-filler templates. Two pages of pattern. Done.

**(a) Draft template:**

> *"Write [type of document] in Indian business English for [job/context]. I am [your background in 2–3 simple sentences]. Keep it under [word count]. Here is [posting or other context]."*

**(b) Explain template:**

> *"What does [word or phrase] mean in simple English? Give me one example."*

**(c) Critique template:**

> *"Read this and tell me what sounds generic, wrong, or in the wrong register for a Hyderabad job: [paste your draft]."*

The third template is the AI helping you review your own writing. This is the closest you can get on a free-tier phone-based AI to the GPT Tutor architecture from the research study. The AI is critiquing — not generating. You are still the writer.

### 5. The Indian-context analogy that works

For you, the closest analogy is the auto-rickshaw quote.

When you ask an auto-rickshaw driver for a fare from Secunderabad to Madhapur, the first number he gives you is high. You do not say *no, you are wrong*. You do not refuse the auto. You say *the meter price is ₹180, I will pay ₹200*. You negotiate. You trust the meter. You do not trust the driver's first number.

AI is the same. The first output is the driver's first number. It is a starting point. It is not the meter.

The meter — for AI — is your real life, the real Hyderabad job market, and the real register your hiring manager uses. You negotiate the AI's first output against the meter. You keep what matches. You change what does not.

This analogy works. Keep it in your head. Every time you read an AI draft, ask yourself: *what is the meter here, and does this match the meter?*

## Worked example — Priya drafts a thank-you message after an interview

(Priya here is the running learner-vignette in this book — a composite, not a real student.)

Priya completed a walk-in interview at a Hyderabad BPO yesterday. The HR Executive — Ms. Reddy — gave her a card. She wants to send a thank-you message on WhatsApp this evening. She has never written one before. She decides to use AI as her drafting assistant.

### Step 1 — Priya's prompt

She types this into ChatGPT on her phone:

> *Write a short professional thank-you WhatsApp message in Indian business English. I am applying for a Customer Service Associate position at a Hyderabad BPO. I had a walk-in interview yesterday with Ms. Reddy, who is the HR Executive. The interview went well. She said I should hear back this week. I want to thank her for her time and say I am available for next steps. Keep it under 80 words.*

### Step 2 — AI draft

ChatGPT produces:

> *Dear Ms. Reddy,*
>
> *I would like to extend my sincere gratitude for the wonderful opportunity to interview with you yesterday for the Customer Service Associate position. I thoroughly enjoyed our conversation and am even more excited about the prospect of joining your dynamic team and contributing to the company's customer experience excellence.*
>
> *Please let me know if you require any additional information. I look forward to hearing back from you and am readily available for next steps.*
>
> *Best regards,*
> *Priya*

### Step 3 — Priya reads every line

She goes through it slowly. Here is what she finds.

- *"Dear Ms. Reddy,"* — fine. Consultative greeting.
- *"I would like to extend my sincere gratitude for the wonderful opportunity"* — too long. Sounds over-formal, like a court summons. She wants *"Thank you for your time yesterday."*
- *"the wonderful opportunity to interview with you"* — wordy. Cut.
- *"thoroughly enjoyed our conversation"* — borderline filler. She did enjoy it. But the sentence is generic. She can keep it short.
- *"even more excited about the prospect of joining your dynamic team and contributing to the company's customer experience excellence"* — **this is filler.** It could be about any company. It says nothing about Concentrix. Cut.
- *"Please let me know if you require any additional information"* — fine. Useful and professional.
- *"readily available for next steps"* — fine. *Readily* is a new word for her, but she just looked it up — means *willingly, easily*. She decides to keep it.
- The sign-off has no phone number. Add it.

### Step 4 — Priya rewrites

> *Good evening Ms. Reddy,*
>
> *Thank you for your time yesterday. I enjoyed our conversation about the Customer Service Associate role and I am keen to take the next step.*
>
> *Please let me know if you need any further information from my side. I am readily available for the next interview round.*
>
> *Best regards,*
> *Priya [last name]*
> *+91 [phone]*

Shorter. Specific. In her voice. The thank-you is at the front (the part the recruiter actually wants to hear). The filler is gone. The phone number is at the bottom.

### Step 5 — Priya asks AI to explain one word

She is not sure her use of *"keen"* is right. She types:

> *"What does 'keen' mean in simple English in a professional message? Give me one example."*

ChatGPT replies:

> *"Keen" means very interested or eager. In a professional message it shows you are excited about the opportunity in a polite, controlled way. Example: "I am keen to learn more about the role."*

Good. Her use matches. She sends the message.

The next morning, Ms. Reddy replies on WhatsApp: *"Thank you Priya. We will revert by Friday with next steps."*

**Notice what just happened.** Priya used AI to draft (slow part). She did the review (her actual work). She caught the filler. She kept the parts she could defend. She asked AI to teach her one new word. Her message is professional. Her vocabulary grew by one word — *keen*. She owns every sentence in the message.

That is the GPT Tutor move in self-directed form. Done on a phone. Free.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Copying the AI's first draft and sending it.** This is Girl 1 from the opening case. Smooth output sounds like quality. It is not quality. **Smoothness is not truth.** Always read every line.

**Mistake 2 — Not putting your real facts in the prompt.** A vague prompt produces a vague draft full of invented specifics. The AI fills the gap with plausible-looking guesses. The fix is to give the AI 2–3 sentences of your real background in every prompt. The prompt is longer. The output is much better.

**Mistake 3 — Trusting fluent English as proof of accuracy.** The hardest trap. Your draft sounds clumsy. The AI's sounds polished. You conclude AI is right and you are wrong. This is wrong. Your clumsy true sentence beats the AI's polished fabricated sentence. Hiring managers are reading for *truth*, not just for *polish*.

**Mistake 4 — Forgetting to specify Indian business English.** Without the phrase *"for a Hyderabad job, in Indian business English,"* the AI defaults to American business English. You get *"Hey, hope this finds you well, just wanted to circle back."* Wrong register. Add the phrase to every prompt.

**Mistake 5 — Using AI to fake fluency instead of build fluency.** This is the deepest trap. A girl who never uses ChatGPT to explain a word, and only uses it to produce output, gets callbacks but does not learn. Her English stays at 350 words. Her interviews fail. A girl who asks ChatGPT *"what does this word mean"* five times a day builds her vocabulary while she applies. After three months, the second girl is genuinely more hireable. **Use AI to teach yourself. Not to hide yourself.**

## Exercises

### Exercise 1 — Use AI to draft a thank-you message, then find one AI error *(Bloom: Apply + Evaluate)*

Pretend you completed a mock interview yesterday with a Humanitarians AI volunteer. Use the prompt template from §4.

**Steps:**

1. Write a clear prompt. Include who you are, what the interview was for, and what register you want.
2. Get the AI's draft.
3. Read it line by line. Find at least one thing that is *generic filler* and at least one thing that is *invented* (something the AI added that is not true about you).
4. Rewrite the message in your voice, keeping only what is true and specific.
5. Bring all four — the prompt, the AI draft, your marked-up version, the final message — to your Zoom session.

This four-version exercise is the unit's load-bearing practice. Do it slowly the first time. After three rounds, the workflow becomes automatic.

### Exercise 2 — Use AI to explain a word from a real job posting *(Bloom: Apply)*

Open Naukri.com on your phone. Search: *customer service hyderabad fresher*. Pick any posting. Find one word in the posting you do not understand fully. For Track A girls, look for words like *voice process, AHT, FCR, KPI, escalation, blended process.*

Ask ChatGPT (use the Explain template from §4):

> *"What does '[word]' mean in simple English? Give me one example from a Hyderabad BPO context."*

Read the AI's reply. Then write one sentence in your own words explaining the word back to yourself — in English, not Telugu. That sentence is the test that you understood. If you cannot write the sentence, ask the AI again.

Bring three words and three of your own-words sentences to the Zoom session.

### Exercise 3 — Give AI a bad instruction on purpose, then improve it *(Bloom: Analyze + Evaluate)*

This exercise teaches you what a bad prompt looks like — by showing you.

Type into ChatGPT: *"Write me a cover letter."* Just that. No context. No background. No specifics.

Look at what comes out. It will be generic. It will probably invent specifics about you. It may use the wrong register. **Save the output.**

Now write a better prompt for the same task — using the Draft template from §4. Get a new output.

Compare the two. Make a list of three things the second output got right that the first one got wrong.

This list is your evidence. The next time you are tempted to write a one-line prompt — remember the list. The AI is only as good as your prompt.

## What you can do now

You can:

- Use a three-part prompt (document type, who you are, the situation) to get a useful draft.
- Identify four kinds of AI mistakes — fabricated facts, wrong company facts, generic filler, wrong register — and fix them.
- Add the phrase *"for a Hyderabad job, in Indian business English"* to pull AI into your register.
- Ask AI to explain any word in a posting you do not understand.
- Read AI output critically — line by line, asking *is this true about me, does this sound like me*.
- Decide which AI lines to keep, which to rewrite in your voice, and which to delete.

You are now using AI as an assistant, not a replacement. **The output is yours.**
