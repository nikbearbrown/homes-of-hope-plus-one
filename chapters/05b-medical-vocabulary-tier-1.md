# Unit 5B — Medical Vocabulary Tier 1

*The 50 words every hospital front desk uses.*

---

## Learning outcomes

By the end of this unit, you will be able to:

- **(Remember + Apply)** Recognize and use the 50 most common medical and hospital-administration terms used at a Hyderabad hospital front desk — including *appointment, prescription, ward, insurance, referral, vital signs,* and 44 others — in spoken and written sentences a real front desk would produce.

---

## The problem this unit solves

You think *"medical English"* means anatomy, drug names, and disease names.

It does not. Not for you. Not for this job.

You are not training to be a nurse. You are training to sit at a front desk at Apollo, Yashoda, KIMS, AIG, Care, or Continental — and *register patients, book appointments, route them to the right department, take down insurance details, and call a supervisor when something is outside your authority*. That work runs on a smaller, more specific vocabulary. It is medical-administrative English. The words are common. The words are repeatable. The words are countable. About fifty of them carry most of an eight-hour shift.

This unit teaches those fifty.

A small honest flag at the top: **the final list of 50 needs validation by a working Hyderabad hospital receptionist or admin supervisor before this chapter publishes.** The 50 here are the chapter author's best candidate set, built from job postings at Apollo and Yashoda, from the NABH front-desk competency framework, and from Indian medical-ESL textbooks (Manjula 2010; Ramaiah 2014 `[verify edition]`). They may shift by three to five words after a domain reader sees them. Take the list as a strong starting deck, not as final word.

## Opening case

Priya walks into the OPD reception at Apollo Hospitals, Jubilee Hills, on a Monday morning at 9:15 AM. The senior associate at counter 1 — Lakshmi — has been at the desk for seven years. Priya is shadowing her for the day before her own interview on Friday.

(Priya here is the running learner-vignette — a composite, not a real student. The situation matches many girls in Homes of Hope residences preparing for Track B.)

The first patient walks up to Lakshmi.

> Lakshmi: *"Good morning, sir. Do you have an appointment today, or are you a walk-in?"*
> Patient: *"Appointment. 9:30 with Dr. Reddy. Cardiology."*
> Lakshmi: *"Thank you, sir. Could I have your UHID number, please?"*
> Patient: *"HD-2042197."*
> Lakshmi: *"Got it. I can see your appointment confirmed for cardiology OPD, follow-up consultation, Rs. 850. Do you have insurance, sir?"*
> Patient: *"Star Health. Card is here."*
> Lakshmi: *"Thank you. I will check cashless eligibility. Please proceed to the second floor — cardiology is to the right of the lift. The nurse will take your vital signs before Dr. Reddy sees you."*

Ninety seconds. Patient routed. Next patient already at the counter.

Priya counts the words Lakshmi just used. *Appointment. Walk-in. UHID. Cardiology. OPD. Follow-up. Consultation. Insurance. Cashless. Eligibility. Vital signs.* Eleven words from one of the standard front-desk word lists. Lakshmi did not pause once. The words came out as fast as the sentences.

That is the goal of this unit. Not new English. Not better English. **Fifty specific words, so automatic that they come out at sentence speed.**

Priya turns to Lakshmi between patients. *"How long did it take you to learn this?"* Lakshmi laughs. *"About two weeks for the words. About two months for the speed. The words you can put on flashcards. The speed is the job."*

This chapter gives you the flashcards. Unit 6B teaches the speed.

## Core content

### 1. Why these fifty — and not the fifty in your school textbook

You may have seen a list of medical English words at school. That list probably had *cardiology, oncology, neurology, gastroenterology, dermatology, ophthalmology*. A list of department names. Maybe disease names — *diabetes, hypertension, pneumonia, tuberculosis*. Maybe drug names — *paracetamol, ibuprofen, antibiotic*.

That list is for someone training to be a clinician. ("Clinician" — new word. It means *anyone who treats patients medically: doctor, nurse, physiotherapist, pharmacist*. You are not a clinician. You are the person who registers the patient before the clinician sees them.)

The right list for you is different. Most of these words come from three groups:

**Group 1 — scheduling words.** *Appointment. Reschedule. Confirm. Cancel. Follow-up. Slot. Walk-in. OPD. IP. OT.* You will use one of these in almost every patient interaction.

**Group 2 — registration and billing words.** *Registration. MRN. UHID. Insurance. Cashless. TPA. Pre-auth. Co-pay. Advance. Discharge.* You will use three or four of these for every admitted patient.

**Group 3 — wayfinding and department words.** *Cardiology. Radiology. Pharmacy. Ward. ICU. Casualty. Lab. Pediatrics. Orthopedics. Scan.* You will say one of these every time a patient asks where to go.

There is also a small fourth group — *clinical-but-not-clinical* — which is the vocabulary nurses and doctors use that the front desk also has to recognize. *Vital signs. BP. Sugar. Symptoms. Fever. Pain. Prescription. Medication. Report. Referral.* You will not produce a diagnosis using these. You will recognize them when a patient or a nurse says them.

And a fifth group — *administrative escalation words.* *Supervisor. Escalate. Authority. Identifier. Consent. History. Complaint. Attendant. Next of kin. Demographics.* The vocabulary of *who is responsible for what, and what gets passed up the chain*.

Fifty words. Five groups of ten. We will go through each group with a real front-desk sentence for every word.

This is a deliberate teaching choice. **Frequency-based vocabulary teaching outperforms topic-based for working adults under time pressure** (Nation, *Learning Vocabulary in Another Language*, 2nd ed., Cambridge UP, 2013). You learn the words you will use the most, in the order they will fire on the job. The rare clinical terms can wait. They are not in your first week.

### 2. The fifty — group by group

For each word: the word, what it means in plain English, and a real Hyderabad front-desk sentence using it. Where Telugu first-language speakers commonly mispronounce or misspell the word, the pitfall is named.

#### Group A — Appointments and scheduling (10)

1. **Appointment.** A scheduled visit. *"Your appointment with Dr. Reddy is at 11:30 AM."*

2. **Reschedule.** Change the appointment to a new date or time. *"Would you like to reschedule for tomorrow, sir?"*

3. **Confirm.** Check that the appointment is still on. *"Please confirm your appointment one day before."*

4. **Cancel.** Stop the appointment. *"Sir, you cancelled last week — should I book a fresh one?"*

5. **Follow-up.** A second visit on the same problem. *"This is a follow-up — the fee is reduced."*

6. **Slot.** A specific available time. *"The next slot is at 4:15 PM."*

7. **Walk-in.** A patient with no appointment. *"Walk-in patients are seen after appointment patients."*

8. **OPD.** *Outpatient department* — the area for patients who are not admitted. Said as letters: *oh-pee-dee*. *"Cardiology OPD is on the second floor."*

9. **IP / inpatient.** A patient who has been admitted to a room. *"Once admitted, the patient becomes an IP."*

10. **OT.** *Operating theatre.* The room where surgery happens. *"OT is on the third floor."* (Be careful — *OT* is also a hospital word for *occupational therapy*. Front desk usually means *operating theatre*.)

#### Group B — Patient registration (10)

11. **Registration.** Entering the patient's details into the system on first visit. *"First-time patients please register at counter 3."*

12. **MRN.** *Medical record number.* The patient's unique number in the hospital's system. *"Your MRN is on the top of your previous prescription, sir."*

13. **UHID.** *Unique health identification.* Same idea as MRN. Many Indian hospitals use *UHID*; some use *MRN*. *"Could you give me your UHID, please?"*

14. **Record / records.** The patient's history with this hospital. *"Your records are with us from last year."*

15. **Demographics.** Name, age, sex, phone, address — the basic identity fields. *"I need to update your demographics — current phone, current address."*

16. **Attendant.** The family member who comes with the patient. *"Only one attendant per patient in ICU, sir."*

17. **Next of kin.** The emergency contact relative. *"Please give me a phone number for next of kin."*

18. **Consent.** The patient's signed permission for a procedure. *"The doctor will explain, then you will sign the consent form."*

19. **History.** *Medical history* — past illnesses, treatments. *"The doctor will ask about your history."*

20. **Complaint.** The patient's stated reason for the visit — not a complaint about service. *"Chief complaint — chest pain since two days."*

#### Group C — Insurance and payment (10)

21. **Insurance.** A policy that pays part or all of the hospital bill. *"Do you have insurance, sir? Please bring your card."*

22. **Cashless.** The insurance company pays the hospital directly. The patient pays nothing or a small share. *"This procedure is cashless under your policy."*

23. **Reimbursement.** The patient pays first; the insurance company refunds later. *"For reimbursement, we will give you the original bills."*

24. **TPA.** *Third-party administrator.* The company that processes the claim between the insurer and the hospital. *"The TPA will take 30 minutes to confirm."*

25. **Pre-authorization / pre-auth.** TPA approval before a procedure. *"Pre-auth has come — you can proceed for admission."*

26. **Co-pay.** The patient's own share of the bill. *"Your co-pay is 10% — about Rs. 4,000."*

27. **Advance.** The deposit paid at admission. *"Please pay the admission advance at counter 5."*

28. **Deposit.** Often used in place of *advance*. Same thing in most Hyderabad hospitals.

29. **Bill.** The total cost statement. *"The final bill will be ready by 11 AM tomorrow."*

30. **Discharge.** Sending the patient home from admission. *"Discharge will happen once the doctor signs off."* The summary document is the *discharge summary*. **Pronunciation pitfall:** the *ge* at the end is a soft *j* sound — *dis-charj* — not a hard *g*.

#### Group D — Clinical-but-non-clinical (10)

31. **Vital signs.** Blood pressure, pulse, temperature, oxygen — the basic measurements the nurse takes before the doctor sees the patient. *"The nurse will take your vital signs first."*

32. **BP.** *Blood pressure.* Said as letters: *bee-pee*. *"What is the patient's BP today?"*

33. **Sugar.** Informal for *blood glucose.* Used in normal Indian-English hospital talk. *"What is your fasting sugar, sir?"*

34. **Symptoms.** What the patient feels — fever, pain, weakness. *"How many days have these symptoms been there?"*

35. **Fever.** Higher than normal body temperature. *"Is the fever continuous or coming and going?"*

36. **Pain.** What hurts. *"Where exactly is the pain, sir?"*

37. **Scan.** A general word for *MRI, CT, or ultrasound.* *"The doctor has advised a scan."*

38. **Lab.** *Laboratory* — where blood tests, urine tests, and other samples are processed. *"Lab is on the ground floor — follow the green arrows."*

39. **Report.** The result document for a test or scan. *"The report will be ready by 6 PM."*

40. **Medication.** What the pharmacy gives the patient. *"Take your medication as written on the prescription."*

#### Group E — Departments, administration, and escalation (10)

41. **Referral.** A note from one doctor to another doctor. *"I have a referral note from Dr. Sharma."*

42. **Prescription.** The doctor's written instruction for medication. **Different from medication.** The prescription is the paper; the medication is the medicine. *"Please collect your prescription before going to the pharmacy."* **Pronunciation pitfall:** the *pr* at the start is one quick cluster — *pre-scrip-shun* — not *pi-rescription*.

43. **Ward.** A room in the IPD where admitted patients stay. *"The patient is in ward 305."*

44. **ICU.** *Intensive care unit.* Critical patients only. Visitor rules are stricter. *"Only one attendant in ICU, sir."*

45. **Casualty.** The 24-hour emergency walk-in section. Apollo and newer chains often say *emergency* instead. Older chains say *casualty.* *"Casualty is on the ground floor, near the main entrance."*

46. **Pharmacy.** The hospital shop where medication is dispensed. *"Pharmacy is open till 9 PM."*

47. **Radiology.** The department that does X-ray, MRI, CT scans. *"Radiology is on the first floor — please take this requisition."* **Pronunciation pitfall:** *ray-dee-OL-o-jee* — stress on the third syllable.

48. **Cardiology.** The heart-specialty department. *"Cardiology is on the second floor."* **Spelling pitfall:** *cardi-OL-ogy*, not *cardi-AL-ogy*. Five syllables. The *gy* at the end is a soft *jee* sound — *cardi-ol-o-jee*.

49. **Escalate.** Pass the problem up to your supervisor. An internal verb — you will say it at the desk to a colleague, not to a patient. *"I'll escalate this to the floor supervisor."*

50. **Supervisor.** The senior staff member you escalate to. *"Let me call the duty supervisor, sir, please give me one minute."*

That is the fifty.

### 3. The Anki deck — how you will actually learn these

You will not learn these by reading the list once. You will learn them with *spaced repetition* — short, repeated reviews over two weeks. Anki is the standard tool. (You met Anki briefly in Unit 2 — the AI workflow. Now we use it for vocabulary.)

The deck design is specific. Here is what each card looks like:

**Front of the card** — the English word, one Hyderabad-context sentence, and an audio prompt.

> *Cardiology.*
> *"Cardiology OPD is on the second floor."*
> [audio button — Indian English pronunciation]

**Back of the card** — a short plain-English definition, the Telugu equivalent or paraphrase where one exists, and the standard front-desk phrase.

> *The hospital department that treats heart problems.*
> *Telugu: గుండె సంబంధిత విభాగం (gunde sambandhita vibhagam).*
> *Standard usage: "Cardiology is on the [N]th floor."*

A few rules that make the deck work:

- **Audio is non-negotiable.** Indian-English pronunciation, not American or British. Without audio, you will pronounce these words the way you have heard them inconsistently across schools and TV — which is sometimes wrong for the job. A Humanitarians AI volunteer or a working Hyderabad hospital staff member can record the fifty in 20 minutes.
- **One card front per word.** No "list of cards." One word, one card, one sentence, one audio.
- **Review daily for 14 days, then let Anki space them out.** The FSRS algorithm Anki uses (Ye et al. 2024, *npj Science of Learning*) decides the spacing for you. Trust it. Do not skip days.
- **Test target: by Day 14, hear the audio and say the word, the definition, and a sentence in under 10 seconds.** That is the recognition-plus-production threshold the volunteer will check at the Week 4 Zoom session.

### 4. What NABH expects — and why this list is not arbitrary

The [National Accreditation Board for Hospitals (NABH)](https://www.nabh.co/) is the Indian body that accredits hospitals. Most major Hyderabad chains are NABH-accredited. NABH's competency framework for non-clinical front-desk staff explicitly requires that you be able to:

- Identify the patient using two identifiers — name plus date of birth, or name plus UHID. (Two of the 50 above: *identifier* / *UHID*.)
- Direct the patient to the right service area. (*Cardiology, radiology, pharmacy, lab, ICU, ward* — six of the 50.)
- Maintain patient confidentiality. (*Consent, demographics, records* — three of the 50.)
- Escalate complaints through documented channels. (*Escalate, supervisor, complaint* — three of the 50.)

This list is not the chapter author's invention. It is the operational vocabulary NABH expects you to recognize and use correctly. When you learn the fifty, you are not memorizing for a test. You are preparing for the certification standard your future employer is also held to.

### 5. Telugu-to-English pronunciation pitfalls — the five patterns

Some English medical words are hard for Telugu first-language speakers in specific predictable ways. Naming the pattern reduces the freeze. You are not making errors. You are encountering known patterns.

**Pattern 1 — Soft *j* at word endings.** *Discharge, cardiology, radiology, pharmacology.* The final *ge / gy* is a soft *j* sound, not a hard *g*. Audio practice fixes this faster than spelling rules.

**Pattern 2 — Initial consonant clusters.** *Prescription, pediatrics, psychiatry.* The *pr / pe / ps* at the start is one quick sound, not two with a vowel in the middle.

**Pattern 3 — Stress shift in long words.** *Registration, medication, administration.* The stress lands one syllable before the *-tion* — *re-gi-STRA-tion*, *me-di-CA-tion*. Wrong stress is the single most common register signal that someone has read the word but not heard it said.

**Pattern 4 — Silent letters.** *Pneumonia* (silent *p*), *psychiatry* (silent *p*). You will not say these often at Tier 1, but you will read them on referral notes. Know the silence.

**Pattern 5 — Vowel collapse in long medical words.** *Pre-authorization* — five syllables in slow speech, often collapsed to four (*pre-AW-thri-zay-shun*) or replaced with the short form *pre-auth*. The short form is professional Indian-English standard. Use it. The long form should be recognizable.

These five patterns cover most of the pronunciation difficulties in the 50. The audio prompts on the Anki cards drill them.

### 6. The hospital-vs.-clinic distinction — which fifty will you use most?

The fifty are calibrated for a *hospital* front desk — Apollo, Yashoda, KIMS, AIG, Care, Continental. These are multi-specialty hospitals with admissions, surgery, insurance desks, and 24-hour casualty.

A *clinic* is smaller — usually one or two specialties, mostly OPD, often a single doctor. At a clinic, you will use about 30 of the 50 daily. The insurance-heavy words (*cashless, pre-auth, TPA*) are rarer because most clinic patients pay direct cash. The administrative words (*ICU, casualty, ward*) are mostly absent.

If your first job is at a clinic, the same deck still applies. You will use a smaller subset. The remaining 20 words will still be there when you move to a hospital later.

## Worked example — counter 1 at Apollo Jubilee Hills, 9:15 AM

A real ninety-second interaction. The fifty in motion. The numbers in brackets show which words from the list are used.

> *"Good morning, sir. Do you have an appointment [1] today, or are you a walk-in [7]?"*
> Patient: *"Appointment, 11 AM with Dr. Reddy."*
> *"Could I have your UHID [13] number or registered phone number, please?"*
> Patient: *"UHID is HD-2042197."*
> *"Thank you. I can see your appointment confirmed [3] for cardiology [48] OPD [8]. Your fee is Rs. 850 for follow-up [5]. Do you have insurance [21], or will you be paying directly?"*
> Patient: *"Insurance — Star Health."*

Thirteen words from the fifty in 90 seconds. *Appointment, walk-in, UHID, confirmed, cardiology, OPD, follow-up, insurance, cashless, vital signs* — plus *registered, fee,* and *lift* which sit just outside the fifty as everyday English. This is a normal cardiology OPD check-in. Every word on the cards is on the deck because it fires in real shifts like this one.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Trying to learn fifty words in one sitting.** The deck breaks if you try. Twenty minutes a day for two weeks beats two hours on a Saturday. Spaced repetition only works with spacing. Skip days and the schedule drifts; the words start fading.

**Mistake 2 — Skipping the audio.** Reading the words silently and assuming you can pronounce them. You cannot, not yet, not for the medical-specific words. The interviewer's first signal is whether you can say *cardiology* and *radiology* without hesitation. Audio practice is the difference.

**Mistake 3 — Confusing *medication* with *prescription*.** The prescription is the paper the doctor writes. The medication is the medicine the pharmacy hands over. The two are not interchangeable. Patients sometimes mix them up. You should not. *"Please collect your prescription before the pharmacy."* *"Take your medication twice a day."* Different words for different objects.

**Mistake 4 — Using *casualty* when you should say *emergency* (or vice versa).** Both words are correct in Indian English. Different hospitals prefer different ones. Apollo and the newer chains lean toward *emergency*. Older institutional hospitals lean toward *casualty*. On your interview day, listen to which word the interviewer uses. Match it. If you do not know, use *casualty* — it is the more common Indian Standard Business English term.

**Mistake 5 — Treating the *escalate* word as failure.** *"If I have to escalate, I look like I cannot handle the job."* This is the wrong reading. *Escalate* is the professional move — it shows you know the limit of your authority. Hiring managers actively reward candidates who can name *when to escalate*. Memorize the word. Use it confidently. Practice the sentence: *"Sir, I will escalate this to my supervisor — please give me one minute."* That is the sentence that prevents you from being responsible for a clinical question you cannot answer.

## Exercises

These are graded by Bloom level. The fourth exercise is the load-bearing one — the volunteer Zoom session will check it.

### Exercise 1 — Build your Anki deck *(Bloom: Apply)*

Open Anki on your phone. Create a new deck called *"HOH+1 Medical Tier 1 — 50 words."* For each of the fifty words in §2:

a) The front of the card = the English word + the Hyderabad-context sentence given.

b) The back of the card = a short plain-English definition + (where useful) Telugu equivalent + the standard front-desk phrase the word lives in.

Spend ten minutes a day building the deck across three days. Do not try to do all fifty in one sitting; you will tire and the cards will get rushed.

When you finish, ask a Humanitarians AI volunteer or a Salesian sister to record the audio for all fifty words in Indian English. This takes about 25 minutes. Attach the audio to each card. Without audio, the deck loses about 40% of its value.

### Exercise 2 — Recognize the word in a sentence *(Bloom: Remember + Understand)*

Below are eight real front-desk sentences. For each one, name which two words from the fifty appear. Write your answers on paper before checking against the list in §2.

a) *"Sir, your pre-auth has come, you can proceed for admission."*

b) *"The patient is in ward 305 — please ask the floor sister."*

c) *"For reimbursement we will give you the original bills."*

d) *"The doctor has advised a scan; radiology is on the first floor."*

e) *"Please pay the admission advance at counter 5."*

f) *"Walk-in patients are seen after appointment patients."*

g) *"The nurse will take your vital signs before the doctor sees you."*

h) *"I'll escalate this to my supervisor — please give me one minute."*

### Exercise 3 — Distinguish two confusable pairs *(Bloom: Analyze)*

For each pair below, write two short sentences — one using each word — that make the difference clear.

a) *Prescription / medication.*

b) *Inpatient / outpatient (IP / OPD).*

c) *Casualty / emergency.*

d) *Cashless / reimbursement.*

e) *Attendant / next of kin.*

### Exercise 4 — Use the deck in a real sentence under time pressure *(Bloom: Create + Apply)*

This is the load-bearing exercise. You will use it in your Zoom session.

**Situation:** A patient walks up to your imagined front-desk counter. You greet, ask for an identifier, find the appointment, mention the department, mention insurance, mention wayfinding. Try to do it in 60–90 seconds and in the consultative register from Unit 1.

**Steps:**

1. Write out a 90-second script for yourself, in English, using at least eight of the fifty words. Use the worked example above as a structural model — but write *your* version, not Lakshmi's.

2. Read your script aloud and record yourself on your phone. Listen back. Mark any place you hesitated on a word — that is a word that needs more deck practice.

3. Paste your script into ChatGPT or Gemini with this prompt: *"This is a hospital front-desk script for a Hyderabad reception role. Please check whether the medical vocabulary is used correctly and the register is consultative Indian Standard Business English. If anything is off, please flag it and suggest a fix in simple English."*

4. Read the AI's feedback critically. Do you agree? Did the AI shift any word toward American register (*"front office"* for *"front desk"*)? Mark anything you want to keep your way.

5. Produce a final version. Bring it to your Zoom session. The volunteer will read it and run a quick role-play with you as the receptionist.

The drill is not the script. The drill is **the words coming out at sentence speed under mild pressure.** That is what the job actually requires.

### Exercise 5 — The five pronunciation pitfalls *(Bloom: Apply + Self-evaluate)*

Read the following words aloud, recording yourself:

*Cardiology. Radiology. Discharge. Prescription. Pre-authorization. Pneumonia. Registration. Medication.*

Now listen to your recording. For each word, check:

- Did the final *ge / gy* sound soft (*j*) or hard (*g*)?
- Did the stress land on the right syllable?
- Did the initial cluster come out as one quick sound?

Replay the Anki audio for each word. Say the word three more times along with the audio. Re-record. Compare.

This is a small, private drill. You will not be graded on it. The interviewer at Apollo will not say *"your stress on cardiology was wrong."* But she will hear it. The drill prevents the hearing.

## What you can do now

You can:

- Recognize the fifty most common hospital front-desk words in spoken and written sentences.
- Use ten of them in a basic registration script of your own.
- Distinguish the easy-to-confuse pairs (*prescription / medication, IP / OPD, casualty / emergency, cashless / reimbursement, attendant / next of kin*).
- Spell and pronounce the harder words (*cardiology, radiology, discharge, prescription, pre-authorization*) at a level a hiring manager will accept.
- Build and review a spaced-repetition deck of the fifty in Anki.
- Use AI to check your usage and your register.

That is enough to walk into a shadowing day at Apollo, Yashoda, KIMS, AIG, Care, or Continental and not be lost on the vocabulary. The speed and the script are the next two units.
