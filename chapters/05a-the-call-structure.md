# Unit 5A — The Call Structure

*A BPO call is a song with seven verses. Learn the verses and you can sing it.*

---

## Learning outcomes

By the end of this unit, you will be able to:

- **(Apply)** Complete a mock customer-service call from opening to close using the seven-move BPO script structure, staying in English the whole time, given a scripted customer scenario.

---

## The problem this unit solves

You think a customer-service call is a conversation. It is not.

A conversation is two people making up the words as they go. You cannot do that yet in English. Not for eight hours. Not with a stranger on the line. Not with a clock counting your minutes.

A BPO call is a *song*. The kind of song where you know the verses. You know which verse comes after which. You know the chorus. Even when you are tired, the next line is already in your mouth, because you have sung this song a hundred times before.

That is the difference this unit teaches.

You will learn seven verses. They are the seven *moves* every customer-service call passes through, from the moment you pick up the phone to the moment you put it down. Every BPO in Hyderabad — Concentrix in Madhapur, Genpact in HITEC City, Foundever, Teleperformance, HGS — trains agents on a version of these seven moves. The names change. The order does not.

Once you know the seven moves, two things change for you. First, you always know what to say next. The next move is *the next move*, not something you have to invent. Second, your nervous system relaxes — because there is no more unknown. The structure is the friend that stands behind you.

This unit is the first of three units that prepare you for an actual BPO job. Unit 5A teaches the basic call. Unit 6A teaches the call when the customer is angry. Unit 7A teaches the phone screen — the interview call where the company decides if they will hire you. Each unit builds on the one before. This one is the foundation.

## Opening case

Two girls do their first mock customer-service call on the same Saturday morning. Both have completed Units 1–4. Both are applying to BPO jobs in Hyderabad.

(One of them is Priya — the running learner-vignette in this book, a composite, not a real student.)

**Girl 1.** The phone rings on Zoom. She picks up. *"Hello? Yes, customer care, what is the problem?"*

The customer — played by a volunteer — says: *"Hi, my electricity bill is too high this month."*

Girl 1: *"Okay sir, please tell me the problem. Bill is high? How much sir? When did you get the bill? You have your service number? Please tell me. Actually I have to check. One minute sir."*

She places the customer on hold without asking. Two minutes later she comes back. *"Hello sir? Bill is showing 4,800. Maybe meter is wrong. Maybe you used AC. Please check at home first sir. If still problem call again."*

The call ends in four minutes. The customer was not rude. The customer also did not feel helped. The volunteer's feedback later: *"You were trying. The English was correct. But the call had no shape. The customer did not know who you were, what you were doing, or what would happen next. At any moment in that call, the customer was confused."*

**Girl 2 — Priya.** The phone rings on Zoom. She picks up. *"Thank you for calling Customer Care, this is Priya, how may I help you today, Sir?"*

The customer: *"Hi, my electricity bill is too high this month."*

Priya: *"I am sorry to hear that, Sir. I will be happy to check this for you. May I have your service number and registered mobile number, please?"*

The customer gives the numbers. Priya repeats them back. Then she paraphrases the complaint — *"So if I have understood correctly, you are saying that your March bill is around 4,800 rupees, but your usual bill is around 1,200 rupees. Is that correct?"*

The customer says yes. Priya asks for permission to put him on a short hold while she checks the account. She comes back in 90 seconds with what she found. She explains what she will do — raise a meter-reading verification request. She gives a specific next step with a time frame. She confirms the customer understood. She closes with a branded sign-off.

Total call time: 4 minutes 20 seconds. The customer walks away knowing what happened, what is happening next, and when. The volunteer's feedback: *"You sounded like an agent on her fourth month. Not her first day."*

Both girls have the same English. The difference was not vocabulary. The difference was *structure*. Priya knew the seven moves. Girl 1 did not.

This unit teaches the seven moves.

## Core content

### 1. Why structure is the only thing that holds a call together

You are 19. You speak Telugu, Hindi, and basic English. You have not yet had a single paid voice-process call. You are about to take 50 to 80 calls a day for eight hours, from strangers who already feel inconvenienced before they called.

That is a lot of cognitive load. *Cognitive load* (new phrase — used in this book) means: **the amount of thinking work your brain is doing at one moment.** When the cognitive load is too high, something breaks.

For an L2 speaker — that is you, learning a second language — the thing that usually breaks first is the English itself. Under pressure, your brain reaches for the words that come fastest. The fastest words are Telugu words. You switch to Telugu mid-sentence without noticing. The recording flags it. The QA team marks it. The supervisor calls you in.

This is not your fault. It is how every bilingual brain works (Costa & Santesteban, *Journal of Memory and Language*, 2004 — [link](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0749596X04000245)). The fix is not *be braver*. The fix is *reduce the cognitive load so your English does not have to fight for room*.

Structure reduces the cognitive load. When you know the seven moves of a call, you do not have to think *what do I say next?* — that question is the heaviest one. You only have to fill the next slot in the structure with the right English phrase. The slot tells you what kind of phrase you need. The phrase you have already practiced.

A researcher named Alison Wray, in her 2002 book *Formulaic Language and the Lexicon* (Cambridge University Press — [publisher page](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/formulaic-language-and-the-lexicon/16D1F69F44A1DDE07A28D62A6B9B2A8E)), made this point clearly. Most native English speakers, when they sound fluent at work, are not making up new sentences. They are reaching for fixed phrases — *"thank you for that information,"* *"could you please give me a moment,"* *"I am going to check that for you"* — that they have used many times before. The phrases come out as one chunk, not word by word. That is how fluency works in real workplaces.

You can do the same thing. You do not need 5,000 English words. You need about 30 *fixed phrases* placed in the right slots. That is the entire structural English of an entry-level BPO call.

(*Telugu scaffolding.* In Telugu you already do this. When the senior sister visits the residence and asks *"ela undi, amma?"* you do not invent a new answer each time. You reach for *"baagunnaanu sister-gaaru, mee aashirvaadam"* — a fixed polite phrase. The chunk comes out without thought. English in your BPO job will work the same way once you have the chunks. The chunks are the work of this unit.)

### 2. The seven moves of a BPO call

Here are the seven moves, in order. Read them once. We will go deep on each one in the next sections.

1. **Opening** — you greet, name the company, name yourself, ask how you can help. About 5 seconds.
2. **Verification** — you check that the caller is who he says he is. Service number, mobile number, sometimes one more check.
3. **Discovery** — the customer explains the problem. You listen, take notes, paraphrase back so the customer knows you understood.
4. **Hold or transfer** — if you need to check something on your system, you ask permission and place the caller on a short hold. If you need to send him to someone else, you transfer.
5. **Resolution** — you tell the customer what you will do, in specific steps, with time frames.
6. **Wrap** — you confirm the customer has everything he needs, summarize what was done, name the next steps.
7. **Close** — you ask if there is anything else, thank the customer, use the company's branded close phrase. About 10 seconds.

Total call: usually 4 to 8 minutes for entry-level domestic process. Sometimes shorter. Sometimes longer.

Every customer-service call you ever take in your BPO career — at Concentrix, Foundever, HGS, anywhere — will follow some version of these seven moves. The company will give you slightly different words. The shape will be the same.

The most useful thing you can do right now: read the seven moves out loud. Three times. Slowly. Then close your eyes and say them in order from memory. If you can do that, you have the scaffold. The phrases go on top of the scaffold next.

### 3. The opening — the five-second rule

The opening is the first five seconds of the call. It is the *only* part of the call the customer judges before he has told you his problem. So he judges it entirely on *how it sounds*.

There are three things you must do in the opening:

- **Greet and name the company.** *"Thank you for calling [Company]..."* or *"Good morning, [Company] Customer Care..."*
- **Name yourself.** *"...this is Priya..."* or *"My name is Priya..."*
- **Signal you are ready to help.** *"...how may I help you today, Sir?"* or *"...how can I assist you today?"*

The whole opening is one sentence. It takes 4 to 5 seconds at normal speaking pace.

A girl who completes Concentrix Tier 1 training will be told to open every call with the *branded* opening — the exact phrase Concentrix wants on the recording. Something like *"Thank you for calling [client name] Customer Care, this is Priya, how may I help you today?"* You will memorize the branded version when you are hired and trained on a specific client process. Until then, learn the *generic* opening so you have a starting point.

(One important note. Domestic Indian processes — calls from Indian customers — keep *Sir* or *Madam* in the opening. International processes — calls from US, UK, or Australian customers — usually drop the *Sir* and just say *"How may I help you today?"* without the honorific. You will be told which one to use when you are hired. Both forms are correct.)

Time yourself. On your phone. Say *"Thank you for calling [Company] Customer Care, this is Priya, how may I help you today, Sir?"* out loud. Record it. Listen back. If it took less than 3 seconds — you rushed, and the customer did not hear your name. If it took more than 6 seconds — you dragged, and the customer is already impatient. 4 to 5 seconds is the target.

This is the first explicit pacing lesson of the unit. Pacing is one of the four things the phone screen will judge in Unit 7A. Start now.

### 4. Verification — the part you do not skip

After the customer says the first thing — *"Hi, my bill is too high"* — you do not jump to fixing the problem. You verify.

*"I am sorry to hear that, Sir. I will be happy to check this for you. May I have your service number and registered mobile number, please?"*

This is two moves in one phrase. *I am sorry to hear that* is an empathy marker (more on those in §5). *May I have your service number, please?* is the verification request.

The customer gives you the numbers. You repeat them back. *"Thank you. Service number 102-456-789, mobile 9876543210. Could you also confirm the name on the account, please?"*

You do not move on until the verification is done. Why? Because if you start fixing a problem on the wrong account, you make a real mess. Also — TRAI rules (the rules that govern telecom customer-care calls in India) require agent identification and caller verification on a customer-service call for any licensed telecom operator. The verification step is not just good practice. It is legally required on many processes.

(TRAI — new word — stands for *Telecom Regulatory Authority of India*. It is the government body that sets rules for phone and internet service in India. Their [Telecom Consumers Protection Regulations](https://www.trai.gov.in/) `[verify current version]` set standards for how customer-care calls must be handled. You do not need to memorize TRAI rules. You need to know that *the verification step is not optional*, and TRAI is one of the reasons.)

### 5. Discovery — listen, paraphrase, do not fix yet

After verification, the customer tells you the problem. Your job in the discovery move is *not yet to fix anything*. Your job is to make sure you understood.

Three small things go in the discovery move.

**(a) Acknowledgement markers — short.** Every 10 or 15 seconds while the customer is speaking, you say one of these:

- *"I see."*
- *"I understand."*
- *"Okay."*
- *"Got it."*

These are small. They are not full sentences. They tell the customer you are still listening. Without them, the customer thinks the line went silent and starts repeating himself.

**(b) Feeling markers — when the customer expresses emotion.**

- *"I am sorry to hear that."*
- *"I understand this has been frustrating."*
- *"That sounds like a difficult situation."*

You use these when the customer says something with feeling — *"I have been calling for two days"* or *"This is the third time I am explaining this"*. Don't use these when the customer is just stating facts.

**(c) The paraphrase back.** This is the most important phrase in the discovery move.

*"So if I have understood correctly, you are saying that your March bill is around 4,800 rupees, but your usual bill is around 1,200 rupees. Is that correct?"*

You take what the customer said and you say it back in your own words. Then you ask *is that correct?*

This phrase does three jobs at once. **First**, it confirms you understood — and if you got something wrong, the customer corrects you before you act on the wrong information. **Second**, it shows the customer you were listening — which is exactly what an angry customer needs (we will use this hard in Unit 6A). **Third**, it buys you 8 to 10 seconds to think about what to do next. Two of those are real value. One is a hidden gift.

You should memorize this phrase as one chunk. *"So if I have understood correctly, you are saying that... Is that correct?"* Fill in the middle with whatever the customer said. The phrase wraps around the customer's complaint. You do not have to invent the sentence each time. You only have to fill the slot.

### 6. Hold and transfer — the part most agents get wrong

The hold and transfer moves are where new agents lose CSAT scores. *CSAT* (new word — used a lot) stands for *Customer Satisfaction*. It is the 1-to-5 rating the customer gives after the call. Your CSAT score is one of three numbers your supervisor watches every week. (The other two are AHT — average handle time, how long your calls take — and FCR — first call resolution, the share of customers who do not have to call back about the same issue.)

The mistake new agents make is placing the customer on hold without asking, or without saying how long. Then the customer waits in silence. He gets angry. The CSAT drops. The call is now harder.

The correct hold has four parts. Memorize them as one block:

1. **Permission.** *"Could you please give me a moment while I check that for you?"* You wait for the customer to say yes.
2. **Action statement.** *"I am going to put you on a brief hold."* You signal the silence is coming.
3. **Time estimate.** *"It will take me about two minutes."* You set the expectation.
4. **Check-in if you go over.** *"Thank you for holding. I am still working on this for you. May I have another minute, please?"* You reset the customer's patience.

That is the hold. Same for transfer, slightly different words:

- **Why the transfer.** *"I would like to connect you with our specialist team who can resolve this faster for you."* Notice the framing — *resolve this faster*, not *I cannot help you*. The transfer is presented as a service, not as a brush-off.
- **Consent.** *"Is that all right with you, Sir?"*
- **Information bridge.** *"I will pass on your details to my colleague so you don't have to repeat yourself."* This is the move that decides whether the transfer feels caring or careless.
- **Branded transfer phrase.** *"Please hold the line while I connect you."*

The phrase *"so you don't have to repeat yourself"* is the most important phrase in the transfer move. Customers who get transferred and then have to re-explain the whole problem to the next agent are the angriest customers in any BPO. You can prevent that with one phrase.

### 7. Resolution — what you will do, in specific steps, with a time frame

The resolution move is where you tell the customer what is going to happen.

The grammar of this move matters. You use *future indicative* — that is the *I will* and *I am going to* form. You name the action. You name the time frame.

*"Here is what I am going to do, Sir. I am going to raise a meter-reading verification request. Our field team will visit within five working days. You will receive an SMS confirmation within 30 minutes with the complaint reference number, and another SMS when the visit is scheduled."*

Notice four things about that sentence:

- *Here is what I am going to do* — you announce the plan. The customer knows something definite is coming.
- *I am going to raise a meter-reading verification request* — specific action. Not *I will see what I can do*.
- *Within five working days* — specific time frame. Not *as soon as possible*.
- *You will receive an SMS within 30 minutes with the complaint reference number* — specific next step the customer can verify on his own phone.

The wrong version of this is: *"Sir, we will check and let you know."* That sentence has no action and no time frame. The customer hangs up not knowing what is happening. He calls back tomorrow. Your FCR drops.

If you cannot solve the issue yourself, the resolution move turns into the *escalation*:

*"This requires our specialist team to review. I will transfer you now so this gets resolved faster for you. Is that all right with you, Sir?"*

(We will go deeper on the escalation move in Unit 6A. For now: knowing that you *can* escalate when you need to is part of what makes you confident enough to handle the call.)

### 8. Wrap and close — the last 20 seconds that decide the CSAT

The customer's lasting impression of the call comes from the last 20 seconds. Most agents rush it because they are tired. This is exactly when *not* to rush.

The wrap and close together have five parts:

1. **Summary.** *"To confirm, Sir: I have raised a meter-reading verification request for your service number 102-456-789. You will receive an SMS within 30 minutes. The field team will visit within five working days."* You recap what was done and what is next. The customer's memory has a chance to catch up.
2. **Anything-else check.** *"Is there anything else I can help you with today?"* This gives the customer one more chance to raise a secondary issue. If you skip this, he will call back tomorrow about the second thing — which costs you FCR.
3. **Thanks.** *"Thank you for being our customer."* Or *"Thank you for your patience, Sir."* Short. Sincere. One sentence.
4. **Branded close.** *"Thank you for calling [Company]. Have a good day."* — this is the phrase the company will train you to say verbatim. The QA team will check the recording for this exact phrase. They will mark you down if you skip it. You memorize the version your company gives you and you use it on every call, even the last one of the day when you are exhausted.
5. **Wait for the customer to disconnect.** Two or three seconds of silence. The customer hangs up first. If you hang up first while the customer was mid-thought, the call gets escalated.

The branded close is one of the two things QA scorecards check on every single call (the other is the opening). It is not optional. It is part of why companies hire agents who can follow structure. A girl who consistently misses the branded close phrase will be flagged even if her resolution rate is high.

(One honest note. The branded close can feel artificial after the 50th call of the day. *"Thank you for calling [Company], have a good day"* spoken to the air at 6 PM on a Friday is its own kind of work. The work is real. The branded close is what gets you the next month's salary. We will talk about this kind of work — emotional work — in Unit 6A.)

## Worked example — Priya handles a full electricity-billing call

This is the central worked example of the unit. Read it through once. Then read it again, with the seven moves named in the margin of your mind. Notice the slot fillers — the fixed phrases — in each move.

(The customer in this example is Sridhar Reddy, 47, from Saidabad, Hyderabad. He has called his electricity provider's customer care because his March bill is much higher than usual. Priya is the agent.)

---

**[Move 1 — Opening. About 5 seconds.]**

Priya: *Thank you for calling Customer Care. My name is Priya. How may I help you today, Sir?*

---

**[Move 2 — Verification + start of Discovery. About 25 seconds.]**

Sridhar: *Hello, my bill is showing 4,800 rupees this month but normally I pay only 1,200. There must be some mistake.*

Priya: *I am sorry to hear that, Sir. I will be happy to check this for you. May I have your service number and registered mobile number, please?*

Sridhar: *Service number is 102-456-789, mobile is 9876543210.*

Priya: *Thank you. Could you also confirm the name on the account, please?*

Sridhar: *Sridhar Reddy.*

---

**[Move 3 — Discovery. About 30 seconds.]**

Priya: *Thank you for that information, Mr. Reddy. So if I have understood correctly, you are saying that your March bill is around 4,800 rupees, but your usual bill is around 1,200 rupees. Is that correct?*

Sridhar: *Yes, that is correct. Last month was 1,300. Suddenly this month it is four times that.*

Priya: *I understand. That is a significant difference. Has there been any change recently — a new appliance, an air conditioner, anything that might use more electricity?*

Sridhar: *No, nothing new.*

---

**[Move 4 — Hold. About 90 seconds total — 10 seconds of phrasing, 80 seconds of silence.]**

Priya: *Thank you, Sir. I am going to check your billing details on the system. Could you please give me a moment while I check that for you? It will take about two minutes.*

Sridhar: *Yes, please go ahead.*

*(Priya places the call on hold. She checks the billing system. Previous month: 142 units. This month: 480 units. Big jump for one month, no new appliance — likely a meter misreading.)*

Priya: *Thank you for holding, Mr. Reddy. I have checked your account.*

---

**[Move 5 — Resolution. About 45 seconds.]**

Priya: *I can see that your previous month bill was for 142 units, and this month the meter reading is showing 480 units. That is a very unusual jump. Here is what I am going to do, Sir. I am going to raise a meter-reading verification request so that our field team can come and check the meter at your home. They will verify the reading. If there is a misreading, the bill will be corrected.*

Sridhar: *Yes, please do that. When will they come?*

Priya: *The field team will visit within five working days. You will receive an SMS confirmation with a complaint reference number, and another SMS when the visit is scheduled.*

---

**[Move 6 — Wrap. About 20 seconds.]**

Priya: *To confirm, Mr. Reddy: I have raised a meter-reading verification request for your service number 102-456-789. You will receive an SMS within the next 30 minutes with the complaint reference number. Our field team will visit within five working days. If there is a meter error, the bill will be corrected and a revised bill will be sent. Is that clear?*

Sridhar: *Yes, thank you very much.*

---

**[Move 7 — Close. About 15 seconds.]**

Priya: *Is there anything else I can help you with today, Sir?*

Sridhar: *No, that is all.*

Priya: *Thank you for calling Customer Care, Mr. Reddy. Have a good day.*

*(Two seconds of silence. Sridhar disconnects.)*

---

Total call: 3 minutes 40 seconds, plus hold time. Well within standard AHT for an entry-level domestic-process call. `[verify current 2026 AHT targets for Hyderabad electricity-utility BPO contracts]`

**Three things to notice when you re-read this call:**

- Priya never switched to Telugu. The customer's name and the way he said his bill amount were Telugu-coded. Priya could have switched. She did not. The script structure kept her in English. Without the structure, she would have reached for Telugu under load. With the structure, she did not need to.
- Priya used three active-listening markers — *I am sorry to hear that, I understand, thank you for that information* — without thinking about them. They were chunks. She did not invent them on the call. She had practiced them.
- The paraphrase — *so if I have understood correctly, you are saying that...* — did two jobs. It showed the customer she had heard him. It also gave her 8 seconds to think about what to check on the system. The paraphrase is the most useful phrase you will learn in this unit.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Skipping the branded greeting and just saying *"Hello, customer care."*** This is what Girl 1 did in the opening case. It costs you nothing to say the full opening. It costs you the QA score if you skip it. Memorize the branded greeting. Say it every single time. Even on call number 50 of the day.

**Mistake 2 — Putting the customer on hold without permission.** *"One minute sir"* and silence is not a hold. A hold is asking permission, naming the action, giving a time estimate, checking in if you go over. Four parts. Memorize them as one block.

**Mistake 3 — Trying to solve the problem during the discovery move.** The customer is still talking. You jump in with *"Sir, what you should do is..."* The customer has not finished explaining. He feels interrupted. He repeats himself longer. Your call is now five minutes longer than it needed to be. The paraphrase-back move solves this. Hear the customer out. Paraphrase. Then act.

**Mistake 4 — *"Sir actually..."*** The word *actually* is a small word that does a big damage. It is a contradiction marker. The customer hears *you are wrong, I am right*. Almost any sentence starting with *Sir actually* is on its way to a customer complaint. We will rehearse the alternatives hard in Unit 6A. For this unit, the simple rule is: take *actually* out of your customer-service English. Substitute *I see, I understand, let me check that*.

**Mistake 5 — Rushing the close.** You are tired. It is 5:50 PM. You have done 47 calls. The 48th call is wrapping up. You skip the *Is there anything else I can help you with today*. You skip the branded close. You just say *"Okay sir, have a good day"* and hang up. The QA team listens to a random sample of your calls each week — this is the call they will sample, because you rushed. The fix: the close is the same five parts every time, no matter how tired you are. It takes 20 seconds. Worth it.

**Mistake 6 — Switching to Telugu under pressure.** This is the L1-reversion failure mode. We will treat it in depth in Unit 6A. For now, the prevention: if you feel the Telugu coming, reach for the rescue phrase — *"Could you please give me a moment, Sir? I want to make sure I give you the right information."* That phrase is a hold-permission move structurally, but it works as a reset. It buys you 30 seconds to breathe and re-enter in English. Pre-script this rescue phrase. Memorize it. It is one of the most useful phrases in this entire course.

### Exercise 3 — Pace your opening *(Bloom: Apply)*

Open your phone's voice recorder. Record yourself saying:

*"Thank you for calling Customer Care, this is Priya, how may I help you today, Sir?"*

Time the recording. Aim for 4 to 5 seconds.

- Under 3 seconds? You are rushing. Slow down by one beat between each clause.
- Over 6 seconds? You are dragging. Reduce the small pauses.

Re-record three times. Pick the version that lands closest to 4.5 seconds. That is your target opening. Memorize the rhythm of that one.
