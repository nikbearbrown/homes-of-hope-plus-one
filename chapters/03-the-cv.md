# Unit 3 — The CV

*One page. Your story. In hirable English.*

## Learning outcomes

By the end of this unit, you will be able to:

## The problem this unit solves

You think you have nothing to put on a CV.

You are wrong.

You have six years of community living at a Homes of Hope residence. You have a completed multi-tier English literacy program. You have two-and-a-half languages — Telugu (native), Hindi (good), English (working, basic professional). You have smartphone fluency. You have basic ChatGPT fluency. You have documented residential placement with the Salesian sisters. You have daily routine, structured responsibilities, and a real track record of doing what you said you would do.

None of this is *"work history"* in the sense a BPO recruiter expects. **All of it is hirable** — if it is described in CV language. Your problem is not blank. Your problem is *translation* — from biography into CV-grade English.

This unit teaches that translation. We will start with your real life. We will pull thirty CV-relevant data points out of one short paragraph about you. Then we will use AI to draft a one-page CV. Then we will review the AI's draft and catch its mistakes. By the end of the unit, you will have a real, one-page, defensible CV — saved as a PDF on your phone — ready to attach to a Naukri application.

## Core content

### 1. The Indian fresher CV is a specific genre — with specific conventions

A "fresher CV" is the standard Indian first-job document. ("Fresher" — new word, but you will see it everywhere on Naukri. It means *someone applying for their first job with 0–1 years of work experience.* You are a fresher. So is every girl in your residence applying for her first BPO job. Recruiters are looking for freshers. They are not embarrassed by you. They are paid to hire you.)

The Indian fresher CV has tighter rules than the American resume. We will follow the Indian rules. Your job market is Hyderabad, not Boston.

Standard sections, in order:

**Header.** Your full name (top, centered or left-aligned). Phone number with +91. Professional-sounding email — *firstname.lastname@gmail.com*, not *partygirl1999@gmail.com*. City — *Secunderabad, Hyderabad, Telangana* is correct. Optional: a small, professional, plain-background passport-style photograph in the top-right. Indian fresher CVs commonly include a photo. Multinational firms increasingly do not require it. For Hyderabad BPO entry-level, the photo helps more than it hurts. Include one.

**Profile / Career objective.** Two or three lines. This is where you say what you are looking for and what you bring. Indian fresher CVs almost always have this section, where American resumes have dropped it. Keep it; recruiters expect it. We will write yours in the worked example.

**Education.** Your education comes *before* work experience — because work experience is missing. List most recent qualification at the top. Format: qualification, institution, year, percentage or CGPA. Indian recruiters expect percentages. *"Intermediate (12th class), [College Name], Hyderabad, Expected 2026, 60%."* Do not hide percentages. A 62% in SSC plus the rest of your CV produces a viable BPO candidate. **Hiding the 62% gets the CV deleted.**

**Languages.** Telugu, Hindi, English — in the right order for the job. For BPO English-process roles, put English first. For Apollo Hospitals reception, put English, Telugu, Hindi (the order on most Apollo postings). For NGO admin, English first.

**Skills.** Concrete, specific, recruiter-keyword-aware. Not *"hard worker"* (filler). Try: *"Smartphone applications: WhatsApp, ChatGPT, Google Search, YouTube. Customer interaction: 6 years residential community coordination. Time management: daily routine in structured residence."* Each skill ties to evidence.

**Training & Experience.** Even without paid work, this section is where structured non-traditional experience goes. The Salesian sisters' character and conduct training. The literacy program completion. Any volunteering. Frame each as a *named program* with start date, end date, and 2–3 specific responsibilities.

**Achievements** (optional). Short list. Any prize, certificate, or completed program.

**Availability.** This is a feature, not a weakness — if your residence rules limit you to day shift, **say so.** Hiding it wastes the recruiter's time and yours. Disclosing it filters out roles you cannot accept and signals honesty.

**Declaration.** *"I hereby declare that the above information is true to the best of my knowledge."* + signature + date. This is the most distinctively Indian CV convention. Multinational firms increasingly skip it. Indian fresher recruiters expect it. Include it.

One page. That is the constraint. Two pages signals lack of self-editing at the fresher level. Hold to one page even if it hurts.

### 2. The six-second scan

A common research finding (often-cited [Ladders eye-tracking study](https://www.theladders.com/static/images/basicSite/pdfs/TheLadders-EyeTracking-StudyC2.pdf), updated 2018) [verify] reports that the first-pass CV review is about six to ten seconds. Hyderabad recruiters at Concentrix and Apollo and CRY do the same scan on their phones. They are not reading. They are scanning.

What they scan for, in order:

1. Your name and phone — a real, contactable applicant.
2. Education — does she meet the minimum (usually 12th pass for BPO entry)? Does the percentage clear the cutoff (often 50% or 60%)?
3. Skills line — does English appear? Anything else that matches the posting keywords?
4. **One thing that catches the eye** — any specific detail that suggests this is a real person, not a copy-paste candidate.

What they do not read in the first scan: the Career Objective (formulaic), the Declaration (default), most of the experience section unless they are hunting for something.

The design implication for you: **the top third of the page is the high-value land.** Put your name, your education, your most striking skill, and the one specific detail (your residence, your literacy program, your willingness on day shift) in the first third of the page. If the recruiter reads only the first six seconds, those should be the six seconds.

### 3. Non-traditional experience can be CV-grade — if you describe it in three moves

This is the move that does the most work in the unit.

*"I lived in a residence for six years"* is biography. The recruiter reads this and learns nothing he can use.

*"Resident, Homes of Hope Hyderabad (Secunderabad) — 6 years, 2019–2025. Completed structured character and conduct training under Salesian Sisters. Daily community responsibilities including meal preparation for 12+ residents, study supervision of younger residents (ages 8–12), and shared housekeeping."* — this is CV.

Three moves turned the first sentence into the second.

**Move 1 — Structure.** Frame it as a *program*. Name it. Give it a start date and an end date. *"Resident Program"* is a program. *"Salesian character and conduct training"* is a structured curriculum. The structure-language signals *"this was organized, supervised, and finite"* rather than *"this was just my life."*

**Move 2 — Specific names.** Specific institution names beat generic descriptors. *"Salesian Sisters of St. John Bosco"* beats *"Catholic nuns."* *"Homes of Hope, Hyderabad"* beats *"a residence."* Specific names are checkable. Specific names sound real. Specific names also let the recruiter recognize a network if he knows it — and Homes of Hope is known among Hyderabad NGO and HR circles.

**Move 3 — Quantification.** Numbers. *"6 years."* *"Ages 8–12."* *"12+ residents."* *"Daily."* Recruiters trust numbers more than adjectives. *"Hard worker"* is filler. *"Helped 6 younger residents with reading practice three evenings per week for two years"* is evidence. Wherever you can attach a number, do.

This is the same translation move a girl from a traditional family makes when she lists *"household management, meal coordination for 8-member joint family, study supervision for younger siblings"* on a CV. You have done the work. You are just describing it in CV language.

### 4. What does NOT need to be on your fresher CV

Equally important. Trying to add things you do not have makes the CV worse, not better.

- **Work history.** A fresher CV with no paid work history is *normal*. Entry-level postings say *"freshers welcome"* and mean it. Do not invent internships. The lie is checkable. It is the fastest way to be blacklisted.
- **References.** *"References available upon request"* is fine. You do not need to list names.
- **Date of birth.** Include — it is the Indian convention.
- **Marital status, father's name.** Omit. These appeared on older Indian CVs. Modern multinational employers do not want them. Skip.
- **A photograph.** Include a small, plain-background, passport-style photo. This remains the Hyderabad fresher convention.
- **Hobbies section.** Optional. If you include one, be specific — *"Volunteer reading tutor for younger residents at Homes of Hope"* is useful and could move up to Experience. *"Reading and music"* alone is filler. Cut.

### 5. The AI CV workflow — three passes

You will not write the CV from a blank page. You will use AI to draft, then you will own the review.

**Pass 1 — Information dump.** You tell the AI your background in simple sentences. **In your natural English.** It does not have to be polished. The AI's job is to take your simple paragraph and produce a one-page CV.

**Pass 2 — AI draft.** AI produces a draft with standard sections — Profile, Education, Languages, Skills, Experience, etc. You read every line. Two questions:

- *Is this true about me?* (Catch fabricated specifics — schools, percentages, certifications, internships you do not have.)
- *Does this sound like me, or could it be any girl in any city?* (Catch generic filler — *"passionate and dedicated team player seeking to make meaningful contributions."*)

**Pass 3 — Correction and personalization.** Replace fabricated specifics with truth. Replace generic filler with one specific thing from your real life. Verify every percentage matches your actual marksheets. Add your real phone and email.

This is the same workflow from Unit 2 — prompt, review, correct, ask back — applied to a higher-stakes document. The CV is the first long piece of writing where every single line has to defend itself in a phone screen.

## Worked example — Priya builds her CV from scratch

(Priya here is the running learner-vignette in this book — a composite, not a real student.)

### Step 1 — Priya's actual background, in her own words

Priya sits with her phone. She has a blank document open. She is intimidated. She tells the AI what she knows about herself, in her natural English:

> *My name is Priya [last name]. I am 19 years old. My phone is +91 [number]. I live in Secunderabad. I have been at the Homes of Hope residence for 6 years. Before that I was with my family in [village]. I passed 10th class in 2023 with 62% from [name of school]. I am studying for Intermediate, second year, at [college], expected pass in 2026 with around 55–60%. I speak Telugu (first language), Hindi (good), English (basic — about 350 words from training). I can use a smartphone, WhatsApp, YouTube, basic ChatGPT. At the residence I help with meal preparation and I read with younger girls in the evening. I completed the literacy training program from Homes of Hope this year. I want a customer service job in BPO. I am free to work day shift or evening shift. I cannot work night shift because the sisters do not allow it before age 21.*

That is 165 words. Priya thinks this is "nothing." She thinks she has no CV material.

**Count what is in that paragraph:** her name, phone, city, residence, years at residence, prior location, two completed qualifications with percentages, current qualification in progress, three languages with self-rated levels, smartphone fluency, AI literacy, two specific residence responsibilities, a completed literacy program, a target sector, a shift preference, a shift constraint, an explanation for the constraint.

That is roughly **30 CV-relevant data points** in one paragraph. Priya's problem is not blank. Her problem is translation.

Use Indian fresher conventions — include Profile, Education with percentages, Languages, Skills, Training & Experience, Availability, Declaration. One page only. Plain format, no fancy templates, no icons. Here is my background: [paste her paragraph from Step 1].*

### Step 4 — Priya reviews line by line

She catches six things.

**(a) "Dynamic and motivated recent Intermediate graduate"** — She has not graduated yet. Expected 2026. The line is wrong on a fact. It is also generic. *Replace with:* *"Intermediate (12th class) student in final year, seeking entry-level customer service role in Hyderabad BPO. 6 years residential community living under Salesian sisters. Available immediately for day or evening shift."* Specific. True. Register-correct.

**(b) "English (fluent)"** — She has 350 working English words. *Fluent* is a serious overclaim. The hiring manager will catch this gap in 30 seconds on the phone screen. *Replace with:* *"English (working — basic professional communication, 350+ vocabulary from recent training)."* Indian recruiters parse this honestly. **Honest understatement beats false fluency every time.**

**(c) "MS Office (Word, Excel, PowerPoint)"** — She does not have laptop Microsoft tools experience. She has phone fluency. *Replace with:* *"Smartphone applications: WhatsApp, basic ChatGPT, Google Search, YouTube. Quick to learn new apps."* True. Useful for BPO that increasingly runs on mobile-first tools.

**(d) "Strong communication and interpersonal skills"** — Pure filler. Cut. *Replace with one specific line:* *"Comfortable with structured conversations; experienced in daily coordination with 12+ residents and 4 supervising sisters."*

**(e) "St. Mary's High School"** — AI made up a school name. She did not attend St. Mary's. *Replace* with her actual school name.

**(f) Missing availability disclosure.** The AI did not put in her day-or-evening-shift constraint. *Add an Availability section:* *"Day shift or evening shift, Monday–Saturday. Night shifts available from age 21."* This is honesty as strategy. The day-shift recruiter reads this and books her. The night-shift recruiter does not waste her time.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1 — "I have nothing to put on a CV."** The single largest emotional barrier in this unit. Priya thinks her residence years are "just her life," not CV material. They are CV material. The three moves — structure, specific names, quantification — turn biography into evidence. Use them. Re-read your information dump. You have more material than you think.

**Mistake 2 — Overstating English fluency.** The default fill on a fresher CV is *"English fluent."* Hiring managers know this is usually false. They check on the phone screen. The gap between *"fluent"* on the CV and 350 words on the phone is the most common reason fresher CVs get callbacks and then fail interviews. Honest stratification — *native / fluent / professional working / working / basic / beginner* — saves you from this. *Working* or *basic* is what 350 words supports. Use it.

**Mistake 3 — Hiding low percentages.** A 62% in SSC is not great. It is also normal. Many Hyderabad freshers have similar numbers. Recruiters scan past CVs without percentages, because the absence implies the candidate is hiding something worse. **Include the number.** The rest of your CV — residence, literacy program, willingness to learn — produces a viable candidate around a 62%.

**Mistake 4 — Using a fancy template with icons and colored bars.** Canva templates look impressive on your phone. They confuse Naukri's resume parser. They confuse the Apollo recruiter's eye. Indian fresher CV convention is plain, single-column, standard font (Times New Roman or Arial, 11–12pt), one page, Word/PDF format. **Pretty CV = unread CV.** Plain.

**Mistake 5 — Inventing things.** ChatGPT will fluently invent an internship, a school name, or a percentage. You will be tempted to keep it because it makes your CV look stronger. *Do not.* The hiring manager will ask you about the invented internship. You will freeze. The interview ends. You also get flagged in the recruiter's system as someone who lies. **Truth is not a constraint on your CV. Truth is what makes the CV worth reading.**

## Exercises

### Exercise 1 — List five things from your life that could go on a CV *(Bloom: Analyze)*

You will underestimate this. The exercise proves you wrong.

Open a blank page on your phone. Write the heading: *Things from my life that could be on a CV.*

Write five items. Anything from your residence years, your studies, your training, your community responsibilities, your phone fluency, your language skills, your hobbies, your help at the residence, your role at any religious or community event. Not "good listener." Specific things. *"I helped Sister [Name] organize the school supply distribution for younger girls in August 2024."* *"I completed the Anki Tier 1–2 English vocabulary deck in nine weeks."* *"I am the residence WhatsApp group coordinator."*

Five items minimum. If you write more than five — better.

Bring this list to the Zoom session. The volunteer will help you turn each item into one CV-grade line.

### Exercise 2 — Draft your CV with AI, then find what AI invented *(Bloom: Create + Evaluate)*

This is the load-bearing exercise of the unit. Plan for one full hour.

**Steps:**

1. Write your information-dump paragraph (the Priya Step 1 paragraph in §worked example). Your real background, in your natural English, in 150–200 words.

2. Prompt the AI using the template from §5. Get a CV draft.

3. **Find at least three things the AI invented or got wrong about you.** Most CVs will have three. Some will have five. Mark them.

4. **Find at least two lines that are generic filler** — sentences that could be about any girl in any city. Mark them.

5. Rewrite the CV in your voice. Replace invented specifics with truth. Replace generic filler with one specific thing from your real life. Keep the structure the AI gave you.

6. Save it as a PDF on your phone.

7. Bring all three to the Zoom session: your information-dump paragraph, the AI's first draft (with your marks on it), your final CV.

### Exercise 3 — Exchange CVs with another girl in the program *(Bloom: Evaluate — Zoom session activity)*

In the Zoom session, you will pair up with another girl from your residence or another residence. You will exchange CVs. Each of you reads the other's CV in 10 seconds (the recruiter's first pass). Then you give each other three specific notes:

1. *What jumped out as the catching detail in the first 10 seconds?* If nothing — the top third of the page needs work.
2. *What sounded like AI filler?* Be specific. Point to the line.
3. *What is missing that a hiring manager would want?* Phone number formatted correctly. Availability constraint. Percentage on SSC. A specific catching detail.

Three notes. Not *"your CV is good."* The volunteer will help if you get stuck. The exchange is the most useful thing you will do in this Zoom session — because *you* are reading the way the recruiter reads, and *your partner* is reading the way the recruiter reads your CV.
