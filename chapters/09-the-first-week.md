# Unit 9 — The First Week

*Getting the job is not the finish line. Surviving Friday is.*

---

## Learning outcomes

By the end of this unit, you will be able to:

- **(Apply)** Describe what to expect in the first five working days of an entry-level job in your chosen track — what to wear, how to address colleagues, what AI tools you can use without asking, and what to do when you do not understand an instruction.
- **(Apply)** Use the exact phrase for asking clarification at work — the phrase that signals professional competence rather than ignorance.
- **(Apply)** Recognize and live up to the two highest-leverage first-week behaviors. Neither is English.

---

## The problem this unit solves

Here is the part nobody told you.

The two biggest reasons new hires fail in the first week — in Hyderabad BPO, in hospital reception, in NGO offices — are **not** English.

You expected English. You have spent four months of this course worrying about your English. You have decided in advance that your English is the thing that will hold you back, get you noticed, get you fired.

The data says otherwise.

The two biggest reasons are:

1. **Punctuality.** Showing up on time, every day, the first five days.
2. **Asking for clarification correctly when you do not understand an instruction.**

Both are behaviors. Neither is vocabulary. Neither is grammar. Neither requires a single new English word.

This is the most counterintuitive thing in this course. It is also the most useful. The girl with 350 English words who shows up on time every day and asks specifically what she does not understand — that girl keeps the job. The girl with 800 English words who arrives late twice in the first week and tries to fake competence — that girl loses it.

`[verify these specific reasons against current Hyderabad-region HR practitioner sources; the framing here is the chapter's pedagogical compression of converging practitioner evidence from NASSCOM workforce reports, TeamLease staffing data, and Indian HR trade press, rather than a single quoted study]`

This unit teaches the small set of behaviors that get you to Friday with the job still in place. Not how to become a star. Not how to get promoted. *Just how to survive the first five days.* That is enough work for one unit. The rest comes later.

A note on scope. The first week is not the whole job. The first week is not the first year. The first week is also not the first paycheck (that comes in week 4 or 5, and it has its own surprises, which belong to Course 3). This unit ends on Friday afternoon of week 1, with you going home for the weekend, the joining letter still in force, the supervisor knowing your name. That is the goal. Nothing more, nothing less.

## Opening case

Two girls start work on the same Monday at the same Hyderabad BPO. Both have completed Units 1–8. Both got the offer the same week. Both are 19. Both have basic English.

(One of them is Priya — the running learner-vignette in this book, a composite, not a real student.)

**Girl 1.** Monday: alarm fails to ring. She arrives at 9:20 AM for an 8:30 AM joining. HR is annoyed. She apologizes seven times. She signs paperwork. She is sent home at 1 PM.

Tuesday: she arrives on time. Training begins. The trainer gives an instruction about a *call summary form*. Girl 1 does not understand whether to fill it during the call or after the call. She does not ask. She is embarrassed. She tries to figure it out. She fills two forms wrong. The trainer notices on Wednesday morning. She is now the girl who got it wrong.

By Friday, Girl 1's supervisor has a feeling — *this girl is not following instructions*. The feeling is not yet specific enough to fire her. It is specific enough that next month, when there are 30 trainees and only 25 floor positions, Girl 1 is among the five who are let go.

Her English was not the problem. She speaks the same English as the 25 who keep their jobs. The problem was the missed alarm and the unasked question. The two things this unit names.

**Girl 2 — Priya.** Monday: she arrives at 7:30 AM for an 8:30 AM joining. (Her residence routine taught her to be early — the sisters wake the residents for prayer at 5:30; 7:30 is not difficult.) She waits in the security lobby. At 7:55 she reports to HR.

HR hands her a stack of forms. Joining letter, NDA, bank account verification. Priya does not understand one section on the NDA — the part about *intellectual property* of the company. She uses the exact phrase she memorized from this unit: *"Ma'am, before I sign this, could you walk me through what this section is for? I want to make sure I understand."*

HR reads the section to her. Explains it in two sentences. Priya signs. The HR person makes a small mental note — *this girl reads what she signs and asks before signing*. That note is not a problem. It is the opposite of a problem.

By Friday, Priya has been on time every day. She has asked three specific clarifying questions, all of them framed professionally. She has completed every small task by the deadline given. The supervisor knows her name. He does not yet know she is a star. He knows she is *the kind of new hire who is going to be fine.* That is enough.

Same job. Same week. Same English. Different two behaviors. Different outcome.

This is what we are teaching this unit.

## Core content

### 1. The two biggest reasons new hires fail — and why neither is English

The academic frame is from organizational psychology — a body of research on what makes a new employee adjust well to a new job. The most-cited synthesis is from Talya Bauer and Berrin Erdogan, who reviewed thirty years of research and found four main predictors of successful onboarding ([Bauer & Erdogan 2011, "Organizational Socialization," *APA Handbook of I/O Psychology, Vol. 3*](https://www.apa.org/pubs/books/4311508)):

- **Role clarity** — do I know what I am supposed to do?
- **Self-efficacy** — do I believe I can do it?
- **Social acceptance** — do colleagues treat me as part of the team?
- **Knowledge of organizational culture** — do I know how things actually work here?

New hires who establish these four in the first month adjust well. New hires who do not — even ones with strong technical skills — drop out.

For *Hyderabad entry-level* positions specifically — BPO, hospital reception, NGO office work — the academic literature is thinner. What practitioner reports converge on across [NASSCOM workforce data](https://nasscom.in/knowledge-center/publications), [TeamLease staffing reports](https://www.teamlease.com), and Indian HR trade press: the two biggest first-week failures are *attendance breakdown* and *failure to ask for clarification competently.* Both are operational behaviors. Both are downstream of the four Bauer-Erdogan factors above — punctuality maps to *role clarity and self-efficacy* (I know what is required; I can do it), clarification-asking maps to *role clarity and social acceptance* (I understand my work; I am part of the team).

`[verify the "top two reasons" framing against the most current available Hyderabad-region HR practitioner sources at time of publication]`

**Reason 1 — punctuality and attendance breakdown.**

Not "showed up late once." A *pattern* across the first week. Late arrival on day 2, no-show on a personal issue without notification on day 3, late again on day 5. The supervisor sees this and updates her mental model — *this girl cannot be relied on*. The update is hard to undo.

The mechanism is mundane. The girl has a new schedule. Her body has not yet built the wake-and-commute habit at the new timing. One missed alarm cascades into a missed shift. She does not yet know how to recover professionally — she does not call ahead, she does not name the problem clearly, she over-apologizes when she arrives. The recovery itself makes the problem worse.

What this looks like in your favor. You already live in a residence with a structured daily routine. Wake time. Prayer time. Meal time. Study time. The sisters have built this habit in you for years. You do not have to learn it from zero. You have to *port* the habit you already have onto the new schedule. Set the alarm. Set a backup alarm. Lay out the clothes the night before. Charge the phone. Know how long the commute will take, and add 15 minutes. These are small operational moves. You already do versions of them at the residence.

**Reason 2 — failure to ask for clarification competently.**

This is the more interesting failure mode. Notice the word *competently*. Not "failure to ask." Most new hires *do* ask. The problem is *how* they ask.

[Lee 1997, *Journal of Applied Psychology*, "When the Going Gets Tough, Do the Tough Ask for Help?"](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1997-43178-002) — studied workplace help-seeking and found something the chapter wants you to remember. The information sought may be exactly the same. The social reading of the asking is *opposite*, depending on how the question is phrased.

Compare:

- *"Sir, I don't understand. Can you explain?"* — signals *I could not follow the instruction.* The supervisor's next thought is *can she do this job?*
- *"Sir, I want to make sure I have this right. Could you walk me through the call-summary form one more time? I want to be sure I do it correctly."* — signals *I have engaged with the instruction and identified the specific part I need to confirm.* The supervisor's next thought is *good, she is careful.*

The information sought is identical. The social reading is opposite. The second girl gets confirmed and the supervisor notes her as careful. The first girl gets confirmed and the supervisor notes her as struggling.

This is not magic. It is a small set of phrases, memorized in advance, deployed when needed. You will memorize a few of them in §4 of this chapter. Memorizing scripts here is the right move — these phrases are deployed under time pressure, and rehearsed scripts hold up better than improvised ones.

(One careful note. Asking the question is necessary. *Phrasing* it correctly is what determines whether it lands as competent or as incompetent. Do not over-learn this and decide *do not ask if you cannot phrase perfectly*. Asking imperfectly beats not asking at all by a wide margin. But asking with the right phrase beats asking with the wrong phrase by another wide margin. Both improvements are real.)

### 2. The values the sisters taught you — as professional assets

This is worth naming directly. The sisters' daily teaching has built three things in you that the workplace specifically rewards. Not in a religious way. In a practical, hireable way.

**Punctuality.** Built by years of fixed routines — wake at 5:30, prayer at 6:00, breakfast at 7:00, study at 7:30. This is the *single highest-leverage first-week behavior* (§1, Reason 1). You already have the habit. Most of your peers in the training batch do not. They are figuring out how to be on time. You already know. This is not a small advantage.

**Honesty.** When you do not know something, you say so (Unit 8, §4). When you make a mistake, you name it and ask how to fix it. The sisters have built this habit by treating dishonest answers as more serious than wrong answers. Indian supervisors notice this and value it. The dishonest fresher who hides mistakes gets caught in week 2. The honest one keeps the job and gets the difficult task on Tuesday.

**Respect.** *Sir / Madam* address. Listening to the supervisor without interrupting. Treating peers with consideration. These are professional behaviors that high-power-distance Indian workplaces specifically reward ([Hofstede 1980 and onward](https://books.google.com/books/about/Culture_s_Consequences.html?id=Cayp_Um4O9gC); [Sinha 1995, *The Cultural Context of Leadership and Power*](https://www.sagepub.in/books/the-cultural-context-of-leadership-and-power)). They are also things the sisters have built in you.

The chapter wants you to see this clearly. These are not religious virtues being marketed as workplace skills. They are universal professional behaviors that a disciplined upbringing — including but not limited to a Salesian residence — develops reliably. Many girls in your batch will be learning these for the first time at week 1. You learned them at year 1. This shows up in the supervisor's quiet noticing throughout the week.

Name this to yourself. The residence is not something to hide. The residence is the reason you arrive on time and ask honestly and respect the supervisor. The residence is your first qualification. The CV says so (Unit 3). The first week confirms it.

### 3. How to address colleagues and supervisors — and what changes by week 2

Indian workplace hierarchy norms are still strong at entry level in Hyderabad. The first-week defaults:

**Supervisors and anyone visibly senior.** *Sir* or *Madam* (or *Ma'am*). This was the rule for written register in Unit 1. It is at least equally true for spoken workplace interaction. *Sir* or *Madam* is the default address until you are explicitly invited to use the first name. Even when invited — *"Please call me Rohan"* — most entry-level girls in Hyderabad continue to use *Sir Rohan* or just *Sir* in formal moments and *Rohan* only in casual moments. This is normal. This is not a failure to follow instruction.

The asymmetry is normal too. The supervisor will probably call you by your first name. You call him *Sir* or *Mr. Kumar*. The asymmetry is not disrespect from him. It is Indian workplace convention.

For senior staff in international NGOs (e.g., a CRY country director, an Apollo Hospitals administrator who has worked abroad), *Mr. Kumar / Mrs. Reddy / Ms. Reddy* + surname is the slightly more current alternative. The chapter's safe rule: use whichever form you hear other juniors using when they address the same senior. Listen for a day. Then match.

**Peers — colleagues at your same level.** First names are normal. *"Hi Sneha. Did you finish the form?"* — fine. The diminutive forms (*Anjali-akka* — elder sister; *Rohan-bhaiya* — elder brother) are used in some Hyderabad workplaces with slightly older peers. They are warm and culturally legible. Using them is fine. Not using them is also fine. The mistake is using *akka* / *bhaiya* with a *supervisor* — even a young supervisor is *Madam*, not *akka*.

**What changes by week 2.** Not much. The "invited to first names" invitation does happen at some workplaces. When it does, hold off on switching for another week or two. Continuing to say *Sir* or *Madam* for the first two weeks even when invited is the safer error. The opposite mistake — switching to first-name on day 3 because the supervisor used your first name on day 2 — reads as forward. Wait. The supervisor will notice you waiting and respect it.

**Set phrases you will hear.** Indian Business English has standard set phrases that may sound strange the first time you hear them. *"Please do the needful."* *"Please revert by EOD."* *"Kindly intimate."* These are Indian-Business-English conventions ([Sailaja 2009, *Indian English*, Edinburgh University Press](https://edinburghuniversitypress.com/book-indian-english.html)). They are not wrong English. They are not insulting English. They are professional Indian English.

Translations for the first time you hear each:

- *"Do the needful"* = *do whatever is required.* The supervisor is telling you to take the action that fits. If you do not know what action fits, *ask* — using the §4 phrasing. Do not panic. The phrase is a way of saying *figure it out and act*. It assumes you can.
- *"Revert by EOD"* = *reply by end of day.* EOD in most Hyderabad offices is 6 PM or 7 PM, sometimes earlier in some companies. If you do not know your office's EOD time, ask a peer (this is a peer-level question, not a supervisor question).
- *"Kindly intimate"* = *please let me know.* Polite, slightly older register. Read it as *please tell me*.

The chapter's rule on these. *Recognize them. Do not be intimidated by them. Do not use them yourself yet — the newer Indian Business English forms from Unit 1 (*could you please let me know, please share the next step*) are slightly more modern.* But you are not wrong if you do use them. You sound a generation older. That is the only cost.

**Meetings.** Most entry-level meetings in your first week will be *announcement-style*, not *discussion-style*. The supervisor or trainer speaks. The juniors listen. Juniors ask clarifying questions at the end, or after — not by jumping in with an opinion in the middle. This is normal Indian corporate convention. You are not expected to contribute opinions in week 1. You are expected to listen and write down what you do not yet understand.

Asking a *clarifying question at the end of a meeting* — *"Sir, could you tell me a bit more about how the X part works?"* — is professional and welcomed. Jumping into the middle of a meeting with an opinion is not. The chapter's first-week meeting rule: *listen, write, ask at the end.*

### 4. The exact phrase for asking clarification — and why phrasing matters

The single most important script in this unit.

There are many ways to ask the same question. They are not equal. Some signal competence. Some signal struggle. The information sought is the same. The social reading is different. ([Lee 1997](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1997-43178-002); [Morrison 1993, *Academy of Management Journal*, "Newcomer Information Seeking"](https://journals.aom.org/doi/10.5465/256659).)

Compare two versions, again:

**Bad phrasing:** *"Sir, I don't understand. Can you explain?"*

This signals: *I could not follow your instruction.* The supervisor wonders if you can do the job. The information sought may be valid. The phrasing makes the supervisor doubt you.

**Good phrasing:** *"Sir, I want to make sure I have this right. Could you walk me through that one more time? I want to be sure I do it correctly."*

This signals: *I have engaged with the instruction, identified the specific part I need to confirm, and am asking for targeted clarification.* The supervisor thinks: *good, she is careful.*

Memorize the good phrasing. Use it as the default. Variations:

- *"Could you walk me through the [step / form / system] one more time? I want to be sure I have it right."*
- *"I want to confirm — when you said [X], did you mean [my understanding]? Or something different?"*
- *"I have [partial understanding] — could you fill in the part about [specific gap]?"*
- *"Let me repeat back what I heard, and you can correct me where I have it wrong."*

All four do the same work. They each demonstrate three things: you engaged with the instruction, you identified the specific gap, you are asking for targeted clarification — not full re-teaching. This is the professional-competence signal.

(There is a Telugu version of this move you already know. When a sister gives an instruction and you want to confirm, you do not usually say *naaku teliyadu* — *I don't know*. You say something more like *nenu sariga ardham chesukunnana ledu* — *did I understand correctly?* — followed by your version of the instruction. The Indian Business English versions above are doing the same work in English. You are not learning a new social skill. You are translating a skill you already have, into English.)

**When to use it.**

- Anything that affects what you have to do *today*. Procedure, deadline, format, recipient.
- Anything you are about to sign. *"Before I sign this, could you walk me through what this section is for?"*
- Anything where doing it wrong would mean redoing it. Forms, reports, lists with specific formats.

**When *not* to use it.**

- Looking up an individual word. Use AI (§5) or a dictionary. Do not interrupt the trainer for *what does AHT mean.*
- Things you can figure out by watching one more time. Watch first. Ask only if watching does not resolve it.
- The same question, twice. *Write the answer down the first time.* Asking twice for the same thing converts the *careful* signal into a *forgetful* signal.

The rule of thumb: *write it down, look it up, ask only if you still cannot figure it out and the answer affects what you do next.*

### 5. AI tools at work — what you can use without asking, and what to ask first about

You have spent Unit 2 learning to use AI as a careful assistant. You drafted, reviewed, corrected. You did not let AI think for you.

At work, the same workflow applies — with one new layer. *Not everything is yours to decide.* Some AI use is private — your own learning, your own first drafts. Some AI use crosses into customer-facing or data-touching territory — and there your employer's policy decides, not you.

`[verify specific AI use policies at Concentrix, Genpact, Apollo Hospitals, and named Hyderabad NGOs for 2026; vendor surveys (Microsoft Work Trend Index 2024–2025, LinkedIn Workforce Confidence Index) provide directional indicators only; actual policies vary by employer and may have changed during the 2024–2026 AI-adoption period]`

**AI tools you can use without asking (in the 2026 Hyderabad market).**

These are private uses. The output is for you only. The data going in is about you only.

- **Drafting an internal message.** A WhatsApp / Slack / Teams message to a colleague or supervisor — you draft, review, and send. Asking AI to check the register is fine. You own the message you send.
- **Looking up word definitions and translations.** Same as a dictionary. *What does AHT mean? What is the difference between PF and ESI on my payslip? What does NACH mandate mean on this form?* These are private learning questions. Use AI freely.
- **Checking your own English for spelling and basic clarity.** Same as Word's spell-check. Same as Grammarly. Standard.
- **Asking AI to explain a concept in simple terms.** *What is first-call resolution? What does compliance mean here? What is the difference between an in-patient and an out-patient?* Private learning. Use freely.

**AI tools you should ask about first.**

These cross into territory where your employer's policy decides what is allowed.

- **Anything customer-facing.** Drafting an email or message that will be sent to a customer outside the company. Generating chat-script responses. Using AI to handle a customer inquiry in real time. The default in Hyderabad BPO in 2026 is *some* customer-facing AI use is permitted under specific systems built by the company; most *unscripted* ChatGPT-into-customer use is not. **Ask before you do this.** Once. Get a clear answer. Then follow it.
- **Anything that uses customer data.** Pasting a customer's name, phone number, address, or account number into ChatGPT or Gemini — *do not do this.* Not for "checking" purposes. Not even if you remove the name. Customer data leaving the company's controlled systems is a privacy violation under [India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023](https://www.meity.gov.in/data-protection-framework) — and most large Hyderabad employers (BPO, hospitals, large NGOs) have policies stricter than the law. The DPDP Act is the legal floor. Your employer's policy is usually above it.

  Hospital reception (Track B) — *patient* data is even more sensitive than customer data. Names, ID numbers, diagnoses, appointment details. *Never* paste these into a public AI. This is one of the few absolute rules of the workplace.

- **Anything internal touching policy, HR, or financial data.** Same logic. Internal company information that should not leave your employer's systems should not be pasted into a third-party AI service.
- **Anything where you are uncertain.** If you cannot decide whether AI use is okay for a specific task — *ask the supervisor first.* The asking is itself the professional move. Asking first is much better than discovering after, that the use was not permitted.

**The one-sentence rule.** *Use AI freely on things that are only about your own work and your own learning. Ask before using AI on anything that touches customers, customer data, or company-policy material.*

**A note on the gap between policy and practice.** Vendor surveys ([Microsoft Work Trend Index](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/worklab/work-trend-index); [LinkedIn Workforce Confidence Index](https://www.linkedin.com/business/talent/blog/talent-acquisition/workforce-confidence)) report that in 2024–2025, employee AI use grew faster than employer policy could keep up with. Many colleagues at your office in 2026 will be using AI in ways the official policy does not explicitly permit. The chapter teaches you to *follow the policy, not the practice.* If you follow the practice and get caught, the practice will not defend you. The policy will be what you are evaluated against. Follow the policy.

### 6. What to wear — by sector

Each track has its own dress conventions. The general rule across all three: *for the first three days, dress one notch more formal than you think is necessary. By Wednesday you have observed what colleagues at your level actually wear. From Thursday, you can adjust.*

The over-formal first-day error is small. The under-formal first-day error is much larger.

`[verify specific dress code policies at Concentrix, Genpact, Foundever (Track A); Apollo Hospitals, Care Hospitals, Yashoda (Track B); CRY, MV Foundation, Mahita (Track C) against current 2026 policies at time of publication]`

**Track A — BPO (Concentrix, Genpact, Foundever, Tech Mahindra BPS, Wipro BPS).**

Business casual. Two acceptable defaults for women:

- *Salwar-kameez or kurti + churidar / leggings* in muted colors — navy, beige, charcoal, dark green, white. Not bright pinks or florals. Dupatta optional but common.
- *Western business-casual* — collared shirt or blouse + trousers + closed-toe shoes (not sandals, not heels above 2 inches). Less common at entry level but increasingly acceptable.

Other practical operational notes for BPO:

- Minimal jewelry. *Small* earrings — they must clear the headset cup. No large bangles — they interfere with headset wear.
- Nails clean, short or moderately long. No extreme nail art.
- Hair tied back so the headset boom sits properly.
- ID badge worn at all times when on-site. Non-negotiable in BPO security culture.

**Track B — Hospital reception / patient coordinator (Apollo, Care Hospitals, Yashoda, Continental).**

Often uniform-supplied — typically by Day 1 or Day 2. While waiting for the uniform, default to kurti + churidar with dupatta in conservative colors. Once the uniform is issued (a kurti-style or sari-style uniform with the hospital logo and your name badge), wear it on every shift.

Healthcare adds two specific rules:

- *Nails very short. No nail polish.* Infection-control protocol at most Hyderabad hospitals (`[verify with specific employer]`).
- *Closed-toe shoes.* For safety in case of spills, sharp objects, or running between departments.
- *Hair tied back* — non-negotiable.

**Track C — NGO (CRY, HelpAge, MV Foundation, Mahita, Ashray, Vidya Bharati).**

The most variable. For field-coordinator roles that involve community visits — kurti + leggings or salwar-kameez, closed-toe walking shoes, dupatta for cultural appropriateness in conservative neighborhoods. For office-based admin roles — similar to BPO casual, often slightly less formal.

NGO culture is less hierarchical-via-clothing than BPO or hospital. For the first week, the same conservative kurti + churidar default as the other tracks is safe. By Friday you have seen what colleagues wear and can adjust.

### 7. What to do when you make a mistake in week 1

You will make a small mistake. A form filled incorrectly. An email sent to the wrong person. A name mispronounced. A process step skipped. This is normal. This is *expected*.

What is *not* normal is hiding the mistake. The honest move is fast, named, and short.

A four-step recovery script:

**Step 1 — name the mistake to the person who needs to know.** *"Sir, I need to tell you — I sent the email to the wrong person. I have already sent a correction. I am sorry."* Specific. Quick. No long explanation.

**Step 2 — one apology is enough.** Indian workplace convention is one *I am sorry*. Five *I am sorries* is unprofessional. The first apology is acknowledgment. The fifth is performance. Stop at one.

**Step 3 — ask what you should do differently.** *"Could you tell me what I should have done? I want to make sure I do it correctly next time."* This is the §4 clarification phrasing again — same shape, applied to recovery.

**Step 4 — move on.** Once the mistake is named, the correction is in motion, and you have asked the question — the conversation ends. Continuing to ruminate verbally about the mistake reads as inability to handle pressure. Take the lesson. Update what you do next. Get back to work.

The Indian supervisor reads this favorably. *She knew she made a mistake. She handled it like a professional. She is going to be fine.*

## Worked example — Priya's first week at Foundever

The full five-day arc. Each day's load-bearing moves. The shape you can imitate when your first week comes.

(Priya is the running learner-vignette in this book — a composite, not a real student. Sneha and Aarti, the peers introduced here, are also composite.)

**Setup.** Priya received a job offer from Foundever Hyderabad — domestic process, day shift, Customer Service Associate. Joining letter dated Monday 8:30 AM, Madhapur office. She has completed the mock interview (Unit 8). She has accepted the offer in writing.

### Sunday evening

The night before is when the first week is set up — or set up to fail.

Priya's Sunday-night checklist:

- Joining letter, original 10th certificate, original 12th certificate (in progress — provisional letter for now), original Aadhaar card, two passport-size photographs, all in one folder. Folder placed by the door.
- Clothes laid out: navy kurti, white churidar, matching dupatta, closed-toe shoes, small earrings, hair-tie. All on the chair next to the door.
- Phone fully charged. Backup power bank also charged. Office address pinned on Google Maps with the route saved offline.
- Alarm set for 6:00 AM. Backup alarm at 6:05.
- One sentence prepared, in English, for when she is introduced: *"Good morning. I am Priya. I am joining the customer service team today. I am happy to be here."*

### Monday — Day 1

She wakes at 6:00. Bathes, dresses, eats the breakfast Sister has left on the table. Out the door by 7:00. Reaches Madhapur at 7:30 — 30 minutes before the joining time.

She waits in the security lobby. At 7:55 she reports to HR.

The HR onboarding day is mostly paperwork. Joining letter signature. ID badge issuance. Bank-account verification (for salary credit). NDA signature. Training-batch assignment. Form after form.

The temptation: sign quickly to look efficient. The right move: *read what you sign, and ask before signing anything you do not fully understand.*

One section on the NDA mentions *intellectual property*. Priya does not know what this means in this context. She uses the §4 clarification phrasing:

*"Ma'am, before I sign this, could you walk me through what this section is for? I want to make sure I understand."*

HR is used to this question from new hires. She explains in two sentences — anything Priya creates as part of her job (call records, summaries, suggestions for process improvement) belongs to the company, not to Priya personally. Priya nods. Signs. The HR person updates her mental note — *this girl reads what she signs.*

She is assigned to the Tuesday training batch. Sent home at 1 PM. Day 1 is done.

### Tuesday — Day 2

Training begins at 9:00 AM. Priya arrives at 8:30. She has used Google Maps to scope the room location yesterday afternoon, so she does not get lost in the building.

The training batch is about 30 new hires. The trainer is a senior employee — addressed as *Madam* by everyone. Priya sits in the middle of the room — not the front (over-eager), not the back (avoiding-attention).

The trainer covers: the company, the client, the product. Priya understands maybe 70% of what is said. The other 30% falls into two groups:

- *Vocabulary she does not know yet.* KPI. AHT. FCR. CRM. (She has seen some of these from Unit 4 pre-prep. Hearing them spoken is different from reading them.)
- *Procedural details she has not yet integrated.* Which form goes where. What happens at the end of a call. Where the call summary form lives.

The chapter's rule applies: *do not interrupt the trainer to ask about every vocabulary word.* Write down the unfamiliar word. Look it up at lunch or in the evening. Save clarification questions for procedural matters that affect what she has to do tomorrow.

At lunch, she eats with two peers from her batch — Sneha (from Karimnagar, Telugu) and Aarti (from Hyderabad, Hindi-Telugu). The peer-level conversation is mixed Telugu / Hindi / English — the standard Hyderabad code-switch. Priya participates. This is the *social-acceptance* dimension of onboarding (Bauer & Erdogan, §1). Learning their names is today's social goal. She does.

At the end of training she has one specific clarification question — about a procedure. She uses the §4 phrasing:

*"Madam, I want to make sure I have this right. The call summary form — do I fill it during the call or after the call ends?"*

The trainer says: *"After. As soon as the call ends."*

Priya: *"Thank you, Madam."*

The interaction is professional. It adds one specific piece of clarity. The trainer updates her mental model — *this girl asks specific questions. She listens.*

### Wednesday — Day 3

Training continues. New material on the product specifics.

In the evening, at the residence, Priya opens ChatGPT on her phone. She has two concepts from today she did not fully follow:

- *What is a "ticket" in BPO context?*
- *What does "first-call resolution" mean in practice?*

She asks ChatGPT. ChatGPT explains both in two paragraphs each. Priya makes notes. This is the §5 *use AI freely for own learning* move. She does not paste any customer data, any company-specific information. The questions are about general BPO vocabulary. Private learning. Safe use.

She does not use ChatGPT for anything customer-facing. She has not seen a customer yet anyway — training comes first.

### Thursday — Day 4

Day where her observation of colleagues' dress pays off. By now, she has seen — most peers wear kurti + churidar similar to hers; a few wear western shirts and trousers; the trainer wears a saree, but the trainer is senior, not a peer. Priya's choice was correct for her level. She continues the same dress code.

Mid-morning, she receives her first instruction from her *shift supervisor* — a different person from the trainer. The supervisor sends an internal message:

> *"Priya, please prepare a list of the names in your training batch and email it to me by EOD. Thanks, Anjali Reddy."*

Priya's first reaction: *I do not know exactly what should be on the list. Names only? Names and phone numbers? Names with the process they are assigned to?*

She does not guess. She uses the §4 phrasing:

*"Ma'am, before I prepare the list, could you tell me — should it be just names, or names with phone numbers, or names with our assigned processes?"*

Supervisor: *"Names and phone numbers. By 5 PM. Thanks."*

Priya makes the list. She uses ChatGPT to check her email's English — register, spelling, structure — before sending. (Unit 2 workflow, applied at work. Private use, no customer data, safe.)

She sends the email by 4:00 PM. Well before EOD. Sign-off: *Best regards, Priya.* (Unit 1 register.)

The supervisor opens the email at 4:30, reads it, marks the task complete. Updates her own mental model — *Priya asked the right question first, delivered before deadline, formatted cleanly.* A small piece of professional reliability has been established.

### Friday — Day 5

Last day of week 1. Training continues. The product training is approaching its first quiz on Monday.

At the end of the day, the trainer does a brief check-in with each new hire individually. She tells Priya:

- *"You ask specific clarifying questions. That is good."*
- *"You take notes. That is good."*
- *"You have been on time every day. That is good."*

These are exactly the §1 indicators of successful onboarding. Priya has cleared the first-week bar.

What she has *not* done. She has not received any customer calls. She has not handled any customer data. She has not impressed anyone as the standout new hire of the month. She has been a respectful, prompt, attentive trainee.

This is enough.

By the Bauer-Erdogan four-factor framework — *role clarity, self-efficacy, social acceptance, culture knowledge* — she has cleared the first-week version of each. She knows what is expected of her in training. She believes she can do it. Her peers in the batch greet her by name. She has a working sense of the company's hierarchy and culture.

She goes home for the weekend. The job is still in place. Course 2 ends here.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Thinking your English is the problem.** Almost every girl in your batch will arrive on Monday convinced her English is what will get her fired. The data says otherwise (§1). Your English is enough for week 1. What is in question is punctuality and clarification-asking. Spend your worry budget on those two, not on English. Your English will improve naturally over the next six months. Punctuality is a Monday decision.

**Mistake 2 — Not asking, because you are embarrassed.** This is the most common version of failure mode #2 from §1. The supervisor said something. You did not fully follow. You did not ask. You decided you would figure it out. You are embarrassed to look like you did not understand.

The honest, hard truth: *not asking, here, costs much more than asking*. The supervisor expects new hires to ask. The supervisor is *worried* about new hires who never ask — because that means either they understood everything perfectly (rare) or they are bluffing (common). The clarification question, asked using §4 phrasing, is *the way* a new hire signals competence. Skipping it does not save face. It loses face later, when the work comes out wrong.

**Mistake 3 — Asking too much, about the wrong things.** The opposite failure. You ask about every word the trainer uses. Every vocabulary item. Every small detail. This signals you cannot work independently. The rule from §4: *write it down, look it up, ask only if you still cannot figure it out and the answer affects what you do next.*

**Mistake 4 — Pasting customer information into ChatGPT.** The single fastest way to get fired in week 1 — at a BPO, a hospital, or an NGO that handles personal data. The DPDP Act 2023 makes this a legal issue. Your employer's policy makes it a firing issue. The supervisor's trust makes it a relationship issue. *Never* paste a customer name, account number, phone number, or any identifying detail into a public AI tool. Not even to "check the spelling of the name." The data leaving the company's controlled system is the violation. The intent does not change the violation.

**Mistake 5 — Trying to make friends with your supervisor in week 1.** Indian workplace hierarchy makes the supervisor relationship a *professional* relationship in week 1, not a personal one. You do not need to know what music she likes, where she grew up, or what her weekend is. You need to address her correctly, listen to her, complete her tasks on time, and ask the right clarification questions. By month 3 — *maybe* — a casual personal exchange becomes natural. Not in week 1. Forced friendliness with the supervisor reads as forward. Take your time.

**Mistake 6 — Using "first names" with the supervisor on day 3 because the supervisor used your first name on day 2.** The asymmetry is normal (§3). The supervisor uses your first name. You use *Madam* or *Sir*. This is not a sign of distance. This is correct Indian Business English workplace register. Hold it through week 1 and week 2 even if invited. By week 3 you can switch in casual moments. Even then, *Sir* or *Madam* in formal moments is appropriate.

**Mistake 7 — Feeling like a fraud and waiting to be exposed.** Most new hires feel this in week 1. Researchers call it *imposter phenomenon* ([Clance & Imes 1978, original paper](https://www.paulineroseclance.com/pdf/ip_high_achieving_women.pdf)). The feeling is widely shared and is largely *uncorrelated* with how well you are actually performing. The girl who feels most confident is often performing worst. The girl who feels most fraudulent is often performing within normal range. Your feelings about your performance are not your performance. Trust the Friday check-in (§Worked example), not the inside-your-head monologue.

### Exercise 1 — Write your Sunday-night checklist *(Bloom: Apply)*

Before your first real first-day, you will need a checklist. Write yours now, even if you do not yet have the job offer.

Use these five categories. Fill in the specifics for your track.

a) **Documents.** What papers will you carry on day 1? (Joining letter, original certificates, Aadhaar, photos, anything sector-specific.)

b) **Clothes.** What will you wear? (Specific colors and items — kurti color, dupatta, shoes. Use §6 for guidance by track.)

c) **Phone.** Battery charged, address pinned, backup option saved.

d) **Alarms.** Time. Backup time. (One alarm fails sometimes. Two never do.)

e) **One sentence prepared in English** for when you are introduced. Write it out. Read it aloud once.

Bring this checklist to your Zoom session. The volunteer will check it with you.

### Exercise 2 — Rewrite three clarification questions *(Bloom: Apply)*

Below are three questions phrased the *bad* way (§4). Rewrite each in the good way — the one that signals professional competence.

a) *"Sir, I don't understand the form. Can you tell me what to do?"*

b) *"Ma'am, this is confusing. Please explain."*

c) *"I don't know what this word means. Help?"*

Use the variations from §4. Try each one out loud, in front of a mirror or recorded on your phone. Listen for whether it sounds like the §4 examples — same register, same shape.

### Exercise 3 — Identify the AI-use scenario *(Bloom: Analyze)*

For each scenario, decide: *(a) Use AI freely without asking, or (b) Ask the supervisor first.* Use the §5 rule.

1. Drafting an internal WhatsApp message to a peer about lunch plans.
2. Looking up what *AHT* means after hearing it in training.
3. Drafting an email reply to a customer who has complained about a billing error.
4. Asking ChatGPT to explain *first-call resolution* in simple English.
5. Pasting a customer's name and phone number into ChatGPT to ask for the right way to address them.
6. Asking AI to check the spelling of a status-update message you are about to send to your supervisor.
7. Generating a script in real time to handle a difficult customer on the phone.
8. Asking AI to translate a Hindi phrase a peer used at lunch.

(Answers in the spirit of §5: 1 — use freely. 2 — use freely. 3 — ask first. 4 — use freely. 5 — *do not do this even if permitted* — this is a customer-data violation. 6 — use freely. 7 — ask first. 8 — use freely.)

### Exercise 4 — The first-week plan *(Bloom: Create)*

Write a one-page plan for your first five days. Include:

a) **Monday — joining day.** What will you bring? When will you leave the residence? What is your one rehearsed introduction sentence?

b) **Tuesday–Thursday — training days.** Where will you sit? What will you write down? At what time of day will you save your specific clarification questions for the trainer? How will you spend your lunch break?

c) **Friday — end of week 1.** What will you have done by Friday afternoon that means the supervisor knows your name, has seen you arrive on time every day, has heard you ask one or two specific clarifying questions, and has seen you complete a small task by deadline?

This is the *goal of the first week*, written in your own words for your own situation. You can use ChatGPT or Gemini to check your draft — paste the plan and ask: *"Is this a reasonable first-week plan for a BPO / hospital / NGO entry-level role in Hyderabad? Is anything missing or unrealistic?"* Read the AI's response carefully. Apply only the changes that are actually realistic for you.

## For volunteers / sisters' notes

*A note for Humanitarians AI volunteers running this week's Zoom session, and for the Salesian sisters supporting the girls between sessions and into the first week of the real job.*

**The framing claim of this unit — that punctuality and competent clarification-asking outweigh English fluency in the first week — comes from organizational socialization research applied to the Hyderabad entry-level context.** The theoretical anchor is the Bauer-Erdogan four-factor model of newcomer adjustment ([Bauer & Erdogan 2011, *APA Handbook*](https://www.apa.org/pubs/books/4311508); [Bauer et al. 2007 meta-analysis, *Journal of Applied Psychology*](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/2007-07951-006)). The four factors — role clarity, self-efficacy, social acceptance, organizational culture knowledge — translate operationally for first-week entry-level work into the two behavioral skills the chapter foregrounds. The framing is the author's pedagogical compression of converging evidence from NASSCOM workforce reports and Hyderabad HR practitioner consensus rather than a single quoted study. `[verify with current Hyderabad HR practitioner interviews where possible]`

**The clarification-phrasing rule is anchored in the workplace help-seeking literature.** ([Lee 1997, *Journal of Applied Psychology*, "When the Going Gets Tough, Do the Tough Ask for Help?"](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1997-43178-002); [Morrison 1993, *Academy of Management Journal*, "Newcomer Information Seeking"](https://journals.aom.org/doi/10.5465/256659).) The key finding — that *manner* of help-seeking determines the social reading, not the *content* of the help-seeking — is robust across these studies. The specific phrasings in §4 are pedagogical scripts derived from this evidence base; they are designed to memorize, not to compose under pressure. The rehearsal-in-the-session is the move that consolidates them. Have each girl say each variant out loud once before the session ends.

**The "values as professional assets" reframe (§2) is the most editorially delicate move in the chapter.** The TIKTOC explicitly calls for this framing — the sisters' teaching has prepared the girls for the workplace, and this should be named. The risk is one of two failure modes: patronizing (the values are residence-program-specific products), or sectarian (the values are a religious endorsement). The chapter's intended framing is between these — *punctuality, honesty, and respect are universal professional behaviors that a disciplined upbringing, including a Salesian residence, reliably develops.* The sisters' role in supporting the girl through week 1 of the real job is the *holding* role — noticing whether she is sleeping, eating, arriving on time; supporting the wake-up if necessary; being available for an evening debrief about a difficult moment. The work is no longer instruction. It is presence.

**On the *I am going to be exposed as a fraud* anxiety (Mistake 7).** The Pekrun control-value frame from Unit 8 ([Pekrun 2006, *Educational Psychology Review*](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-006-9029-9)) applies again here. Week-1 imposter feelings respond to *increases in perceived control* — predictable structures, rehearsed responses, the small visible wins of the Friday check-in. They do not respond to *reassurance about confidence*. If a girl calls the residence on Wednesday evening reporting that she feels like she is going to be fired tomorrow — the volunteer or sister move is *not* to reassure ("you are doing great!"). The move is to walk her through the next concrete control-raising action — what is she preparing for Thursday morning, has she set her alarm, what is the one specific thing she will ask the trainer about tomorrow. The action restores the control. The feeling steps back, eventually.

**On the gap between AI policy and AI practice at Hyderabad employers in 2026.** Vendor surveys (Microsoft Work Trend Index, LinkedIn Workforce Confidence Index) suggest employee AI use has outpaced employer policy adaptation. The chapter teaches the policy, not the practice. This is the safer error. A girl following the official policy who notices peers using AI more liberally should *not* take that as license. Peer use does not create policy permission. If caught, peer use does not defend her. Follow the policy. (If a peer's AI use looks like it is going to compromise customer data — that is reportable to the supervisor. Not in week 1, generally. By week 3 it becomes the responsible move.)

**The Course 2 / Course 3 boundary.** The hardest editorial discipline in this chapter. The reader will want career-trajectory work — promotion, raise negotiation, first-paycheck financial literacy, longer-term role-fit conversations. The chapter rigorously does not provide this. Course 3 will. Volunteers and sisters: please honor this boundary too. A girl asking *"How long until I get a raise?"* in week 1 is asking the wrong question. The right answer is *"Let us focus on getting to Friday with the job in place. We can talk about the longer arc after the first month."* The discipline is for her good, not for the program's tidiness.
