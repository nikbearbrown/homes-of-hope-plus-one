# Unit 7A — The Phone Screen

*Five questions. Twelve minutes. Survive, don't outshine.*

---

## Learning outcomes

By the end of this unit, you will be able to:

- **(Apply)** Answer the five most common BPO phone-screening questions in clear professional English — including *"tell me about yourself"* and *"why do you want this job"* — in a 10-to-15 minute screening call with HR or a team lead.

---

## The problem this unit solves

In the next four weeks, you will apply to several BPO jobs. Some of those applications will produce a phone call. The call will come on a weekday morning, around 11 AM, from a Hyderabad number you do not recognize. The caller will say her name is Pooja Sharma or Anjali Reddy or Rohan Verma, that she is calling from Concentrix HR or Genpact Talent Acquisition or Foundever Recruitment, and that she has 10 to 15 minutes to talk to you about the role you applied for.

That call is the **phone screen**.

The phone screen is not the interview. The phone screen is the gate before the interview. If you pass it, you are invited to a walk-in at the company office, where the actual hiring interview happens. If you do not pass it, the company never calls back. Most of the people who apply for BPO jobs in Hyderabad do not pass the phone screen. The people who do pass it are usually not the most talented — they are the ones who prepared for the five known questions.

This is the most reassuring sentence in this whole course: **the phone screen is a known sequence with known questions**. Almost every entry-level BPO phone screen in Hyderabad asks the same five questions. We know what they are. We can rehearse them. By the end of this unit you will have prepared answers for all five.

You will also learn something the offshoring stereotypes do not tell you: **the Hyderabad BPO entry-level phone screen does not test for an American accent.** It does not test for British accent. It does not test for accent at all. It tests for *structure under load* — can you hold a 90-second answer in clear English without trailing off, without switching to Telugu, without panic. That is a real test. You will pass it with what you already have. The accent question is a 2000s memory that the industry has substantially moved past. We will name this directly and dismiss it directly. (More in §6.)

One honest framing for the whole unit. The phone screen logic is **asymmetric**. The screener is not looking for the best candidate in the world. She is looking for *no obvious reason to reject*. She does 20 to 40 of these calls a day. She has a checklist. Your job is to clear the checklist, not to dazzle the screener. A girl who tries to be impressive triggers the screener's *this candidate will be high-maintenance* alarm. A girl who is calm, clear, and structured passes through. **Surviving is the goal. Not outshining.**

That is the most useful sentence in the unit. Read it again.

## Opening case

Two girls receive a phone-screen call on the same Tuesday morning at 11 AM. Both applied to a Customer Service Associate role at Concentrix Hyderabad through Naukri last week. Both have completed Units 1 through 6A. Both have basic English.

(One of them is Priya — the running learner-vignette in this book, a composite, not a real student.)

**Girl 1.** Her phone rings. She picks up. *"Hello?"* The screener says her name and the company. Asks *"Is this a good time?"*. Girl 1 says *"Yes ma'am."*

The first question arrives: *"Tell me a little about yourself."*

Girl 1's answer: *"My name is [name]. I am 19 years old. I am from Hyderabad. I have completed 12th class. I want to work in BPO because I need a job and I think it is a good opportunity. My family is supportive. I am willing to work hard. Thank you, ma'am."*

The answer takes 35 seconds. There is nothing wrong with any individual sentence. The problem is that there is no structure, no specificity, and no signal of what she brings to the role. The screener's pen marks *generic, short, no structure*.

Next question: *"Why are you specifically interested in Concentrix?"*

Girl 1: *"Ma'am, Concentrix is a very good company. I want to grow. I want to learn. I am very interested in this opportunity."*

The screener has heard this answer 60 times this month. Pen marks *generic*.

By question 4 — *"What is one area you would like to improve in yourself?"* — Girl 1 says *"I don't think I have any major weakness, ma'am, I am always willing to learn."* Pen marks *not self-aware, auto-filter*.

The call ends at 9 minutes. The screener thanks Girl 1 and says *we will be in touch*. The company does not call her back. Girl 1 spends the next two weeks waiting for the call. She does not understand why it does not come.

**Girl 2 — Priya.** Her phone rings. She picks up. *"Yes, this is Priya. Good morning."*

Pooja Sharma, the HR recruiter, introduces herself and the role. Confirms Priya has 15 minutes. Asks the first question.

Priya delivers a 90-second answer. Present (who she is now), past (the residence, the course, what she has prepared for), future (the role she is looking for, why Concentrix). The structure is invisible because the answer sounds natural. The structure is doing the work underneath.

Question 2 — *Why Concentrix?* Priya names one concrete fact about the company (HITEC City and Madhapur operations, voice and chat customer experience for international clients). She names what attracts her (structured training for new associates, growth path). She names what she wants to do (start as customer service associate, grow into team lead over time). Specific. Three components. 60 seconds.

Question 3 — strength. Priya names one strength — staying calm when others are upset — and gives one specific example from the residence. The example is the answer. The strength is the label on top.

Question 4 — weakness. Priya names a *real* weakness — speaking English faster in spoken conversation. She names what she is doing about it — daily practice, AI-mediated role play, course role-play exercises. She names where the weakness becomes a non-issue — on the job, through practice. Honest. Non-disqualifying. With an improvement plan.

Question 5 — behavioral. *"Tell me about a time you handled a difficult situation."* Priya draws on the residence story about two younger residents sharing a study table. Situation, what she did, what happened, what she learned, and one sentence connecting it to customer service. The whole answer takes 90 seconds.

The screener does the logistics check. Priya handles the night-shift question honestly (residence curfew constraint, willing on day shift first). She names a sensible salary range she has researched. She asks one question at the end about the next step.

The call ends at 12 minutes. The screener marks Priya for the walk-in. The talent team calls her three days later.

Same job, same morning, same two 19-year-old girls. The difference was not English. The difference was that Priya had rehearsed the five questions. Girl 1 had not.

This unit teaches you to be Priya on that Tuesday morning.

## Core content

### 1. The phone screen is a known sequence

Hyderabad BPO entry-level phone screens follow a consistent shape across the major employers — Concentrix, Genpact, Foundever, Teleperformance, HGS, Tech Mahindra BPO. This is documented across [Naukri Insights](https://www.naukri.com/blog/) `[verify exact post]`, [Glassdoor interview reviews](https://www.glassdoor.co.in/), and [AmbitionBox interview-question repositories](https://www.ambitionbox.com/) `[verify with current 2026 reviews]`. The seven-step shape:

1. **Greeting and identification** (1–2 minutes) — screener says her name, the company, the role. Asks if you have 10–15 minutes. Sometimes asks if you can do the call in English.
2. **Self-introduction** (2–3 minutes) — *"Tell me about yourself."*
3. **Motivation** (1–2 minutes) — *"Why this company / why this job?"*
4. **Strength and weakness** (2–3 minutes) — sometimes one question, sometimes both asked separately.
5. **Behavioral scenario** (2–3 minutes) — *"Tell me about a time you handled a difficult situation"* or *"How would you handle an angry customer?"*
6. **Logistics check** (1–2 minutes) — shift availability, travel time, salary expectations, notice period, willingness to attend the walk-in.
7. **Your questions** (1 minute) — *"Do you have any questions for me?"*

The five canonical questions from the learning outcome map onto steps 2, 3, 4, and 5. Step 6 is logistics. Step 7 is your turn.

Each step has a known structure. Each known structure has a known good answer pattern. We will go through them one at a time.

### 2. "Tell me about yourself" — the present / past / future structure

This is the question every interviewer in every sector asks first. It is also the question most candidates answer worst. The mistake: treating it as biography. The fix: treating it as a *professional summary* with three parts.

**Present (about 30 seconds).** Who are you right now?

*"My name is Priya. I am 19 years old, and I am from Secunderabad, Hyderabad. I have completed my intermediate — 12th class — and I have been preparing for an entry-level role in customer service."*

**Past (about 40 seconds).** What in your background prepares you for this role?

*"For the last six years, I have lived at the Homes of Hope residence run by the Salesian sisters. During that time I helped take care of younger residents, including helping them with their homework and resolving small disagreements between them. So I have practical experience in handling people and finding solutions. Over the last two months, I have also completed an intensive English communication and customer-service training program — including how to handle a customer-service call using a structured approach, with role-play exercises."*

**Future (about 20 seconds).** Why does this role come next?

*"I am looking for an entry-level customer service position where I can use these skills, and I am interested in Concentrix because of its large operations in Hyderabad and its focus on customer experience. That is why I am here today."*

Total: 90 seconds. Time it on your phone when you practice. Going significantly over 90 seconds is the most common entry-level mistake — the nervous candidate fills time with detail the screener does not need.

**Three rules for this answer.**

**Rule 1 — Professional summary, not autobiography.** This is the hardest teaching move in the unit. Your instinct is to tell more. The natural answer — the one you would give to a sister or a friend — is too personal for the register.

The screener does not need to know:

- Why you live at the residence
- The circumstances of your family
- Anything about your parents unless directly relevant
- Anything about your siblings unless directly relevant

The screener does need to know:

- You have lived in a structured residence community for six years
- You have practical experience helping younger residents
- You completed a recent professional training program
- You are looking for an entry-level customer service role

The screener will hire you for *what you can do*. Not for *what you have been through*. The professional summary version of your past is not denying your story. It is the version of your story that the workplace context calls for. The fuller version is for people who have earned it.

**Rule 2 — Build the answer once and reuse it.** You write the present/past/future answer once. You edit it (with AI assist for register — Unit 2 workflow). You memorize the structure. You practice it spoken until it comes out without translation. Once you have a 90-second polished answer, you have it. Different interviews do not need different *Tell me about yourself* answers. The same answer works for every entry-level voice-process role you apply to. The reuse is the point.

**Rule 3 — Match the answer to the CV.** Whatever you say in the spoken answer, the CV must say the same thing. If your CV (Unit 3) says you are *in* 12th class, do not say *"I have completed 12th class"* on the call. The screener will check. The mismatch will cost you. The 90-second answer is built from CV-grade English, in spoken form. The CV and the answer must agree.

### 3. "Why this company" — the three-part answer

The motivation question has three components in the well-answered version. Practitioner interview-coaching consensus across Naukri, Glassdoor, AmbitionBox, and LinkedIn Learning converges on this shape.

**Component 1 — What you know about the company.** One concrete fact. *"Concentrix is one of the largest customer experience companies in Hyderabad, with operations in Madhapur and HITEC City, and I know the customer service associate role serves international clients in voice process."* You do not need to be impressive. You need to be *specific*. The fact tells the screener you spent five minutes researching the company before the call.

**Component 2 — Why the role fits you.** The bridge from your background to the role. *"I am looking for a structured customer service role where I can use the communication training I have completed. Voice process work matches what I have prepared for."* Notice — this connects to what you said about yourself in the previous answer. The two answers reinforce each other.

**Component 3 — What you hope to do there.** The forward statement, calibrated for realism. *"I want to start as a customer service associate, learn the operations thoroughly, and grow into a team-lead role over time."* This avoids the two trap answers — *"I want to grow"* (too vague) and *"I want to be CEO in 5 years"* (too aspirational and unreal).

Total answer: 45 to 60 seconds.

**Four things to actively avoid in this answer:**

- **The generic answer.** *"I want to grow."* The screener has heard this 60 times this week. Replace it with the specific three-part answer above.
- **The mercenary answer.** *"I need a salary."* Even if true, the screener cannot use this. Translate need into role-fit: *"I am looking for an entry-level role where I can apply my training."*
- **The flattery answer.** *"Concentrix is the best company in the world."* Reads as filler.
- **Confusing the company with the role.** Saying *"because I want to work in BPO"* when the question was *"why Concentrix specifically"*. The answer must name the specific company.

### 4. Strength — one named strength, one specific example

This question tests one thing — can you assess yourself and back it up with evidence?

The shape is *one named strength + one specific example + one sentence linking it to the role*. About 60 seconds.

*"One of my strengths, ma'am, is that I stay calm when other people are upset. For example, when there were disagreements between younger residents at the home, I was usually the one who could sit with both sides, listen to what each one wanted, and help find a solution that worked for both. I think this strength would be useful in customer service, especially when a customer is frustrated and needs someone who can stay calm and listen."*

Three parts visible in that answer:

- *One of my strengths is [staying calm when others are upset]* — the named strength.
- *For example, [at the home, between younger residents, here is what I did]* — the specific example.
- *This would be useful in customer service when [the customer is frustrated]* — the link to the role.

The example is the answer. The strength is the label on top of the example. A strength without an example is empty. An example with a clear strength label is evidence.

Alternative strengths that work for this audience:

- *Patience and steady follow-through.* Example: tutoring a younger resident through four months of evening English homework. Link: same patience helps with customers who need the situation explained more than once.
- *Reliability and punctuality.* Example: the residence's morning routine, never missing a duty, the sisters' training in showing up on time. Link: voice-process work depends on agents who are at their desk on time and through the shift.
- *Listening before deciding.* Example: any residence situation where you heard both sides before suggesting a fix. Link: discovery move in customer service — let the customer explain before acting.

Pick the one closest to your real experience. The example must be real. The screener will sometimes ask a follow-up — *"Tell me more about what you did with the younger residents"* — and your follow-up must be specific.

### 5. Weakness — the most contested question, and why "I work too hard" is a fail

The weakness question is the one most candidates fear. It is also the one with the clearest right answer pattern.

**The three failure modes — all auto-filter.**

**Failure 1 — Fake-weakness-that-is-a-strength.** *"My weakness is that I work too hard."* *"I am too much of a perfectionist."* *"I care too much."* Every recruiter blog across Glassdoor, Naukri, and AmbitionBox names this as auto-filter. The screener has heard it thousands of times. She reads it as *this candidate is not self-aware and is willing to be dishonest with me*.

**Failure 2 — Disqualifying weakness.** *"I get angry easily."* *"I cannot handle stress."* *"I struggle with English."* For a customer-service voice role, naming a disqualifier in the weakness question is exactly that. Pick something *real but not role-disqualifying*.

**Failure 3 — No weakness.** *"I don't really have any weaknesses, ma'am."* Auto-filter. Reads as *not self-aware*.

**The shape that works.** *A real, non-disqualifying weakness + the specific thing you are doing to improve it.* About 45 to 60 seconds.

The template that works for most girls in your position:

*"One area I am still working on is speaking English faster in spoken conversation. My vocabulary is good and I can read and write professional English, but in spoken conversation I sometimes need a small moment to choose the right word. I have been practicing every day for the last two months, including with role-play exercises and an AI practice tool, and I have improved significantly. I will continue to practice on the job, and I am confident I will improve further."*

Why this works:

- It names a real weakness the screener can already hear on the call. Naming what she can hear *disarms* her suspicion — instead of *this candidate is not self-aware*, the read is *this candidate is honest about herself*.
- It shows specific action — *daily practice, role-play exercises, AI tool*. Not vague intention.
- It does not disqualify — entry-level voice process is exactly the job where this improves through practice.
- It ends with confidence — *I will continue to practice* — not with hedging.

If you want a different weakness, two alternatives that also work:

- *"Time management when I am working on multiple things at once. I am still learning to prioritize. I have started using a simple list every day to track what I need to do first."*
- *"Speaking up in a group when I disagree with someone older. I tend to listen first and not interrupt. I am learning to share my view politely when I have something useful to add."*

Pick the one closest to your real experience. The honesty itself is part of the answer. Trying to hide what the screener can already hear damages the credibility of everything else you say.

(One supporting note. The structured-interview research literature — Schmidt and Hunter's 1998 meta-analysis in *Psychological Bulletin* of 85 years of personnel-selection research — shows that *structured behavioral answers* predict job performance more reliably than unstructured rambling. The honesty-plus-improvement-plan structure is one of those structures. It works in the data and it works in the room. [ResearchGate link](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/13782725).)

### 6. The fifth question — behavioral scenario — and how to handle it without paid work history

The fifth canonical question is the behavioral scenario. Common Hyderabad BPO variants:

- *"Tell me about a time you handled a difficult person."*
- *"How would you respond if a customer was rude to you?"*
- *"Tell me about a time you made a mistake. What did you do?"*
- *"Tell me about a time you worked in a team and someone disagreed with you."*

The answer shape is the *situation framework* — Situation, what you did, what happened, what you learned. Same shape Unit 8 will use for the full mock interview. About 90 seconds total.

The hard problem for you: you do not have paid work history. Your STAR-style example must come from somewhere else.

**Your three real source pools:**

- **Six years at the Homes of Hope residence.** Community-living responsibilities. Helping younger residents. Routine domestic situations. Working within a residential community structure.
- **The sisters' character and conduct training.** Punctuality. Honesty. Working with others. The values the sisters taught you that the workplace also expects.
- **This course itself.** Two months of disciplined work. Role-play exercises. Working with the Humanitarians AI volunteer. Completing the assignments week after week.

**The load-bearing teaching move of this unit: residence experience IS work experience for behavioral-question purposes.** The screener does not need a paid-job context. She needs a situation where you were responsible for something, took action, produced a result, and reflected on what you learned. That can come from anywhere real.

Worked example. Question: *"Tell me about a time you handled a difficult situation, maybe a disagreement between people that you helped resolve."*

Answer:

> *"Last year, two younger residents at the home — one was 8 years old, the other was 10 — had an ongoing argument about sharing a study table during evening homework time. They were getting upset every evening, and the sister in charge was busy with other students. I was 18 then, and one of the older residents responsible for evening study supervision. I sat down with both of them separately, asked each one what she wanted, and then suggested a schedule — one would use the table from 6 to 7, the other from 7 to 8. They both agreed to try it. After one week, the schedule was working and there were no more arguments. What I learned was that letting each person say what they wanted before suggesting a solution made the solution acceptable to both of them. I think this would help in customer service when a customer is upset — letting the customer explain first, before suggesting a resolution."*

Structure visible: Situation (residence, two younger girls arguing) → Task (she was responsible for supervision) → Action (separate conversations, proposed schedule) → Result (worked after one week) → Learning (let people explain first; connects to customer service).

This answer is not *impressive* in absolute terms — it is two children sharing a table. For an entry-level voice-process role with a 19-year-old fresher, it is exactly the right register. The screener hears: she has been responsible, she takes initiative, she gets results, she reflects on what she learned, and she connects her experience to the role. That is the full signal the question tests for.

**The exercise to do this week:** generate three of these story answers from your real life. Three gives you flexibility — the screener may ask about teamwork, conflict, or a mistake, and you can pick the most-fitting story.

(One special version of this question is *"How would you handle an angry customer?"* — pure hypothetical. The answer is a compressed HEARD walkthrough from Unit 6A. *"First, I would listen to the customer without interrupting. Then I would acknowledge what they are feeling — for example, 'I can hear this has been frustrating for you.' Then I would apologize for the inconvenience. Then I would explain what I am going to do, in specific steps, with a time frame. Then I would confirm at the end that they have everything they need."* That is a 30-second answer. You compress HEARD. You do not say *HEARD* by name — you describe the moves. The screener recognizes the framework.)

### 7. What the screen actually tests — and the accent myth dismissed

This is the load-bearing section of the unit. Read it twice.

The Hyderabad BPO entry-level phone screen tests five things:

- **Clarity.** Can the screener understand what you are saying without straining?
- **Pacing.** Can you hold a 90-second answer without trailing off?
- **Comprehension.** Can you understand the screener's questions without needing repeated clarification?
- **Structure.** Can you produce a coherent answer that addresses the question, not orbiting around it?
- **Sustainability.** Can you hold English for 10–15 minutes without code-switching to Telugu?

That is the test. Those five things.

The phone screen does **not** test:

- **American or British accent.** This is the most important sentence in this entire chapter. The Hyderabad BPO entry-level filter is **structure under load, not accent**. Accent neutralization — if any company asks for it at all — is Tier 2 training, paid for and delivered by the employer *after* hire. It is not a candidate-side prerequisite. The stereotype that you have to sound American to work at Concentrix or Genpact is largely a 2000s memory. The 2026 Hyderabad BPO industry hires for clarity and pacing in Indian Business English. You are the right voice for this job.

The linguistic research backs this up. Indian English is a legitimate variety of English — millions of professionals speak it every day. (Pingali Sailaja, *Indian English*, Edinburgh University Press, 2009 — [publisher page](https://edinburghuniversitypress.com/book-indian-english.html). Braj Kachru's foundational [1985 paper on the "outer circle" of English](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-971X.1985.tb00423.x) places Indian English at the same level of legitimacy as American or British English.) Even when companies do offer accent training, the modern approach is about *MTI reduction* — keeping your first-language pronunciation from blocking intelligibility — not about producing a midwestern-American voice. `[verify current Concentrix / Genpact / Foundever onboarding accent-training scope, 2026]`

- **Vocabulary richness.** A 400-word working vocabulary is sufficient for a phone screen if used clearly. Bigger words do not help. *"Could you please tell me more about the role?"* is exactly as professional as any longer version of the same question.

- **Speed of speech.** The chapter actively recommends *slower* than natural conversational pace. About 120 words per minute is fine. 150+ is rushing. The screener is comparing your pace to the 39 other candidates she has talked to this week — the ones who passed sounded *paced*, not fast.

- **Perfect grammar.** As long as the message is clear, minor grammatical slips are not phone-screen filters. The screener is filtering on *clarity and structure*, not on perfect syntax. (Imperfect grammar is what *MTI reduction* training would polish post-hire, if at all. Not a screen filter.)

The phone screen tests whether you sound like someone who can hold a customer call. **You can.** The course has taught you the structure. The phone screen is the rehearsal of what you already know.

### 8. The logistics step — handle it honestly, name the constraints

After the five questions, the screener will run a logistics check. Standard questions:

- *"Are you comfortable with night shifts?"* (especially for international voice processes serving US/UK/Australian clients)
- *"What is your travel time from [your area] to our [office area]?"*
- *"What are your salary expectations?"*
- *"What is your notice period?"* (zero for freshers — you can start immediately)
- *"Are you willing to attend a walk-in / assessment center if shortlisted?"*

For each, the answer pattern is the same: **honest, with a specific number or constraint named, and a fallback proposed where possible.**

**Night shift.** If you have a residence constraint — many Homes of Hope residences have evening curfews, especially for residents under 21 — you say so. *"Ma'am, I am comfortable with day shifts and evening shifts. For night shifts I would need to confirm with the sisters at the residence regarding pickup and drop arrangements, because of the residence's evening curfew. Could you tell me whether the role would require night shifts immediately, or whether there is a day-shift option for the first few months?"*

Naming the constraint honestly is **more attractive** than saying *yes to everything*. The willing-to-do-anything candidate flakes in week three when the actual night shift starts. The candidate who named the constraint at screen time and proposed a fallback is the candidate the recruiter trusts. Most major BPOs — Concentrix, Genpact, Foundever — start freshers on domestic day-shift processes for the first 1–3 months before moving to international night-shift. So the day-shift-first answer often is the actual job. `[verify current 2026 Concentrix / Genpact fresher rotation policies]`

**Travel time.** Specific number. *"It is about one hour each way by bus or metro."* Then add a sensible fallback if the BPO offers pickup/drop — *"Concentrix's pickup and drop facility would help, especially for the night-shift period."*

**Salary.** For entry-level freshers in Hyderabad BPO, the package is largely fixed at the level. The safe answer is *open to standard package + verified knowledge of the range*. *"I am open to the standard fresher package for the role, ma'am. I understand the entry-level customer-service associate salary at Concentrix is in the range of 2.2 to 2.8 lakh per annum. That is acceptable to me."* `[verify current 2026 fresher BPO salary range — Concentrix, Genpact, Foundever Hyderabad entry-level customer service associate compensation]`

Two things this answer does. **One** — it shows you researched the salary range before the call, which signals you are a serious candidate, not someone who took a random call. **Two** — it does not commit you to a specific number, which keeps room to negotiate a small bump later if you receive multiple offers.

**Notice period.** *"I am a fresher, so my notice period is zero. I can start as soon as the role is confirmed."*

**Walk-in.** *"Yes, ma'am, I can attend the walk-in. Could you tell me the address and the timing?"* Then write down what she says.

### 9. The candidate question — small but real differentiator

At the end of the call, the screener will often ask *"Do you have any questions for me?"*

The wrong answer: *"No ma'am, you have explained everything clearly."* This sometimes reads as low engagement — the screener notes *this candidate did not prepare a question*.

The right answer: **one prepared, sensible question about the next step.** That's it. One question. Specific. Forward-looking.

Good examples:

- *"Could you tell me what the next step in the hiring process would be, and approximately when I might hear back?"*
- *"Could you tell me a little more about the training program for new associates?"*
- *"Is there anything specific I should prepare for the walk-in if I am shortlisted?"*

Avoid:

- *"How much will you pay me?"* (You already discussed salary.)
- *"Will I have to do night shifts?"* (Already discussed.)
- *"What is the company's history?"* (Recruiter does not have time.)
- Three or four questions in a row — one is enough.

One prepared question is a small positive. No question is a small negative. The asymmetry favors preparing one.

### 10. Three moves for when you do not know an answer

You will be asked at least one question you have not rehearsed. Plan for it.

There are three professional responses. One fatal one.

**Move 1 — Clarifying question.** *"Could you tell me a little more about what you mean? I want to make sure I answer the question you are asking."* Buys you 3-5 seconds. Often produces a clearer version of the question.

**Move 2 — Honest admission with redirect.** *"I have not been in that exact situation before. The closest I have done is [X]. Would that be useful to share?"* The redirect must be sincere — *X* has to be genuinely similar.

**Move 3 — Structured thinking out loud.** *"Let me think for a moment. First, I would [step 1]. Then [step 2], because [reason]. Finally [step 3]. Does that make sense?"* The screener is *more impressed* by this than by a rehearsed answer for some questions, because real customer-service work is exactly this — problem-solving in real time.

**The fatal move:** silence past 8 seconds with no acknowledgement, or a confident wrong guess. Twelve seconds of silence reads as panic. A confident wrong guess gets exposed on the next question.

The rule: **the cost of *"I don't know, here is what I would do"* is much lower than the cost of a confident guess.** The screener can hear the difference between rehearsed-honest and fabricated-confident across the phone line.

If you feel yourself freezing for more than 5 seconds, say one sentence out loud: *"Let me think for a moment."* That sentence is permitted. That sentence is professional. Memorize it as your reset move. Three seconds bought without the silence being a problem.

## Worked example — Priya's full phone screen with Concentrix HR

This is the central worked example of the unit. A full 12-minute phone screen, end to end. Read it once for the shape. Read it again with the question types named in your mind.

(Setup: Priya applied to a Customer Service Associate role at Concentrix Hyderabad through Naukri last week. She receives a call from Pooja Sharma, Concentrix HR Recruiter, on Tuesday at 11 AM.)

---

**[Opening — Pooja's side, 2 minutes]**

Pooja: *Hello, am I speaking with Priya?*

Priya: *Yes, this is Priya. Good morning, ma'am.*

Total call: 12 minutes. Three days later, Concentrix talent team calls Priya to schedule the walk-in.

**Three things to notice when you re-read this call:**

- Priya's *English-faster-in-conversation* weakness is **the audible-on-the-call weakness**. Naming it disarms the screener's likely suspicion. If Priya had claimed perfect English, Pooja — who can hear the small pauses — would have flagged her as not self-aware. The honesty makes everything else more credible.
- The night-shift question was handled honestly, with the residence-curfew constraint named and a day-shift-first fallback proposed. This is exactly the kind of negotiation entry-level candidates avoid because they think any constraint disqualifies them. In fact, **the honest constraint is more attractive than the willing-to-do-anything candidate who flakes in week three.**
- The candidate question (*what is the next step, when might I hear back?*) is a small but real differentiator. It signals engagement and that the candidate is treating the call as a two-way conversation, not a one-way examination.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Treating *Tell me about yourself* as biography.** The natural impulse is to tell more. The professional summary version is shorter, more structured, and more useful to the screener. Write the answer once, with AI assist for register, and memorize the present/past/future shape. Use it across every interview.

**Mistake 2 — Generic answers to *Why this company*.** *"I want to grow."* *"I am hardworking."* *"I want to learn."* All three are heard in 60% of phone screens at this level. The three-component answer — *one fact about the company, why the role fits you, what you hope to do* — is what passes the filter.

**Mistake 3 — Fake weakness.** *"I work too hard."* *"I am a perfectionist."* *"I care too much."* All auto-filter. The honest-real-weakness-with-improvement-plan answer is the one the screener wants. Use the template in §5.

**Mistake 4 — Saying yes to night shifts when the residence cannot support it.** This is the most expensive small mistake. You get hired. You start the job. Week three the night shift begins. You cannot leave the residence after 9 PM. You miss shifts. You are let go. **Name the constraint at the screen.** Propose the fallback. Most BPOs accommodate this.

**Mistake 5 — Trying to outshine the screener.** This is the biggest psychological trap. The Hyderabad BPO entry-level phone screen rewards *survival*, not brilliance. A candidate who tries to sound impressive — fancy vocabulary, big promises, dramatic stories — triggers the screener's *high-maintenance* alarm. The candidate who is calm, clear, and structured passes. Aim for clear. Not impressive.

**Mistake 6 — Worrying about your accent.** Already covered in §6. Listed again here because this is the worry that produces the most needless anxiety. The screen is for **structure under load, not accent.** Indian English is the operating language. Your voice is the right voice for this job. Worry about pacing and structure. Stop worrying about accent.

**Mistake 7 — No prepared question at the end.** *"No ma'am, you explained everything."* Reads as low engagement. One sensible question about the next step. Prepared in advance. That is the move.

**Mistake 8 — Saying *"actually"* when correcting yourself mid-answer.** *"I have completed 12th class — actually, I am in my final year of 12th class."* The word *actually* signals you are correcting an error in real time, which makes the screener wonder what else is wrong. The fix: rehearse the answer enough that you do not need to correct mid-flow. If you do need to correct, say *"Let me clarify"* — *"Let me clarify — I am currently in my final year of 12th class, expected to complete in 2026."* Same correction, no negative signal.

## Exercises

These are graded by Bloom level. Exercise 4 is the unit's central exercise — you will bring the result to your Zoom session.

### Exercise 1 — Write your present/past/future answer *(Bloom: Create)*

Open a note on your phone. Write your *Tell me about yourself* answer in three paragraphs.

- Paragraph 1 — **Present.** Two sentences. Name, age, location, education status, what you are preparing for.
- Paragraph 2 — **Past.** Three to four sentences. The residence (how long, what you did there), the course (what you learned), any other relevant experience.
- Paragraph 3 — **Future.** One or two sentences. The kind of role, why this company, why now.

Target word count: about 180–200 words. That is 90 seconds spoken at a normal pace.

Once written, paste it into ChatGPT with this prompt:

> *"Please check this answer to 'Tell me about yourself' for a Hyderabad BPO phone screen. Is the register right for Indian Business English? Is it specific enough — not too generic? Are there any phrases that sound American instead of Indian? Please give me feedback in simple English and rewrite anything that does not match Indian Business English register."*

Read the AI's rewrite line by line. Keep what improves the answer. Reject anything the AI added that is not true about you. Read your final version out loud and time it. Target: 85 to 95 seconds.

Memorize the *shape* — not the exact words. Different words each time is fine, as long as the present/past/future structure holds.

### Exercise 2 — Build three behavioral stories *(Bloom: Create)*

You need three stories ready for the behavioral question. The screener will ask one — you do not know which kind, so three different stories give you flexibility.

For each story, fill in the four parts:

- **Situation.** Where, when, what was the context.
- **What you did.** The action you took. The core of the answer.
- **What happened.** The result.
- **What you learned.** One sentence connecting to customer service.

Pick three stories from different categories:

- **Conflict / disagreement story.** Two younger residents arguing, a misunderstanding between roommates, a disagreement during a study group.
- **Teamwork story.** Helping organize a residence event, working with the sisters on a project, a group activity in the course.
- **Mistake / what I learned story.** A small mistake at the residence or in school, what you did to fix it, what you changed afterward.

Three stories. Each one written out. Each one timed at about 90 seconds spoken.

### Exercise 3 — Practice the weakness answer out loud *(Bloom: Apply)*

This is the question girls fear most. Practice it until the fear goes.

1. Pick the weakness from §5 closest to your real experience — *English-faster-in-conversation*, *time-management-when-multitasking*, or *speaking-up-when-I-disagree*. Or write a fourth that is real for you.
2. Write the four-part answer: name the weakness + show self-awareness + name specific action + end with confidence.
3. Say it out loud, on your phone's voice recorder. Time yourself. Target: 40 to 60 seconds.
4. Listen back. Did the answer end with confidence (*"I will continue to practice"*) or with hedge (*"I am still trying to improve"*)? Re-record if the ending was weak.
5. Three takes. Pick the best one. That is your weakness answer. Memorize the shape.

### Exercise 4 — A full mock phone screen with ChatGPT *(Bloom: Create + Evaluate)*

This is the load-bearing exercise. You will bring the transcript and your work items to the Zoom session.

**Step 1.** Open ChatGPT or Gemini. Use this prompt:

> *"You are Pooja Sharma, an HR recruiter at Concentrix Hyderabad. You are conducting a 12-minute phone screen for an entry-level Customer Service Associate role. Please ask me the standard five questions in order — (1) tell me about yourself, (2) why Concentrix, (3) what is your strength, (4) what is your weakness, (5) tell me about a difficult situation you handled. After each of my answers, briefly acknowledge it and move to the next question. After the five questions, ask me about night-shift comfort, travel time, salary expectations. End by asking if I have any questions for you. Begin when I say I am ready."*

**Step 2.** Type *"I am ready"* when you are. Then run through the whole call. Type your answers in English. Aim for 60 to 90 seconds per answer (about 150 to 200 words written).

**Step 3.** When the call ends, ask ChatGPT to evaluate. Use this prompt:

> *"Please evaluate my phone screen as a Hyderabad BPO recruiter would. Specifically: Was my 'tell me about yourself' answer structured around present/past/future, and did it stay around 90 seconds? Was my 'why Concentrix' answer specific to the company rather than generic? Did my strength answer include a specific example? Did my weakness answer name a real non-disqualifying weakness with an improvement plan? Did my behavioral answer follow the situation framework? Did I handle the night-shift question honestly with constraints named? Was my candidate question sensible? What two things should I work on most? Please answer in simple English."*

**Step 4.** Read the evaluation. Mark two things to work on most.

**Step 5.** Run the same prompt again with a *spoken* version. Open your phone's voice recorder. Type ChatGPT's first question, then *speak* your answer aloud to the recorder. Type the next question, speak the answer. Six questions total. Then listen back to all six answers, one after the other. Is your pacing consistent? Does your voice trail off at the end of any answer? Does any answer go significantly over 90 seconds?

**Step 6.** Bring the chat transcript, the audio recording (or notes), and your two work items to the Zoom session. The volunteer will play Pooja Sharma and run a live 10-minute mock screen. The live feedback is the calibration — voice quality, pacing, warmth, the things AI cannot judge.

### Exercise 5 — Memorize your sentences *(Bloom: Remember)*

By Friday this week, these should be in your mouth, not on your paper:

1. Your full *Tell me about yourself* answer — 90 seconds, present/past/future.
2. Your *Why this company* answer — 50 seconds, three components.
3. Your strength answer — 50 seconds, named + example + link.
4. Your weakness answer — 45 seconds, honest + improvement plan.
5. Three behavioral stories — 90 seconds each, situation framework.
6. The phrase *"Let me think for a moment."* — your reset move when you do not know an answer.
7. One prepared question for the end of the call.

Total memorization: about 8 minutes of spoken content + 1 reset phrase + 1 candidate question. Practice each one 10 times this week, out loud, until they come without effort.

## What you can do now

You can:

- Answer the five canonical BPO phone-screen questions in clear professional English.
- Structure *Tell me about yourself* in present/past/future, target 90 seconds.
- Answer *Why this company* with three specific components — one fact, role fit, forward statement.
- Pair every strength claim with a specific example and a link to the role.
- Name a real, non-disqualifying weakness with a specific improvement plan.
- Tell three behavioral stories from your residence and course experience in the situation framework.
- Handle the logistics questions honestly, naming constraints and proposing fallbacks.
- Ask one prepared, sensible candidate question at the end.
- Use the three professional moves for *"I don't know"* — clarifying question, honest admission with redirect, structured thinking out loud.
- Recognize that the phone-screen filter is **structure under load, not accent**, and stop worrying about something that does not appear in the test.

That is the full phone-screen skill set of a Tier 1 entry-level BPO candidate. The Hyderabad recruiter sees 30 phone screens this week. The ones who clear are the ones who prepared for these specific questions. You will be one of them.

## For volunteers / sisters' notes

*A note for Humanitarians AI volunteers running this week's Zoom session, and for the Salesian sisters supporting the girls between sessions.*

The girls will bring their Exercise 4 transcripts and self-identified work items. Your job this week: **run one live 10-minute mock phone screen, playing the HR recruiter**. Stay in character — moderately friendly, moderately efficient, on a checklist. Ask the five canonical questions plus the logistics step. Do **not** coach mid-call. The session is rehearsal of the screen itself, not a teaching session. Coaching happens in the debrief.

Volunteer brief — three binary judgments:

1. Did the candidate produce a complete 90-second *Tell me about yourself* answer with present/past/future structure? (Yes / no)
2. Did the candidate name a real, non-disqualifying weakness with an improvement plan? (Yes / no)
3. Did the candidate produce a behavioral scenario with situation / action / result / learning structure? (Yes / no)

Three judgments. About 5 seconds each. Then one specific thing she did well and one specific thing to try differently before the real screen.

The supporting structured-interview research (Schmidt and Hunter 1998 — [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/13782725)) shows that *structured behavioral answers* predict job performance more reliably than unstructured rambling. Your one feedback move: name the place where the structure was strongest, and name the place where the structure was weakest. The girl can fix the weakest place before the real call.

The TIKTOC's Risk 3 mitigation applies with full force here: this is the rehearsal that decides whether the girl goes into the real Concentrix or Genpact phone screen with confidence or with dread. Be a *moderately competent screener* — not friendly enough to flatter her, not harsh enough to break her. Calibrated practice. Not assessment.

For the sisters: the five answers are something you can quiz the girls on at evening study time. *"Priya, tell me about yourself for the BPO interview."* — she says it. *"What is your weakness?"* — she says it. *"Tell me about a difficult situation you handled."* — she picks one of the three stories. Ten minutes a day. The repetition is what gets the answers from *on paper* to *in her mouth*.

One important note. Some girls will resist the *professional summary, not autobiography* rule. They will feel that keeping the family situation out of the answer is denying part of who they are. Hold the line. The professional summary is the version of her story that the workplace context calls for. The fuller version remains hers. The two are not in conflict. They serve different relationships. The sisters know this distinction in their own work — what they say to a girl, what they say to a donor, what they say at a board meeting. Same person. Three registers. Helping the girls see that the workplace register is one register among many — not a denial — is the supporting move that lets them do this exercise without feeling small.
