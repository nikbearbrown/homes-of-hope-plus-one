# Unit 7C — The NGO Interview

*How to tell your story without over-sharing or under-selling. The redirect-to-capability move.*

---

## Learning outcomes

By the end of this unit, you will be able to:

- **(Apply + Evaluate)** Articulate your personal story and its connection to social work in professional English — without over-sharing or under-selling — in a mock interview format, using the four-part narrative architecture (lived experience → observed pattern → desired contribution → why this organization) and the redirect-to-capability move when needed.

---

## The problem this unit solves

You have the most powerful interview asset and the most dangerous interview trap in the same fact about yourself.

You grew up in a Salesian-sisters' residence. You lived in a small, structured organization for six years. You watched the sisters and the staff run a daily program — morning routines, school coordination, evening study, the moments when a younger girl was struggling and needed extra attention. You learned, by being inside it, how a well-run program works.

That is exactly the experience NGO hiring should value. *And* the NGO interviewer — well-intentioned, charitable in disposition, often herself a former social-work student — can produce an interview that hires you *for the story* rather than *for the capability*. The condescending interview is real. It produces hires that fail on day one because the candidate was filtered for being the program's target population, not for being able to do the program's work.

The unit's job is to teach you the **redirection** move: how to honor your story, deploy it as one specific motivating fact, and then immediately move the conversation to capability. Three sentences. *I lived in the residence for six years. What that taught me about what NGOs do is X. The skill I built that translates is Y.* The story is named, the lesson is specific, the capability is on offer. Not a memoir. Not an apology. **A redirection.**

This is the hardest of the three Track-Unit-7 interviews to teach because the failure modes are emotional, not cognitive. You may over-share because the interviewer's warmth pulled you in. You may under-sell because your own training was about humility. Both fail. The chapter teaches *controlled disclosure* — a learnable behavior under interview conditions.

A second hard rule for this unit: **the stipend will be lower than at BPO or hospital reception.** Entry-level NGO admin in Hyderabad pays roughly Rs. 12,000–18,000/month, compared to Rs. 18,000–25,000 at BPO entry, Rs. 15,000–22,000 at hospital reception entry `[verify with current 2026 compensation data]`. The chapter must be honest about this. Track C is *lower entry stipend, slower trajectory in money terms, sector identity and meaning as compensation*. You should choose Track C with eyes open.

## Opening case

Priya has two interviews this week. One at Magic Bus Hyderabad. One at Care & Share, a smaller residential-children's NGO in Hyderabad. Both are entry-level Program Associate roles. Both interviewers have read her CV and seen the residence background.

(Priya here is the running learner-vignette — a composite, not a real student.)

The Care & Share interviewer opens with what sounds like warmth.

> Interviewer: *"Priya, I read your CV, and your story really touched me. Tell me more about your childhood. What was it like growing up without your parents in that home?"*

This is the moment.

Priya has two paths. **Path A** — the candidate who has not rehearsed this question lets the interviewer's warmth pull her in. She tells the story. Twenty minutes pass. The interviewer is now emotionally invested in *Priya the story*. The interviewer asks no scenario questions, no behavioral questions, no capability questions. Priya is offered the job at the end of the interview. **She accepts.** On day one she is asked to draft a quarterly donor report. She freezes. Nobody has tested whether she can write a four-move paragraph. She is set up to fail.

**Path B** — Priya has rehearsed this question. She knows the redirect-to-capability move. She answers:

> Priya: *"Thank you for asking, sir. The residence was a structured and safe environment, and I'm grateful for the six years there. What I'd really like to share with you is what that experience taught me about how a small organization runs — could I tell you about a time I helped coordinate the evening study schedule for younger residents?"*

Path B does three things in three sentences. It acknowledges the question with respect. It gives a single positive characterization of the residence (so the interviewer is not worried about an unstable childhood). It explicitly pivots to capability with a concrete offer (*coordinated the evening study schedule*) that the interviewer can engage with as an interview move.

The interviewer notices. *"Yes, please tell me about that."* Now Priya is running a STAR-framework answer about a real administrative skill. The interview has been redirected. The next 25 minutes are about capability. The hire that comes out of this interview will succeed, not because the interviewer was filtered for the right thing, but because *Priya* steered the interview to the right thing.

That is the move this unit teaches. By the end you will have rehearsed it for the five most common NGO behavioral questions plus the *charitable interview* trap.

## Core content

### 1. The NGO entry-level interview structure

Standard hiring flow at CRY, Pratham, Akshaya Patra, Magic Bus, HelpAge for Program Associate / Field Coordinator / Fundraising Assistant entry-level roles `[verify with current 2026 HR practice]`:

**Round 1 — Application screen.** CV + cover note + sometimes a writing sample. Filtered by HR or program-team coordinator. They look for: educational background (graduate often preferred at CRY/Pratham; non-graduate considered at smaller orgs and for field roles), English proficiency, residence near the field site (for field roles), motivational fit.

**Round 2 — HR / coordinator interview (20–30 minutes).** Behavioral and motivational questions. *Why social work? Why this organization? Walk me through your CV.* Looking for: composure, motivation quality, narrative coherence, willingness to do administrative work (entry roles are usually more admin than field).

**Round 3 — Program team / director interview (30–45 minutes).** Substantive and scenario-based. *Tell me about a community-living experience that taught you something useful. How would you handle X situation in the field.* Looking for: thinking quality, role-awareness, donor-facing capability.

**Round 4 — Sometimes a writing assessment.** *Draft a 100-word situation summary on the following information.* On-the-spot or as a take-home. CRY and Pratham use this commonly. Smaller orgs less often. Be ready.

**Round 5 — Final HR + offer.** Stipend negotiation (modest, see §5 below). Joining date. Documentation.

Smaller Hyderabad NGOs — MV Foundation, Care & Share, Ashray, Mahita, Vidya Bharati — sometimes compress rounds 2 and 3 into a single 30-minute interview. Walk-in or interview-on-the-day formats also exist for smaller orgs.

The chapter focuses on rounds 2 and 3 because those are where the most can be taught with rehearsal. Round 4 (writing assessment) is what Unit 5C prepared you for; that unit's four-move structure is what gets you through.

### 2. The four-part narrative architecture for the motivational question

The motivational question — *"Why do you want to work in social work?"* or *"Why this NGO?"* — is the highest-stakes question in the NGO interview. The four-part architecture below is the answer that does not over-share and does not under-sell.

**Part 1 — Lived experience (one sentence).**

> *"I grew up in a Salesian sisters' Homes of Hope residence in Secunderabad for six years."*

One sentence. Names the experience. Does not dwell. Does not perform pain. Does not perform gratitude. Just states the fact.

**Part 2 — Observed pattern (one or two sentences).**

> *"In that environment, I watched the sisters and the staff manage a small organization that supported younger girls — the morning routines, the school coordination, the evening study time, the moments when a girl was struggling and needed extra attention. I saw how a well-run program looks from the inside."*

You are naming what you *observed* in the residence — not what you *suffered*. The frame is *professional observation*, not biography. This single shift is the difference between *the girl who survived a hard childhood* and *the candidate who has watched program operations for six years*.

**Part 3 — Desired contribution (one or two sentences).**

> *"I want to work in NGO administration because that's the work I've watched happen from inside, and I think I understand it. I am better suited to this than to customer service or hospital reception — those are different jobs, and I respect them, but they are not the work I want to do."*

This is an *affirmative choice*. You are choosing this sector, not defaulting to it. The contrast with the other Course 2 tracks (BPO, healthcare) is important — it signals you considered alternatives and chose this one deliberately.

**Part 4 — Why this organization (one sentence, organization-specific).**

> *"I'm applying to Magic Bus specifically because your sport-and-life-skills program for adolescents is structurally similar to what the sisters' residence did informally. I think the analogy will let me contribute faster."*

Specific to the org. Not interchangeable. Demonstrates research. The interviewer hears *this candidate is not applying to every NGO in Hyderabad with the same answer; she applied to us specifically*.

**Total length: 60–90 seconds. Five to seven sentences.** Practice it until you can deliver it without sounding rehearsed. The architecture is the scaffold; the words inside should be your words.

### 3. Trauma-informed self-disclosure — the SAMHSA framework, adapted

The trauma-informed care framework was developed for *service delivery to people who have experienced trauma* — by the [Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA)](https://www.samhsa.gov/resource/dbhis/samhsas-concept-trauma-and-guidance-trauma-informed-approach). The chapter adapts the framework for a different purpose: *self-presentation by people whose biographies include difficult experience*.

A small honest flag: this adaptation is not what SAMHSA designed the framework for. The principles travel; the application is the chapter's. Treat the rules below as a working translation, not as cited SAMHSA authority.

Three operational rules for interviewing:

**Rule 1 — The one-sentence rule.** Personal experience appears in one sentence at most. Anything more than one sentence shifts the interview from professional to biographical. You are not trying to hide your background — you are trying to control how much of the interview is about it. One sentence states the fact. The redirect-to-capability sentence that follows brings the conversation back to where it should be.

**Rule 2 — The redirect-to-capability rule.** Every personal sentence is followed by a capability sentence. *"I grew up in the residence. What that taught me about [organizational skill] was..."* The structural pivot prevents the interview from settling on the story.

**Rule 3 — The "I'd rather keep that private" rule.** Some questions — usually well-intentioned but inappropriate — can be declined politely. *"Can you tell me more about your family situation?"* — answer:

> *"There are some things from my early life I'd rather keep private. What I can share is that I've had six years of stable community-living experience at the residence, and that has been the foundation of my preparation for this kind of work."*

This redirect is professionally acceptable. The interviewer has no entitlement to your full biography. You decide what is disclosed. The redirect preserves your privacy and keeps the interview moving.

A small caution. Rule 3 is not for use on every personal question. Most personal questions in NGO interviews are appropriate and can be answered using Rules 1 and 2. Rule 3 is for the *too far* questions — questions about specific family members, specific incidents, specific traumas. You will know one of these when you hear it. The redirect phrase above is what to say.

### 4. The condescending interview — and the redirect-to-capability move

A specific failure mode of NGO entry-level interviews is the *charitable* interview. The interviewer is well-meaning. She has decided in the first minute that you are sympathetic. She proceeds to *interview the story rather than the candidate*. Symptoms:

- Questions skew biographical: *"What was your childhood like?"*, *"How did the sisters help you?"*, *"How does it feel to be sitting here today?"*
- Questions skip capability: no scenario questions, no behavioral questions, no skills assessment.
- The interviewer's affect is performative empathy rather than evaluative scrutiny.

The redirect-to-capability move is *protective of you*, not cold. **If the interviewer hires you for the story, you will be set up to fail on day one.** The work is the work; the interviewer needs to see that you can do the work. Your redirect helps the interviewer make a hire that succeeds — including for you.

The pattern, in three moves:

**Move 1 — Acknowledge briefly.**

> *"Thank you for asking. The residence was a structured and safe environment, and I'm grateful for those six years."*

One sentence of acknowledgment. Positive characterization. No detail. Do not perform gratitude beyond the single sentence.

**Move 2 — Pivot to capability.**

> *"What I'd really like to share with you is what I learned there that I think would help in this role. Could I tell you about the time I helped coordinate the evening study schedule for younger girls?"*

You are making the interviewer an offer. You are giving her permission to redirect the conversation. Most interviewers will take the offer because it is the conversation they should have been having anyway.

**Move 3 — Deliver the capability content.**

Run a STAR-framework story (situation → task → action → result → learning) that demonstrates concrete administrative skill. You will have several of these ready (see §6 below). Pick the one most relevant to the role you are interviewing for.

The chapter is explicit about why this move matters. *The candidate's story is real, valuable, and motivating. And the interview is about capability.* The two things are not in conflict. The redirect lets both be present — the story is named in one sentence; the capability runs for ninety seconds.

A small voice note: when you run the redirect, your voice should be *warmer*, not colder, than your normal voice. The interviewer needs to feel that you have heard her question with respect. The cold redirect — *"I would rather talk about my skills"* — does the structural work but offends the interviewer. The warm redirect — *"Thank you for asking. The residence was a safe environment, I'm grateful. What I'd love to share with you is..."* — does the same structural work and preserves the relationship.

### 5. The five NGO-sector-specific behavioral questions

Behavioral questions in NGO entry-level interviews converge on five themes ([Idealist Careers](https://www.idealist.org/en/careers); practitioner-blog content from senior Indian NGO HR officers; CSR-portal commentary `[verify with current 2026 HR practitioner]`). Memorize the structure of each answer.

#### Question 1 — *"Tell me about a time you had a conflict in a community-living or group setting. How did you handle it?"*

The chapter teaches: residence-life conflict (a roommate disagreement, a disagreement with a sister about a rule, a disagreement with a younger resident you were helping) is *legitimate community-work experience*.

**Structure (STAR, 60–90 seconds):**

> *"Last year, a younger resident — 11 years old — refused to share textbooks during evening study because another girl had used hers carelessly the week before. I was the evening-study coordinator for that group at the time. I sat with both girls separately, listened to what each was upset about — the older girl wanted apology, the younger girl wanted her textbook back without being shamed — and proposed a small rule: textbooks would be checked in and out at evening study, with the borrower responsible for return condition. I implemented it with the sister-in-charge's permission. Both girls were satisfied. What I learned was that most conflicts are about respect, not the surface issue, and that small visible rules can preserve the respect."*

STAR structure. Residence experience translated into administrative-skill evidence. Specific actions named (*sat with both girls separately, proposed a small rule, implemented with sister's permission*). Ends on a generalizable lesson. **Passes.**

#### Question 2 — *"Tell me about a time you worked with someone who was hard to help."*

The trap: defaulting to non-failure answers (*"I always make people feel comfortable"*). The chapter teaches: pick a real difficult case, describe it honestly, name what you learned.

**Structure (STAR, 60–90 seconds):**

> *"In Class 11, a new resident — 9 years old — was placed in our home after a difficult family situation. For the first three weeks she would not speak to anyone, would not eat at common meals, would not join evening study. I was asked to be her morning buddy — sit with her at breakfast, walk her to school. I tried different things: talking to her in Telugu, sitting silently with her, bringing a younger girl with us. Nothing worked for the first two weeks. In the third week she started eating breakfast next to me without saying anything. After about a month she started speaking. What I learned was that for some kinds of hurt, the right thing is patient presence, not active help. I had been trying to do something. The right move was to just be there."*

Real difficulty. Honest description. No defensiveness. Generalizable lesson. **Passes.**

#### Question 3 — *"Tell me about a time something you did didn't work."*

The trap: deflecting with *"I always finish what I start"*. The chapter teaches: NGO work involves failed interventions constantly. The candidate who cannot honestly describe one signals lack of self-awareness.

**Structure (STAR, 60–90 seconds):**

> *"In Class 11, I tried to start a weekly Telugu reading session for younger residents who were struggling with their Telugu-medium classes. I prepared materials for two weeks. The first session had eight girls; the second had four; the third had two; the fourth was cancelled because no one came. I went to the sister-in-charge and asked what I had missed. She said the timing — 6 PM — clashed with cricket practice for that age group. I had not asked them what time would work; I had picked what worked for me. After that I always asked first. The reading session restarted later at 4 PM and ran for the rest of the year."*

Real failure. Specific data (*8, 4, 2, 0*). Honest description. No blame-shifting. Generalizable lesson — *ask the user about constraints before designing the program*. **Passes.**

#### Question 4 — *"What would you do if a donor's reporting requirement was generating burden for the field team?"*

This is a higher-level scenario that may not always come up at entry-level. When it does, the answer signals understanding of the *prepare, do not resolve* boundary.

**Structure (45–60 seconds):**

> *"I would surface this to my supervisor with specifics — what the donor needs, what the field team is finding difficult — and ask how to negotiate. I would not unilaterally change the reporting; I would not silently suffer it. This seems like a conversation between the two parties that I would help prepare for — by writing up the field team's concerns in clear language and identifying the parts of the reporting that are creating the burden — but I would not try to resolve it myself."*

The teaching point: *prepare, do not resolve*. Entry-level associates should not unilaterally negotiate with donors. They should not silently absorb burden either. The middle move is to surface and prepare. The interviewer is testing for that exact boundary awareness.

#### Question 5 — *"Are you planning to stay in NGO work, or is this a stepping-stone?"*

Indian NGO HR has high attrition concerns. The acceptable answer signals trajectory and stability.

**Structure (45–60 seconds):**

> *"I am committed to NGO administration as a sector. I want to start in program-associate work, learn the operations, build toward a coordinator role over two to three years, and eventually take on grant-writing and donor-relations responsibility. I am not looking to switch sectors — I want to build a career in this work, not use it as a stepping-stone to something else."*

Specific named next-step roles. Signals stability. Does not over-promise.

The chapter also previews the **empathy-in-NGO-work question** (*"What does empathy mean in NGO work?"*) — covered in Unit 7B for hospital reception, adapted here:

> *"It means listening carefully enough to understand what someone actually needs, not what I assume they need. At the residence I learned this when I would try to comfort a younger girl who was crying about her family — and I would say 'don't be sad' — and the sister would say, 'don't tell her how to feel; ask her what would help right now.' At an NGO I think empathy is the same skill, scaled up: listening to what beneficiaries are actually telling us before we design what we think they need. And listening to the field team about what the donors don't understand."*

Defines empathy as a specific skill, not a sentiment. Personal example. Generalizable to professional context.

### 6. The stipend conversation — honest, modest, professional

Round 5 is the offer round. The stipend will likely be lower than you hoped. Track C entry-level NGO admin in Hyderabad pays roughly Rs. 12,000–18,000/month at smaller orgs, Rs. 15,000–22,000 at larger named NGOs (CRY, Pratham, Akshaya Patra, Magic Bus) `[verify with current 2026 compensation data]`. This is meaningfully lower than BPO (~Rs. 18,000–25,000) or hospital reception (~Rs. 15,000–22,000) at the same level.

**Why is it lower?** NGOs are not profit-making organizations. Stipends come from donor funds or CSR allocations and are tightly budgeted. The trade is sector identity, mission, and growth in non-profit administration as compensation. This is real. It is also a real reduction in monthly income. Choose with open eyes.

The negotiation move at entry-level NGO is *honest, small-ask, professional* — not *hardball*.

> *"Thank you for the offer, ma'am. I'm grateful. Could I ask whether there is room to start at Rs. 16,000 instead of Rs. 14,000? I'm asking because [specific reason — commute cost is Rs. 2,500/month, my younger sibling is also dependent on my income, the rent at the working-women's hostel I'm targeting is Rs. 7,000/month]."*

Three things this move does:

- Thanks first.
- Names a specific number (not *"could it be higher?"* — name the number).
- Names a specific reason (not *"because I need more"* — specific cost figure).

If the answer is *yes*, accept gracefully. If the answer is *no*, you have two options. Accept gracefully. Or decline gracefully. Either is professional. *"Thank you, ma'am — could I have a day to think about it?"* is also acceptable; you can call back the next day with your decision.

What you do not do: try to play offers against each other (*"BPO is offering me Rs. 22,000, can you match?"*). NGOs cannot match BPO compensation. The signal you send by trying is *I am here for the money, not the mission*, and the offer will quietly be withdrawn.

### 7. The patient-is-not-a-customer logic, translated — for NGO interviews

You learned in Unit 7B that BPO trains *delight the customer* and hospital reception trains *respect the patient*. The two registers are different.

For NGO interviews, the analogous shift is: **the beneficiary is not a customer, and not a patient — the beneficiary is a partner.**

Three substitutions to memorize:

- *Customer* (BPO) / *patient* (healthcare) becomes *beneficiary* or *community member* (NGO).
- *Delight* / *support* becomes *serve* or *work alongside*.
- *Resolve* becomes *understand* or *address with*.

The voice change: warm, thoughtful, slightly slower than BPO, slightly more reflective than hospital. The NGO interviewer is listening for *whether you treat the people you would serve as agents in their own development* — not as recipients of charity.

The wrong register: *"I want to help the poor children who cannot help themselves."* Pity register. Disqualifies. The right register: *"I want to work alongside families and young people who are doing the work of changing their own situations. Our job is to provide the tools, the space, and the structure — not to replace their agency."* Partnership register. Qualifies.

The substitutions are small. They reorganize every answer.

### The condescending interview — counter-example

Suppose Mr. Verma asks at Q2: *"It must have been hard growing up without your mother. How did you cope?"*

**A weak answer** — Priya lets the warmth pull her in: a detailed personal disclosure that takes the next 10 minutes. She tells the story of her early years. Mr. Verma's affect softens. He stops asking scenario questions. He decides she is sympathetic. The interview is now about the story. Priya is offered the job. On day one she is asked to draft a quarterly donor report and freezes.

**The trained answer** — Priya runs the three-move redirect:

> *"Thank you for asking, sir. The residence was a structured and safe environment, and I'm grateful for the six years there. What I'd really like to share with you is what that experience taught me that I think would help in this role — could I tell you about the time I coordinated the evening study schedule for younger girls?"*

Mr. Verma takes the offer. *"Yes, please."* Priya runs the STAR answer. The interview returns to capability. The hire that comes out of this interview will succeed — for the organization and for Priya.

The redirect costs Priya nothing emotionally. It costs Mr. Verma nothing emotionally. It earns both of them an interview that does its job.

This is the move that distinguishes prepared candidates from unprepared ones at NGO interviews. Practice it until you can deliver it in your warm voice.

## Common mistakes

**Mistake 1 — The over-share trap on the motivational question.** A warm interviewer, an open question, an emotionally resonant story. You begin disclosing more than you intended. Prepare the one-sentence version in advance. Rehearse it until it is automatic. Do not improvise additional content under interview warmth. The volunteer Zoom session will role-play this exact scenario.

**Mistake 2 — The under-sell trap on the capability question.** The opposite failure. You have been trained for humility. *"I don't know if I can really say I'm good at anything yet."* This is not humility — it is *under-selling*. The right register: *"I'm new to this role, but here's what I have done and what I'm prepared to learn."* Confidence about what you can do is not arrogance.

**Mistake 3 — Pity-acceptance.** You sense the interviewer's sympathy and lean into it because it feels safer than capability evaluation. This is the failure mode that hires you for the wrong reason. The redirect-to-capability move is *protective of you*, not cold. You do not have to choose between being kind to the interviewer and being honest about your capability. Both are present in the redirect.

**Mistake 4 — Treating the residence as something to apologize for.** The residence is your six-year preparation for this role. *Not* a background to explain. *Not* a gap to fill. Treat it as you would treat any other six-year community organization experience — as professional preparation, named matter-of-factly. The interviewer will hear the framing.

**Mistake 5 — Mismatched register — pity language for beneficiaries.** *"I want to help the poor children who cannot help themselves."* This is the language that disqualifies. Replace with partnership language. *"I want to work alongside families and young people who are doing the work of changing their own situations."* Same impulse. Different register. One disqualifies. One qualifies.

**Mistake 6 — Hardball stipend negotiation.** *"BPO is offering me Rs. 22,000."* Do not play offers against each other at entry-level NGO. The signal *I am here for the money* withdraws the offer. The honest small-ask negotiation works (§6). Trying to negotiate beyond it does not.

**Mistake 7 — Skipping the writing sample if asked.** If Round 4 includes a writing assessment, your Unit 5C four-move paragraph is what gets you through. Bring two or three sample paragraphs you have written (situation summaries, introduction emails) to every NGO interview. If the assessment is on-the-spot, you have the structure rehearsed; if it is a take-home, you have samples to draw on.

## For volunteers / sisters' notes

*A note for Humanitarians AI volunteers running this week's Zoom session, and for the Salesian sisters supporting the girls between sessions.*

The girls will bring their Exercise 3 answer sets and their rehearsed redirect-to-capability move. **One main move this week: the full 30-minute mock with a charitable-interview challenge.**

You play the HR coordinator at her target NGO. Ask the five questions in §5 in order. Then — and this is the key teaching move — at some point in the interview, *ask a charitable-interview question* deliberately:

> *"It must have been hard growing up without your family. Can you tell me more about that?"*

Watch what she does. Two outcomes:

**Outcome A** — She takes the bait. She starts disclosing. Five minutes pass. Stop the role-play at minute six. Give specific feedback: *"That was a charitable-interview pull. Let me play it again, and try the redirect."*

**Outcome B** — She runs the three-move redirect. Note that it landed. Continue the interview.

After the 30 minutes, give three specific notes per girl:

1. *One thing that landed well* — the answer she nailed (often Q1 or Q5).
2. *One specific phrase to tighten* — a sentence in one of the answers that drifted toward over-formal, supplicant, or pity register.
3. *Redirect quality* — did the redirect come out in her warm voice, or did it sound cold and rehearsed? Cold redirects offend the interviewer; warm redirects do not. If hers came out cold, model the warmer version.

A specific watch-out: **the redirect-to-capability move is professionally appropriate, but it must come out with warmth, not coldness.** A girl who has under-rehearsed will deliver it as a robotic deflection. Coach her toward *thank you for asking + brief positive characterization + warm offer of capability content*.

For the sisters: between sessions, the daily mirror-rehearsal of the redirect matters most. Five minutes a day. The voice tone is what builds with repetition.

A flag for both groups: **the redirect-to-capability move and the trauma-informed self-disclosure adaptation in this chapter draw on practitioner experience and an adaptation of the SAMHSA framework that SAMHSA did not design for this use.** The adaptation is the chapter's translation move, not cited SAMHSA authority. A working NGO HR coordinator at CRY, Pratham, or similar should review the question set and the redirect framing before this chapter is published as final. Until that review is in, treat the move as a strong starting practice — the actual interview at a specific NGO may have its own rhythms.

A second flag for both groups: **the stipend ranges in §6 are practitioner estimates from 2026; they should be verified against current sector data before publish.** The order of magnitude (NGO entry < hospital reception ≈ BPO entry, with NGO trade being sector identity and growth) is robust.
