## Book Concept Summary

This course teaches a girl with basic English and smartphone literacy to apply for, interview for, and perform in an entry-level job in Hyderabad's BPO, healthcare support, or NGO sector — using AI tools as a professional assistant, not a crutch — in 8 weeks, on her phone, with Indian volunteer human feedback on her actual spoken and written English, at no cost.

**Thesis:** A girl with basic English and AI tool fluency is more employable in Hyderabad's 2026 job market than a girl with strong English and no AI skills — because the jobs are changing faster than the training programs, and the girls who adapt first have an advantage that compounds.

**It succeeds if:** The reader completes a mock interview with a Humanitarians AI volunteer, applies for a real job in her chosen sector, and knows what to do on day one if she gets it.

---

## Book Type & Deployment

**Type:** Practitioner handbook — task-organized, self-contained units, no required linear sequence after the foundation weeks.

**Primary deployment:** Girls aged 17–22 in or recently graduated from Homes of Hope Hyderabad residences. Post-Tier 1–2 English literacy deck. Basic English vocabulary (200–500 words) in place.

**Secondary deployment:** Girls in other Homes of Hope cities with similar employment markets.

**Platform stack:**
- Asynchronous: Kindle content (Feynman-style) + Medhavy Quiz Me + Anki domain-specific word decks
- Synchronous: Weekly 60-minute Zoom with Humanitarians AI Indian volunteers
- AI tools: ChatGPT/Gemini on her phone for drafting, checking, practicing

**Not designed for:** Academic English assessment, grammar instruction, British or American English register, laptop-dependent activities.

## Learner Profile

**Her name is Priya.** 19 years old. Homes of Hope residence in Secunderabad, 6 years. Telugu first language. Tier 1–2 Anki deck complete — approximately 350 English words including basic workplace vocabulary. Can read a simple WhatsApp message in English. Cannot write a professional email. Cannot hold a 2-minute phone conversation in English without switching to Telugu. Has seen job postings on her phone but does not know how to apply. Has used ChatGPT once to translate something.

**Safe to assume:** Telugu literacy, 200–350 English vocabulary words, smartphone fluency (WhatsApp, YouTube, basic apps), some passive AI exposure, understanding that she needs a job, the sisters' values (punctuality, honesty, work ethic).

**Not safe to assume:** Professional email register, what a CV is or how to write one, what a BPO phone screen sounds like from inside, how to use AI as a work tool rather than a translator, professional English under pressure, what "customer service" means as a job function.

**Misconceptions to address:**
- "AI will do everything for me" — AI drafts. She decides. She speaks. She is accountable.
- "My English is not good enough" — Indian professional English is the target, not BBC English.
- "I need a computer for these jobs" — BPO phone screens happen on a phone.

## Three-Act Learning Arc

**Arc statement:** This course takes a girl from "I have basic English but I cannot get a job" to "I have applied, interviewed, and I know what to do when I walk in on Monday" — by first showing her that her current English is enough to start, then giving her the specific language her chosen job requires, then putting her through a real practice run with human feedback.

### Act One — You Already Have What You Need *(Weeks 1–2, Units 1–4)*

**Starting state:** "My English is not good enough for a professional job."

**Act One moves:** Show her that the English she has is enough to start. Professional register is learnable in days. AI is her assistant, not her replacement. She already has a story worth putting on a CV.

**Ending state:** She has a CV, she can write a professional message, she knows how to use AI without letting it think for her. She has chosen her track.

**Inciting question:** "Can I actually do the job?"

**Transition gate:** Student can write a professional WhatsApp message, produce a basic CV with AI assistance, and demonstrate she knows when AI is wrong. Capability demonstration with volunteer feedback — not a test score.

---

### Act Two — The Job, in Detail *(Weeks 3–6, Units 5–7, track-specific)*

**Path split at Week 3.** Girl selects one track based on interest and local job availability:

- **Track A:** BPO / Customer Service
- **Track B:** Healthcare Reception / Support
- **Track C:** NGO Administration

**What this act provides:** The specific language, scenarios, and skills of her chosen job. Not generic professional English — the actual words a BPO call requires, the actual vocabulary a hospital reception desk uses, the actual register an NGO donor expects.

**Hardest moment:** The first time she does a mock call or mock interview in English with a stranger and hears herself struggle. The volunteer facilitator's response at this moment determines whether she continues or retreats. (See: Risk 3 and volunteer briefing protocol.)

**Ending state:** She can perform the core function of her target job in a practice setting.

**Transition gate:** Student has completed 3 path units and participated in at least one Zoom mock practice session.

---

### Act Three — Ready *(Weeks 7–8, Units 8–9)*

**What this act delivers:** A real mock interview with a real volunteer who gives real feedback — and then what to do on day one of the actual job, because getting hired is not the finish line. Surviving the first week is.

**Ending capability:** She applies. She interviews. She knows what to do when she walks in on Monday.

#### Unit 1 — Professional Register
*How to sound professional in written English when you know 500 words*

**Learning outcomes:**
- (Apply) Write a professional WhatsApp message and basic professional email using appropriate register, without switching to Telugu, given a workplace situation prompt

**Problem it solves:** She knows the words but not the register. "Please help me" and "I would like to request your assistance" contain the same vocabulary but signal completely different professional levels. She does not know this distinction exists.

**Opening:** Two WhatsApp messages side by side. Same request. Different register. A volunteer shows a hiring manager both. One gets a callback. One does not. Why? That is this unit.

**Core content:**
1. What register means and why it matters in Hyderabad specifically — Indian corporate culture, not British or American
2. The five most important register shifts: greetings, requests, apologies, updates, sign-offs
3. How to use AI to check register — paste your message, ask "is this professional enough for a job application?"
4. Common Telugu-English register errors that Indian professional English does not make (specific, named, not condescending)

**Worked example:** She writes a message to a hiring manager asking about an open position. First attempt from her current level. AI feedback. Revised version. Volunteer confirms the revision is hirable-register.

**Exercises:**
1. Rewrite three casual WhatsApp messages in professional register
2. Identify the register error in five provided sentences
3. Write a professional follow-up to a job application using AI assistance, then review and correct the AI output

---

#### Unit 2 — AI as Work Tool
*How to use ChatGPT as an assistant without letting it think for you*

**Learning outcomes:**
- (Apply) Use ChatGPT/Gemini to draft a message, check her own writing for errors, and ask a clarifying question
- (Evaluate) Identify when AI output is wrong and needs correction

**Problem it solves:** She either avoids AI (intimidated) or over-relies on it (copies whatever it produces). The girl who uses AI as a drafting assistant — and who can catch its mistakes — is more valuable than the one who doesn't use it at all.

**Opening:** Two girls apply for the same job. One copies the AI's CV directly. One uses the AI to draft, then reviews it herself, catches two errors the AI made about her background, and corrects them. Same AI. Different result.

**Core content:**
1. What AI is good at for this population: drafting, translating register, suggesting vocabulary, checking spelling
2. What AI gets wrong and why: hallucinated facts about her, wrong register for Indian corporate culture, generic language that doesn't reflect her actual experience
3. The two-step workflow: prompt → review → correct. Never skip the review.
4. Prompting in simple English — "Write a professional email asking about this job [paste the job posting]" is enough

**Worked example:** She uses ChatGPT to draft a cover message for a real BPO job posting on Naukri. AI produces a draft. She identifies three things the AI got wrong or made generic. She corrects them. Volunteer reviews final version.

**Exercises:**
1. Use AI to draft a thank-you message after a mock interview. Find and fix one AI error.
2. Prompt AI to explain a word from the job posting she doesn't understand
3. Give AI a bad instruction and observe what it produces. Why did it fail? What would have made the instruction better?

---

#### Unit 3 — The CV
*One page. Her story. In hirable English.*

**Learning outcomes:**
- (Create) Produce a one-page CV appropriate for an entry-level Hyderabad job posting, using AI to assist drafting and her own judgment to verify accuracy

**Problem it solves:** She has never written a CV. She thinks she has nothing to put in it. She is wrong.

**Opening:** Naveena's story — a girl who grew up in a Homes of Hope residence is now pursuing an MBA in HR. What is on her CV? She has no work history in the traditional sense. She has something more valuable: a story. This unit teaches her how to write it.

**Core content:**
1. What a one-page CV for an entry-level Hyderabad job actually contains — and what it does not need (she does not need work history to apply for entry-level BPO)
2. How to describe the sisters' training in CV language — "moral values education" becomes "character and professional conduct training, Salesian sisters"
3. Skills she has that she doesn't know are CV-worthy: multilingual (Telugu, Hindi, basic English), community living experience, Anki literacy deck completed, smartphone and app fluency
4. The AI CV workflow: she tells AI her background in simple sentences, AI drafts, she reviews for accuracy, volunteer confirms hireability

**Worked example:** Priya's CV built from scratch. From "I have nothing to put on a CV" to a one-page document a hiring manager will read.

**Exercises:**
1. List five things from your life that could go on a CV (she will underestimate this — the exercise proves her wrong)
2. Draft your CV with AI assistance. Review it. Find anything the AI invented or got wrong.
3. Exchange CVs with another girl in the program. What would you change? *(Volunteer Zoom session activity)*

---

#### Unit 4 — The Job Search
*How to find the right posting and know if you qualify*

**Learning outcomes:**
- (Analyze) Find a relevant entry-level job posting in her target sector, identify the three most important requirements, and assess whether she currently meets them

**Problem it solves:** Job postings are written in language that assumes the reader already knows what the job involves. She cannot tell a realistic posting from an aspirational one, or know whether "fluent English" means her level or something higher.

**Opening:** Three real Hyderabad job postings. Same sector. One she is ready for now. One she could be ready for in six months. One she is not ready for. How to tell the difference.

**Core content:**
1. Where to look: Naukri.com, LinkedIn (basic profile only), company websites for Apollo Hospitals, Concentrix, Genpact, CRY, HelpAge India
2. How to read a job posting: required vs. preferred, what "fresher" means, what "English mandatory" actually means in practice vs. on the posting
3. AI as posting interpreter: "paste the posting and ask AI to explain what this job actually does day-to-day"
4. The honest self-assessment: can she do the three most important things in the posting? If two of three, she applies.

**Worked example:** Priya finds a real posting, uses AI to interpret it, honestly assesses her readiness, and decides whether to apply.

**Exercises:**
1. Find one real posting in your chosen track on Naukri. What are the three most important requirements?
2. Ask AI to explain what a word in the posting means in practice
3. Write one sentence explaining why you are applying for this specific job — not a generic sentence, a specific one

#### Unit 8 — The Mock Interview
*Practice until the nerves are smaller than the preparation*

**Learning outcomes:**
- (Evaluate + Create) Complete a full mock job interview with a Humanitarians AI volunteer, receive specific feedback on pronunciation, register, and content, and revise her answers based on that feedback

**Problem it solves:** She can do everything this course teaches in the safety of her phone. She has never said it out loud to a stranger over a video call who is evaluating her. That gap is where most people fail — not knowledge, nerves.

**Opening:** The five things every interviewer is actually evaluating that they will not tell you they are evaluating. Composure. Clarity. Confidence when you don't know the answer. Recovery when you make a mistake. The ability to listen.

**Core content:**
1. The four universal interview questions she will be asked in every sector: tell me about yourself / why do you want this job / what is your strength / what would you do if [scenario]
2. The answer framework: situation → what she did → what happened → what she learned. Not longer than 90 seconds.
3. What to do when she doesn't know the answer: the three acceptable responses and the one that ends the interview
4. Indian professional interview culture specifically — eye contact, titles, how to address the interviewer, what punctuality means in this context

**Volunteer role (Zoom session):** A Humanitarians AI volunteer from the relevant sector runs a full 15-minute mock interview. Real questions. Real feedback. Specific: not "your English is good" but "when you said X, it sounded uncertain — here is how to say it with more confidence."

**Exercises:**
1. Record a 90-second "tell me about yourself" on her phone. Listen back. What would she change?
2. Answer three mock interview questions in writing using the situation framework
3. Complete the Zoom mock interview — **this is the unit completion gate. She does not pass this unit without the live session.**

---

#### Unit 9 — The First Week
*Getting the job is not the finish line*

**Learning outcomes:**
- (Apply) Describe what to expect in the first week of a job in her chosen track — what to wear, how to address colleagues, what AI tools she can use without asking, what she should never do without asking

**Problem it solves:** Girls who have never had a professional job do not know what to do on day one. The professional norms — when to speak, when not to, how to address a supervisor, what to do when you don't understand an instruction — are not obvious. Getting this wrong in the first week costs the job.

**Opening:** The two biggest reasons new hires fail in the first week in Hyderabad BPO and healthcare. Neither is English. Both are professional behavior gaps that nobody told them about.

**Core content:**
1. What to wear (track-specific — BPO dress code is different from hospital support dress code)
2. How to address colleagues and supervisors — Indian corporate hierarchy norms
3. The AI tools she can use without asking: drafting messages, looking up words, checking spelling. The AI tools she should ask about first: anything customer-facing.
4. What to do when she doesn't understand an instruction — the exact phrase to use that signals professional competence, not ignorance

**Special note:** This is the unit the sisters' teaching has been preparing her for for years. The values she learned — punctuality, honesty, respect — are professional assets here. Name them explicitly.

## Out of Scope

| Topic | Reason for exclusion |
|---|---|
| Grammar instruction | The course builds professional capability through pattern exposure and practice, not grammar rules. If she writes professional messages that get callbacks, the grammar is working. |
| Academic English (IELTS, TOEFL, university application) | Different product for a different population |
| Coding or technical skills | Require a computer, a longer timeline, and a different prerequisite foundation. Course 3. |
| Job placement guarantee | This course produces readiness. The sisters' network produces placement. These are not the same thing and the course should not conflate them. |
| Certificate or credential | The credential is the job offer. A certificate from a nonprofit program nobody has heard of has no market value. Building market credibility for the credential is Course 4 or 5, not now. |

**Coherence note:** The excluded topics — coding, credentials, job placement — together suggest a clear Course 3 and Course 4. Scope this book to readiness. Readiness is enough.

## Top Adoption Risks

### Risk 1 — Volunteer quality inconsistency in Zoom sessions
**Likelihood:** High. **Impact:** High.

The mock interview and mock call sessions are the load-bearing human layer. A volunteer who gives vague positive feedback ("your English is good!") produces no learning. A volunteer who is harsh or culturally insensitive produces withdrawal.

**Mitigation:** One-page volunteer briefing before each session: the girl's track, her current level, the three specific things to give feedback on, the two things never to say. Volunteer coordinator reviews session notes after each call.

---

### Risk 2 — Path choice paralysis
**Likelihood:** Medium. **Impact:** Medium.

Being asked to choose BPO vs. healthcare vs. NGO at Week 2 may produce anxiety in girls who have never been asked to choose a professional path before. Wrong choice does not cost the course — all paths converge at Week 7 — but the paralysis itself does.

**Mitigation:** The sisters know which girls are interested in which sectors before the course begins. Brief the sisters on the three paths before Course 2 launches. Sisters guide the choice in conversation, not as a formal assessment.

---

### Risk 3 — The first mock call failure
**Likelihood:** High. **Impact:** High.

The first time a girl does a mock BPO call or mock interview in English with a stranger, she will probably struggle. If the volunteer response is evaluative rather than coaching, she will disengage. This is the Pekrun anxiety-quadrant failure mode: low perceived control plus high stakes equals withdrawal.

**Mitigation:** The first Zoom session is explicitly framed as "practice, not evaluation." The volunteer's only job in Session 1 is to complete the exercise and say one specific thing she did well and one specific thing to try differently. Not a grade. Not a judgment. A coaching note.
